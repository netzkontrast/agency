# Choir

## Genre Overview

Choral music traces its roots to the earliest forms of communal singing in Western civilization, with plainchant from the 9th century representing the oldest notated examples. During the medieval period, organum introduced multiple melodic parts, and the Renaissance (1400-1600) elevated sacred choral writing to its peak through composers like Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina, whose masses and motets for the Vatican defined polyphonic perfection, and Thomas Tallis, whose 40-voice motet *Spem in Alium* (c. 1570) remains one of the most ambitious choral works ever composed. Josquin des Prez, William Byrd, and Orlando di Lasso further established the choral tradition that would become the foundation of Western classical music.

The Baroque era brought Johann Sebastian Bach's cantatas, passions, and the monumental *Mass in B Minor*, while George Frideric Handel's *Messiah* (1741) became perhaps the most performed choral work in history. The Classical and Romantic periods expanded choral forces: Mozart's *Requiem* (1791), Beethoven's *Missa Solemnis* (1823), Brahms's *Ein deutsches Requiem* (1868), and Verdi's *Requiem* (1874) pushed the form toward orchestral scale and dramatic intensity. The 20th century shattered conventions through Stravinsky's *Symphony of Psalms* (1930), Britten's *War Requiem* (1962), and Arvo Part's minimalist sacred works beginning in the 1970s.

Today, choral music thrives across sacred and secular contexts, from cathedral choirs and professional ensembles to community choruses and competitive a cappella groups. Eric Whitacre's virtual choirs have brought choral participation to thousands worldwide, Pentatonix (three Grammy Awards, over 10 million albums sold) have made a cappella a mainstream pop phenomenon, and ensembles like VOCES8 and The Sixteen continue the tradition of elite vocal performance. Contemporary composers including Whitacre, Ola Gjeilo, and John Rutter write for choirs at every level, while gospel, barbershop, and world choral traditions maintain distinct identities within the broader choral landscape.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Human voices arranged in parts (typically SATB: soprano, alto, tenor, bass); may be a cappella or accompanied by organ, piano, orchestra, or chamber ensemble. Period instruments for historically informed performance. Some modern works incorporate electronics or non-traditional sound sources.
- **Vocals**: Trained classical vocal production with emphasis on blend, intonation, and unified vowel sounds. Ranges from solo voices within choral texture to massed choirs of 100+ singers. Vibrato use varies by tradition: early music ensembles favor straight tone, Romantic repertoire embraces full vibrato, contemporary works may specify either.
- **Production**: Acoustic performance is the standard; recording prioritizes natural room ambience and spatial imaging. Cathedral and concert hall acoustics are integral to the sound. Modern a cappella production may use close-miking, compression, and effects processing. Studio choral recordings for film and media use layered overdubs and reverb to create massive soundscapes.
- **Energy/Mood**: Spans the full emotional spectrum: transcendent and meditative in sacred works, jubilant in festival pieces, devastating in requiems, playful in madrigals, raw and powerful in contemporary settings. The collective human voice carries inherent emotional weight that few other ensembles can match.
- **Structure**: Varies enormously by era and form. Mass settings follow liturgical structure (Kyrie, Gloria, Credo, Sanctus, Agnus Dei). Motets and anthems are through-composed or sectional. Modern choral works may use strophic, rondo, or free-form structures. A cappella pop arrangements follow verse-chorus form.
- **Tempo**: Highly variable: slow sustained chords at 40-60 BPM in Renaissance polyphony and meditative works, moderate 60-100 BPM for hymns and anthems, up to 120-160 BPM for energetic gospel, barbershop tags, and rhythmically driven contemporary pieces.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Varies by tradition. Hymns: ABAB or AABB. Madrigals: follows the poetry set (often ABAB or free verse). Sacred Latin texts: no rhyme scheme (prose). Contemporary choral: often free verse or through-composed poetry.
- **Rhyme quality**: Depends on text source. Original English-language choral works favor clean end rhymes in hymn style; art music settings prioritize the poetry's inherent structure over imposed rhyme. Internal rhyme used sparingly.
- **Verse structure**: Hymns: 4-line stanzas (common meter, long meter, short meter). Art music: follows the source text. A cappella pop: standard verse-chorus with 4-8 line sections. Liturgical texts have fixed structures.
- **Key rule**: Text clarity is paramount. Every syllable must be intelligible through the choral texture. Word painting (matching musical gesture to textual meaning) is a centuries-old tradition that remains central to effective choral writing.
- **Avoid**: Overly dense text that gets lost in polyphonic texture. Awkward syllable stress that fights the musical meter. Sibilant-heavy passages that create ensemble hissing. Text that doesn't breathe -- singers need phrase breaks.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 70-100 BPM. Choral music requires space for harmonies to resonate. Topics: 1/verse. Fewer words with longer sustained notes work better than dense text. Suno handles choral textures best with simple, repeated phrases.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Renaissance Polyphony** | Complex interwoven vocal lines moving independently in 4-6+ parts; Latin sacred texts; pure intonation and blended tone; contrapuntal mastery with imitative entries and suspensions. | Palestrina, Tallis, Byrd, Josquin des Prez |
| **Baroque Choral** | Grand choral-orchestral works including cantatas, oratorios, passions, and masses; figured bass accompaniment; terraced dynamics; fugal writing and dramatic text painting. | J.S. Bach, Handel, Vivaldi, Purcell |
| **Romantic Choral** | Large-scale works for choir and orchestra with expanded harmonic language and emotional intensity; massive dynamic range; virtuosic choral writing demanding professional-level ensembles. | Brahms, Verdi, Faure, Dvorak |
| **Contemporary Classical Choral** | Modern compositional techniques including cluster chords, extended vocal techniques, aleatoric elements, and minimalist repetition; texts range from sacred to poetry to original. | Eric Whitacre, Arvo Part, John Rutter, Morten Lauridsen |
| **Gospel Choir** | African American sacred tradition with call-and-response, improvisation, and emotional intensity; powerful lead soloists backed by massed choir; Hammond organ and rhythm section accompaniment. | Edwin Hawkins Singers, Brooklyn Tabernacle Choir, Kirk Franklin |
| **A Cappella Pop** | Unaccompanied vocal arrangements of popular songs with vocal percussion (beatboxing), bass vocals, and tight harmonies; originated in collegiate singing groups and expanded through competition shows. | Pentatonix, Straight No Chaser, Rajaton, Rockapella |
| **Barbershop** | Four-part close harmony singing (lead, tenor, baritone, bass) emphasizing ringing overtones and locked chords; predominantly American tradition dating to the late 19th century; both quartet and chorus formats. | Ringmasters, Crossroads, Sweet Adelines |
| **English Cathedral** | Anglican choral tradition featuring boy trebles and adult men; daily services with psalms, canticles, and anthems; pure tone, impeccable diction, and restrained emotional expression in resonant stone acoustics. | Choir of King's College Cambridge, Westminster Abbey Choir, Choir of St John's College |
| **World Choral** | Choral traditions from outside Western classical canon including South African isicathamiya, Baltic song festival choirs, Bulgarian women's choirs, and Georgian polyphony; each with distinct vocal techniques and harmonic systems. | Ladysmith Black Mambazo, The Bulgarian State Television Female Vocal Choir, Estonian Philharmonic Chamber Choir |

## Reference Tracks

- **Thomas Tallis - "Spem in Alium"** -- A 40-voice Renaissance motet composed around 1570, where eight five-part choirs create a surround-sound effect of staggering complexity. The work builds from a single voice to a full 40-part climax, demonstrating the peak of Renaissance polyphonic ambition.

- **J.S. Bach - "Komm, susser Tod" (BWV 478)** -- Bach's chorale harmonizations set the standard for four-part writing that every choral musician studies. His ability to create expressive, complete harmonic worlds within a simple hymn tune remains unmatched.

- **Handel - "Hallelujah Chorus" from *Messiah*** -- Premiered in Dublin in 1742, this chorus became the most recognized piece of choral music in the Western world. Its alternation between homophonic proclamation and fugal writing exemplifies Baroque choral drama.

- **Mozart - "Lacrimosa" from *Requiem*** -- Left incomplete at Mozart's death in 1791, the "Lacrimosa" builds from a weeping orchestral figure to a devastating choral climax. It demonstrates how choral writing can convey grief with unbearable intensity.

- **Brahms - "Wie lieblich sind deine Wohnungen" from *Ein deutsches Requiem*** -- The fourth movement of Brahms's 1868 requiem wraps the choir in warm, luminous harmony. It shows how Romantic choral writing balances intimacy and grandeur without theatrical excess.

- **Arvo Part - "Spiegel im Spiegel"** -- Part's tintinnabuli style, developed in the 1970s, strips choral music to its harmonic essence. His sacred works for choir including *Da Pacem* demonstrate how minimal means can produce maximum spiritual impact.

- **Eric Whitacre - "Sleep"** -- Whitacre's signature work (2000) layers dense cluster chords that shimmer and resolve in waves. The piece popularized a lush, cinematic choral sound that influenced a generation of composers and became a standard for advanced high school and collegiate choirs.

- **Pentatonix - "Daft Punk"** -- This 2013 a cappella medley won the Grammy for Best Arrangement, Instrumental or A Cappella, and demonstrated that five voices could replicate electronic production with studio polish. It brought a cappella to mainstream pop audiences.

- **Allegri - "Miserere"** -- Written for the Sistine Chapel in the 1630s, this psalm setting features a soaring soprano line reaching high C above the staff. The Vatican kept the score secret for over a century, adding to its legendary mystique.

- **Edwin Hawkins Singers - "Oh Happy Day"** -- Released in 1969, this gospel arrangement reached No. 4 on the Billboard Hot 100 and introduced gospel choir music to a global pop audience. Its call-and-response structure and joyful energy defined the modern gospel choir sound.

- **Ladysmith Black Mambazo - "Homeless"** -- Featured on Paul Simon's *Graceland* (1986), this track introduced South African isicathamiya choral singing to worldwide audiences. The gentle, interlocking male voices create rhythmic and harmonic textures entirely distinct from Western choral traditions.

- **Morten Lauridsen - "O Magnum Mysterium"** -- Composed in 1994, this setting became one of the most performed choral works of the late 20th century. Its slow-moving harmonies and luminous climax demonstrate how contemporary sacred choral music can achieve universal emotional resonance.
