# Future House

## Genre Overview

Future house emerged in the early-to-mid 2010s as a fresh mutation of house music, fusing deep house warmth, UK garage rhythmic DNA, and modern EDM production polish. The term was coined by French DJ and producer Tchami (Martin Music Brun), who tagged his 2013 remix of Janet Jackson's "Go Deep" as "future house" on SoundCloud. Tchami later clarified in a 2015 interview that he meant the label loosely -- "any kind of house music that hasn't been invented yet" -- but the name stuck. Around the same time, Dutch producer Oliver Heldens was developing a parallel sound: his 2013 track "Gecko" caught the attention of Tiesto, who signed it to his Musical Freedom label. The vocal version "Gecko (Overdrive)" featuring Becky Hill became a UK top-five hit in 2014, and the genre suddenly had a mainstream face. Heldens and Tchami traded influence and occasional public friction, but their combined output defined the genre's sonic boundaries.

By 2015-2016, future house had solidified into a recognized genre with a growing roster of producers. Don Diablo brought a polished, pop-leaning approach through his Hexagon label, scoring Billboard Hot Dance/Electronic top-three hits with tracks like "On My Mind" (2016). Spinnin' Records became a major pipeline for the sound, signing acts like Curbi, Mike Williams, Brooks, and Mesto, who pushed the genre toward brighter, bouncier territory that some producers began calling "future bounce." Tchami's Confession label championed a darker, funkier strain rooted in bass house and fidget influences. The genre's accessibility made it a staple of festival main stages and Spotify playlists alike, bridging the gap between underground house credibility and pop-crossover appeal. Regional scenes developed in the Netherlands, France, and the UK, each adding distinct flavors.

The current future house landscape has matured considerably since its mid-2010s peak. Oliver Heldens continues to release prolifically through Heldeep Records, collaborating with artists ranging from Kylie Minogue to Sérgio Mendes. Tchami delivered his debut album *Year Zero* in 2020, a 16-track statement that expanded the genre's emotional range with collaborations featuring Gunna and ZHU. Don Diablo's *Future* (2018) remains a touchstone album for the pop-inflected wing of the genre. The sound's influence has bled into mainstream pop production, tropical house, and bass house, while newer producers continue to push its boundaries with harder bass design, organic instrumentation, and vocal-driven arrangements. Future house's metallic basslines, pitched vocal chops, and sidechain-pumped grooves have become part of the broader electronic music vocabulary.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Synthesizers (Serum, Massive, Sylenth1 dominant in production), frequency-modulated basslines with metallic or rubbery timbres, pitched vocal chops and samples, plucked synth leads, piano stabs, four-on-the-floor kick drums, layered claps and snares, shaker and hi-hat patterns, string and pad textures for breakdowns
- **Vocals**: Pitched and chopped vocal samples as melodic hooks, soulful or pop-style toplines on vocal tracks, processed vocal stabs used rhythmically, Auto-Tuned or vocoder-treated lead vocals, breathy female vocals common on crossover tracks, minimal lyrical content in instrumental versions
- **Production**: Four-on-the-floor kick pattern at 124-128 BPM, prominent sidechain compression creating rhythmic pumping effect, frequency-modulated (FM) bass synthesis producing the genre's signature metallic/elastic tone, layered builds with filter sweeps and risers, clean and polished mix aesthetic, heavy use of reverb and delay on melodic elements, crisp transients on percussion
- **Energy/Mood**: Uplifting and feel-good at its core, ranging from funky and groovy (Tchami school) to bright and euphoric (Heldens/Don Diablo school); energetic without aggression; designed for peak-time festival sets and daytime parties; accessible and pop-friendly
- **Structure**: Standard EDM arrangement (intro, build, drop, breakdown, second build, second drop, outro), typically 3-5 minutes for singles; drops feature the signature FM bass and vocal chop hooks; breakdowns strip back to pads and vocal elements; builds use filtered percussion, risers, and snare rolls; DJ-friendly intros and outros
- **Tempo**: 124-128 BPM standard, occasionally stretching to 130 BPM; straight 4/4 feel with shuffled hi-hat patterns adding groove; half-time feel sometimes used in breakdowns

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Minimal -- short phrases or single-line hooks repeated over drops. AABB or free form when present.
- **Rhyme quality**: Incidental. Vocal hooks prioritize singability and rhythmic fit over rhyme complexity.
- **Verse structure**: No traditional verse structure in most tracks. Pop-crossover tracks may use a short 4-8 line verse leading into a pre-chorus build, but many tracks are instrumental or use vocal chops only.
- **Key rule**: The vocal serves the groove and the drop -- it must lock to the rhythm and enhance the energy arc, never compete with the bass or melodic hooks.
- **Avoid**: Dense lyrical content, complex storytelling, heavy emotional weight. Future house vocals are functional and euphoric, not confessional.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2 lines/verse** at 126 BPM. Groove-driven -- single hook phrases repeated. Pop-crossover tracks: **4 lines/verse** max. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Future House** | The original Heldens/Tchami template: FM bass wobbles, pitched vocal chops, groovy percussion, and soulful atmospheres; the sound that defined the genre from 2013-2016 | Oliver Heldens, Tchami, Don Diablo, Curbi |
| **Future Bounce** | Brighter, bouncier evolution with prominent supersaw chords, uplifting melodies, and poppier structures; emerged around 2016-2017 as a distinct offshoot | Brooks, Mike Williams, Mesto, Lucas & Steve |
| **Bass House / Future Bass House** | Heavier variant emphasizing distorted, growling basslines and more aggressive drops; blurs the line between future house and bass house | Tchami (darker material), Malaa, Habstrakt, JOYRYDE |
| **Pop Future House** | Radio-friendly crossover productions with full vocal performances, verse-chorus structures, and polished pop songwriting over future house production | Don Diablo, Sam Feldt, Lucas & Steve |
| **Dark Future House** | Moodier, deeper strain with minor-key melodies, atmospheric pads, and heavier bass design; draws from deep house and tech house | Tchami, Confession label artists, Brohug |
| **Organic Future House** | Incorporates live instruments -- guitars, brass, pianos -- into the future house framework; warmer and more textured than the synth-pure approach | Disclosure (adjacent), Watermat, Bakermat |

## Reference Tracks

- **Tchami - "Promesses" (feat. Kaleem Taylor)** -- Released in 2014, this track is widely considered one of the genre's founding documents. The combination of a funky, filtered bassline with soulful vocal delivery over shuffled house percussion established the template for Tchami's strain of future house. It reached number 7 on the UK Singles Chart in 2015.
- **Oliver Heldens - "Gecko (Overdrive)" (feat. Becky Hill)** -- The 2014 vocal version of Heldens' 2013 instrumental breakout. The track's rubbery FM bassline, chopped vocal hooks, and groovy four-on-the-floor beat became the sonic blueprint for mainstream future house. It peaked at number 4 on the UK Singles Chart and introduced the genre to pop audiences.
- **Oliver Heldens & Shaun Frank - "Shades of Grey" (feat. Delaney Jane)** -- A 2015 release that demonstrated future house's pop-crossover potential, pairing Heldens' signature bass design with a full vocal performance and verse-chorus songwriting. Showed the genre could accommodate traditional song structures.
- **Tchami - "Afterlife" (feat. Stacy Barthe)** -- A darker, deeper entry in the future house catalog that showcased the genre's range beyond its feel-good default. The moody atmospherics and restrained vocal performance demonstrated that future house could carry emotional weight.
- **Don Diablo - "On My Mind"** -- Released in 2016, this track peaked at number 3 on Billboard's Hot Dance/Electronic Songs chart. Don Diablo's polished, melodic production and pop sensibility made it a gateway track for listeners crossing over from mainstream pop to future house.
- **Oliver Heldens - "Last All Night (Koala)"** -- Another early Heldens staple that cemented the genre's sonic identity. The bouncy, percussive groove and pitched vocal chops became hallmarks that dozens of producers would emulate.
- **Tchami - "World to Me" (feat. Luke James)** -- A soulful, vocally driven future house track that connected the genre to its R&B and garage roots. Luke James' vocal performance elevated the production beyond typical club fare.
- **Curbi - "Hoohah"** -- A high-energy, percussion-forward future house track that demonstrated the genre's ability to generate raw dancefloor power without relying on big-room tropes. Curbi's breakout single and a Heldeep Records landmark.
- **Don Diablo - "Bad"** -- A slick, bass-driven future house track with a pop-friendly vocal hook. Representative of Don Diablo's ability to balance dancefloor functionality with radio-ready accessibility.
- **Brooks - "Boomerang" (with GRX)** -- A future bounce anthem that pushed the genre in a brighter, more euphoric direction. The supersaw chords and bouncy drop became defining elements of the future bounce offshoot.
- **Disclosure - "Latch" (feat. Sam Smith)** -- While Disclosure's sound is rooted in UK garage, "Latch" (2012) was a direct influence on future house's vocal approach and rhythmic template. Sam Smith's breakout vocal over shuffled beats and pitched bass prefigured the genre's pop-crossover wing.
- **Mesto & Curbi - "Bruh"** -- A collaborative track that captured the playful, youthful energy of the Dutch future house scene. The stripped-back, groove-focused production showcased the genre's fun side.
