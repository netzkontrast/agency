## Reference Tracks

### Blink-182
- "Dammit" (Dude Ranch, 1997) — The breakout; simple, perfect pop-punk hook with a "growing up sucks" message
- "What's My Age Again?" (Enema of the State, 1999) — Peak Blink-182; irresistible melody, juvenile humor, Barker's drumming adding depth
- "All the Small Things" (Enema of the State, 1999) — Biggest hit; pop-punk love song that crossed over to mainstream pop
- "Adam's Song" (Enema of the State, 1999) — Suicidal loneliness on an album full of jokes; devastating Hoppus vocal
- "I Miss You" (Blink-182, 2003) — Atmospheric, emotionally raw; acoustic intro, tender vocal, whispered bridge
- "Feeling This" (Blink-182, 2003) — Explosive opening statement; alternating vocals building to combined chorus
- "Stay Together for the Kids" (Take Off Your Pants and Jacket, 2001) — Divorce from the child's perspective; heavy and genuine
- "One More Time" (One More Time..., 2023) — Reunion anthem; bandmates confronting mortality and friendship

### NOFX
- "Linoleum" (Punk in Drublic, 1994) — Quintessential NOFX; fast, melodic, emotionally devastating
- "Don't Call Me White" (Punk in Drublic, 1994) — Promotional single from breakthrough album
- "Stickin in My Eye" (White Trash, Two Heebs and a Bean, 1992) — El Hefe era debut; trumpet in fast punk
- "Bob" (White Trash, Two Heebs and a Bean, 1992) — Classic NOFX humor and hooks
- "The Decline" (The Decline EP, 1999) — 18:20 progressive punk epic
- "The Idiots Are Taking Over" (The War on Errorism, 2003) — Peak political NOFX
- "Franco Un-American" (The War on Errorism, 2003) — Political awakening anthem
- "Leaving Jesusland" (Wolves in Wolves' Clothing, 2006) — Anti-theocracy, emotionally complex

### Lagwagon
- "Violins" (Hoss, 1995) — Most well-known track; fast, melodic, emotionally devastating
- "May 16" (Let's Talk About Feelings, 1998) — Widely recognized via Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 2
- "Razor Burn" (Hoss, 1995) — Lighthearted breakup song; popular video
- "Island of Shame" (Trashed, 1994) — Mature lyrics about small-town homophobia
- "Rifle" (Hoss, 1995) — Plourde's drumming apex
- "Move the Car" (Hoss, 1995) — Odd time signatures; technical showcase
- "Ride the Snake" (Hoss, 1995) — Wah pedal work on heroin struggle narrative
- "Heartbreaking Music" (Resolve, 2005) — Plourde tribute

### Green Day
- "Basket Case" (Dookie, 1994) — Quintessential Green Day; anxiety-driven pop-punk hook
- "Longview" (Dookie, 1994) — Iconic bass intro; suburban boredom as punk anthem
- "When I Come Around" (Dookie, 1994) — Rock radio staple; power chords + melody
- "Welcome to Paradise" (Dookie, 1994) — East Bay punk at most anthemic
- "American Idiot" (American Idiot, 2004) — Opening salvo of rock opera
- "Boulevard of Broken Dreams" (American Idiot, 2004) — Grammy Record of the Year
- "Holiday" (American Idiot, 2004) — Anti-war anthem with building intensity
- "Jesus of Suburbia" (American Idiot, 2004) — 9-minute five-movement punk suite

### Masked Intruder
- "Heart Shaped Guitar" (Masked Intruder, 2012) — Call-and-response duet with doo-wop harmonies
- "25 to Life" (Masked Intruder, 2012) — Hyper-catchy opener; criminal love song
- "Crime Spree" (M.I., 2014) — Best balance of crime and love themes; Watch Dogs 2 feature
- "No Case" (III, 2019) — Arena-bound punk anthem
- "Stick 'em Up" (Masked Intruder, 2012) — Fastest, most punk track
- "B & E" (III, 2019) — Breaking and entering as romantic gesture
- "Hello Beautiful" (Masked Intruder, 2012) — Home invasion turns love at first sight
- "Almost Like We're Already In Love" (M.I., 2014) — Entirely a cappella doo-wop

### Mest
- "Cadillac" (Destination Unknown, 2001) — Quintessential Mest; fast, catchy, energetic live staple
- "Drawing Board" (Destination Unknown, 2001) — Frantic beat, urgent vocals, pure pop-punk adrenaline
- "Jaded (These Years)" (Mest, 2003) — Biggest single; feat. Benji Madden of Good Charlotte
- "Rooftops" (Mest, 2003) — Anthemic pop-punk with emotional hook
- "What's the Dillio?" (Wasting Time, 2000) — First major-label single; raw pop-punk energy
- "Fuct Up Kid" (Destination Unknown, 2001) — Grittier, punk-leaning; skate punk edge
- "Kiss Me, Kill Me" (Photographs, 2005) — More mature, darker pop-punk evolution
- "Take Me Away (Cried Out to Heaven)" (Photographs, 2005) — Introspective vulnerability

### Rancid
- "Roots Radicals" (...And Out Come the Wolves, 1995) — Quintessential Rancid; ska-punk groove, slurred vocals, melodic bass
- "Ruby Soho" (...And Out Come the Wolves, 1995) — Most melodic and accessible; pop hooks in street punk
- "Time Bomb" (...And Out Come the Wolves, 1995) — Definitive ska-punk; upstroking guitars, brass accents, rude boy narrative
- "Maxwell Murder" (...And Out Come the Wolves, 1995) — Famous punk bass solo; blistering hardcore energy
- "Salvation" (Let's Go, 1994) — Driving hooky punk; first mainstream exposure
- "Fall Back Down" (Indestructible, 2003) — Anthemic friendship punk; massive singalong chorus
- "Radio" (Let's Go, 1994) — Co-written with Billie Joe Armstrong; punk ode to music
- "Journey to the End of the East Bay" (...And Out Come the Wolves, 1995) — Scene tribute; celebrated bassline

### Me First and the Gimme Gimmes
- "Somewhere Over the Rainbow" (Are a Drag, 1999) — Most recognizable track; show tune as punk; featured in Dodgeball
- "Jolene" (Love Their Country, 2006) — Dolly Parton cover; desperate pleading becomes frantic punk urgency
- "Danny's Song" (Have a Ball, 1997) — Loggins and Messina soft rock turned punchy pop-punk
- "Goodbye Earl" (Love Their Country, 2006) — Dixie Chicks murder ballad amplified by punk energy
- "Country Roads" (Denver EP, 1995) — Their very first recording; the song that started it all
- "Elenore" (Blow in the Wind, 2001) — Opens with Clash "London Calling" riff; punk crossover moment
- "My Heart Will Go On" (Are We Not Men? We Are Diva!, 2014) — Titanic theme as breakneck punk
- "End of the Road" (Take a Break, 2003) — Boyz II Men R&B transformed into charging punk

### Bad Religion
- "Suffer" (Suffer, 1988) — Title track of the album that changed everything; fast, tight, intellectually charged; the "oozin' aahs" at full power
- "No Control" (No Control, 1989) — Breakneck speed, philosophical lyrics about determinism, three-part harmonies over relentless power chords
- "21st Century (Digital Boy)" (Against the Grain, 1990) — One of punk's most iconic riffs; pre-internet alienation anthem; reference for catchy intellectual punk
- "American Jesus" (Recipe for Hate, 1993) — Mid-tempo by Bad Religion standards; searing critique of American exceptionalism; Eddie Vedder sings backing vocals
- "Sorrow" (The Process of Belief, 2002) — One of punk's most emotionally direct songs; the "oozin' aahs" carry the melody
- "I Want to Conquer the World" (No Control, 1989) — Bright, anthemic, harmonies soaring over fast punk; optimistic despite the ironic title
- "Los Angeles Is Burning" (The Empire Strikes First, 2004) — Their biggest chart single; driving, urgent, politically charged
- "The Kids Are Alt-Right" (Age of Unreason, 2019) — Late-career proof they still had teeth; punning title, sharp lyrics, classic energy

### The Offspring
- "Come Out and Play (Keep 'Em Separated)" (Smash, 1994) — The breakthrough; Middle Eastern-scale guitar intro over driving punk; #1 Modern Rock
- "Self Esteem" (Smash, 1994) — Mid-tempo grinding riff, self-deprecating lyrics, one of punk's most recognizable hooks
- "The Kids Aren't Alright" (Americana, 1998) — Dark suburban social commentary wrapped in an anthemic punk package; #1 Active Rock
- "Pretty Fly (for a White Guy)" (Americana, 1998) — Satirical pop-punk at its most commercial; sardonic humor-driven punk with massive pop hooks
- "Gone Away" (Ixnay on the Hombre, 1997) — Slow-building punk power ballad about grief; Holland at his most emotionally vulnerable; #1 Mainstream Rock
- "You're Gonna Go Far, Kid" (Rise and Fall, Rage and Grace, 2008) — 11 weeks at #1 on Modern Rock; driving, anthemic, irresistible melody
- "All I Want" (Ixnay on the Hombre, 1997) — Blistering speed, breakneck vocal delivery, pure SoCal punk aggression
- "Bad Habit" (Smash, 1994) — Road rage as punk catharsis; fast, loud, furious; dark humor at full throttle

### Descendents
- "Myage" (Milo Goes to College, 1982) — The quintessential Descendents track; yearning, melodic, personal lyrics at hardcore speed
- "Hope" (Milo Goes to College, 1982) — So perfectly pop-punk that Blink-182 covered it note-for-note; the blueprint in miniature
- "Suburban Home" (Milo Goes to College, 1982) — "I want to be stereotyped / I want to be classified"; anti-suburban anthem with humor and velocity
- "I'm the One" (Milo Goes to College, 1982) — Urgent, lovesick, impossibly catchy; romantic desperation as a hardcore war cry
- "Good Good Things" (I Don't Want to Grow Up, 1985) — Mature melody, earnest emotion, technical precision; the band's growth while retaining core identity
- "ALL" (ALL, 1987) — The philosophical centerpiece; total commitment as punk ethos; first recording with the classic Egerton/Alvarez lineup
- "Coffee Mug" (Everything Sucks, 1996) — The caffeine anthem; perfectly captures jittery, wired energy and the mundane made urgent
- "Feel This" (Hypercaffium Spazzinate, 2016) — Three decades in and still blistering; proof that the formula ages well when the sincerity is genuine

### Propagandhi
- "Anti-Manifesto" (How to Clean Everything, 1993) — Album opener and statement of intent; fast, catchy, politically charged skate punk
- "Ska Sucks" (How to Clean Everything, 1993) — Tongue-in-cheek track that became an ironic anthem; captures early humor and irreverence
- "...And We Thought Nation-States Were a Bad Idea" (Less Talk More Rock, 1996) — One of their most iconic tracks; the title alone is a political thesis
- "Back to the Motor League" (Today's Empires Tomorrow's Ashes, 2001) — The transformation made undeniable; critique of punk scene conformity over thrash-influenced riffing
- "A Speculative Fiction" (Potemkin City Limits, 2005) — Won the 2006 SOCAN Songwriting Prize; technical, furious, and lyrically dense
- "Night Letters" (Supporting Caste, 2009) — Live staple; displacement and political violence over technically demanding dual-guitar interplay
- "Note to Self" (Failed States, 2012) — Six-minute opening opus; sprawling progressive structure; architecturally ambitious punk
- "Victory Lap" (Victory Lap, 2017) — Title track and one of their heaviest moments; the most thrash metal Propagandhi track
