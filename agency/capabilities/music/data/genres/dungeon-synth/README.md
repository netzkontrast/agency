# Dungeon Synth

## Genre Overview

Dungeon synth is an electronic music genre that emerged from the early 1990s Norwegian black metal scene, combining dark ambient textures with medieval and fantasy themes to create immersive, instrumental soundscapes. The genre's origins are inseparable from black metal: musicians who played in extreme metal bands began recording ambient, synthesizer-based compositions as side projects or interludes on metal albums. Mortiis, the project of Havard Ellefsen (formerly bassist of Emperor), released his first demo in 1993 and was the first to use the phrase "dark dungeon music" to describe the style. His albums *Fodt til a Herske* (1993) and *Anden som Gjorde Oppror* (1994) established the foundational sound: lo-fi synthesizer recordings evoking castle corridors, enchanted forests, and mythological landscapes.

The genre developed through the mid-1990s alongside the second wave of black metal. Varg Vikernes of Burzum, while imprisoned, recorded *Dauoi Baldrs* (1997) and *Hlidskjalf* (1999), entirely synthesizer-based albums composed in prison that became cornerstones of the style. Other key early acts included Depressive Silence, whose *Depressive Silence II* (1996) brought an ethereal, otherworldly quality, and Secret Stairways, whose *Enchantment of the Ring* (1997) infused melancholic medieval atmosphere. Jim Kirkwood, Wongraven (a project of Satyr from Satyricon), and Lamentation also contributed essential early recordings. Before 2011, the music existed without a unified name, scattered under labels like "dark dungeon music," "neoclassical," or simply filed alongside dark ambient and darkwave.

The genre was formally named and codified in 2011 when Andrew Werdna founded The Dungeon Synth Blog, creating a community around the scattered recordings. This catalyzed a revival: French artist Erang launched his project in 2012 with *Another World Another Time* (2013), becoming one of the most prolific modern practitioners. Old Tower, Hole Dweller, Fief, and dozens of Bandcamp-based projects emerged through the 2010s, expanding the genre's palette from strict medieval gloom to include comfy dungeon synth (warm, cozy atmospheres), forest synth (nature themes), and winter synth (cold, isolated soundscapes). The genre maintains a deliberately underground, DIY ethos, with cassette tapes and Bandcamp as primary distribution channels and hand-drawn fantasy art as standard cover aesthetics.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Analog and digital synthesizers (Casio, Roland, Korg), MIDI keyboards, digital audio workstations; flute and recorder samples, choir patches, string pads, bells, harp samples; deliberately limited to keyboard-generated sounds in most cases; occasional acoustic instruments (recorder, acoustic guitar) in modern productions
- **Vocals**: Predominantly instrumental. When present, vocals are typically distant chanting, whispered narration, choral pads, or sampled medieval-style singing; no conventional singing or rapping; spoken-word samples from fantasy films occasionally used
- **Production**: Deliberately lo-fi in the classic style: cassette-tape hiss, limited frequency range, mono or narrow stereo; newer productions range from faithful lo-fi to cleaner digital production; reverb used heavily to create cavernous spaces; minimal mastering; the lo-fi quality is an aesthetic choice evoking ancient, degraded recordings
- **Energy/Mood**: Meditative, atmospheric, melancholic, mysterious, foreboding, enchanted; ranges from dark and oppressive dungeon atmosphere to tranquil forest wandering; escapist fantasy and introspection; isolation and solitude as emotional constants; nostalgia for imagined medieval worlds
- **Structure**: Long-form compositions often 5-15 minutes; minimal development with gradual layering of textures; repetitive motifs that evolve slowly; suite-like structures with distinct movements; no verses, choruses, or pop structures; albums designed as continuous journeys
- **Tempo**: Slow to mid-tempo; typically 60-100 BPM when rhythmic elements exist; much of the genre is arhythmic or uses sustained pads without clear pulse; when percussion appears, it is sparse and ceremonial (timpani, hand drums); march-like rhythms at 80-90 BPM in more structured pieces

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Almost entirely instrumental. When text appears, it functions as narration or incantation rather than conventional lyrics.
- **Rhyme quality**: N/A for most dungeon synth. Narrated passages use archaic, formal English or invented fantasy language.
- **Verse structure**: N/A. Text, when present, follows prose or free-verse narration rather than song structure.
- **Key rule**: The music must transport the listener to another world. Every sound should serve the atmosphere of a specific imagined place: a torch-lit corridor, a snow-covered ruin, a moonlit forest path. If it sounds like the present day, it has failed.
- **Avoid**: Modern language, pop song structures, beats that feel contemporary, clean digital production that breaks the medieval illusion; anything that reminds the listener they are in the 21st century.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **instrumental** or **2-4 lines** of narration/chant per section at 70-90 BPM. Dungeon synth is not a lyric-driven genre. If vocals are used, keep them sparse, archaic, and atmospheric. Topics: 1 per piece, sustained throughout.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Dungeon Synth** | The original 1990s sound: lo-fi synthesizer recordings with medieval and dark fantasy themes; cassette-quality production, simple but evocative melodies, direct lineage from black metal ambient interludes | Mortiis (Era I), Depressive Silence, Secret Stairways |
| **Comfy Synth** | Warmer, more inviting variant emphasizing cozy tavern atmospheres, gentle pastoral scenes, and hobbit-hole comfort; lighter mood than traditional dungeon synth while maintaining the medieval fantasy framework | Hole Dweller, Fief, Kobold |
| **Forest Synth** | Nature-themed variant with emphasis on woodland atmospheres, birdsong samples, and druidic/pagan imagery; greener and more organic-sounding than the stone-and-torch dungeon aesthetic | Foglord, Utred, Old Tower (some works) |
| **Winter Synth** | Cold, isolated soundscapes evoking snow-covered landscapes, frozen wastelands, and arctic solitude; minimalist, often using sustained pads and sparse bell tones; the most ambient and minimal variant | Eldamar, Vinterriket, Paysage d'Hiver (ambient works) |
| **Heroic/Epic Dungeon Synth** | More structured and melodic variant with triumphant brass patches, military percussion, and adventuring themes; closer to fantasy film scores in mood; the most accessible entry point for newcomers | Erang, Thangorodrim, Balrog |
| **Dark/Oppressive Dungeon Synth** | Emphasis on dread, darkness, and menace; dissonant textures, deep drones, and claustrophobic atmospheres; closest to dark ambient in approach; associated with the genre's black metal roots | Burzum (ambient albums), Summoning (synth elements), Emyn Muil |
| **Neoclassical Dungeon Synth** | Harpsichord, pipe organ, and classical string patches arranged in baroque or medieval musical forms; more formally composed than typical dungeon synth; draws from actual early music traditions | Wongraven, Jim Kirkwood, Arath |

## Reference Tracks

- **Mortiis - "Reisene til Gransen"** — From *Fodt til a Herske* (1993): the track that established dungeon synth as a concept. Extended synthesizer passages evoking vast underground caverns, with Mortiis's Tangerine Dream influences filtered through black metal aesthetics. The first deliberate "dark dungeon music" recording.

- **Burzum - "Dauoi Baldrs"** — The 1997 title track: composed entirely on synthesizer while Vikernes was imprisoned, using minimal equipment to create a mythological atmosphere drawn from Norse legend. Proved that the connection between black metal and ambient synthesizer music was profound, not incidental.

- **Depressive Silence - "Forest Silence"** — From *Depressive Silence II* (1996): ethereal, dreamlike synthesizer work that elevated dungeon synth beyond simple lo-fi keyboard recordings into genuinely transporting ambient music. Among the most revered recordings in the genre's first wave.

- **Secret Stairways - "Enchantment of the Ring"** — The 1997 title track from Mathew Davis's debut: melancholic medieval melody over lo-fi synthesizer pads, evoking a sense of loss and ancient wonder. Davis's premature death in 2011 makes these recordings irreplaceable artifacts of the genre's origins.

- **Wongraven - "Del I: Aeven"** — From *Fjelltransen* (1995): Satyr's neoclassical side project combining dark ambient drones with more structured melodic passages, bridging the Norwegian black metal scene's atmospheric experiments with formal composition.

- **Erang - "Another World Another Time"** — The 2013 title track that marked the genre's revival: melodic, accessible dungeon synth with a clear fantasy narrative, demonstrating that the style could evolve beyond its lo-fi origins while maintaining its imaginative core.

- **Old Tower - "Rise of the Specter"** — Modern dungeon synth at its most immersive: layered synthesizer textures creating a dense medieval atmosphere, with subtle dynamic shifts sustaining attention across extended duration. Represents the genre's maturation in production quality.

- **Summoning - "Mirdautas Vras"** — From *Let Mortal Heroes Sing Your Fame* (2001): the bridge between black metal and dungeon synth, with programmed drums, synthesizer orchestration, and Tolkien-sourced lyrics creating an epic, martial atmosphere. Summoning's influence on the genre's heroic wing is immeasurable.

- **Hole Dweller - "Flies the Coop"** — The comfy synth landmark: warm, inviting synthesizer melodies evoking a cozy medieval tavern rather than a dark dungeon. Proved the genre could accommodate warmth and comfort alongside its traditional darkness.

- **Fief - "I"** — From the self-titled 2017 debut: pastoral synthesizer pieces with medieval folk melody influences, establishing the gentler, countryside variant of dungeon synth. The numbered album series became a touchstone for the comfy subgenre.
