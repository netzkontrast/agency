# Son Cubano

## Genre Overview

Son cubano is the foundational genre of Cuban popular music, born in the mountainous Oriente province of eastern Cuba in the late 19th century. The style emerged from the meeting of Spanish guitar and vocal traditions with the rhythmic patterns and call-and-response singing of Bantu-descended communities. Early regional variants -- changui, nengon, kiriba -- circulated among rural communities before the son reached Havana around 1909. The first recordings appeared in 1917, and by the 1920s, the Sexteto Habanero and Ignacio Pineiro's Septeto Nacional had codified the classic son format: tres guitar carrying melodic guajeos, Spanish guitar providing harmony, maracas and claves anchoring the rhythm, bongos adding syncopation, bass grounding the harmony, and a trumpet riding above it all. The clave -- a five-note rhythmic cell in either 2-3 (son clave) or 3-2 (rumba clave) orientation -- became the organizing principle that governs every element in the ensemble.

The genre's golden age arrived in the 1940s when Arsenio Rodriguez, the blind tres virtuoso known as "El Ciego Maravilloso," expanded the septeto into the conjunto format by adding congas, a piano, and a multi-trumpet horn section. This larger ensemble created the son montuno -- a sophisticated evolution that extended the improvised montuno section with complex horn arrangements and layered percussion. Rodriguez's innovations became the direct blueprint for salsa. Simultaneously, Beny More, "El Barbaro del Ritmo," demonstrated vocal mastery across son, mambo, bolero, and guaracha, leading his Banda Gigante through performances that remain benchmarks of Cuban musicianship. By mid-century, son had spawned mambo (through the danzon's syncopated final section), cha-cha-cha (Enrique Jorrin's simplified, dancer-friendly variant), and the Afro-Cuban jazz experiments of Machito and Mario Bauza in New York.

The Buena Vista Social Club album (1997), produced by Ry Cooder with veteran Havana musicians including Compay Segundo, Ibrahim Ferrer, Ruben Gonzalez, and Omara Portuondo, sold over eight million copies and revived global interest in classic son and bolero. The project proved that the genre's emotional depth and rhythmic sophistication transcended language and generation. In Cuba itself, son's DNA continued to evolve through Los Van Van's songo innovations in the 1970s and the aggressive timba of NG La Banda and La Charanga Habanera in the 1990s. Today, son cubano remains the root system from which virtually all Cuban and much Latin American popular music grows.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Cuban tres (six-string, three-course instrument carrying melodic guajeos), Spanish guitar, maracas, claves, bongos, upright bass (or later electric bass with anticipated beat-one pattern), congas (added by Arsenio Rodriguez in the 1940s), trumpet(s), piano (playing montuno/guajeo patterns); charanga format uses flute and violins instead of brass
- **Vocals**: Lead singer (sonero) improvises melodic and lyrical variations in the montuno section while a chorus (coro) repeats a fixed refrain; call-and-response (coro-soneo) is the central vocal dynamic; passionate, rhythmically precise delivery that locks to the clave; pregon (street-vendor cry) style in some variants
- **Production**: Traditional recordings emphasize the acoustic interplay of the ensemble; each instrument occupies a distinct rhythmic role within the clave framework; the composite groove exists only in the ensemble interaction, not in any single part; modern productions may add studio polish but the rhythmic architecture remains paramount
- **Energy/Mood**: Danceable, celebratory, and rhythmically propulsive at its core; romantic and nostalgic in ballad tempos; the montuno section builds energy through accumulation rather than chord changes; capable of carrying deep emotion (saudade-like longing in slower son) or explosive joy in uptempo guaracha-son
- **Structure**: Verse section sets up the narrative, then the montuno (coro-soneo) takes over for the second half, building energy through vocal improvisation over a repeating harmonic and rhythmic cycle; many son tracks spend more time in the montuno than in structured verses; the montuno is the destination, not a bridge
- **Tempo**: 100-160 BPM depending on substyle; son tradicional 100-130 BPM, son montuno 130-160 BPM, guaracha-son 140-180 BPM; rhythm feel is swung and syncopated, always organized around the clave

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: ABAB or AABB in verse sections, with the coro (chorus) typically a short, memorable phrase repeated as a refrain. The montuno favors improvised soneo over the coro rather than structured verse.
- **Rhyme quality**: Spanish-language rhyming conventions -- consonant rhyme (rima consonante) and assonance (rima asonante) both standard. Rhythmic fit to the clave matters more than rhyme precision. Internal rhyme and wordplay valued in soneo improvisations.
- **Verse structure**: 4-8 line verses that set up the theme before the montuno section opens. The transition to montuno is the song's structural pivot point. In the montuno, the sonero improvises short phrases (2-4 bars) that alternate with the coro's refrain.
- **Key rule**: Lyrics must lock to the clave. Stressed syllables fall on clave strokes; phrasing breathes with the rhythmic cycle, not against it. The montuno is where the song lives -- verses are setup, the coro-soneo interplay is the payoff.
- **Avoid**: Phrasing that fights the clave or ignores the rhythmic pocket. Overly wordy verses that leave no room for the coro-soneo interplay. Treating son like standard pop verse-chorus -- the montuno is the destination. Placing stressed syllables against the clave.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 110-150 BPM. Leave space for coro responses and instrumental breaks. Short soneo phrases alternating with coro in the montuno. Topics: 1 per verse, with the coro providing the emotional anchor.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Son Tradicional** | The classic septeto format from the 1920s-30s: tres, guitar, bass, bongos, maracas, claves, and trumpet. Defined the golden-age son sound with tight ensemble interplay and elegant simplicity. | Septeto Nacional, Sexteto Habanero, Trio Matamoros |
| **Son Montuno** | Arsenio Rodriguez's 1940s expansion of son into the conjunto format -- congas, piano, multi-trumpet sections -- with extended montuno sections featuring complex horn arrangements and layered percussion. The direct precursor to salsa. | Arsenio Rodriguez, Chappotin y Sus Estrellas, Roberto Faz |
| **Guaracha-Son** | Uptempo, humorous variant emphasizing rhythmic energy, witty lyrics, and danceable grooves. Often features rapid-fire vocal delivery and playful call-and-response. | Celia Cruz, Beny More, La Sonora Matancera |
| **Changui** | Rural precursor to son from Guantanamo province, featuring the tres, bongo, maracas, and marimbula (thumb bass). Rawer and more Afro-Cuban than urban son, with a distinctive rhythmic feel. | Grupo Changui de Guantanamo, Elio Reve |
| **Son-Pregon** | Son built around the melodic cries of street vendors (pregones). The vocal line imitates the sing-song cadence of sellers hawking their wares. "El Manisero" (The Peanut Vendor) is the archetype. | Moises Simons, Abelardo Barroso |
| **Songo** | 1970s modernization by Los Van Van that wove funk, rock, and synthesizers into son's clave framework. Percussionist Changuito created new drum patterns that bridged tradition and modernity. | Los Van Van, Adalberto Alvarez y Su Son |
| **Timba** | Aggressive 1990s Havana style fusing son, rumba, funk, hip-hop, and jazz. Dense arrangements, complex conga patterns, gear-shifting tempo changes, and street-level lyrical attitude. | NG La Banda, La Charanga Habanera, Los Van Van |
| **Son Contemporaneo** | 21st-century son that maintains the clave framework and acoustic ensemble core while incorporating modern production, jazz harmony, and global influences. | Buena Vista Social Club, Orquesta Buena Vista Social Club |

## Reference Tracks

- **Sexteto Habanero - "Tres Lindas Cubanas"** -- An early son recording that captures the genre's foundational septeto sound: tight tres guajeos, interlocking percussion, and vocal harmonies that established the template other ensembles would follow. The track demonstrates son's elegant simplicity before the conjunto expansion.
- **Ignacio Pineiro / Septeto Nacional - "Echale Salsita"** -- Often cited as the origin of the word "salsa" in a musical context. Pineiro's composition showcases the classic septeto interplay and the narrative verse-to-montuno structure that defines son's formal architecture.
- **Arsenio Rodriguez - "El Reloj de Pastora"** -- Son montuno from the man who invented the form. Rodriguez's tres carries the melodic guajeo while his expanded conjunto -- congas, piano, multi-trumpet section -- builds the layered rhythmic architecture that became salsa's blueprint.
- **Beny More - "Bonito y Sabroso"** -- "El Barbaro del Ritmo" leading his Banda Gigante through a son montuno that showcases his extraordinary vocal range and rhythmic flexibility. More shifts between precise phrasing and improvised soneo with an authority that remains unmatched.
- **Trio Matamoros - "Son de la Loma"** -- One of the most recognized Cuban songs worldwide. Miguel Matamoros' composition asks "Where are the singers from?" and answers "From the hills they come down, singing." The tight tres-guitar-vocal trio format captures son's trovador roots.
- **Compay Segundo - "Chan Chan"** -- A deceptively simple son built on a descending bass line and a syncopated tumbao that locks into the clave with effortless grace. Written in the 1980s, it became the opening track and signature song of the Buena Vista Social Club project.
- **Ibrahim Ferrer - "Dos Gardenias"** -- Son meets bolero at its most intimate. Ferrer's voice, rediscovered at age 70, carries decades of lived experience over a spare piano-bass-percussion arrangement that gives every phrase room to breathe.
- **Celia Cruz & Johnny Pacheco - "Quimbara"** -- The Queen of Salsa at full power. Cruz's commanding soneo improvisations over the coro demonstrate the son montuno tradition at its most electrifying, with Pacheco's flute weaving through the rhythm section.
- **Los Van Van - "Sandunguera"** -- Songo in action: Juan Formell's bass lines, Changuito's revolutionary drum patterns, and trombone-driven arrangements demonstrate how Los Van Van modernized son without abandoning its clave foundation.
- **Buena Vista Social Club - "El Cuarto de Tula"** -- A full-ensemble son montuno from the 1997 album that showcases the genre's collective energy. Multiple vocal soloists take turns over the coro while the rhythm section locks into an irresistible groove.
- **Don Azpiazu - "El Manisero" (The Peanut Vendor)** -- The 1930 recording that introduced Cuban music to American audiences. Moises Simons' son-pregon imitates a peanut vendor's street cry over a rhythm section that brought congas, bongos, and claves to international ears for the first time.
- **Eliades Ochoa - "El Carretero"** -- Rural son from Santiago de Cuba's trovador tradition, performed with the Cuarteto Patria. Ochoa's guitar and voice carry the genre's country roots -- less polished than Havana son but deeply felt and rhythmically grounded.
