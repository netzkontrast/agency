# Tech House

## Genre Overview

Tech house emerged in the early 1990s from London's underground club scene as a hybrid that fused the groove and warmth of house music with the stripped-back precision and mechanical edge of techno. The genre coalesced around a handful of DJs who bridged the two worlds: Eddie Richards, widely regarded as the genre's godfather, and Mr. C (Richard West), a former Shamen rapper who co-founded the Plink Plonk label with Paul "Rip" Stone in 1994. The origin story is often traced to a cassette Mr. C gave Richards with one side labeled "tech" and the other "house," distilling the philosophy of the sound into a single object. Richards, Terry Francis, and Nathan Coles ran the Wiggle parties through the mid-1990s, warehouse events that established the template for tech house as a DJ-driven, dancefloor-focused discipline rather than a producer genre. By the late 1990s, Mr. C and Layo Paskin's London nightclub The End had become the unofficial headquarters of the UK tech house scene.

Through the 2000s, tech house spread across Europe, finding particular traction in Spain and Ibiza where clubs like Space and DC10 embraced the sound for marathon daytime sets. Labels such as Poker Flat Recordings (Steve Bug), Saved Records (Nic Fanciulli), and Get Physical Music pushed the genre's sonic boundaries, incorporating deeper textures, Latin percussion, and minimal techno influences. The Ibiza connection proved critical: Carl Cox's residencies blended techno with house grooves, and the island's sunset-to-sunrise culture demanded music that sustained energy without exhausting dancers. Meanwhile, in the US, Derrick Carter and Mark Farina championed a funkier, jazz-inflected variant rooted in Chicago's house heritage.

The 2010s saw tech house explode into the mainstream. Jamie Jones and Lee Foss launched Hot Creations in 2010, redefining the genre's accessibility with vocal-driven, groove-heavy productions. Fisher's 2018 single "Losing It" became a crossover phenomenon, introducing tech house to festival audiences worldwide. By the 2020s, the genre had become the dominant sound at global electronic festivals, with artists like Chris Lake, John Summit, James Hype, and Cloonee filling arenas. Beatport consistently ranks tech house among its top-selling genres. The sound has polarized electronic music communities -- purists criticize its formulaic tendencies and commercial dilution, while supporters argue it represents the most accessible and effective dancefloor music of the era. Modern tech house continues to evolve through bass-heavy variants, Latin-infused productions, and crossover collaborations with pop and hip-hop.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Drum machines (Roland TR-909 kick and hi-hats, TR-808 percussion), synthesizers (bass stabs, chord stabs, filtered pads), sampled vocal chops, percussion loops (congas, bongos, shakers, rim shots), bass synthesizers (sub-bass and mid-range rolling basslines), subtle atmospheric effects, minimal melodic elements
- **Vocals**: Short vocal phrases and one-shots used as rhythmic elements, chopped and looped vocal samples, spoken word or rap snippets repurposed as hooks, soulful vocal stabs rather than full sung performances, pitched and filtered vocal textures, call-and-response chants, heavily processed and effected vocal layers
- **Production**: Four-on-the-floor kick drum pattern with tight, punchy low end; crisp open hi-hats on off-beats; layered percussion creating polyrhythmic grooves; rolling basslines (often mid-range and rhythmically syncopated); sidechain compression for pumping effect; long DJ-friendly intros and outros (32-64 bars); subtle filter sweeps and automation; clean, precise mixdowns with emphasis on low-end clarity; minimal harmonic content favoring rhythm and groove
- **Energy/Mood**: Driving yet controlled; hypnotic and groove-locked; darker and more mechanical than house but funkier and more human than techno; designed for sustained peak-time energy across extended DJ sets; ranges from deep and minimal to aggressive and percussive depending on subgenre
- **Structure**: Extended arrangements (5-8 minutes), gradual introduction and subtraction of elements, breakdown sections that strip back to percussion or bass before rebuilding, minimal verse-chorus form, repetitive loops with subtle variation and automation, tension-and-release dynamics built across 16-32 bar phrases
- **Tempo**: 124-128 BPM, with the majority of productions sitting at 125-127 BPM; straight four-on-the-floor rhythm with shuffled or swung hi-hat patterns adding groove; tempo sits between deep house (120-122 BPM) and techno (128-135 BPM)

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Minimal or none -- single phrases looped, not structured verse writing. When lyrics appear, simple AABB couplets or repeated single lines.
- **Rhyme quality**: Incidental. Rhythmic impact and groove-lock matter more than rhyme craft. Short, punchy phrases that sit inside the beat.
- **Verse structure**: No traditional verses. Vocal elements are typically 1-4 bar loops, chopped one-shots, or short spoken phrases used as rhythmic accents. Full vocal sections rare.
- **Key rule**: Vocals serve the groove, never compete with it. Every vocal element must lock to the rhythm and enhance the dancefloor hypnosis, not distract from it.
- **Avoid**: Extended lyrical passages, complex narratives, melodic vocal hooks that dominate the mix, anything that breaks the percussive drive or demands listener attention away from the groove.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2 lines/verse** (or repeated phrases) at 125-127 BPM. Groove-driven -- single ideas looped as rhythmic elements. Dancefloor clarity over lyrical density. Topics: 1/verse max.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Minimal Tech House** | Stripped-back, hypnotic variant emphasizing space and subtlety; sparse arrangements with micro-variations in percussion and texture; closer to minimal techno in aesthetic but retaining house groove and warmth | Richie Hawtin, Ricardo Villalobos, Steve Bug, Luciano |
| **Ibiza Tech House** | Sun-drenched, percussion-heavy sound developed for Ibiza's open-air clubs; Latin percussion, tribal rhythms, warm basslines, and DJ-friendly extended mixes designed for marathon daytime sets | Jamie Jones, Marco Carola, Loco Dice, The Martinez Brothers |
| **Bass Tech House** | Heavier variant with prominent sub-bass, aggressive basslines, and harder-hitting kicks; designed for large festival sound systems; often incorporates UK bass music and garage influences | Fisher, Chris Lake, Cloonee, Eli Brown |
| **Vocal Tech House** | Groove-driven productions anchored by prominent vocal hooks, sampled phrases, or full vocal performances; more accessible and radio-friendly than instrumental variants | Green Velvet, Patrick Topping, Solardo, Hot Since 82 |
| **Deep Tech** | Darker, moodier variant blending deep house warmth with techno austerity; emphasis on atmosphere and sub-bass over percussion; slower builds and more introspective feel | Nic Fanciulli, Solomun, Tale of Us, Adriatique |
| **Latin Tech House** | Incorporates Afro-Cuban and Latin percussion (congas, bongos, timbales), often layered with organic instrument samples; rhythmically complex with polyrhythmic groove patterns | Dennis Cruz, Cuartero, Hector Couto, Paco Osuna |
| **Funky Tech House** | Retains stronger connections to house music's disco and funk roots; prominent basslines with groove-forward production; often features funk guitar samples and soul vocal chops | Derrick Carter, Mark Farina, DJ Sneak, Bushwacka! |
| **Melodic Tech House** | Greater emphasis on melodic elements, chord progressions, and harmonic content than typical tech house; bridges the gap between tech house and progressive house | John Summit, James Hype, CamelPhat, Meduza |

## Reference Tracks

- **Mr. C - "Tech House"** -- From his 2002 album *Change*, this track is a deliberate genre manifesto from one of tech house's co-creators. The stripped-back production, rolling percussion, and hypnotic repetition demonstrate the original vision: house groove meets techno precision, no frills.

- **Eddie Richards & Nathan Coles - "Wiggle"** -- The Wiggle parties defined early tech house culture, and their productions captured that energy: warm, percussive grooves designed for extended DJ sets. Minimal but magnetic, with every element serving the dancefloor.

- **Terry Francis - "Architecture"** -- Showcases the London tech house sound at its most refined: precise drum programming, subtle harmonic shifts, and a groove that unfolds over minutes rather than bars. A masterclass in understated dancefloor tension.

- **Steve Bug - "Loverboy"** -- Poker Flat Recordings defined the European minimal tech house sound. Tight 909 programming, filtered bassline, and micro-variations in percussion that reward attentive listening while keeping bodies moving.

- **Green Velvet - "La La Land"** -- Chicago acid meets tech house: Curtis Jones's spoken-word delivery over a relentless groove became a dancefloor staple across multiple decades. Demonstrates how minimal vocal elements can carry an entire track.

- **Derrick Carter - "Where You At?"** -- Chicago funk-house filtered through tech house precision. Derrick Carter's productions prove that tech house need not sacrifice soul for mechanical tightness, bridging the genre's house and techno roots.

- **Jamie Jones - "Don't You Remember the Future"** -- Hot Creations' debut release signaled a new era for tech house. Warm, groove-driven, and accessible, it set the template for the label's output and the genre's 2010s expansion.

- **Patrick Topping - "Forget"** -- Vocal-driven tech house at its most effective: a chopped vocal hook locked into a percussive groove, demonstrating how the genre handles vocals as rhythmic instruments rather than melodic features.

- **Fisher - "Losing It"** -- The 2018 track that brought tech house to mainstream consciousness. A driving bassline, simple vocal sample, and relentless energy made it a festival anthem. Love it or critique it, the track redefined the genre's commercial ceiling.

- **Chris Lake & Green Velvet - "Deceiver"** -- Bass-heavy modern tech house connecting Chicago's acid heritage with contemporary festival sound design. Heavy sub-bass, Green Velvet's distinctive vocal delivery, and a groove built for large sound systems.

- **John Summit - "Deep End"** -- The track that launched Summit's meteoric rise. Melodic tech house with an emotional vocal sample, demonstrating the genre's capacity for anthemic moments without abandoning its rhythmic foundation.

- **Hot Since 82 - "Knee Deep in Louise"** -- Deep tech production with warm basslines and atmospheric texture. Represents the moodier, more introspective side of tech house, designed for dark rooms and late-night sets.

- **Solardo - "Tribesmen"** -- Percussion-forward Ibiza tech house with tribal drum patterns and a relentless groove. Represents the genre's rhythmic complexity and its debt to Afro-Latin percussion traditions.
