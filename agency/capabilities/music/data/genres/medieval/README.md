# Medieval

## Genre Overview

Medieval music encompasses the European musical traditions spanning roughly 500 to 1400 AD, a vast period that saw Western music evolve from monophonic sacred chant into the earliest forms of polyphony and secular art song. This millennium of musical development laid the foundations for everything that followed in Western music — notation systems, harmonic concepts, compositional forms, and the very idea that music could be written down and transmitted across time and distance. The era is typically divided into three broad periods: the Early Medieval (500-1000), dominated by Gregorian chant and monastic musical life; the High Medieval (1000-1250), which saw the birth of polyphony at Notre-Dame and the flourishing of troubadour culture; and the Late Medieval (1250-1400), marked by the Ars Nova's rhythmic innovations and the increasing sophistication of secular music.

Sacred music dominated the early centuries. Gregorian chant — monophonic, unaccompanied vocal music sung in Latin — was the musical language of the Roman Catholic Church and the most extensively documented music of the period. Codified under Pope Gregory I (though modern scholarship questions the extent of his direct involvement), plainchant organized melodies into a system of eight modes (scales) that predated and differed from the major/minor system of later Western music. These modes gave medieval music its distinctive, otherworldly quality — neither major nor minor but something more ancient and ambiguous. From this monophonic foundation, the revolutionary concept of polyphony emerged at the Notre-Dame school in Paris around the 12th century, where composers Leonin and Perotin layered independent vocal lines over sustained chant tones, creating the organum that would evolve into all subsequent Western harmony.

Secular medieval music thrived alongside the sacred tradition. The troubadours of southern France (11th-13th centuries) and their northern counterparts the trouveres composed courtly love songs in the vernacular languages, establishing the idea of the composer-performer as individual artist. German Minnesanger pursued similar themes of chivalric love. Wandering musicians (jongleurs, minstrels, goliards) carried songs between courts and towns. Instrumental music existed primarily for dance accompaniment, though few notated examples survive. The modern revival of medieval music spans academic early music ensembles performing from historical manuscripts, neomedievalist bands blending period instruments with rock energy, film and game composers drawing on medieval sonorities, and the Society for Creative Anachronism (SCA) community that keeps medieval performance traditions alive through living history.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Voice is primary; instruments include lute, vielle (medieval fiddle), rebec, psaltery, harp, hurdy-gurdy, portative organ, recorder, shawm, sackbut, tabor (drum), nakers (small kettledrums), and bells; instruments often doubled or accompanied vocal lines rather than playing independent parts; modern neomedievalist groups add bagpipes, cittern, and occasionally electric instruments
- **Vocals**: Monophonic chant (single unison line) in sacred music; early polyphony adds parallel or independent vocal lines; troubadour songs feature solo voice; vocal production emphasizes clarity and purity over vibrato; open-throated, resonant singing in large stone spaces (churches, great halls); Latin for sacred, vernacular languages for secular
- **Production**: Acoustic and natural; the architecture of medieval churches and cathedrals was the "production" — stone walls and vaulted ceilings created long reverb that shaped how music was composed and performed; modern recordings often capture medieval music in period-appropriate spaces; neomedievalist acts may add modern production elements
- **Energy/Mood**: Contemplative and transcendent in sacred music; courtly and refined in troubadour songs; earthy and celebratory in dance music; haunting and modal — the absence of major/minor tonality gives medieval music an emotional ambiguity that modern listeners often find mysterious or otherworldly; drone-based textures create hypnotic, meditative states
- **Structure**: Chant follows liturgical text structure (psalms, hymns, antiphons); secular songs use strophic form (same music for each verse), ballade form (AAB), rondeau, or virelai; organum layers voices over a sustained tenor; isorhythmic motets of the Ars Nova use repeating rhythmic patterns (talea) over repeating pitch patterns (color); dance forms include estampie and saltarello
- **Tempo**: 60-120 BPM; chant is often free rhythm without strict pulse; measured music emerges with Notre-Dame polyphony; dance music has steady, clear pulse; slower tempos for sacred and contemplative pieces; moderate to lively tempos for secular and dance music

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Variable — troubadour songs use sophisticated schemes including ABABCDCD; chant follows prose/psalm structure without rhyme; hymns use simple ABAB or AABB
- **Rhyme quality**: Sacred texts are prose or poetry in Latin following established liturgical forms; secular poetry ranges from simple to highly refined courtly verse; troubadours valued intricate rhyme schemes and wordplay
- **Verse structure**: Strophic songs repeat the same music for each verse (3-6 stanzas typical); chant follows the text's natural divisions; ballade form has three stanzas plus envoi
- **Key rule**: Text drives the music in sacred settings — melody serves the words of scripture and liturgy; in secular songs, the interplay of poetry and melody is the art itself
- **Avoid**: Modern harmonic expectations (major/minor resolution); anachronistic language or concepts; losing the modal character that defines the sound
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 60-120 BPM. Modal melodies with space for resonance. Monophonic or simple polyphonic textures. Topics: 1/verse (courtly love, devotion, nature, morality).

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Gregorian Chant** | Monophonic, unaccompanied sacred vocal music of the Roman Catholic Church; modal melodies following liturgical texts; free rhythm without strict meter; the foundation of Western musical notation | Monks of Santo Domingo de Silos, Ensemble Organum |
| **Notre-Dame Polyphony** | Birth of harmony: organum and conductus from 12th-13th century Paris; sustained chant tones (tenor) with elaborate upper voices; the first composed polyphonic music in Western history | Leonin, Perotin, Anonymous 4 (performers) |
| **Troubadour / Trouvere Song** | Courtly love songs in Occitan (troubadours) and Old French (trouveres); monophonic melodies of great beauty and sophistication; the origin of the singer-songwriter tradition | Bernart de Ventadorn, Adam de la Halle, Martin Best |
| **Ars Nova** | 14th-century French innovations in rhythm and notation; isorhythmic motets with complex rhythmic structures; increased chromaticism and expressiveness; Guillaume de Machaut as the towering figure | Guillaume de Machaut, Philippe de Vitry |
| **Trecento (Italian Ars Nova)** | 14th-century Italian secular music; madrigals, cacce (hunting songs), and ballate; lighter, more lyrical than French Ars Nova; emphasis on melodic beauty | Francesco Landini, Jacopo da Bologna |
| **Medieval Dance Music** | Instrumental music for court and popular dances; estampies, saltarelli, and other dance forms; lively rhythms and clear phrase structures; largely reconstructed from limited surviving manuscripts | Ensemble Unicorn, New London Consort |
| **Neomedievalist / Medieval Rock** | Modern bands using medieval instruments, modes, and aesthetics with rock energy and volume; festival and SCA circuit staples; bridges historical performance and contemporary entertainment | Corvus Corax, In Extremo, Saltatio Mortis |
| **Early Music Revival** | Scholarly performers using period instruments and historical performance practice to reconstruct medieval music from manuscripts; academic rigor combined with artistic interpretation | Sequentia, Gothic Voices, Ensemble Gilles Binchois |

## Reference Tracks

- **Hildegard von Bingen - "O Virtus Sapientiae" (12th century)** — Soaring monophonic chant that demonstrates the expressive power of a single vocal line; Hildegard's melodies range wider than most chant, reaching across an octave with ecstatic leaps; the sound of medieval sacred music at its most transcendent and personal

- **Perotin - "Viderunt Omnes" (c. 1198)** — Four-voice organum from Notre-Dame representing the pinnacle of early polyphony; sustained chant tones in the lowest voice support elaborate rhythmic patterns above; the sound of the medieval cathedral as an instrument; among the first named compositions in Western music history

- **Bernart de Ventadorn - "Can Vei la Lauzeta Mover" (12th century)** — The most celebrated troubadour song: a monophonic melody of heartbreaking beauty set to a poem about unrequited love; demonstrates the sophistication of secular medieval art song; the lark's flight as metaphor for impossible desire

- **Guillaume de Machaut - "Douce Dame Jolie" (14th century)** — Ars Nova virelai combining melodic elegance with rhythmic subtlety; a courtly love song that showcases Machaut's genius for melody within the constraints of medieval form; accessible entry point to 14th-century secular music

- **Anonymous - "Estampie Royale" (13th century)** — Instrumental dance music with driving rhythm and modal melody; the estampie's repeating sections with open and closed endings demonstrate medieval formal structures; lively and energetic, proving medieval music was not all contemplation and prayer

- **Corvus Corax - "Totus Floreo" (Carmina Burana setting)** — Neomedievalist interpretation of a medieval student song: bagpipes, shawms, and drums create a visceral, festival-ready sound; demonstrates how medieval melodies translate to amplified, modern performance contexts while retaining their modal character
