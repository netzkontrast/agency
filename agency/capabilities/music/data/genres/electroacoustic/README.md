# Electroacoustic

## Genre Overview

Electroacoustic music is a broad compositional tradition that encompasses any music in which electronic technology is used to manipulate, generate, explore, or combine sounds from acoustic or environmental sources. The genre's conceptual foundation was laid by French composer and broadcaster Pierre Schaeffer, who coined the term "musique concrete" in 1948 to describe compositions built from recorded real-world sounds rather than notated musical instructions. Schaeffer's *Cinq etudes de bruits* (1948) — five studies in noise created from railway sounds, spinning tops, and other everyday objects — were the first works of their kind. By 1951, Schaeffer, composer-percussionist Pierre Henry, and engineer Jacques Poullin established the Groupe de Recherches de Musique Concrete at RTF in Paris, attracting figures including Karlheinz Stockhausen, Edgard Varese, Iannis Xenakis, and Olivier Messiaen.

The 1950s and 1960s saw rapid expansion as two distinct but overlapping approaches developed. In Paris, the musique concrete tradition continued through Schaeffer and Henry's landmark *Symphonie pour un homme seul* (1950) and the work of younger composers like Luc Ferrari, whose *Presque rien No. 1* (1970) pioneered the "anecdotal" style of unprocessed field recording as composition. In Cologne, Stockhausen's elektronische Musik studio pursued purely electronic sound generation, while his *Gesang der Junglinge* (1956) and *Kontakte* (1960) merged electronic and acoustic sources in ways that anticipated the broader electroacoustic approach. Bernard Parmegiani, working at the GRM from the 1960s onward, produced *De Natura Sonorum* (1975), a 12-movement suite that became a landmark of the classical electroacoustic period and influenced generations of composers.

Contemporary electroacoustic music thrives in both academic and experimental contexts. The acousmatic tradition — music composed for loudspeaker performance, without visible sound sources — continues through composers like Denis Smalley, Natasha Barrett, and Annette Vande Gorne, who work with multichannel speaker arrays and spatial audio. Francisco Lopez creates immense environmental sound works that challenge listeners' perceptions of music, silence, and noise. The laptop and software revolution has democratized electroacoustic practice, with tools like Max/MSP, SuperCollider, and Ableton Live making techniques once confined to institutional studios available to independent artists. Cross-pollination with sound art, field recording, noise music, and experimental electronic music has blurred the genre's boundaries while its core commitment — exploring the musical potential of all sound — remains as vital as when Schaeffer first pointed a microphone at a train.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Microphones and field recording equipment, loudspeaker arrays, acoustic instruments processed through electronics, synthesizers (analog and digital), computers running Max/MSP, SuperCollider, or similar environments, tape machines (historically), multichannel speaker systems for spatial audio
- **Vocals**: When present, typically as recorded speech treated as raw material — processed, fragmented, layered, or spatially distributed; not used for singing or lyrical delivery in most works; the human voice treated as one sound source among many
- **Production**: Sound manipulation through editing, filtering, time-stretching, granular synthesis, convolution, spectral processing, and spatial distribution; emphasis on the "studio as instrument"; careful attention to spectral content and spatial placement; multichannel compositions common (4, 8, 16+ channels)
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges enormously: contemplative and meditative to aggressive and disorienting; focused listening required; often evokes abstract emotional states that resist verbal description; can be deeply unsettling or transcendently beautiful
- **Structure**: Compositional rather than song-based; internal logic derived from the material itself rather than external forms; durations from 3 minutes to multi-hour installations; narrative, spectral, or morphological organization rather than harmonic or rhythmic
- **Tempo**: Generally atemporal; no regular pulse or beat in most works; rhythm exists as the articulation of sound events in time rather than as metered beats; when pulse is present, it emerges from the material rather than being imposed

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: N/A — electroacoustic music does not use lyrics in any conventional sense.
- **Rhyme quality**: Irrelevant. Language, when present, is treated as sound material.
- **Verse structure**: No traditional structure. Text may appear as processed speech fragments, multilingual collage, or phonetic material.
- **Key rule**: Sound itself is the content. All sounds — environmental, electronic, vocal, instrumental — are equal compositional material.
- **Avoid**: Conventional singing, lyrics, song form. Anything that imposes traditional musical hierarchy on the sound material.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **0 lines** — typically fully non-vocal. If text appears, it is spoken and processed, not sung. Suno is not the natural platform for pure electroacoustic composition, but electroacoustic elements (processed sounds, spatial textures, environmental recordings) can enhance other genres.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Musique Concrete** | The founding tradition: compositions built from recorded real-world sounds (not electronically generated) that are edited, processed, and assembled in the studio; emphasizes the musicality of everyday sound | Pierre Schaeffer, Pierre Henry, Luc Ferrari |
| **Acousmatic Music** | Music composed for loudspeaker projection without visible performers; inherits from musique concrete but emphasizes spatial composition and the listener's perceptual experience of disembodied sound | Denis Smalley, Francois Bayle, Annette Vande Gorne |
| **Live Electronics** | Real-time electronic processing of acoustic instruments during performance; the performer and electronics interact dynamically; bridges concert hall and studio traditions | Karlheinz Stockhausen, George Lewis, Richard Barrett |
| **Sound Art** | Installation-based work where sound exists in physical space; site-specific, durational, often interactive; blurs boundaries between music, sculpture, and architecture | Max Neuhaus, Janet Cardiff, Christina Kubisch |
| **Soundscape Composition** | Works built from field recordings that maintain recognizable connection to their source environments; explores the relationship between sound, place, memory, and ecology | R. Murray Schafer, Hildegard Westerkamp, Chris Watson |
| **Noise Music** | Extreme extension of electroacoustic principles: all sound is valid, including feedback, distortion, and physical extremity; confrontational aesthetic that tests perceptual limits | Merzbow, Zbigniew Karkowski, Francisco Lopez |
| **Laptop Music** | Post-2000 electroacoustic practice using laptops and software; real-time processing, generative algorithms, network performance; democratized the genre's tools and methods | Ryoji Ikeda, Alva Noto, Fennesz |
| **Spectral Music** | Compositions based on analysis of sound spectra; acoustic instruments used to recreate or explore the internal structure of complex sounds; bridges electroacoustic analysis with orchestral writing | Gerard Grisey, Tristan Murail, Kaija Saariaho |

## Reference Tracks

- **Pierre Schaeffer - "Etude aux chemins de fer"** — The first piece of musique concrete (1948): railway sounds edited and arranged into a musical composition; the moment recorded sound became a compositional medium, and the birth of an entire tradition

- **Pierre Schaeffer & Pierre Henry - "Symphonie pour un homme seul"** — Landmark 1950 collaboration: human body sounds (breathing, footsteps, vocal fragments) transformed into a multi-movement symphonic work; proved that the everyday human sound world contained musical riches

- **Karlheinz Stockhausen - "Gesang der Junglinge"** — Electronic and acoustic fusion (1956): a boy's voice merged with electronically generated sounds in ways that made the two indistinguishable; one of the most influential electronic compositions ever created

- **Luc Ferrari - "Presque rien No. 1"** — Anecdotal composition (1970): an unprocessed dawn recording at a Yugoslav fishing village presented as a musical work; radically questioned what composition means when the composer selects but does not manipulate

- **Bernard Parmegiani - "De Natura Sonorum: Incidences/Resonances"** — From the 1975 masterwork: explores the behavior of sounds at their attack, sustain, and decay; a textbook in electroacoustic thinking rendered as compelling musical experience

- **Iannis Xenakis - "Concrete PH"** — Two minutes of burning charcoal recorded and processed (1958): stochastic cloud textures created from a single natural sound source; mathematical composition applied to musique concrete

- **Denis Smalley - "Wind Chimes"** — Acousmatic art: the transformation of a simple sound source through studio processing into a rich, spatially complex composition; demonstrates spectromorphological thinking in practice

- **Francisco Lopez - "La Selva"** — Tropical rainforest field recording presented without processing or editing (1998): forces deep listening to environmental sound; challenges the boundary between music and the acoustic environment

- **Hildegard Westerkamp - "Kits Beach Soundwalk"** — Soundscape composition (1989): a narrated walk through Vancouver's soundscape combining field recording with studio processing; ecological awareness through sonic attention

- **Fennesz - "Endless Summer"** — Laptop electroacoustic meets pop sensibility (2001): processed guitar textures creating warm, nostalgic washes of sound; proved electroacoustic techniques could produce emotionally immediate music

- **Ryoji Ikeda - "Data.Matrix"** — Data sonification as music: mathematical datasets converted to sound and light; the digital era's answer to Xenakis's stochastic composition
