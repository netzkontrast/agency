# House

## Genre Overview

House music emerged from the underground clubs of Chicago in the early 1980s, born from the creative collision of disco, electronic experimentation, and Black and queer nightlife culture. The Warehouse nightclub, where DJ Frankie Knuckles held residency from 1977-1982, gave the genre its name and spiritual home. Knuckles, along with Ron Hardy at the Music Box, pioneered a sound built on extended disco edits, drum machine rhythms, and imported European electronic tracks from Kraftwerk, Giorgio Moroder, and Italo disco producers. The defining moment came in 1984 when Jesse Saunders released "On and On," widely considered the first house record, followed by Chip E.'s "Time to Jack" and Farley "Jackmaster" Funk's "Love Can't Turn Around." These tracks established the 4/4 kick drum foundation, synthesizer-driven melodies, and soulful vocal samples that would define the genre.

The second wave of house exploded in the late 1980s when UK audiences discovered Chicago imports. The 1987-1989 "Second Summer of Love" transformed British youth culture as acid house - characterized by the squelchy TB-303 bassline pioneered by Phuture's "Acid Tracks" (1987) - fueled massive illegal raves and warehouse parties. UK producers developed their own variations: the soulful UK garage scene, the stripped-down minimal approach, and the euphoric piano-driven sound that dominated charts. Meanwhile, Detroit's techno pioneers like Derrick May and Kevin Saunderson blurred the lines between house and techno, creating soulful machine music that influenced both genres. By the early 1990s, house had fragmented into dozens of subgenres: the deep, jazzy sounds of Masters At Work in New York; the progressive epic builds of Sasha and Digweed; the filtered disco of French touch; and the aggressive electro house emerging from European festivals.

Today, house music stands as the foundational genre of global dance culture, with an estimated market presence spanning from underground warehouse parties to stadium-filling festivals. The genre's evolution continues through tech house's hypnotic minimalism, tropical house's breezy accessibility, and future house's modern pop sensibilities. What unites all house music is its core mission: to create euphoric, rhythmic experiences that unite dancers in collective movement. The genre's 4/4 heartbeat - kick drum on every beat, typically between 120-130 BPM - remains constant even as production techniques evolve from analog warmth to digital precision. House music's influence extends far beyond dance floors, shaping pop production, R&B rhythms, and electronic music worldwide.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Drum machines (Roland TR-808, TR-909), synthesizers (Juno-106, Prophet-5, TB-303 for acid), sampled vocal loops, bass synthesizers, string pads, Rhodes piano samples, atmospheric effects, percussion elements (congas, claps, shakers)
- **Vocals**: Soulful diva vocals (often sampled from disco or gospel), repetitive hooks and vocal chops, whispered or spoken word samples, pitched and time-stretched vocal textures, minimal lyrical content in instrumental subgenres, call-and-response patterns
- **Production**: Four-on-the-floor kick drum pattern (kick on beats 1, 2, 3, 4), open hi-hats on off-beats, tight quantization with occasional humanized swing, layered builds and filtered breakdowns, sidechain compression creating rhythmic "pumping," long intro and outro sections for DJ mixing, emphasis on groove over melody
- **Energy/Mood**: Euphoric and uplifting at its core, ranging from deep introspective warmth to aggressive peak-time intensity; hypnotic and meditative in minimal variations; celebratory and communal; designed for sustained dance floor engagement over hours
- **Structure**: Extended arrangements (6-10 minutes typical), minimal verse-chorus structure, gradual introduction and removal of elements, breakdown sections stripping to drums or atmosphere before rebuilding, repetitive loops with subtle evolution, emphasis on tension-and-release dynamics

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Minimal — 1–2 bar vocal loops. Groove-driven, not rhyme-driven.
- **Rhyme quality**: Incidental. Short, groovy phrases anchor long mixes.
- **Verse structure**: No traditional verses. Single repeated phrases or very short vocal sections (4–12 words).
- **Key rule**: Groove lock — vocals must serve the rhythm, not compete with it. Uplifting, soulful, or funky tone.
- **Avoid**: Walls of lyrics. Complex rhyme schemes. Anything that breaks the groove.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2 lines/verse** (or repeated phrases) at 120-130 BPM. Groove-driven — single ideas looped. Dancefloor clarity. Topics: 1/verse max.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Deep House** | Soulful, smooth house with jazzy chords, warm analog bass, minimal but intricate percussion, and introspective or sensual atmospheres; emphasizes groove over intensity | Larry Heard, Kerri Chandler, Masters At Work, Moodymann |
| **Tech House** | Fusion of house and techno featuring mechanical rhythms, complex percussion programming, hypnotic grooves, and minimal melodic elements; functional DJ tool-focused | Green Velvet, Jamie Jones, Carl Cox, Hot Since 82 |
| **Progressive House** | Long-form builds with layered instrumentation, epic arrangements, journey-like structure, and emotional arcs; emphasizes gradual evolution over sudden drops | Sasha, John Digweed, Hernan Cattaneo, Guy J |
| **Acid House** | Characterized by squelchy, resonant TB-303 basslines, trippy filtered effects, and often minimal arrangements; originated with Phuture in Chicago, exploded in UK rave scene | Phuture, DJ Pierre, Hardfloor, Josh Wink |
| **Chicago House** | The original form: raw drum machine beats, soulful vocal samples, simple synthesizer lines, and the unmistakable feeling of late-night club energy | Frankie Knuckles, Marshall Jefferson, Larry Heard, Ron Hardy |
| **French Touch/Filter House** | Late 1990s-2000s French sound featuring disco and funk samples processed through heavy filtering, looped grooves, and playful arrangements | Daft Punk, Cassius, Stardust, Bob Sinclar, Etienne de Crecy |
| **UK Garage/2-Step** | British evolution featuring syncopated, shuffled rhythms breaking from the 4/4 grid, soulful R&B-influenced vocals, and sophisticated production | MJ Cole, Todd Edwards, Artful Dodger, Craig David |
| **Electro House** | High-energy, festival-oriented style with big room synthesizers, aggressive builds, heavy drops, and maximal production; dominated late 2000s-early 2010s EDM | Deadmau5, Wolfgang Gartner, Knife Party, Porter Robinson (early) |
| **Tropical House** | Mid-2010s style featuring laid-back tempos, steel drums, marimba, breezy melodies, and summery atmospheres; pop-crossover appeal | Kygo, Thomas Jack, Sam Feldt, Lost Frequencies |
| **Future House** | Modern evolution with pitched vocal chops, prominent sidechain pumping, melodic builds, and pop production sensibilities; emerged mid-2010s | Oliver Heldens, Tchami, Don Diablo, Disclosure |
| **Italo House** | Melodic European style featuring lush string arrangements, romantic piano, nostalgic synthesizers, and uplifting emotional content | Black Box, 49ers, Gigi D'Agostino, Cappella |
| **Afro House** | Contemporary fusion incorporating African rhythms, percussion, and vocal traditions with house production; emphasis on organic textures and tribal energy | Black Coffee, Culoe De Song, Boddhi Satva, Da Capo |

## Reference Tracks

- **Frankie Knuckles - "Your Love"** — Foundational Chicago house: Jamie Principle's sensual vocals over Larry Heard's Rhodes chords and drum machine groove; established the emotional depth possible within house's repetitive framework

- **Marshall Jefferson - "Move Your Body (The House Music Anthem)"** — The track that introduced piano to house music; infectious gospel-influenced melody, joyful energy, and meta-lyrics celebrating the genre itself; Chicago at its most exuberant

- **Larry Heard (Mr. Fingers) - "Can You Feel It"** — Deep house blueprint: minimal drum programming, lush synthesizer pads, and whispered vocals creating spiritual, transcendent atmosphere; proof that house could be profound

- **Phuture - "Acid Tracks"** — The first acid house record (1987); 12 minutes of TB-303 manipulation that spawned an entire subgenre and ignited the UK rave explosion; raw, hypnotic, revolutionary

- **Lil Louis - "French Kiss"** — Nine minutes of building tension, featuring the legendary breakdown that strips to a heartbeat before exploding; demonstrated house music's capacity for narrative and dynamics

- **Derrick May - "Strings of Life"** — Detroit-Chicago crossover masterpiece; orchestral string stabs over pulsing rhythm created new template for emotional, uplifting dance music; one of the most influential electronic tracks ever

- **Black Box - "Ride on Time"** — Italo house breakthrough (1989); Loleatta Holloway's sampled vocals (controversially uncredited) over piano and strings brought house to pop charts worldwide

- **Robin S - "Show Me Love"** — Peak 90s house vocal anthem; Stonebridge production with gospel-influenced diva vocals; template for hands-in-the-air euphoric house that dominated clubs

- **Daft Punk - "Around the World"** — French touch perfection: single vocal phrase looped 144 times over hypnotic, filtered groove; demonstrated how repetition and subtle variation create dance floor magic

- **Armand Van Helden - "You Don't Know Me"** — Late 90s filtered house with Duane Harden's soulful vocals; sophisticated sampling and production that bridged underground and mainstream

- **Disclosure - "Latch" (feat. Sam Smith)** — UK garage revival meets pop crossover; skittering 2-step rhythms, falsetto vocals, and pristine production brought house to a new generation

- **Fisher - "Losing It"** — Modern tech house phenomenon; pitched vocal loop, relentless groove, and massive bassline; represents house music's continued evolution and festival dominance

- **Black Coffee - "Drive" (feat. David Guetta & Delilah Montagu)** — Afro house sophistication; organic percussion, emotional vocals, and hypnotic build demonstrate house music's global evolution beyond its Chicago roots
