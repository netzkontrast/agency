# Throat Singing

## Genre Overview

Throat singing is a vocal technique in which a single performer produces two or more simultaneous pitches by manipulating the resonant cavities of the mouth, throat, and chest to isolate and amplify specific overtones of a fundamental drone. The practice has deep roots in the pastoral nomadic cultures of Central Asia, particularly among the Tuvan people of southern Siberia and the Khalkha Mongols, where it developed as an intimate musical dialogue with the natural landscape -- imitating wind, water, and animal sounds across the open steppe. In Tuva, the art is called khoomei (from the Tuvan word for "throat"), and its origins are tied to animist spiritual practices that predate recorded history. Separately, Inuit throat singing (katajjaq) developed in Arctic Canada as a rhythmic, breath-based game traditionally performed by pairs of women.

Tuvan throat singing gained international attention in the late 20th century after decades of suppression under Soviet communist rule, which had banned it as a "backward" ethnic practice. The 1980s cultural thaw enabled a revival, and groups like Huun-Huur-Tu (formed 1992) and soloist Kongar-ol Ondar brought Tuvan music to Western audiences through international touring and recordings on labels like Shanachie and Warner Bros. The 1999 documentary *Genghis Blues*, which followed American blues musician Paul Pena's journey to Tuva, earned an Academy Award nomination and further popularized the tradition. In 2010, UNESCO inscribed Tuvan throat singing on its Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. Meanwhile, Inuit throat singer Tanya Tagaq developed a radical solo form of katajjaq, winning the 2014 Polaris Music Prize for her album *Animism*, beating Drake and Arcade Fire.

Today, throat singing occupies a unique position at the intersection of ethnomusicology, world music, and experimental art. Tuvan and Mongolian ensembles maintain traditional repertoire while collaborating with Western musicians across jazz, rock, and electronic genres. Artists like Sainkho Namtchylak fuse Tuvan techniques with avant-garde improvisation, while overtone singing has been adopted by Western experimental vocalists and sound healers. The technique has influenced ambient and drone music production, and its physical principles -- harmonic series manipulation -- are studied by acousticians and vocal scientists worldwide.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: The human voice as primary instrument, often accompanied by traditional instruments: igil (two-stringed horsehead fiddle), doshpuluur (three-stringed lute), byzaanchy (four-stringed fiddle), khomus (jaw harp), and frame drums. Inuit throat singing uses no instruments. Modern fusion may incorporate synthesizers, electronics, or Western instruments.
- **Vocals**: The defining technique is simultaneous production of a low fundamental drone and one or more high-pitched overtone melodies. Tuvan styles include khoomei (the "default" mid-range overtone singing), sygyt (high whistle-like overtones over a steady drone), kargyraa (deep sub-harmonic undertone singing using false vocal fold vibration), and borbangnadyr (complex textured overtones). Inuit katajjaq uses rhythmic breathing patterns, voiced and unvoiced sounds, and is typically performed by two women facing each other.
- **Production**: Traditional recordings prioritize natural acoustics and minimal processing to preserve the delicate overtone relationships. Close-miking captures overtone detail. Modern fusion productions may use reverb, delay, and electronic layering. Field recordings from Tuva and Mongolia remain important documentation of the practice.
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges from meditative and trance-inducing (sygyt and khoomei) to guttural and earth-shaking (kargyraa). The emotional register connects to landscape: vast open spaces, wind, rivers, galloping horses. Inuit throat singing is playful and competitive, often punctuated by laughter. Experimental applications can be intense, dissonant, and confrontational.
- **Structure**: Traditional Tuvan songs follow verse structures tied to poetic texts praising nature, horses, and homeland. Improvised passages allow the singer to explore overtone melodies freely. Inuit throat singing follows a call-and-response game structure where two singers interlock rhythmic patterns until one breaks the pattern or laughs. Concert pieces may follow Western structural conventions.
- **Tempo**: Traditional Tuvan songs: 50-80 BPM with a steady, unhurried pulse reflecting horseback riding rhythms. Kargyraa pieces can be very slow (40-60 BPM). Inuit throat singing operates at faster tempos (100-140 BPM) with interlocking rhythmic patterns. Experimental and fusion works vary widely.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Tuvan song texts use paired couplets or quatrains, often with alliterative patterns rather than end rhyme. Mongolian long songs (urtyn duu) use free-form poetic verse. Inuit throat singing has no lyrics per se -- it uses vocables and breath sounds.
- **Rhyme quality**: Alliteration and assonance are more important than end rhyme in Tuvan and Mongolian traditions. When end rhyme occurs, it follows natural Turkic or Mongolian language patterns.
- **Verse structure**: Tuvan songs typically 4-line stanzas with a consistent syllable count per line. Mongolian long songs have extended, ornamented phrases. Inuit katajjaq is structured as interlocking rhythmic cells, not verse-chorus.
- **Key rule**: Nature imagery is central. Traditional texts praise the beauty of the homeland, specific rivers and mountains, the singer's horse, and the open steppe. The voice imitates the sounds it describes -- wind, water, hooves.
- **Avoid**: Urban or modern imagery in traditional-style pieces. Overly complex or wordy text -- throat singing demands sustained vowels and open syllables to allow overtone production. Closed consonant clusters that interrupt the drone.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 60-80 BPM. Extremely sparse text with long sustained vowel sounds. Topics: 1/verse. Open vowels (ah, oh, oo, ee) work best for overtone production. Suno cannot produce true overtone singing, but can approximate the drone-based, meditative vocal quality.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Khoomei** | The foundational Tuvan style producing mid-range overtones over a relaxed fundamental drone; the most accessible and common form; named after the Tuvan word for "throat," it serves as both a specific technique and the generic term for all Tuvan throat singing. | Huun-Huur-Tu, Kongar-ol Ondar, Chirgilchin |
| **Sygyt** | High whistle-like overtone melodies produced over a steady mid-range drone; the most melodically distinct style; the overtone melody sounds like a clear, flute-like whistle floating above the voice; demands precise tongue and lip positioning. | Huun-Huur-Tu, Kaigal-ool Khovalyg |
| **Kargyraa** | Deep sub-harmonic undertone singing using vibration of the false vocal folds (ventricular folds) to produce pitches an octave below the fundamental; creates an extremely low, rumbling, powerful sound; related to Tibetan Buddhist chant techniques. | Huun-Huur-Tu, Albert Kuvezin (Yat-Kha) |
| **Inuit Throat Singing (Katajjaq)** | Rhythmic, breath-based vocal game traditionally performed by two Inuit women standing face to face; uses voiced and unvoiced sounds, inhalation and exhalation, in interlocking patterns; competitive element where the first to break rhythm or laugh loses. | Tanya Tagaq, Nukariik (Karin and Kathy Kettler), Shina Novalinga |
| **Mongolian Long Song (Urtyn Duu)** | Elaborate, highly ornamented vocal style featuring extended melodic phrases with wide vibrato and melismatic passages; may incorporate overtone elements; texts praise the Mongolian landscape, horses, and nomadic life; UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. | Norovbanzad, Namjiliin Norovbanzad |
| **Avant-Garde / Experimental** | Fusion of throat singing techniques with free improvisation, electronic music, noise, and contemporary art music; expands the vocal palette beyond traditional repertoire into abstract sonic exploration; often performed in Western concert and gallery contexts. | Sainkho Namtchylak, Tanya Tagaq, Shelley Hirsch |
| **Folk-Rock / World Fusion** | Traditional throat singing combined with rock instrumentation, blues, or electronic production; maintains the vocal techniques while adapting them to Western song structures and recording aesthetics. | Yat-Kha, Hanggai, The HU |

## Reference Tracks

- **Huun-Huur-Tu - "Oidupaa's Sygyt"** -- A demonstration of sygyt technique by the ensemble's founding members, where a crystalline whistle-like overtone melody floats above a steady drone. This track captures the essence of Tuvan throat singing's most recognizable and technically demanding style.

- **Huun-Huur-Tu - "Orphan's Lament"** -- The title track from their 1994 album combines khoomei singing with igil fiddle over a sorrowful Tuvan melody. It introduced Western audiences to the emotional depth of Tuvan traditional music beyond mere vocal novelty.

- **Kongar-ol Ondar - "Genghis Blues" Performance** -- Ondar's performances in the 1999 Oscar-nominated documentary brought Tuvan throat singing to American cinema audiences. His mastery of sygyt and khoomei, demonstrated alongside American blues musician Paul Pena, showed the music's universal communicative power.

- **Tanya Tagaq - "Sivunittinni"** -- From *Animism* (2014), the Polaris Prize-winning album that beat Drake and Arcade Fire. Tagaq's solo transformation of traditionally duet-based Inuit katajjaq into an intense, visceral, confrontational art form redefines what throat singing can be.

- **Yat-Kha - "Yenisei Punk"** -- Albert Kuvezin's kargyraa growl over distorted electric guitar demonstrated that Tuvan throat singing could fuse with rock without losing its identity. The track title itself declares the hybrid intent.

- **The HU - "Wolf Totem"** -- This 2018 viral hit combined Mongolian throat singing with heavy metal instrumentation, garnering hundreds of millions of views and introducing throat singing to a global rock audience. The morin khuur (horsehead fiddle) riffs alongside electric guitar create a distinctive folk-metal hybrid.

- **Sainkho Namtchylak - "Out of Tuva"** -- The title track from her 1993 album pushed Tuvan vocal techniques into avant-garde territory, pairing traditional khoomei with free improvisation and electronic textures. Namtchylak's work proved that throat singing could function within contemporary experimental music.

- **Chirgilchin - "Chyraa-Khoor"** -- A traditional Tuvan nature song performed with ensemble khoomei singing and igil accompaniment. The track exemplifies the pastoral, landscape-inspired character of traditional Tuvan music -- the voices literally imitating the sounds of the wind and steppe.

- **Tanya Tagaq - "Retribution"** -- From her 2016 album, this track combines katajjaq with electronic production and layered vocals into an intense sonic assault that addresses colonial violence against Indigenous peoples. It demonstrates throat singing as a vehicle for political expression.

- **Alash Ensemble - "Achai"** -- The Tuvan trio, mentored by Kongar-ol Ondar, performs traditional repertoire with youthful energy and technical precision. Their work represents the generational continuity of Tuvan throat singing traditions in the 21st century.

- **Paul Pena - "Kargyraa Moan"** -- The blind American blues musician taught himself kargyraa from a Tuvan radio broadcast and traveled to compete at the Tuvan throat singing festival in Kyzyl, winning a prize. His cross-cultural achievement, documented in *Genghis Blues*, embodies the art form's power to transcend cultural boundaries.
