# Sea Shanties

## Genre Overview

Sea shanties are maritime work songs that originated aboard sailing ships from the 15th through 19th centuries, designed to synchronize the physical labor of hauling ropes, turning capstans, and pumping bilges through rhythmic, communal singing. The word "shanty" (also spelled "chanty" or "chantey") likely derives from the French *chanter* (to sing), and the songs served a purely functional purpose: a shantyman would sing the verse while the crew joined on the chorus, timing their pulls, pushes, or steps to the rhythmic accents. This call-and-response structure, born from necessity on the decks of merchant vessels and warships, produced some of the most durable and infectious song forms in the English-speaking world.

The golden age of sea shanties coincided with the age of sail, roughly the 1700s through the 1870s, when large crews were needed to handle the rigging and anchors of tall ships. Shanties drew from diverse sources — African work song traditions brought by enslaved and free Black sailors (who comprised a significant portion of maritime crews), Irish and Scottish folk melodies, popular songs of the day, and improvised verses born of boredom on long voyages. Different types of shanties evolved for different tasks: short-drag or halyard shanties for quick bursts of hauling, capstan shanties for the sustained effort of raising anchor, pumping shanties for the monotonous work of clearing the bilges, and forecastle (fo'c'sle) songs for leisure hours belowdecks. As steam power replaced sail in the late 19th century, the working shanty died as a living tradition, but the songs survived through folk revival movements.

Sea shanties experienced an extraordinary modern revival beginning in late 2020 when Scottish postman Nathan Evans posted a TikTok video of himself singing "The Wellerman," which went massively viral and introduced the genre to millions of young listeners worldwide. The resulting wave of TikTok duets, harmonized versions, and sea shanty content demonstrated the genre's remarkable resilience — songs designed for communal participation translated perfectly to social media collaboration. Professional groups like The Longest Johns and Fisherman's Friends saw surging interest, and Nathan Evans scored a UK number-one hit. The revival revealed that sea shanties' fundamental appeal — simple melodies, participatory choruses, vivid storytelling — transcends their maritime origins.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Traditionally unaccompanied (a cappella) since shanties were sung during physical labor with no hands free for instruments; modern performances add concertina (squeezebox), fiddle, bodhran, acoustic guitar, tin whistle, banjo, and upright bass; folk revival arrangements may include full band instrumentation; The Longest Johns popularized rich a cappella harmonies for the TikTok era
- **Vocals**: Call-and-response between shantyman (soloist) and crew (chorus) is the defining feature; strong, projected vocals suitable for outdoor singing over wind and waves; unpolished, robust tone preferred over trained precision; group singing with natural harmonies (often in thirds); male voices predominate historically but the tradition is not gender-exclusive
- **Production**: Raw and unpolished — the aesthetic favors authenticity over studio perfection; natural reverb and room sound; live group recording preferred to isolate-and-layer approaches; modern productions may add subtle processing while maintaining the communal, acoustic character; the TikTok revival demonstrated the power of deliberately lo-fi, phone-recorded performances
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges from rousing and boisterous (hauling shanties) to steady and hypnotic (capstan shanties) to melancholic and nostalgic (forecastle songs); communal spirit and camaraderie are constant; a sense of shared labor and solidarity pervades even the lighthearted songs; themes of the sea, distant ports, leaving loved ones, drinking, and hardship
- **Structure**: Verse-chorus with call-and-response; shantyman sings verses (often improvised or adapted), crew responds with a fixed chorus or refrain; verses are typically 2-4 lines; choruses are short, memorable, and rhythmically strong; many shanties use a "heave-ho" or similar work cry integrated into the rhythm
- **Tempo**: 80-140 BPM depending on the type of work; halyard shanties are faster (110-140 BPM) for quick pulls; capstan shanties are moderate and steady (90-110 BPM) for sustained walking; pumping shanties match the pump rhythm; forecastle songs are free-tempo since they are leisure songs, not work songs

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: ABAB or ABCB — simple schemes that allow improvisation within the verse while keeping the chorus fixed
- **Rhyme quality**: Straightforward and functional; near-rhymes and slant rhymes are common since shanties were composed orally by working sailors, not trained poets
- **Verse structure**: 2-4 lines per verse with a fixed chorus/refrain of 2-4 lines; the chorus is the anchor — it never changes while verses can be improvised, extended, or swapped
- **Key rule**: The chorus must be instantly singable by a group with no rehearsal — simple melody, strong rhythm, memorable words
- **Avoid**: Complex vocabulary or abstract imagery; any structure that weakens the call-and-response dynamic; choruses that are hard to remember or sing; losing the rhythmic pulse that defines the genre
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse + 2-4 line chorus** at 80-140 BPM. Repetitive chorus is essential. Strong rhythmic accents on work beats. Clear call-and-response structure. Topics: 1/song (narrative or thematic).

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Halyard / Short-Drag Shanties** | Quick, powerful bursts for hauling ropes and raising sails; short phrases with explosive rhythmic accents on the pull; fastest shanty type with sharp, decisive rhythm | Traditional ("Haul Away Joe," "Blow the Man Down") |
| **Capstan Shanties** | Steady, marching rhythm for the sustained effort of walking around the capstan to raise anchor; longer, more narrative verses since the work was continuous; the most musically developed shanty type | Traditional ("Santiana," "South Australia," "Rio Grande") |
| **Pumping Shanties** | Matched to the rhythmic motion of the ship's bilge pump; monotonous, hypnotic quality suited to repetitive labor; often borrowed from other shanty types | Traditional ("Strike the Bell," "Fire Down Below") |
| **Forecastle Songs** | Leisure songs sung off-duty belowdecks; not true work songs — no synchronization function; broader musical range including ballads, comic songs, and sentimental pieces; closest to mainstream folk song | Traditional ("Spanish Ladies," "Rolling Down to Old Maui") |
| **Folk Revival Shanties** | Maritime songs performed in folk club and festival contexts; polished arrangements with added instrumentation; emphasis on historical authenticity and vocal harmony | Fisherman's Friends, Kimber's Men, The Young Tradition |
| **Modern / TikTok Era** | Contemporary performances and recordings reaching new audiences through social media; a cappella harmonies, sometimes with modern production touches; emphasis on singalong accessibility | The Longest Johns, Nathan Evans, The Wellerman viral trend |
| **Maritime Folk Crossover** | Songs about the sea that draw on shanty traditions but incorporate broader folk, Celtic, or singer-songwriter elements; original compositions alongside traditional material | Stan Rogers, Great Big Sea, The Dubliners |

## Reference Tracks

- **Traditional - "The Wellerman" (arr. The Longest Johns / Nathan Evans)** — The song that launched the 2021 TikTok revival; a New Zealand whaling song with an irresistible chorus ("Soon may the Wellerman come / To bring us sugar and tea and rum"); demonstrates the perfect shanty chorus — instantly memorable, rhythmically compelling, and designed for group participation

- **Traditional - "Drunken Sailor" (various)** — Perhaps the most universally known shanty; a halyard shanty with a driving rhythm and call-and-response structure that exemplifies the genre at its most accessible; the "way-hay and up she rises" chorus is the archetype of a shanty refrain

- **Stan Rogers - "Barrett's Privateers" (1976)** — Modern composition that functions as a perfect shanty: narrative verses tell the story of a disastrous privateering voyage while the "God damn them all" chorus invites full-throated group participation; proof that new shanties can match the power of traditional ones

- **Traditional - "South Australia" (various)** — A capstan shanty with a broad, swinging rhythm perfect for the sustained walking effort of raising anchor; "Heave away, haul away" refrain demonstrates how work cries integrate into melody; narrative verses about leaving port and heading to sea

- **Traditional - "Leave Her, Johnny" (various)** — The most poignant shanty: traditionally sung only when leaving a ship at voyage's end; slow, melancholic verses cataloguing grievances against the ship, captain, and voyage; the emotional depth possible within the shanty form; a reminder that these songs carried real human feeling

- **Great Big Sea - "Donkey Riding" (1993)** — Maritime folk-rock crossover that brings electric energy to a traditional shanty without losing its communal singalong spirit; demonstrates how shanty traditions translate to modern rock arrangements while preserving the call-and-response core
