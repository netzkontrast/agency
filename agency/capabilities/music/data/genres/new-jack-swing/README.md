# New Jack Swing

## Genre Overview

New jack swing is a fusion genre that emerged from Harlem, New York in the mid-1980s, blending the rhythmic grooves of hip-hop with the melodic sensibility of R&B, pop, and dance music. The style was pioneered by producer Teddy Riley, who developed the sound by layering swung hip-hop drum machine patterns beneath smooth vocal harmonies and rap verses. The term was coined by journalist Barry Michael Cooper in an October 1987 Village Voice profile of Riley, combining the slang "new jack" (a term meaning newcomer, borrowed from Grandmaster Caz of the Cold Crush Brothers) with "swing" as an analogy to the speakeasy jazz era. Riley's group Guy, formed in 1987 with Aaron Hall and Timmy Gatling, codified the sound with their 1988 debut album, and tracks like "Groove Me" and "Teddy's Jam" became the genre's foundational texts.

The genre's commercial peak spanned roughly 1988 to 1995, dominating both R&B and pop charts. Keith Sweat's *Make It Last Forever* (1987) was an early landmark, followed by Bobby Brown's *Don't Be Cruel* (1988), which sold over 12 million copies worldwide. The sound rapidly spread across the R&B landscape: Bell Biv DeVoe's *Poison* (1990) brought a harder edge, SWV and TLC brought female perspectives, and Michael Jackson recruited Teddy Riley for *Dangerous* (1991), which remains the best-selling new jack swing album of all time. The genre also produced crossover hits through acts like Heavy D & the Boyz, Al B. Sure!, and Johnny Gill. Producers like Babyface and L.A. Reid, Jimmy Jam and Terry Lewis, and Dallas Austin adapted and expanded the template.

By the mid-1990s, new jack swing's dominance faded as hip-hop soul and neo-soul emerged. Montell Jordan's "This Is How We Do It" (1995) is often cited as the genre's last major hit. However, the style's DNA persists in contemporary R&B production. The swing-influenced drum programming, the fusion of singing and rapping within single tracks, and the emphasis on groove-heavy production all became permanent features of mainstream R&B. Modern producers like Bruno Mars, who explicitly revisited the sound on tracks like "Finesse" (2018 remix), and Chris Brown continue to draw from the new jack swing playbook.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Drum machines (Roland TR-808, Linn LM-1, E-mu SP-1200), synthesizers (Korg M1, Roland D-50), sampled and programmed bass, sampled horn stabs, occasional live guitar and keyboards; beats built on swing-quantized hip-hop rhythms with layered synth pads
- **Vocals**: Smooth R&B singing combined with rap verses or spoken interludes; group harmonies common; ad-libs and vocal runs; tenor and falsetto male vocals prominent; female groups brought tight, synchronized vocal arrangements; vocal layering and stacking
- **Production**: Hip-hop drum programming with swing quantization (the defining technical element); layered synthesizers creating dense harmonic beds; sampled scratches and breakbeats; heavy use of reverb and gated snare; clean, punchy mixes with prominent bass; producer-driven aesthetic where the beat is as important as the vocal
- **Energy/Mood**: Confident, danceable, romantic, and youthful; ranges from party anthems to slow-jam ballads; swagger mixed with vulnerability; street credibility meeting pop accessibility; celebratory and sexy
- **Structure**: Verse-chorus with rap bridge or breakdown; intros often feature spoken or rapped sections; 8-bar verses and 8-bar choruses standard; extended dance breaks and beat breakdowns common; songs often feature a key change or build in the final chorus
- **Tempo**: Typically 95-120 BPM; midtempo grooves with pronounced swing feel; slower ballads around 70-85 BPM; uptempo dance tracks can reach 125 BPM; the swing quantization makes tempos feel looser and more body-friendly than straight-eighth dance music

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB or ABAB — clean, accessible rhyme schemes that prioritize singability and memorability
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean end rhymes with occasional internal rhyme; nothing overly complex; emphasis on smooth delivery and rhythmic fit over elaborate wordplay
- **Verse structure**: 8 lines per verse; rap verses typically 8-16 bars; sung verses shorter and hookier; pre-chorus common as a build to the chorus
- **Key rule**: Every track needs a hook that works as both a sung melody and a rhythmic chant — the best new jack swing choruses are simultaneously singable and rappable
- **Avoid**: Overly literary or abstract lyrics; excessively slow pacing that fights the groove; lyrics that ignore the rhythmic swagger of the beat
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6 lines/verse** at 100-115 BPM. Swing feel gives moderate space per line. Rap bridges can be denser (8 lines). Topics: 1-2/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic New Jack Swing** | The foundational Teddy Riley sound: swung drum machine beats, layered synths, R&B vocals over hip-hop rhythms; the template that defined the genre from 1987-1992 | Guy, Keith Sweat, Bobby Brown, Al B. Sure! |
| **Hip-Hop Soul** | Later evolution emphasizing the hip-hop side: heavier beats, more prominent rap elements, grittier production; bridged new jack swing into the mid-1990s R&B sound | Mary J. Blige, Jodeci, R. Kelly, Total |
| **New Jack Ballad** | Slow-tempo new jack swing applying the genre's production techniques to romantic ballads; drum machine patterns at ballad tempos, synth pads, intimate vocal delivery | Keith Sweat, Johnny Gill, Al B. Sure!, Guy |
| **Pop New Jack** | Radio-friendly, polished variant with broader pop appeal; smoother production, brighter synths, less hip-hop grit; crossed over to mainstream pop charts | Bobby Brown, Bell Biv DeVoe, MC Hammer, C+C Music Factory |
| **New Jill Swing** | Female-led new jack swing; tight vocal harmonies, empowered lyrics, the same rhythmic foundation with distinctly feminine perspective and vocal arrangements | SWV, TLC, En Vogue, Jade |
| **New Jack Gospel** | Gospel music adopting new jack swing production techniques; contemporary Christian artists incorporating swung beats and synth-heavy arrangements into worship music | Kirk Franklin, BeBe & CeCe Winans, Commissioned |
| **Quiet Storm New Jack** | Smoother, more atmospheric variant blending new jack production with quiet storm sensibility; late-night R&B with programmed beats and lush synth arrangements | Babyface, After 7, El DeBarge, Ralph Tresvant |
| **UK Swingbeat** | British interpretation of new jack swing; UK producers and artists adapting the American sound with local flavor; slightly different production aesthetic | Omar, Soul II Soul (later work), Eternal, MN8 |

## Reference Tracks

- **Guy - "Groove Me"** — The foundational new jack swing track: Teddy Riley's swung drum machine pattern, layered synths, and Aaron Hall's raw tenor vocal established every element the genre would build on. This is where the blueprint was drawn.

- **Bobby Brown - "Every Little Step"** — Pop new jack swing at its peak: L.A. Reid and Babyface's production meets Brown's charismatic delivery. The track's irresistible groove and Brown's dancing made it a crossover smash that introduced the genre to mainstream pop audiences.

- **Keith Sweat - "I Want Her"** — Early new jack swing landmark from 1987: Sweat's pleading tenor over a programmed beat with synth pads defined the new jack ballad template. Raw emotion meeting drum machine precision.

- **Bell Biv DeVoe - "Poison"** — Harder-edged new jack swing: the New Edition offshoot stripped the sound to its most aggressive, with a minimal arrangement, prominent rap elements, and a bassline-driven groove that proved the genre could be menacing.

- **SWV - "Weak"** — New jill swing perfection: tight three-part harmonies over a midtempo swung beat, with a melody so strong it reached number one on the pop charts. Demonstrated that female artists owned this sound as much as anyone.

- **Michael Jackson - "Remember the Time"** — Teddy Riley producing the King of Pop: lush new jack swing production with Jackson's vocals gliding over the swung rhythm. The biggest artist in music validating the genre's commercial dominance.

- **En Vogue - "My Lovin' (You're Never Gonna Get It)"** — Four-part vocal group harmonies over Denzil Foster and Thomas McElroy's new jack production: sophisticated and sexy, with a chorus hook that remains one of the era's most recognizable.

- **Heavy D & the Boyz - "Now That We Found Love"** — Party-rap new jack swing at its most joyful: Heavy D's charismatic flow over a classic reggae sample flipped into a swung groove. Bridged the hip-hop and R&B audiences effortlessly.

- **Jodeci - "Come and Talk to Me"** — The bridge from new jack swing to hip-hop soul: DeVante Swing's production is darker and grittier than classic new jack, while K-Ci's gospel-trained vocal brings raw emotional intensity. Pointed the way forward.

- **Teddy Riley & Guy - "Teddy's Jam"** — Pure production showcase: Riley demonstrating every technique in the new jack swing toolkit over an instrumental that became a DJ staple. Swung beats, synth stabs, scratches, and breakdowns.

- **Al B. Sure! - "Nite and Day"** — Smooth pop-R&B new jack swing: Sure's silky tenor over a midtempo groove with lush synth arrangements. Proved the genre could be sophisticated and tender without losing its rhythmic identity.

- **Montell Jordan - "This Is How We Do It"** — The genre's victory lap and swan song: released in 1995 as new jack swing's commercial era was ending, this party anthem distilled the sound to its most universal and celebratory. A fitting final chapter.
