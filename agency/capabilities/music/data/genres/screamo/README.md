# Screamo

## Genre Overview

Screamo is a subgenre of hardcore punk and emo that emerged in the early 1990s, built on willfully experimental dissonance, extreme dynamics, and screamed vocals delivering raw emotional catharsis. The genre originated in San Diego, California, where bands like Heroin, Angel Hair, and Antioch Arrow began pushing the sound of emotional hardcore into more chaotic, abrasive territory. Gravity Records, founded in 1991 by Heroin bassist Matt Anderson, became the first label to document this new sound, releasing the short, explosive recordings that would define the genre's early identity. Unlike mainstream conceptions that later applied "screamo" loosely to any rock band with screamed vocals, the original scene was rooted in DIY hardcore networks, house shows, and a deliberate rejection of commercial ambition.

The genre's second phase took shape in the late 1990s as East Coast bands pushed screamo into new extremes. In New York, Saetia fused literary lyrics with devastating dynamic shifts. In Amherst, Massachusetts, Orchid merged screamo with powerviolence and grindcore to create what would be called "emoviolence." In Sterling, Virginia, pg.99 built a sprawling eight-piece collective that blurred the line between screamo and noise rock, while their side project City of Caterpillar folded in post-rock atmospherics. In Connecticut, Jeromes Dream stripped songs down to sub-minute bursts of shrieked vocals and mathematical chaos. These four acts — Saetia, Orchid, pg.99, and Jeromes Dream — are widely regarded as the genre's foundational pillars, and their influence rippled outward through an international underground connected by zines, tape trading, and small-run vinyl pressings.

Screamo continued to evolve through the 2000s and into the present day. In Japan, Envy expanded the genre's scope by weaving post-rock grandeur into screamo's intensity, influencing a generation of bands worldwide. European scenes in Italy, France, and Germany developed their own regional variations, with bands like Raein and La Quiete adding melodic sophistication. In the 2010s, a new wave of bands — including Touche Amore, La Dispute, and Pianos Become the Teeth — brought screamo-adjacent sounds to larger audiences on independent labels, while underground acts like Loma Prieta and Comadre kept the rawer traditions alive. The retronym "skramz" emerged in online communities to distinguish the original DIY screamo lineage from the broader misuse of the term, preserving the genre's identity as something fundamentally different from mainstream post-hardcore with screamed vocals.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Dissonant, angular guitar work with rapid shifts between clean passages and walls of distortion; bass often buried in the mix or used as a low-end anchor for chaotic guitar interplay; blast beats, irregular time signatures, and frantic drumming; songs frequently under two minutes, though atmospheric variants stretch to eight or more
- **Vocals**: Screamed, shrieked, or howled delivery — throat-shredding and desperate rather than growled or gutturally "metal"; clean vocals used sparingly for contrast, often whispered or spoken; multiple vocalists common in larger collectives; lyrics frequently delivered at the edge of intelligibility
- **Production**: Deliberately raw and lo-fi in the classic tradition; recorded in basements, practice spaces, and budget studios; tape hiss and room noise treated as texture rather than flaws; later and more atmospheric variants allow for fuller, reverb-heavy production while retaining dynamic extremes
- **Energy/Mood**: Cathartic intensity that cycles between explosive violence and fragile quiet; emotional devastation rather than mere anger; tension-and-release as the primary structural engine; a sense of urgency and impermanence — songs often feel like they could collapse at any moment
- **Structure**: Quiet-loud dynamics pushed to extremes; abrupt transitions with little warning; rejection of verse-chorus convention in favor of through-composed emotional arcs; false endings, sudden drops to silence, and eruptions from near-stillness; some tracks are 30-second bursts, others are slow-building epics
- **Scene/Ethos**: DIY at its core — self-released vinyl and tape, hand-screened artwork, house shows and basements; anti-commercial stance more rigid than most punk subgenres; strong overlap with anarchist and radical politics; international underground network connected by small labels and distros

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Rhyme is secondary to emotional intensity. When present, loose ABCB or no fixed scheme. Many screamo lyrics read closer to poetry or prose fragments than traditional song lyrics.
- **Rhyme quality**: Near-rhyme, assonance, and internal rhyme used for texture rather than structure. Forced perfect rhymes sound out of place in this genre.
- **Verse structure**: Highly irregular — 2-6 lines per section, but sections blur into each other. No obligation to repeat structures. A song might have one long stanza that builds to a breaking point.
- **Key rule**: Imagery-driven and visceral. Metaphor, literary allusion, and abstract emotional language over literal storytelling. Vulnerability expressed through extremity — the scream is the confession.
- **Avoid**: Cliched angst without specificity. Metal-style aggression without emotional core. Neatly resolved narratives. Anything that sounds like it belongs in a pop-punk chorus.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/section** at 160-200+ BPM for chaotic passages. Quiet sections can stretch to 6-8 lines at slower tempos. Extreme tempo shifts within a single song are expected. Topics: 1-2/section, expressed obliquely.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Era | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-----|-------------------|
| **San Diego Screamo** | The original wave; short, chaotic hardcore with screamed vocals and dissonant guitar work, rooted in the Gravity Records scene | 1991-1995 | Heroin, Angel Hair, Antioch Arrow, Swing Kids |
| **East Coast Screamo** | The genre's defining era; literary lyrics, extreme dynamics, and more developed song structures from New York, Virginia, and New England scenes | 1997-2003 | Saetia, Orchid, pg.99, Jeromes Dream |
| **Emoviolence** | Fusion of screamo with powerviolence and grindcore; blast beats, breakneck tempo shifts, sub-minute songs, maximum aggression | 1997-2005 | Orchid, In/Humanity, Majority Rule, Bucket Full of Teeth |
| **Skramz** | Retronym for DIY-rooted screamo in the original tradition; distinguishes underground screamo from mainstream misuse of the term | 1990s-present | Saetia, Jeromes Dream, Lord Snow, Loma Prieta |
| **Post-Rock Screamo** | Atmospheric screamo incorporating post-rock dynamics, extended instrumental passages, and cinematic builds alongside harsh vocals | 2000-present | City of Caterpillar, Envy, Funeral Diner, Pianos Become the Teeth |
| **European Screamo** | Continental scenes in Italy, France, and Germany; often more melodic and politically engaged than American counterparts | 2000s-present | Raein, La Quiete, Daiai, Amanda Woodward |
| **Japanese Screamo** | Tokyo and broader Japanese scene; post-rock and shoegaze influences, longer compositions, atmospheric grandeur | 1995-present | Envy, Heaven in Her Arms, killie, Swarrrm |
| **Screamo Revival / The Wave** | 2010s bands on larger indie labels bringing screamo-adjacent intensity to wider audiences; more polished but retaining emotional core | 2010-present | Touche Amore, La Dispute, Pianos Become the Teeth, Loma Prieta |
| **Sasscore** | Art-punk and no-wave influenced screamo; angular, danceable rhythms mixed with chaotic hardcore energy | 1992-2000s | Antioch Arrow, The Blood Brothers, An Albatross, The Locust |
| **Blackened Screamo** | Screamo incorporating black metal elements — tremolo-picked guitars, blast beats, atmospheric darkness | 2005-present | Funeral Diner (later work), Sed Non Satiata, Respire, Oathbreaker |

## Reference Tracks

### San Diego Pioneers
1. **Heroin - "Pale"** (1992) - The template for screamo's sound: 90 seconds of explosive dissonance, frantic drumming, and screamed vocals over angular guitar work. Shows how hardcore punk could be pushed into emotional extremity without sacrificing speed.

2. **Antioch Arrow - "The Scream Box"** (1993) - Art-punk meets screamo; jagged, angular riffs with a danceable undercurrent buried beneath chaos. Demonstrates the sasscore branch of early screamo.

### East Coast Pillars
3. **Saetia - "Some Natures Catch No Plagues"** (1998) - Alternates between hushed, melodic passages and devastating screamed eruptions. Literary lyrics delivered at the breaking point. The emotional blueprint for second-wave screamo.

4. **Orchid - "Epilogue of a Car Crash"** (1999) - Emoviolence distilled to its core: blast beats, shrieked vocals, and powerviolence intensity crammed into barely a minute. Every second is deliberate violence.

5. **pg.99 - "In Love with an Apparition"** (2001) - Virginia screamo at its most expansive; noise, melody, and chaos woven through an eight-piece collective. Shows how screamo could be abrasive and inventive simultaneously.

6. **Jeromes Dream - "In the Summer We Move Faster"** (2000) - Sub-minute mathematical chaos with shrill, almost inhuman vocal shrieks. The extreme end of what screamo's brevity and intensity could achieve.

### Post-Rock Screamo
7. **City of Caterpillar - "A Little Change Could Go a Long Ways"** (2002) - Post-rock patience meeting screamo ferocity; extended quiet passages erupt into devastating walls of sound. The defining statement of atmospheric screamo.

8. **Envy - "A Warm Room"** (2003) - Tokyo post-rock grandeur married to screamo intensity. Cinematic builds, orchestral dynamics, and throat-tearing vocals coexisting within a single composition. Proved screamo could be epic in scope.

9. **Funeral Diner - "We Become Buried"** (2005) - Blackened atmosphere and crushing heaviness drawn from screamo's emotional well. The bridge between screamo and post-metal.

### Emoviolence
10. **Orchid - "Dance Tonight! Revolution Tomorrow!"** (2000) - Title track showcasing Orchid at their most unhinged; grindcore-speed transitions, feedback squalls, and vocals that sound like they're shredding the singer's throat in real time.

### International
11. **Raein - "Tigerberry"** (2009) - Italian screamo with melodic sophistication; proves the genre can carry beauty alongside devastation without losing its edge. The European scene's emotional range on display.

### The Wave / Revival
12. **Touche Amore - "Honest Sleep"** (2011) - Modern screamo economy: 90 seconds of Jeremy Bolm's desperate delivery over angular post-hardcore riffs. Every word lands because none are wasted.

13. **La Dispute - "King Park"** (2011) - Spoken-word narrative building to a devastating screamed climax. Literary ambition meeting raw emotional catharsis. Shows how screamo's intensity can serve storytelling.

14. **Pianos Become the Teeth - "I'll Get By"** (2011) - Post-rock screamo at its most devastating; slow-building atmospheric tension that collapses into walls of screamed catharsis. Demonstrates the genre's patience.

15. **Loma Prieta - "Trilogy 0 (Debris)"** (2012) - Modern skramz that honors the genre's roots while pushing forward; dissonant, urgent, and uncompromising. The underground lineage continuing without dilution.
