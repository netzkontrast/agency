# Post-Dubstep

## Genre Overview

Post-dubstep is an umbrella term for a range of musical styles that emerged in the late 2000s from the UK dubstep scene, retaining dubstep's sparse, syncopated rhythms and heavy sub-bass while incorporating elements of R&B, soul, ambient, garage, and indie pop. The genre's pivotal figure is Burial, whose self-titled debut (2006) and landmark second album *Untrue* (2007) reimagined dubstep as an emotionally complex, deeply melancholic form. *Untrue*, with its pitched and timestretched vocal samples, skipping drum patterns, and rain-soaked atmosphere, earned a Mercury Prize nomination and established a template for introspective, sample-based electronic music that countless producers would follow. Where dubstep's original South London scene centered on bass weight and physical impact, post-dubstep turned inward, prioritizing mood, texture, and emotional vulnerability.

The genre coalesced around 2009-2011 as a wave of producers expanded on Burial's emotional vocabulary while introducing their own influences. Mount Kimbie's debut EPs *Maybes* and *Sketch on Glass* (both 2009), followed by their album *Crooks & Lovers* (2010), fused dubstep rhythms with ambient textures, post-rock dynamics, and sampled guitars. James Blake emerged as the genre's most commercially successful figure: his self-titled debut album (2011) foregrounded his own piano and vocals within a bass-music framework, drawing from gospel and soul traditions while maintaining dubstep's rhythmic DNA. Jamie xx brought post-dubstep aesthetics into pop through his production for The xx, while Four Tet, already an established artist, incorporated post-dubstep's spatial qualities into his own evolving sound. Other key figures included Darkstar, whose album *North* (2010) blended vocoder-processed vocals with dubstep-influenced production, and Joy Orbison, whose 2009 single "Hyph Mngo" on Hotflush Recordings became a genre-defining anthem.

Post-dubstep was a relatively short-lived phenomenon as a distinct genre label, largely absorbed by 2013 into broader categories like UK bass, alternative R&B, and experimental electronic music. However, its influence has been enormous. James Blake's integration of electronic production with singer-songwriter vulnerability helped shape the sound of alternative pop throughout the 2010s. Burial's atmospheric approach continues to influence ambient, electronic, and even hip-hop producers. The aesthetic of emotional, bass-inflected electronic music that post-dubstep pioneered persists in the work of artists like Arca, Yves Tumor, and SOPHIE, even as the genre label itself has faded from use.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Software synthesizers, sampled and processed vocals, sub-bass, sparse percussion (often incorporating garage and 2-step rhythms), ambient pads, piano, guitar (often sampled and manipulated), field recordings, vinyl crackle and atmospheric noise
- **Vocals**: Central to many post-dubstep tracks; often pitch-shifted, time-stretched, or heavily processed; R&B and soul influences; can be sampled fragments or original performances; emotional vulnerability is key; James Blake's falsetto became a defining vocal approach
- **Production**: Sparse arrangements with cavernous space; heavy sub-bass balanced with delicate upper-register textures; syncopated rhythms drawn from UK garage and 2-step; extensive use of reverb and delay; digital manipulation of samples; attention to micro-detail and textural nuance
- **Energy/Mood**: Melancholic, introspective, atmospheric, vulnerable, nocturnal, yearning; emotional depth prioritized over dancefloor functionality; a sense of urban loneliness and late-night contemplation; beauty found in imperfection and fragmentation
- **Structure**: Looser than conventional pop; builds and dissipates rather than following verse-chorus patterns; tracks may center on a single emotional moment or texture; quiet/loud dynamics used subtly; songs typically 3-6 minutes; some extended ambient passages
- **Tempo**: 130-140 BPM with 2-step or shuffled rhythms creating a perceived slower feel; some tracks at 100-120 BPM; the rhythmic feel is more important than specific BPM, often incorporating the skippy, syncopated patterns of UK garage

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Free or loose; ABAB or ABCB when present. Lyrics prioritize emotional authenticity over formal structure.
- **Rhyme quality**: Soft, understated. Slant rhyme and near-rhyme preferred over hard rhyme. The voice blends with the production rather than dominating it.
- **Verse structure**: Short, fragmented lines, 2-4 per section. Repetition of key phrases creates mantra-like effect. Space between lines is as important as the words.
- **Key rule**: Emotional vulnerability is everything. Lyrics should feel confessional, intimate, and unguarded.
- **Avoid**: Aggressive or confrontational tone. Dense or clever wordplay that breaks the mood. Anything that feels performative rather than genuine.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/section** at 130 BPM. Sparse, with long pauses between phrases. Vocal delivery soft and intimate. Topics: 1/track, usually personal and emotional.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Burial School** | Atmospheric, sample-based post-dubstep directly in Burial's lineage; pitched vocal samples, skipping rhythms, rain-soaked atmosphere, vinyl crackle textures; the genre's emotional core | Burial, Zomby (early), Vacant |
| **Future Garage** | Post-dubstep variant emphasizing UK garage rhythms with ambient textures and emotional vocals; more rhythmically active than Burial's approach, but equally atmospheric | Mount Kimbie, Disclosure (early), Jamie xx |
| **Bass Ballad** | Singer-songwriter approach to bass music; foregrounding original vocals and piano over sub-bass and sparse electronic production; James Blake's primary contribution | James Blake, Jamie Woon, Sampha |
| **UK Bass** | Broader category absorbing post-dubstep's rhythmic and production aesthetics into a more diverse palette; grime, garage, dubstep, and R&B all feeding into a flexible electronic framework | Joy Orbison, Pearson Sound, Hessle Audio roster |
| **Night Bus** | Ambient-leaning post-dubstep focused on nocturnal urban atmosphere; field recordings of city sounds layered with sub-bass and ghostly textures; soundtrack to late-night London | Burial (ambient works), Raime, Demdike Stare |
| **Wonky** | Pitched and detuned synthesizers over skewed rhythms; playful disorientation within post-dubstep's atmospheric framework; more colorful and experimental than the genre's melancholic mainstream | Rustie, Hudson Mohawke (early), Joker |

## Reference Tracks

- **Burial - "Archangel"** — From *Untrue* (2007): the post-dubstep anthem — pitched R&B vocal samples over skipping garage rhythms and sub-bass, drenched in vinyl crackle and urban melancholy; defined the genre's emotional vocabulary in four minutes

- **Burial - "Untrue"** — The title track: ghostly pitched vocals, shuffling 2-step rhythms, and an atmosphere of nocturnal longing that captures everything the genre represents; bass music as emotional confession

- **James Blake - "Limit to Your Love"** — Feist cover reimagined through post-dubstep: solo piano and falsetto giving way to earth-shaking sub-bass; the moment post-dubstep entered mainstream consciousness

- **James Blake - "CMYK"** — Early single (2010): glitched vocal samples, massive sub-bass drops, and sparse arrangement; more rhythmically aggressive than his later work, closer to the dancefloor roots

- **Mount Kimbie - "Carbonated"** — From *Crooks & Lovers* (2010): sampled guitar textures over shuffling rhythms and sub-bass; warm, tactile production that brought post-dubstep into living rooms

- **Joy Orbison - "Hyph Mngo"** — Genre-defining single (2009): two-step garage rhythms, pitched-up vocal sample, infectious melody over bass weight; bridged the dancefloor and the headphone experience

- **Jamie xx - "Far Nearer"** — Steel drums over sub-bass and garage rhythms: tropical warmth meeting London bass culture; showed post-dubstep could be joyful as well as melancholic

- **Darkstar - "Aidy's Girl Is a Computer"** — Vocoder-processed vocals over dubstep-influenced production (2009): robotic emotion, cold but affecting; Hyperdub's contribution to the post-dubstep canon

- **Four Tet - "Love Cry"** — From *There Is Love in You* (2010): sampled vocal fragments creating a devotional atmosphere over shuffling rhythms; spiritual post-dubstep from an established experimentalist

- **Sampha - "Blood on Me"** — From *Process* (2017): post-dubstep's emotional DNA carried into art-pop; skittering percussion, sub-bass, and raw vocal performance; the genre's legacy in contemporary form

- **Jamie Woon - "Night Air"** — Soul vocals over Burial-produced bass music (2010): the meeting of singer-songwriter craft and post-dubstep production; proved the genre could support conventional singing

- **Rustie - "Ultra Thizz"** — From *Glass Swords* (2011): wonky post-dubstep at maximum color and energy; detuned synths and maximalist production that offered a brighter alternative to the genre's melancholic mainstream
