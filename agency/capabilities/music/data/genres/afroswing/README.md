# Afroswing

## Genre Overview

Afroswing is a UK-born fusion genre that emerged from London's African and Caribbean diaspora communities in the mid-2010s. The term was coined by rapper Kojo Funds around 2016, describing a sound that blends Afrobeats rhythms and melodies with dancehall riddims, UK rap, grime energy, and contemporary R&B. Unlike Nigerian Afrobeats, which grew from Lagos's music scene and draws on highlife, juju, and fuji traditions, Afroswing is a distinctly British creation shaped by the multicultural experience of second-generation West African and Caribbean youth in London. The genre's rhythmic foundation borrows from Afrobeats percussion but layers in Jamaican dancehall's bounce and the lyrical directness of UK rap, producing something that belongs to neither parent genre alone.

The sound coalesced through a series of breakthrough moments between 2015 and 2018. WSTRN's "In2" reached number four on the UK Singles Chart in 2015 after receiving a co-sign from Drake's OVO Sound Radio. Kojo Funds and Abra Cadabra's "Dun Talkin'" won Best Song at the 2017 MOBO Awards. J Hus's debut album Common Sense (2017), executive produced by Jae5, entered the UK Top 10 and earned a Mercury Prize nomination, establishing the template for Afroswing as album-length art rather than a singles-driven scene. Not3s's "Addison Lee" became a viral anthem, while NSG's "Options" featuring Tion Wayne reached number seven on the UK Singles Chart in 2019. Producer Jae5, a British-Ghanaian from Plaistow in East London, became the genre's defining sonic architect, crafting the warm, rhythmically complex instrumentals that underpinned many of these hits.

Afroswing's cultural significance extends beyond chart positions. It represented the first UK genre to center the African diaspora experience on its own terms, rather than through the lens of grime or UK garage. The sound provided a framework for artists to express dual cultural identities -- African heritage and British upbringing -- without choosing one over the other. By 2018, Afroswing artists including WSTRN, Yxng Bane, Not3s, and Hardy Caprio appeared at SXSW in Texas, marking the genre's first international festival representation. Though the initial commercial wave subsided in the early 2020s as UK drill and amapiano drew attention, Afroswing's DNA persists in British pop, and NSG launched a "Make Afroswing Great Again" campaign in 2023, hosting concerts to revive the sound. The genre's influence on how British audiences receive African-rooted music remains significant.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Synth-driven melodies over programmed drums, warm chord progressions (piano, Rhodes, guitar), 808 bass kicks, dancehall-influenced percussion patterns, shakers and hi-hat rolls, occasional horn or brass stabs, minimal live instrumentation with polished digital production
- **Vocals**: Melodic singing blended with conversational rap flows, smooth delivery switching between English, Caribbean patois, and West African Pidgin, ad-libs and vocal hooks designed for sing-along, male vocals predominate with an intimate or flirtatious tone
- **Production**: Drum patterns emphasize the snare on the third beat (a signature identified by producer Steel Banglez), layered percussion combining Afrobeats polyrhythm with dancehall swing, sidechain compression on bass, clean and radio-ready mixes with space for vocal performance
- **Energy/Mood**: Feel-good and danceable with romantic or celebratory themes, summer-ready warmth, flirtatious energy balanced with street credibility, ranges from laid-back cruising tracks to uptempo party anthems
- **Structure**: Verse-chorus format with strong melodic hooks, short and radio-friendly (typically 3-4 minutes), catchy refrains built for repetition, instrumental drops or breakdowns that strip back to percussion before the hook returns
- **Rhythm**: BPM typically ranges from 95-115, syncopated grooves that split the difference between Afrobeats swing and dancehall bounce, shuffled hi-hats with triplet feels, bass patterns that are rhythmically active rather than sustained

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Verse-chorus with melodic hooks. Sung refrains carry the track; verses alternate between melodic delivery and conversational rap bars. Rhyme supports the groove rather than dominating it.
- **Rhyme quality**: Smooth and accessible over technically dense. Internal rhyme and near-rhyme preferred; lyrics should feel effortless, not labored. Repetition of key phrases builds memorability.
- **Verse structure**: 4-8 line verses with strong pre-chorus builds into melodic hooks. Code-switching between English, patois, and Pidgin within verses adds texture and authenticity.
- **Key rule**: Lyrics reflect dual cultural identity -- London street life layered with African and Caribbean references. Flirtatious, romantic, and aspirational themes dominate. Slang should be British, not American. Track and place-name references ground songs in London geography.
- **Avoid**: Overly aggressive delivery that fights the melodic groove. American slang or idioms where British terms exist. Dense multisyllabic rap schemes that sacrifice singability. Ignoring the dancehall and Afrobeats vocal traditions that define the style.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6 lines/verse** at 95-115 BPM. Melodic, laid-back delivery with room to breathe. Hooks should be short, catchy, and repeatable. Topics: 1-2/verse. Love, nightlife, loyalty, and aspiration dominate.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | BPM Range | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Core Afroswing** | The foundational sound: Afrobeats rhythms fused with dancehall bounce and UK rap delivery, warm production with melodic hooks and flirtatious lyrics. Represents the commercial peak of the genre (2016-2019). | 95-110 | J Hus, Kojo Funds, Not3s, NSG |
| **Afrobashment** | Heavier dancehall and bashment influence with more prominent Caribbean riddim patterns, ragga-influenced vocal delivery, and party-oriented energy. Sometimes used interchangeably with Afroswing but leans harder into Jamaican sounds. | 100-115 | WSTRN, Yxng Bane, Hardy Caprio |
| **Afroswing R&B** | Smoother, more melodic variant emphasizing R&B vocal techniques and romantic themes over Afrobeats-influenced production. Less rap, more singing; bedroom and slow-wine energy. | 90-105 | Yxng Bane, Kojo Funds, Darkoo |
| **Afroswing Rap** | Harder-edged variant where UK rap bars take priority over sung melodies, but the instrumental retains Afrobeats and dancehall rhythmic DNA. Street narratives and boastful lyricism over warm, percussive beats. | 95-110 | MoStack, J Hus, Abra Cadabra |
| **Afrowave** | More atmospheric and synth-heavy variant with spacious production, reverb-laden vocals, and a slightly darker or moodier tone. Less party-oriented, more introspective. | 95-108 | Yxng Bane, EO |
| **UK Funky Revival** | Draws from the pre-Afroswing UK funky house tradition (see `genres/uk-garage/`) with four-on-the-floor kicks and African percussion, bridging the gap between club music and Afroswing's pop sensibility. Donae'o is the key ancestor figure. | 120-130 | Donae'o, Various crossover acts |
| **Grime-Afroswing Crossover** | Tracks where grime's 140 BPM aggression and rapid-fire MCing collide with Afroswing's melodic warmth and Afrobeats percussion. Typically features grime artists adopting Afroswing elements rather than vice versa (see `genres/grime/`). | 130-140 | Abra Cadabra, Kojo Funds ft. grime MCs |
| **Afroswing Pop** | Most commercially accessible variant, polished for mainstream radio with pop song structures, major-key melodies, and universal lyrical themes. Production retains African rhythmic markers but prioritizes crossover appeal. | 100-115 | Ramz, EO, Afro B |

## Reference Tracks

- **J Hus - "Did You See"** -- The Afroswing anthem. Jae5's production layers warm synths and rolling percussion under J Hus's half-sung, half-rapped delivery. The hook is deceptively simple, built for crowd participation, while the verses demonstrate J Hus's ability to switch between street narrative and melodic crooning. Reached the UK Top 10 and became the calling card for the entire genre.

- **Kojo Funds ft. Abra Cadabra - "Dun Talkin'"** -- The track that named the genre. Kojo Funds's smooth, dancehall-inflected delivery plays against Abra Cadabra's harder grime-adjacent bars, demonstrating how Afroswing bridges Caribbean and African sounds with UK rap edge. Won Best Song at the 2017 MOBO Awards.

- **Not3s - "Addison Lee"** -- Named after London's private hire taxi firm, the track captures Afroswing's everyday London storytelling. Minimal production lets the infectious hook carry everything, and the track became a viral sensation that soundtracked nights out across the UK. Shows that Afroswing does not need maximalist production to land.

- **NSG ft. Tion Wayne - "Options"** -- The six-member collective's breakthrough, produced by Jae5. Trading verses between members creates energy and variety, while the hook locks in instantly. Peaked at UK number seven and proved Afroswing groups could compete with solo artists on the charts.

- **WSTRN - "In2"** -- An early milestone. Drake's co-sign on OVO Sound Radio propelled this three-piece group's debut to UK number four in 2015, before the genre even had a name. The track's dancehall-heavy production and smooth vocal harmonies laid groundwork for everything that followed. Best-selling debut by a new group that year.

- **Yxng Bane - "Rihanna"** -- The R&B-leaning side of Afroswing. Flirtatious lyrics ride a minimal, groove-first instrumental, with Yxng Bane's relaxed delivery selling effortless cool. Certified Platinum in the UK with over 600,000 units sold. Demonstrates the genre's capacity for slow-burning romantic records alongside party tracks.

- **J Hus - "Dem Boy Paigon"** -- Earlier and rawer than "Did You See," this 2015 track became a club staple in London before J Hus's mainstream breakthrough. Street-level energy with Afrobeats percussion and dancehall influence; the track that proved the Afroswing formula worked before anyone had named it.

- **Ramz - "Barking"** -- Afroswing's biggest pop crossover moment. Named after the East London borough, the romantic track peaked at UK number two in January 2018. Accessible enough for mainstream radio while retaining the genre's rhythmic identity. Proved Afroswing could compete at the very top of the charts.

- **Afro B - "Drogba (Joanna)"** -- Named after footballer Didier Drogba, this 2018 track sparked a global dance challenge and reached UK number five. Afro B's Ivorian-British perspective brought a West African flavor distinct from the genre's Nigerian and Ghanaian roots. Club-ready energy with a hook that crossed language barriers.

- **EO - "German"** -- A teenage breakthrough that reached UK number 13 in 2018. The track's catchy, uptempo production by Ak Marv and EO's youthful energy demonstrated Afroswing's appeal to the next generation. Signed to Columbia Records on the strength of this single alone.

- **MoStack - "No Words" ft. Dave** -- A collaboration that bridged Afroswing with UK rap's lyrical end. MoStack's conversational flow and Dave's sharper bars trade off over warm, bouncy production. Peaked at UK number 17 and showed how Afroswing could absorb UK rap's storytelling without losing its melodic core.

- **Yxng Bane - "Vroom"** -- Packed with 90s pop interpolations (from Ricky Martin to Beenie Man), this 2018 single became a dancefloor staple. The track's referential playfulness and Yxng Bane's charismatic delivery embody the genre's celebration-first ethos. Over 20 million YouTube views.

- **Kojo Funds - "Check"** -- A smoother, more melodic showcase for the man who named the genre. Dancehall-influenced rhythm with warm, sun-drenched production and Kojo Funds's signature patois-inflected delivery. Represents Afroswing at its most relaxed and self-assured.
