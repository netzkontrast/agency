# Future Bass

## Genre Overview

Future bass is a style of electronic dance music that emerged in the early 2010s, blending elements of dubstep, EDM trap, synth-pop, and R&B into a warmer, more melodic alternative to the aggressive bass music dominating the scene at the time. The genre's roots trace back to the "wonky" electronic experiments of Scottish producers Rustie and Hudson Mohawke on Glasgow's LuckyMe label, whose maximalist approach to synthesizer textures and off-kilter rhythms laid the groundwork. By 2012, a new wave of producers including Flume, Cashmere Cat, and ODESZA had crystallized the sound into something distinct: lush supersaw chords, pitched vocal chops, half-time drum patterns, and an emotional core that distinguished it from the harsher bass music of the era.

The genre's development was inseparable from the internet. SoundCloud served as its primary incubator, where young producers shared tracks and built audiences without traditional gatekeepers. Australia's Future Classic label became a key hub, signing Flume, Wave Racer, and Cashmere Cat. By 2015-2016, future bass had broken through to mainstream consciousness. San Holo's "Light" sat at number one on Spotify's US Viral 50 for twelve weeks in 2016, Flume's "Never Be Like You" earned a Grammy nomination, and Marshmello's helmet-wearing persona turned the genre into a pop-crossover juggernaut. The vocal-driven side of the genre flourished during this period, with producers increasingly collaborating with pop and R&B singers to create tracks that could work on both festival stages and mainstream radio.

Today, future bass has matured from a niche SoundCloud movement into a broad stylistic umbrella encompassing everything from the kawaii future bass of Japan's Snail's House to the orchestral emotionalism of Illenium and the indie-electronic textures of Mura Masa. Its production techniques, particularly supersaw chord stacks, sidechain-modulated pads, and pitched vocal processing, have become standard vocabulary in pop and electronic music production. While the genre's initial hype cycle has settled, its influence persists across melodic dubstep, hyperpop, and mainstream pop, and its emphasis on emotional resonance over pure dancefloor aggression continues to attract new producers and listeners.

## Characteristics

- **Tempo and Feel**: Typically 130-170 BPM with a half-time drum feel, where the snare lands on beat 3 creating an effective groove of 65-85 BPM; this tension between fast harmonic movement and slow, heavy percussion defines the genre's signature push-and-pull energy
- **Synthesis and Sound Design**: Supersaw chord stacks (multiple detuned sawtooth oscillators layered together) form the harmonic backbone; LFO-modulated filter cutoffs create rhythmic "wobbling" on pads and bass; extensive use of sidechain compression tied to kick drums produces the characteristic pumping, breathing texture on synth chords
- **Harmonic Language**: Extended chord voicings (7ths, 9ths, 11ths, 13ths) rather than simple triads; jazz-influenced progressions with suspended and added-tone chords; major keys and bittersweet minor progressions both common, often shifting between the two within a single track
- **Vocal Treatment**: Pitched vocal chops (short syllables transposed across the keyboard as melodic elements), granular vocal processing, formant-shifted textures, and heavily reverbed or delayed vocal layers; full vocal performances common in mainstream-leaning tracks, often with R&B or indie-pop influenced phrasing
- **Drum Programming**: Half-time patterns with punchy, processed kicks on beats 1 and 3, snappy snares or claps on beat 3, and sparse hi-hat patterns with trap-influenced rolls and fills; drums frequently drenched in reverb for a spacious, larger-than-life feel; 808-style sub-bass kicks in trap-influenced variants
- **Structure and Dynamics**: Build-drop architecture inherited from dubstep, but drops emphasize melodic chord progressions and textural density rather than aggressive bass wobbles; breakdowns often strip to piano, guitar, or bare vocals before reintroducing the full supersaw wall; transitions use white noise risers, reverse crashes, and filter sweeps

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Loose or absent. Emotional resonance and vocal melody take priority over formal rhyme structures.
- **Rhyme quality**: Simple, vowel-heavy rhymes preferred for singability. Near-rhymes and assonance work well given the heavy vocal processing.
- **Verse structure**: Short verses (4-8 lines) leading into pre-drop vocal hooks. Choruses are often instrumental drops with chopped vocal fragments rather than sung sections. Repeated one-line hooks ("I'll never be like you," "I got the light") anchor the track.
- **Key rule**: Lyrics must survive pitch-shifting, chopping, and heavy reverb. Short, open-vowel phrases that sound clear when processed. Emotional directness over cleverness.
- **Avoid**: Dense wordplay, complex narratives, rapid-fire syllable packing. Anything that becomes unintelligible when time-stretched or formant-shifted. Cynicism or irony that clashes with the genre's earnest emotional tone.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 140-160 BPM (half-time 70-80). Vocal hooks need space around them for the instrumental drop to breathe. Keep it sparse and emotionally direct. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | BPM Range | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Melodic/Vocal Future Bass** | 140-160 | Vocal-driven tracks blending pop songwriting with supersaw drops; mainstream crossover territory with full sung performances and anthemic hooks | Marshmello, Illenium, Louis The Child |
| **Experimental Future Bass** | 130-170 | Boundary-pushing production emphasizing unconventional sound design, glitchy textures, and nonlinear arrangements over conventional drops | Flume, Rustie, Mr. Carmack |
| **Kawaii Future Bass** | 150-175 | Japanese-influenced variant incorporating chiptune sounds, anime/video game samples, soft square waves, and bright, sugary timbres with a playful, cute aesthetic | Snail's House, YUC'e, Yunomi |
| **Organic Future Bass** | 130-150 | Blends live instrumentation (guitar, brass, strings, woodwinds) with electronic production; emphasis on acoustic-electronic fusion and cinematic textures | ODESZA, Mura Masa, Petit Biscuit |
| **Future Bass Trap** | 130-160 | Heavier, more aggressive variant leaning into trap drum patterns, 808 bass, and hip-hop influenced rhythms while retaining supersaw harmonic elements | Wave Racer, Lido, Graves |
| **Chill Future Bass** | 120-145 | Downtempo-leaning, atmospheric variant with gentler drops, ambient textures, and a relaxed energy suited for playlists rather than festival stages | Said The Sky, Jai Wolf, Kasbo |
| **Post-Future Bass** | 130-160 | Guitar-driven, indie-rock inflected evolution moving away from supersaw drops toward organic textures and singer-songwriter structures; San Holo coined the term "post-EDM" for this direction | San Holo, Dabin, Felly |
| **Jersey Club Future Bass** | 130-145 | Incorporates the rapid-fire kick patterns, bed-squeak samples, and syncopated rhythms of Jersey club into a future bass framework | Cashmere Cat, Naderi, Sango |

## Reference Tracks

- **Flume - "Never Be Like You" (feat. Kai)** (2016) -- Grammy-nominated track from *Skin* that defined mainstream future bass; Kai's R&B vocals float over Flume's textured production, with pitched vocal chops, glitchy percussion, and a drop built on lush chords rather than aggression; proved the genre could be emotionally sophisticated and commercially successful simultaneously

- **San Holo - "Light"** (2016) -- Instrumental future bass anthem that topped Spotify's US Viral 50 for twelve weeks; a single soaring supersaw melody carries the entire track over half-time drums, demonstrating the genre's ability to create massive emotional impact without any vocals; became a gateway track for an entire generation of future bass listeners

- **ODESZA - "Say My Name" (feat. Zyra)** (2014) -- From *In Return*, this track showcases ODESZA's signature blend of organic samples (marching drums, brass stabs) with electronic production; Zyra's vocals build over a restrained verse before the drop unleashes layered supersaws and chopped vocal textures; established the duo's cinematic approach to future bass

- **Cashmere Cat - "Mirror Maru"** (2012) -- One of the earliest definitive future bass tracks; Jersey club-influenced rhythms meet pitched-up R&B vocal samples and delicate bell-like synths; the Norwegian producer's debut single demonstrated how future bass could be both experimental and graceful, influencing the entire genre's aesthetic direction

- **Marshmello - "Alone"** (2016) -- Festival-ready future bass with a simple, memorable melody that builds to a massive supersaw drop; stripped the genre down to its most accessible elements without losing its emotional core; the track's viral success (over two billion YouTube views) cemented future bass as a mainstream electronic genre

- **Illenium - "Crawl Outta Love" (feat. Annika Wells)** (2017) -- Emotional melodic future bass from *Awake* blending piano, strings, and orchestral elements with heavy drops; the verse-to-drop dynamic range is extreme, moving from intimate acoustic textures to wall-of-sound supersaws; helped establish the "melodic bass" movement that continues to fill festival main stages

- **Louis The Child - "It's Strange" (feat. K.Flay)** (2015) -- Laid-back future bass with a hip-hop-influenced groove, warm synth pads, and K.Flay's conversational vocal delivery; the drop is subtle compared to heavier variants, emphasizing groove and vibe over pure impact; exemplifies the chiller, more accessible end of the genre spectrum

- **Rustie - "Big Catzz"** (2011) -- From the landmark *Glass Swords* album, this track is proto-future bass at its most exhilarating; maximalist synth stacks, relentless energy, and a sense of chromatic overload that would directly influence the genre's aesthetic; the Glasgow producer's work proved that electronic music could be both abrasive and euphoric

- **ODESZA - "A Moment Apart"** (2017) -- Title track from their Grammy-nominated third album; builds from a sparse, ambient intro to a cinematic climax with layered brass, strings, and vocal samples; demonstrates how future bass production can serve an orchestral, almost film-score sensibility while maintaining dancefloor momentum

- **Flume - "Say It" (feat. Tove Lo)** (2016) -- Tove Lo's pop vocals over Flume's characteristic fractured production; the drop chops and rearranges the vocal into a rhythmic instrument, demonstrating the genre's defining technique of treating vocals as textural elements rather than traditional song anchors

- **Wave Racer - "Rock U Tonite"** (2013) -- Hyper-colorful, video-game-influenced future bass with rapid arpeggios, pitched-up vocal samples, and a relentlessly upbeat energy; one of the earliest Future Classic releases to define the label's buoyant, neon-bright approach to the genre

- **Mura Masa - "Lovesick" (feat. A$AP Rocky)** (2017) -- Future bass meets hip-hop with steel drum melodies, minimalist percussion, and A$AP Rocky's laid-back flow; Mura Masa strips the genre back to its barest elements, proving that future bass production does not require massive drops to be effective

- **San Holo - "We Rise"** (2015) -- Early San Holo track that helped establish his signature sound: a single emotional melody built from a processed guitar tone layered over half-time drums and warm sub-bass; the track's simplicity and sincerity became a template for the "feels" side of future bass
