# Nu Jazz

## Genre Overview

Nu jazz (also called electro jazz or future jazz) emerged in the late 1990s as electronic music producers and jazz musicians found common ground between programmed beats and live improvisation. The genre grew from multiple scenes simultaneously: in Germany, producers like Jazzanova and Compost Records artists blended jazz samples with house and downtempo beats; in Norway, trumpeter Nils Petter Molvaer fused jazz with ambient electronica and trip-hop on his 1997 album *Khmer*, while the collective Jaga Jazzist combined a large acoustic ensemble with electronic processing; in the UK, The Cinematic Orchestra built mood-based orchestral jazz with electronic production; and in France, Ludovic Navarre recorded as St Germain, layering traditional jazz and blues licks over hypnotic house grooves.

Unlike acid jazz, which placed live bands in dialogue with DJ culture, nu jazz more thoroughly integrated electronic production into the compositional fabric. Bugge Wesseltoft, another Norwegian pioneer, explicitly coined the term "New Conception of Jazz" for his project, signaling a break from acoustic purism. The genre occupied a space between club culture and concert hall, with some acts leaning toward the dancefloor (St Germain, Jazzanova) and others toward atmospheric listening (Cinematic Orchestra, Nils Petter Molvaer). Labels like Compost Records, Ninja Tune, and Blue Note's revival imprints helped define and distribute the sound. The genre peaked commercially in the early 2000s, with St Germain's *Tourist* (2000) selling over three million copies worldwide.

Nu jazz's influence has diffused into the broader landscape of genre-fluid contemporary music. Artists like GoGo Penguin, Portico Quartet, and Mammal Hands blend acoustic jazz instrumentation with electronic aesthetics without necessarily using the nu jazz label. The London jazz scene of the 2010s and 2020s, including Shabaka Hutchings, Nubya Garcia, and Ezra Collective, absorbed nu jazz's lesson that jazz and electronic music are natural allies. The genre demonstrated that jazz could engage with contemporary production techniques without losing its improvisational soul, opening a path that many current artists walk without looking back.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Acoustic jazz instruments (saxophone, trumpet, piano, double bass) combined with electronic production (drum machines, samplers, synthesizers, laptops); turntables and DJ equipment; vibraphone and Rhodes electric piano bridge the acoustic and electronic worlds; live drums often layered with programmed beats; guitar effects processing

- **Vocals**: Both instrumental and vocal; when present, vocals range from sampled jazz vocal snippets to original singing with electronic processing; spoken word; cool, understated delivery; some acts feature prominent vocalists (Cinematic Orchestra with Fontella Bass); voice used as textural element as much as melodic carrier

- **Production**: Sophisticated electronic production blended with live instrumentation; downtempo and broken-beat rhythms; sidechain compression, filtering, and spatial effects; warm analog synthesizers alongside acoustic instruments; careful layering and arrangement; club-ready mixes with sub-bass and clear high frequencies; the studio itself is an instrument

- **Energy/Mood**: Sophisticated, urban, late-night; ranges from downtempo chill to dancefloor-ready grooves; atmospheric and immersive; contemplative but never boring; cinematic and evocative; a sense of nocturnal metropolitan life; intellectual without being inaccessible; cool and composed

- **Structure**: Song-form and DJ-friendly structures (long intros, buildups, breakdowns); verse-chorus elements borrowed from electronic music; extended passages for improvisation over loops and vamps; gradual layering of elements; 4-8 minutes typical; album-oriented rather than singles-focused

- **Tempo**: 80-130 BPM predominantly; downtempo at 80-100 BPM; broken beat at 120-140 BPM; four-on-the-floor house-influenced grooves at 115-125 BPM; drum and bass-influenced tracks at 160-175 BPM; tempo diversity within albums

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Free-form or no fixed scheme; when lyrics exist, they follow electronic music conventions — short phrases, repeated hooks, atmospheric fragments
- **Rhyme quality**: Atmospheric over structural; assonance and internal echo valued; lyrics serve the groove and mood rather than telling narrative stories
- **Verse structure**: Flexible; 2-6 lines per section; lyrics often appear only in parts of otherwise instrumental tracks; hook-based rather than verse-chorus
- **Key rule**: Nu jazz straddles instrumental and vocal traditions. When lyrics appear, they must blend into the production — never fight the electronic textures or acoustic improvisation. Cool, understated delivery.
- **Avoid**: Overwrought emotional delivery; dense storytelling that distracts from the groove; lyrics that ignore the electronic production context; conventional pop song structures forced onto nu jazz arrangements
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 90-130 BPM. Understated vocal delivery over electronic-acoustic hybrid production. Often instrumental. Topics: 1/track. Urban life, nocturnal moods, movement, abstraction.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Club Nu Jazz** | Dancefloor-oriented fusion of jazz instrumentation with house, broken beat, and downtempo production; DJ-friendly structures with extended mixes | St Germain, Jazzanova, 4hero |
| **Future Jazz** | Norwegian-pioneered style emphasizing ambient textures, processed trumpet or saxophone, and electronica production; atmospheric and spacious | Nils Petter Molvaer, Bugge Wesseltoft, Eivind Aarset |
| **Cinematic Nu Jazz** | Orchestral, mood-driven compositions with electronic beats; film-score-like atmosphere; strings and acoustic instruments over programmed rhythms | The Cinematic Orchestra, Amon Tobin, Bonobo |
| **Nu Jazz Big Band** | Large acoustic ensembles incorporating electronic elements; post-rock dynamics applied to jazz orchestra; genre-fluid compositions | Jaga Jazzist, Snarky Puppy |
| **Broken Beat Jazz** | London-originating style combining jazz harmony with broken beat rhythms (syncopated, off-kilter patterns); dancefloor-oriented but rhythmically complex | 4hero, Kaidi Tatham, IG Culture |
| **Laptop Jazz** | Solo or duo artists combining live jazz instruments with real-time laptop processing; improvisation mediated through software; the musician-programmer hybrid | Bugge Wesseltoft, Nils Petter Molvaer, Squarepusher |

## Reference Tracks

- **St Germain - "Rose Rouge"** (from *Tourist*, 2000) — A seven-minute deep house-jazz fusion built on a Marlena Shaw vocal sample and live jazz instrumentation over a four-on-the-floor beat. The track demonstrates nu jazz at its most dancefloor-ready: sophisticated harmonic content wrapped in an irresistible groove.

- **The Cinematic Orchestra - "All That You Give"** (from *Every Day*, 2002) — Fontella Bass's vocals float over strings, piano, and programmed beats in a piece that bridges jazz, classical, and electronic music. The Cinematic Orchestra's gift for emotional depth through atmospheric production is fully on display.

- **Nils Petter Molvaer - "Khmer"** (from *Khmer*, 1997) — Processed trumpet over ambient textures and trip-hop beats: Molvaer's debut album title track defined future jazz as a genre where acoustic instruments and electronics merge into something that belongs entirely to neither world.

- **Jaga Jazzist - "Animal Chin"** (from *A Livingroom Hush*, 2001) — A Norwegian collective of eight musicians navigating through post-rock dynamics, electronic beats, and jazz horn arrangements in a single composition, proving that nu jazz could be as complex and rewarding as any progressive music.

- **Jazzanova - "Fedime's Flight"** (from *In Between*, 2002) — Berlin production sophistication: jazz piano samples, live strings, and programmed rhythms merge into a warm, enveloping groove that captures the genre's Berlin nightlife origins.

- **Bugge Wesseltoft - "Change"** (from *New Conception of Jazz*, 1996) — Piano and electronics in real-time dialogue, with Wesseltoft improvising over his own programmed beats and textures. The track embodies the "new conception" that gave the movement its Norwegian name.

- **4hero - "Star Chasers"** (from *Two Pages*, 1998) — Broken beat meets lush string arrangements and jazz harmony, with a vocal by face that rides the syncopated rhythm. This track helped define the London broken beat sound that fed into nu jazz's rhythmic vocabulary.

- **GoGo Penguin - "Hopopono"** (from *v2.0*, 2014) — An acoustic piano trio playing with the precision and energy of an electronic act: the drums hit like programmed beats, the piano loops like a synthesizer, but everything is played live. The next generation of nu jazz thinking.

- **Bonobo - "Kerala"** (from *Migration*, 2017) — Layered production combining jazz-influenced live instrumentation with electronic textures and world-music percussion, creating an immersive sonic environment that demonstrates nu jazz's evolution into broader ambient-electronic territory.

- **The Cinematic Orchestra - "To Build a Home"** (from *Ma Fleur*, 2007) — Patrick Watson's vocal over sparse piano and slowly building orchestral arrangement became one of the most widely licensed tracks in film and television, proving nu jazz's emotional power could reach far beyond the jazz audience.

- **Portico Quartet - "Knee-Deep in the North Sea"** (2008) — Hang drum, saxophone, and electronics create an otherworldly atmosphere that dissolves genre boundaries entirely. The track represents nu jazz's openness to unconventional timbres and its willingness to sound like nothing else.
