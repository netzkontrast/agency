# Complextro

## Genre Overview

Complextro emerged around 2010-2011 as a production-intensive variant of electro house that replaced the genre's typically sustained bass drops with rapid-fire sequences of different synthesizer sounds, glitchy transitions, and complex arrangements layered over a standard 4-on-the-floor kick pattern. The term — a portmanteau of "complex" and "electro" — was coined to describe a wave of producers who were pushing electro house's sound design to new extremes, treating each bar as an opportunity to cycle through multiple bass timbres, synth textures, and rhythmic patterns rather than riding a single idea.

The genre's golden era coincided with the broader EDM explosion of 2010-2014, and its key figures became some of electronic music's biggest names. Porter Robinson's early work, particularly "Spitfire" and "Unison," demonstrated how complex sound-switching could maintain dancefloor energy while dazzling listeners with production virtuosity. Madeon, who burst onto the scene at age 17 with "Pop Culture," brought a melodic sophistication that made complextro's technical complexity feel effortless. Feed Me (Jon Gooch) added a darker, more aggressive edge, while Savant's superhuman output pushed the boundaries of how many ideas could fit into a single track. Kill The Noise brought a heavier, more industrial-influenced variant.

Complextro's peak was relatively brief — by 2014, its key artists had largely moved on to other sounds (Porter Robinson to synthpop/dream pop, Madeon to French house-influenced pop, Feed Me to a broader electronic palette). The genre's technical demands — each track required dozens of unique sound patches and precise automation — made it unsustainable as a long-term creative approach for most producers. However, complextro's legacy lives on in modern bass music production, where its emphasis on rapid sound design and dynamic arrangements continues to influence dubstep, future bass, and experimental electronic music. The genre remains a benchmark for production craft in electronic music.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Multiple synthesizer patches per track (often 10-20+ unique bass/lead sounds), glitchy transition effects, 4-on-the-floor kicks, punchy snares and claps, layered percussion, sidechain-pumped pads, supersaw chords, square wave basses, FM synthesis leads, bitcrushed textures, video game-inspired sound effects; the variety of timbres within a single track is the genre's signature

- **Vocals**: Often absent or minimal; when present, typically pop-style vocals in intros and breakdowns that give way to instrumental drops; vocal chops used as rhythmic elements within drops; pitched and processed vocal stabs as part of the sound-switching palette; some tracks feature full vocal performances in a pop-EDM format

- **Production**: Rapid sound-switching (new synth sound every beat or half-beat in drops), precise automation of filters, pitch, and effects, complex arrangement over standard 4-on-the-floor rhythm, heavy use of sidechain compression, glitch effects and stutter edits, bright and punchy mastering, meticulous gain staging to maintain clarity despite dense layering; each drop is a showcase of sound design

- **Energy/Mood**: Energetic, playful, technically impressive; dancefloor-oriented but with a "wow factor" from production complexity; fun and colorful rather than dark; celebratory and maximalist; the musical equivalent of a fireworks display — constant surprises within a familiar framework

- **Structure**: Standard EDM arrangement (intro → buildup → drop → breakdown → buildup → drop → outro) but with drops that are internally complex; 8-bar or 16-bar drops packed with sound changes; breakdowns provide melodic or vocal contrast; buildups use risers and snare rolls; second drops typically introduce new sound palettes while maintaining energy

- **Tempo**: 128 BPM (standard electro house tempo, occasionally 126-130 BPM)

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB or ABAB — pop-standard when vocals are present; most complextro is instrumental or near-instrumental
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean, pop-friendly; lyrics serve the buildup-to-drop format and should feel anthemic or emotionally direct
- **Verse structure**: 4 lines per verse in buildup sections; drops are instrumental; vocals appear primarily in intros and breakdowns
- **Key rule**: Vocals set up the drop — they provide the emotional context that makes the instrumental payoff land. Write for buildup sections, not drops. Keep it simple and anthemic.
- **Avoid**: Vocals competing with drops; complex lyrical passages during sound-switching sections; dark or brooding themes that fight the genre's playful energy
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 128 BPM for vocal buildups only. Drops should be instrumental with possible vocal chops. Buildup lyrics should create anticipation. Topics: 1/track. Anthemic, simple, emotionally direct.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Melodic Complextro** | Emphasizes chord progressions and melodic hooks alongside complex sound design; more accessible and emotionally resonant | Madeon, Porter Robinson (early) |
| **Aggressive Complextro** | Heavier, more bass-driven variant with industrial and dubstep influences; darker timbres and harder-hitting drops | Kill The Noise, Feed Me, Knife Party |
| **Chiptune Complextro** | Incorporates 8-bit and video game sound aesthetics into the sound-switching palette; playful and nostalgic | Savant, Nitro Fun, Lemaitre |
| **Vocal Complextro** | Features prominent pop vocals in buildups with complex instrumental drops; crossover with mainstream EDM | Madeon, Zedd (early), Porter Robinson |
| **Maximalist Complextro** | Pushes the concept to extremes with the highest density of sound changes per bar; technical showcase above all | Savant, Virtual Riot (some works) |

| Porter Robinson | Melodic Complextro | *Spitfire* EP, "Unison," "Language" | Defined complextro's melodic potential; *Spitfire* EP is a genre landmark; later evolved into synthpop/dream pop with *Worlds* |
| Madeon | Melodic Complextro | *Adventure*, "Pop Culture," "Icarus" | French prodigy who debuted at 17; effortless melodic complexity; "Pop Culture" mashup went viral; brought pop songcraft to complextro |
| Feed Me | Aggressive Complextro | *Feed Me's Big Adventure*, *Calamari Tuesday* | Jon Gooch's darker, heavier variant; cartoon monster branding; aggressive sound design with melodic sensibility; Sotto Voce label |

| Knife Party | Aggressive Complextro | *100% No Modern Day Junk*, "Internet Friends" | Rob Swire and Gareth McGrillen (ex-Pendulum); aggressive electro with meme-culture humor; genre-defining early releases |

## Reference Tracks

- **Porter Robinson - "Spitfire"** — The complextro thesis statement: every bar introduces a new bass sound while maintaining relentless dancefloor energy; the rapid-fire sound-switching became a production benchmark; defined what the genre could achieve at its most intense

- **Madeon - "Pop Culture"** — While technically a mashup of 39 songs, this track demonstrated the sound-switching aesthetic that defined complextro and launched Madeon's career at age 17; proves complex arrangement can feel effortless and joyful

- **Madeon - "Icarus"** — Melodic complextro perfected: anthemic chord progressions give way to drops packed with synth variety; accessible without sacrificing complexity; one of the genre's most emotionally resonant tracks

- **Feed Me - "One Click Headshot"** — Aggressive complextro at its peak: dark, heavy sound design cycling through menacing bass patches over a relentless 4-on-the-floor groove; demonstrates the genre's heavier side without losing its playful spirit

- **Kill The Noise - "Kill Kill Kill"** — Industrial-tinged complextro: harder and darker than the melodic variant; aggressive sound design and distorted textures pushed through the rapid-switching format; bridged complextro and bass music

- **Savant - "Splinter"** — Maximalist complextro: more ideas per minute than almost any other electronic track; Savant's virtuosic sound design on full display; video game energy channeled into dancefloor chaos

- **Knife Party - "Internet Friends"** — Complextro meets meme culture: the "you blocked me on Facebook" vocal hook builds into a devastatingly complex drop; humor and aggression in equal measure; defined a moment in EDM culture
