# Hindustani

## Genre Overview

Hindustani classical music is the classical music tradition of North India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Nepal, tracing its theoretical roots to the Vedic period and the Natya Shastra (circa 200 BCE-200 CE). The tradition diverged from its South Indian counterpart (Carnatic music) around the 12th-13th centuries as Persian and Central Asian musical influences entered North India through the Delhi Sultanate and later the Mughal Empire. Amir Khusrau (1253-1325), poet and musician at the court of the Delhi Sultanate, is credited with introducing the qawwali form and systematizing aspects of Hindustani music, including the development of new ragas and the tarana vocal style. The legendary Tansen (1500-1586), court musician to Mughal Emperor Akbar and one of the "Navratna" (nine gems) of his court, composed influential ragas including Miyan ki Todi, Miyan ki Malhar, and Darbari Kanada that remain central to the repertoire.

The gharana system — schools of musical lineage transmitted from master to student (guru-shishya parampara) — crystallized in the 18th and 19th centuries and remains the primary organizational structure of Hindustani music. Major vocal gharanas include Gwalior (the oldest, emphasizing clear, open-throated singing), Kirana (emphasizing slow, ornamental raga development), Jaipur-Atrauli (known for rare ragas and rhythmic precision), and Agra (emphasizing powerful voice production and rhythmic complexity). The introduction of recorded sound in the early 20th century transformed the tradition: Abdul Karim Khan (1872-1937) of the Kirana gharana and Faiyaz Khan (1886-1950) of the Agra gharana became the first widely recorded vocalists. The All India Music Conference of 1916 and Vishnu Narayan Bhatkhande's (1860-1936) systematic classification of ragas into ten parent scales (thaat) brought theoretical standardization. Ravi Shankar's concerts at the Monterey Pop Festival (1967) and his collaboration with George Harrison introduced Hindustani music to Western counterculture audiences.

Contemporary Hindustani music operates across traditional concert performance, festival circuits, recording, and cross-genre collaboration. Zakir Hussain (born 1951) became the most internationally recognized tabla player through decades of cross-cultural work with artists from Mickey Hart to Charles Lloyd. The Saptak Festival in Ahmedabad (founded 1980) and Dover Lane Music Conference in Kolkata (founded 1952) remain major annual gatherings. Younger artists like Kaushiki Chakraborty and Niladri Kumar maintain gharana traditions while engaging global streaming audiences. Hindustani rhythmic and melodic concepts have influenced Western minimalism (Terry Riley, La Monte Young), jazz (John Coltrane, Alice Coltrane), and electronic music, while the tradition's emphasis on real-time improvisation within structured frameworks continues to attract musicians from outside the classical Indian world.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Sitar (long-necked plucked lute with movable frets, sympathetic strings, and a gourd resonator — the most internationally recognized Hindustani instrument), sarod (fretless plucked lute with a metal fingerboard allowing continuous glissando), tabla (pair of tunable hand drums — the treble dayan and bass bayan — providing rhythmic accompaniment and solo percussion), tanpura/tambura (four-string drone instrument sustaining the tonal center), sarangi (bowed short-necked lute with gut strings and extensive sympathetic strings, used for vocal accompaniment), shehnai (double-reed aerophone used in ceremonial and concert contexts), bansuri (bamboo transverse flute), santoor (hammered dulcimer from Kashmir), harmonium (bellows-driven reed organ, controversial for its fixed pitch but ubiquitous in vocal accompaniment)
- **Vocals**: Khayal (the dominant vocal form — slow and elaborate raga exposition in vilambit followed by faster drut composition); dhrupad (the oldest vocal form — austere, powerful, focused on raga purity without ornamentation for its own sake); thumri (light classical form emphasizing emotional expression and text painting); tarana (fast-tempo vocal style using meaningless syllables similar to scat singing); extensive use of meend (slow pitch glides), gamak (rapid oscillations), murki (quick ornamental turns), and taan (rapid melodic passages); vocal delivery ranges from meditative whisper to full-throated power depending on raga and emotional context
- **Production**: Predominantly acoustic and live-recorded; traditional concert recordings capture the natural room acoustic with minimal processing; tanpura drone is constant throughout; studio recordings may add subtle reverb; fusion projects incorporate multi-tracking, electronic processing, and Western studio techniques; the live concert recording remains the gold standard for classical releases
- **Energy/Mood**: Governed by raga theory — each raga is associated with a specific time of day (prahar), season, and emotional mood (rasa); performances build organically from meditative stillness (alap) through increasing rhythmic energy (jor, jhala) to composed sections with tabla accompaniment; the emotional arc of a single raga performance can span contemplation, tension, ecstasy, and resolution over 30-90 minutes; lighter forms (thumri, ghazal) occupy a more intimate, romantic emotional space
- **Structure**: Classical performance follows a specific architectural sequence — alap (unmetered raga exposition without rhythm, exploring each note and phrase), jor (introduction of pulse without tabla), jhala (fast rhythmic climax with drone strings), and gat/bandish (composed section with tabla in a specific tala cycle); within the composed section, performers improvise through taan, toda, and jhala passages; the performance concludes with a tihai (rhythmic cadence repeated three times to land on sam, the first beat)
- **Tempo**: Three main tempo ranges: vilambit (slow, ~30-60 BPM — the primary space for elaborate raga exploration), madhya laya (medium, ~80-120 BPM), and drut (fast, ~160-300+ BPM); tala cycles range from 6 beats (dadra) to 16 beats (teentaal, the most common) to complex cycles of 10 (jhaptaal), 12 (ektaal), and 14 (dhamar); layakari (rhythmic variation) involves playing in double, triple, or quadruple subdivisions of the beat

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Bandish (compositions) in khayal use couplet-based structures where the mukhra (opening phrase returning to sam) serves as the hook; rhyme is secondary to melodic shape — the text exists to carry the raga; antara (second section) typically reaches the upper octave; no strict Western-style rhyme scheme, but end-syllable matching is common
- **Rhyme quality**: Lyric content ranges from devotional praise and romantic longing to nature imagery tied to the raga's time and season associations; text in Braj Bhasha, Hindi, Urdu, or Persian; quality measured by how naturally the syllables fit the melodic contour rather than by rhyme density; dhrupad texts are Sanskrit or Hindi with austere, compressed imagery
- **Verse structure**: Bandish: sthayi (first section, 2-4 lines, establishes raga and tala) and antara (second section, 2-4 lines, explores upper register); some compositions add sanchari (third section) and abhog (concluding section); the mukhra (opening phrase of sthayi) functions as the returning hook throughout improvisation
- **Key rule**: The text serves the raga, never the reverse — syllables must align with the melodic phrase in a way that allows for improvised elaboration; the mukhra must land naturally on sam (the first beat of the tala cycle) to function as the compositional anchor during extended improvisation
- **Avoid**: Dense, wordy lyrics that leave no room for melodic expansion — Hindustani classical singing stretches single syllables across many beats; imposing Western prosodic stress patterns; treating the lyrics as independent poetry when they are inseparable from their melodic setting; ignoring the raga's emotional register when writing text
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/section** at highly variable tempos. Vilambit (slow) khayal: **2 lines** stretched over 60+ seconds with extensive ornamentation. Drut (fast): **4 lines** at 160+ BPM. Thumri: **4-6 lines** at 80-100 BPM with emotional text painting. Leave maximum space for melodic elaboration — Hindustani music breathes through its silences and sustained notes.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Dhrupad** | The oldest surviving vocal form, dating to the 15th century; austere, powerful singing emphasizing raga purity and voice production; four-part structure (sthayi, antara, sanchari, abhog); accompanied by pakhawaj drum rather than tabla; associated with temple devotion and Mughal court tradition | Dagar Brothers, Gundecha Brothers, Uday Bhawalkar |
| **Khayal** | The dominant vocal form since the 18th century; two compositions (vilambit/slow and drut/fast) in a raga provide frameworks for extensive improvisation through taan, bol-taan, and sargam; more ornamental and flexible than dhrupad; the form most audiences associate with Hindustani vocal music | Bade Ghulam Ali Khan, Bhimsen Joshi, Kishori Amonkar, Rashid Khan |
| **Thumri** | Light classical vocal form emphasizing emotional expression (bhava) and text painting over structural rigor; romantic and devotional themes in Braj Bhasha; allows greater melodic freedom including mixing of ragas; intimate, conversational delivery | Begum Akhtar, Girija Devi, Siddheshwari Devi |
| **Sitar** | The long-necked plucked lute as solo instrument; the sitar's sympathetic strings and bent-wire frets allow for gamakas and meend; alap-jor-jhala-gat structure; became the most internationally visible Hindustani instrument through Ravi Shankar's global career | Ravi Shankar, Vilayat Khan, Nikhil Banerjee |
| **Sarod** | Fretless plucked lute with a polished metal fingerboard enabling continuous glissando; deeper, more resonant tone than sitar; the absence of frets allows for vocal-like meend (pitch glides); associated with the Maihar gharana tradition | Ali Akbar Khan, Amjad Ali Khan, Ustad Aashish Khan |
| **Tabla Solo** | The tabla pair as a solo concert instrument, showcasing complex compositions (kaida, rela, tukra, chakradar) and rhythmic improvisation; performers demonstrate mastery of layakari and cross-rhythmic patterns; the tabla's tonal range allows for melodic as well as rhythmic expression | Alla Rakha, Zakir Hussain, Kishan Maharaj, Swapan Chaudhuri |
| **Bansuri (Flute)** | Bamboo transverse flute performance requiring circular breathing; the flute's capacity for continuous pitch bending makes it ideal for Hindustani meend; breathy, meditative tone in slow passages; associated with Lord Krishna imagery | Pannalal Ghosh, Hariprasad Chaurasia, Rakesh Chaurasia |
| **Santoor** | Hammered dulcimer from Kashmir adapted for Hindustani classical music by Shivkumar Sharma; the instrument's sustain and harmonic richness give it a distinctive shimmer; rapid passages achieved through double-striking technique | Shivkumar Sharma, Rahul Sharma |
| **Jugalbandi** | Duet format pairing two soloists (typically different instruments or vocal-instrumental) in collaborative and competitive improvisation; performers trade phrases, mirror each other, and engage in rhythmic interplay; highlights individual virtuosity within a dialogue framework | Ravi Shankar & Ali Akbar Khan, Zakir Hussain & various partners |
| **Indo-Jazz/Fusion** | Cross-genre experiments blending Hindustani raga and tala systems with jazz improvisation, rock instrumentation, or electronic production; preservation of raga identity within non-classical contexts; emerged through Ravi Shankar's Western collaborations in the 1960s | Shakti, Remember Shakti, L. Shankar, Trilok Gurtu |

## Reference Tracks

- **Ravi Shankar - "Raga Jog" from *Three Ragas* (1956)** — One of the earliest internationally distributed Hindustani classical recordings; Shankar's sitar exposition of this evening raga demonstrated to Western audiences the depth and sophistication of the alap-jor-jhala-gat structure

- **Shivkumar Sharma, Hariprasad Chaurasia & Brij Bhushan Kabra - *Call of the Valley* (1968)** — Santoor, bansuri, and guitar interpreting Kashmiri folk themes through Hindustani classical treatment; one of the best-selling Indian classical albums of all time; proved that classical raga-based music could reach popular audiences

- **Ali Akbar Khan - "Evening Ragas" recorded live in San Francisco (1978)** — Sarod performance with Zakir Hussain on tabla captured live in the Bay Area; demonstrates the depth of the Maihar gharana tradition and the sarod's capacity for vocal-like expression through its fretless fingerboard

- **Ravi Shankar & Yehudi Menuhin - *West Meets East* (1967)** — Grammy-winning collaboration between sitar and Western violin; the recording that symbolized the 1960s cross-cultural encounter between Hindustani and Western classical music; historically significant as a bridge between musical worlds

- **Hariprasad Chaurasia - "Venu" (1974)** — Chaurasia on bansuri with Zakir Hussain on tabla; the recording that established the bamboo flute as a serious Hindustani solo instrument capable of sustaining full-length raga performances

- **Bhimsen Joshi - Raga Miyan ki Todi (concert recordings)** — Joshi's powerful voice in this Tansen-created morning raga exemplifies the Kirana gharana approach: slow, deliberate exploration building to explosive taan passages; the emotional weight of his singing set the benchmark for devotional khayal

- **Zakir Hussain - "Making Music" (1987)** — Hussain with Jan Garbarek, John McLaughlin, and Hariprasad Chaurasia; tabla as a melodic and conversational instrument rather than mere accompaniment; demonstrated how Hindustani rhythmic thinking could enter improvised dialogue with jazz and world music frameworks

- **Kishori Amonkar - Raga Bhimpalasi (concert recordings)** — Amonkar's meditative, deeply personal approach to this afternoon raga; her extended alapanas could stretch a single raga for over an hour, revealing emotional dimensions through patience and restraint rather than technical display

- **Alla Rakha & Zakir Hussain - Jugalbandi Concert Recordings** — Father and son on tabla in duet format; the cross-generational dialogue showcasing the tabla's full range from whispered bass to explosive treble; defining performances of the tabla jugalbandi tradition

- **Rashid Khan - Raga Darbari Kanada (concert recordings)** — Khan's luminous voice in one of Hindustani music's most majestic night ragas; his effortless taan technique and natural vocal beauty represented the continuation of the classical vocal tradition into the contemporary era

- **Shakti - "Mind Ecology" from *Natural Elements* (1977)** — John McLaughlin's acoustic guitar, L. Shankar's violin, Zakir Hussain's tabla, and T.H. Vinayakram's ghatam in high-speed rhythmic dialogue; the recording that proved Hindustani rhythmic complexity could fuse with jazz improvisation without compromising either tradition
