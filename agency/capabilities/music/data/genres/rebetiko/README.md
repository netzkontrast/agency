# Rebetiko

## Genre Overview

Rebetiko is a Greek urban folk music tradition that emerged in the early 20th century from the hashish dens, port tavernas, and refugee quarters of cities like Piraeus, Thessaloniki, and Athens. Often called the "Greek Blues," rebetiko was the music of society's margins: refugees, prisoners, laborers, and the urban poor. Its earliest documented forms appeared in the late 19th century in the cafe aman salons of Smyrna (Izmir) and Istanbul, where Greek, Turkish, Armenian, and Jewish musicians played together. The catastrophic population exchange following the Greco-Turkish War of 1919-1922 drove over a million Greek-speaking refugees from Asia Minor into mainland Greece, and with them came the musical traditions that would crystallize into rebetiko. Pioneering figures like Roza Eskenazi, a Sephardic Jewish singer who recorded prolifically from 1929 onward, and Markos Vamvakaris, the "patriarch of rebetiko" who revolutionized the genre with his bouzouki-centered Piraeus ensemble in the early 1930s, established the genre's core identity.

Rebetiko evolved through distinct phases. The Smyrna style (Smyrneika) of the 1920s featured Ottoman-influenced instrumentation including oud, santouri, and violin, with elaborate melodic ornamentation rooted in the makam modal system. By the mid-1930s, the Piraeus style (Peiraiotika) had emerged as the dominant sound, with Vamvakaris's "kompania" lineup of two bouzoukis, baglamas, and guitar replacing the Anatolian instruments. The genre was banned and suppressed by the Metaxas dictatorship (1936-1941) for its associations with hashish use and criminality. After World War II and the German occupation, during which Vasilis Tsitsanis composed the unofficial national anthem "Synnefiasmeni Kyriaki" ("Cloudy Sunday," c. 1943), rebetiko entered its golden age. Tsitsanis, who composed over 500 songs in his lifetime, transformed the genre from underground subculture to mainstream Greek music, bridging rebetiko into the laiko tradition that would follow.

Today, rebetiko is recognized as a foundational pillar of modern Greek music and was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2017. A revival movement beginning in the 1960s and 1970s, driven by younger musicians and intellectuals, rescued the genre from near-obscurity and reframed it as a vital cultural heritage. Contemporary artists perform rebetiko in dedicated venues (rebetadika) across Greece, and the genre's influence runs through laiko, entechno, and modern Greek rock. Its raw emotional honesty, modal melodic language, and improvisational spirit continue to inspire musicians far beyond Greece.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Bouzouki (the defining instrument, both trichordo/3-course and tetrachordo/4-course), baglamas (small bouzouki used as rhythm instrument), guitar (nylon-string), and in the Smyrna style: oud, santouri (hammered dulcimer), kanonaki (zither), violin; occasional accordion and piano in later recordings
- **Vocals**: Raw, emotionally direct singing with modal ornamentation (melisma); raspy, lived-in vocal timbres valued over polish; nasal quality common in early recordings; call-and-response between vocalist and bouzouki; improvisational vocal passages (amanes) in Smyrna style
- **Production**: Historically recorded with minimal processing in small studios; modern rebetiko retains an acoustic, intimate sound; reverb used sparingly; emphasis on natural room sound and instrument bleed; mono or narrow stereo image in classic recordings
- **Energy/Mood**: Melancholic, defiant, resigned, passionate; themes of exile, unrequited love, prison, hashish, poverty, death, and urban survival; can shift from deep sorrow to fierce celebratory energy within a single performance; the zeimbekiko dance rhythm embodies solitary, introspective intensity
- **Structure**: Verse-based with instrumental taximi (improvised modal preludes); songs typically 3-5 minutes; no standard chorus in early rebetiko (verse-only forms common); later works adopt verse-chorus form; instrumental breaks feature bouzouki improvisation over drone
- **Tempo**: Zeimbekiko in 9/8 time at 60-80 BPM (slow, heavy, introspective); hasapiko in 2/4 at 80-100 BPM (moderate, processional); hasaposerviko in 2/4 at 120-140 BPM (fast, lively); tsifteteli at 80-100 BPM (sensual, belly-dance derived); Smyrna-style amanes are rubato (free tempo)

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB (couplet) — direct, conversational couplets are the norm in Piraeus-style rebetiko
- **Rhyme quality**: Simple end rhymes; natural speech rhythms over forced rhyme; slant rhyme acceptable; the melody carries emotional weight more than clever wordplay
- **Verse structure**: 4-6 lines per verse; couplet-based; often each couplet is a self-contained thought; repetition of lines common for emphasis
- **Key rule**: Emotional authenticity above all — lyrics must sound like lived experience, not literary exercise; first-person confessional voice dominates
- **Avoid**: Flowery or abstract language; multi-layered metaphor; anything that sounds educated or bourgeois; rebetiko lyrics are street-level and direct
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 70-90 BPM. Zeimbekiko (9/8 time) is extremely slow — 2-4 lines max. Hasapiko at 80-100 BPM: 4-6 lines. Leave space for instrumental taximi between verses. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Smyrna Style (Smyrneika)** | The Anatolian branch: oud, santouri, and violin-based ensembles playing in Ottoman makam modes; elaborate melodic ornamentation; amanes vocal improvisation; predates the bouzouki era | Roza Eskenazi, Marika Papagika, Dimitris Semsis |
| **Piraeus Style (Peiraiotika)** | The bouzouki-centered mainland sound: rougher, more stripped-down than Smyrneika; two bouzoukis, baglamas, and guitar; emerged in the 1930s from the port city underworld | Markos Vamvakaris, Giorgos Batis, Stratos Pagioumtzis |
| **Classic/Golden Age Rebetiko** | The post-war mainstream period (1945-1955): Tsitsanis's refined compositions bridging underground and popular; wider orchestration; smoother vocals; the sound that became the foundation of laiko | Vasilis Tsitsanis, Sotiria Bellou, Marika Ninou |
| **Archontorebetiko** | "Upscale rebetiko" — a more polished, nightclub-friendly variant that emerged in the 1950s as rebetiko moved from tekedes (hashish dens) to bouzoukia (nightclubs); larger ensembles, cleaner production | Manolis Chiotis, Giorgos Zambetas, Tolis Harmas |
| **Rebetiko Revival** | 1960s-1980s academic and folk revival movement that returned to pre-war repertoire; performed with period instruments and authentic style; driven by younger generation rediscovering roots | Domna Samiou, Babis Tsertos, various ensemble recordings |
| **Neo-Rebetiko** | Contemporary artists writing new compositions in rebetiko style while incorporating modern influences; maintains traditional instrumentation but with updated recording techniques | Psarogiorgis, Rebetien, modern Athenian scene |

## Reference Tracks

- **Markos Vamvakaris - "Frankosyriani"** — The most iconic rebetiko composition: Vamvakaris wrote this haunting love song after seeing a woman on the island of Syros; the simple bouzouki melody and his rough, yearning voice capture the genre's essence of desire and distance; recorded in the 1930s, it remains the genre's calling card

- **Vasilis Tsitsanis - "Synnefiasmeni Kyriaki" ("Cloudy Sunday")** — Composed in Thessaloniki during the German occupation (c. 1943), this song became an unofficial national anthem for Greece; Tsitsanis's bouzouki playing over a slow zeimbekiko rhythm, with lyrics of sorrow that resonated far beyond their wartime context; has been covered by countless artists across decades

- **Roza Eskenazi - "Kanarini Mou" ("My Canary")** — Early Smyrna-style rebetiko from the late 1920s: Eskenazi's clear, ornamented soprano over oud and santouri accompaniment; demonstrates the Ottoman-influenced melodic sophistication that predates the bouzouki era

- **Sotiria Bellou - "Sinnefiasmeni Kyriaki" (Bellou version)** — Bellou's interpretation of Tsitsanis's masterpiece became definitive: her deep, smoky voice and rebellious spirit transformed the song from lament into defiance; she recorded extensively with Tsitsanis and brought a raw intensity that male singers rarely matched

- **Markos Vamvakaris - "Kathe Vrady Sto Pasalimani"** — A Piraeus waterfront song showcasing the classic kompania sound: two bouzoukis weaving around each other, baglamas keeping rhythm, Vamvakaris's voice telling stories of nighttime wandering along the harbor

- **Vasilis Tsitsanis - "Ola Ta Kano Thalassa"** — Golden-age rebetiko at its most refined: Tsitsanis's compositional sophistication merged with emotional directness; shows the genre's evolution from underground rawness to mainstream artistry without losing its soul

- **Marika Ninou - "To Vouno"** — Ninou's smoky alto voice paired with Tsitsanis's bouzouki; the intimate interplay between vocalist and instrumentalist that defines rebetiko's emotional core; recorded in the early 1950s during the genre's golden era

- **Giorgos Zambetas - "To Minore Tis Avgis"** — Bridge between rebetiko and laiko: Zambetas's guitar and bouzouki work updating the rebetiko vocabulary for 1960s audiences; melodically rich, emotionally direct, and rhythmically rooted in the zeimbekiko tradition

- **Roza Eskenazi - "Tzivaeri Mou"** — Smyrna-style amanes singing: Eskenazi's voice floats over a minimal instrumental backdrop in free rhythm, demonstrating the improvisational vocal art that rebetiko inherited from Ottoman cafe music

- **Grigoris Bithikotsis - "Strose To Stroma Sou"** — Bithikotsis's gravelly voice singing Manos Hadjidakis's composition bridges rebetiko emotion with the art-song tradition; shows how rebetiko's vocal style influenced all subsequent Greek popular music
