# Glitch

## Genre Overview

Glitch is an electronic music genre that emerged in the mid-1990s, built on the deliberate use of digital errors, malfunctions, and artifacts as primary compositional material. Pioneered by artists like Oval (Markus Popp), who famously scratched CDs to create skipping, stuttering textures, and Alva Noto (Carsten Nicolai), who composed with raw digital signal noise, glitch transformed technological failure into aesthetic intention. The genre grew alongside the development of laptop music tools and software like Max/MSP, SuperCollider, and early digital audio workstations.

The sonic vocabulary of glitch includes CD skips, buffer glitches, bit-crushing artifacts, granular deconstruction, digital clicks and cuts, static bursts, and microsound — tiny sonic events arranged into rhythmic and textural patterns. Tracks can range from abrasive and confrontational to delicate and beautiful, with the best glitch producers finding musicality within apparent chaos. The genre challenged fundamental assumptions about what constitutes a musical sound, arguing that the noises computers make when they fail are as valid as any synthesizer patch or sampled instrument.

Glitch's influence extended far beyond its niche origins. The Mille Plateaux and Raster-Noton labels became institutional homes, while the aesthetic permeated IDM, experimental electronic music, and eventually mainstream production through artists like Radiohead (Kid A era) and Bjork. By the mid-2000s, glitch techniques had been absorbed into the broader electronic music toolkit, with producers across genres incorporating glitch processing alongside conventional sounds. Pure glitch remains an active experimental practice, while its DNA lives in virtually every branch of contemporary electronic music production.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Digital artifacts (CD skips, buffer errors, bit-crushed signals), granular synthesis, microsound events, processed noise, clicks and cuts, sine tones, DSP artifacts, glitched audio samples; any sound source subjected to digital destruction and reconstruction

- **Vocals**: Extremely rare in pure glitch; when present, heavily processed through granular synthesis, bit-crushing, and time-stretching until unrecognizable; vocal fragments as raw material for glitch processing rather than melodic content

- **Production**: Laptop-centric; software-driven processing (Max/MSP, SuperCollider, Ableton); extreme editing at the micro level; granular synthesis as core technique; precise arrangement of tiny sonic events; mastering varies — some works are quiet and delicate, others push into harsh digital territory

- **Energy/Mood**: Cerebral, alien, precise, unsettling yet fascinating; ranges from meditative minimalism to aggressive digital noise; clinical and detached; the beauty of broken systems; intellectual engagement over emotional response

- **Structure**: Non-traditional; pieces may follow algorithmic or generative logic; some works are meticulously composed at the sample level, others emerge from controlled randomness; duration varies widely; no verse-chorus expectations; rhythmic patterns emerge from clusters of micro-events

- **Tempo**: Variable or none — some works are rhythmic (80-140 BPM range), others are arrhythmic or pulse-based

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: None — glitch is almost entirely non-lyrical
- **Rhyme quality**: Not applicable
- **Verse structure**: No verse structure; any vocal material is processed beyond intelligibility
- **Key rule**: Glitch does not use conventional lyrics. For Suno, if any text is included, it should be minimal and intended for extreme processing — a word or phrase that will be granularly deconstructed. The glitch IS the content.
- **Avoid**: Singing; intelligible speech; verse-chorus form; any conventional lyric approach
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **0-1 lines/verse**. Tempo varies. If text is needed, single words or short phrases intended for digital destruction. Topics: not applicable in traditional sense. Abstract, technical, or absent.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Microsound** | Ultra-minimal: compositions from tiny sonic events, sine tones, and near-silence; academic rigor | Ryoji Ikeda, Alva Noto, Taylor Deupree |
| **Clicks & Cuts** | Rhythmic glitch with percussive digital artifacts forming beat-like patterns; dancefloor-adjacent | Vladislav Delay, Frank Bretschneider, various Mille Plateaux artists |
| **Glitch Ambient** | Glitch textures layered into ambient frameworks; beautiful digital decay | Fennesz, Tim Hecker (early work), William Basinski (adjacent) |
| **Digital Noise** | Harsher, more confrontational glitch emphasizing raw digital error and system feedback | Merzbow (digital work), Peter Rehberg, Farmers Manual |
| **Laptop Improvisation** | Live performance-oriented glitch using real-time software manipulation | Oval (live), AGF, various SuperCollider performers |

## Reference Tracks

- **Oval - "Aero Deck"** — The glitch manifesto: Markus Popp's scratched-CD technique generating stuttering, looping textures that are simultaneously broken and beautiful; the sound that launched a genre

- **Alva Noto - "Xerrox Isola"** — Microsound precision: tiny digital events arranged with architectural exactness; demonstrates that glitch can be deeply beautiful and emotionally resonant in its minimalism

- **Fennesz - "Endless Summer"** — Glitch ambient masterpiece: guitar melodies dissolving into shimmering digital grain; proves that glitch techniques can carry genuine warmth and nostalgia

- **Ryoji Ikeda - "Data.Matrix"** — Data-driven composition at its most extreme: sine tones, noise bursts, and digital signals organized with mathematical precision; pushes music toward pure information

- **Autechre - "Gantz Graf"** — Glitch-IDM at its most aggressive: chaotic digital rhythms and abrasive textures that somehow cohere into a compelling, if brutal, listening experience

- **Pan Sonic - "Lahetys"** — Physical glitch: sine waves and digital noise rendered with industrial intensity; demonstrates that digital artifacts can hit as hard as any distorted guitar
