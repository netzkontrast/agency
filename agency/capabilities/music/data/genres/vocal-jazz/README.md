# Vocal Jazz

## Genre Overview

Vocal jazz emerged as a distinct art form in the 1920s and 1930s when jazz singers began treating the voice as an improvising instrument equal to the trumpet or saxophone. Louis Armstrong's gravelly scat improvisations in the late 1920s established the foundational vocabulary, but it was Ella Fitzgerald who elevated vocal jazz into a virtuosic discipline. Her 1945 recording of "Flying Home" was described by The New York Times as "one of the most influential vocal jazz records of the decade," and her scat singing -- fluid, rhythmically inventive, and harmonically sophisticated -- set the standard against which all subsequent vocal jazz has been measured. Billie Holiday, though less of a technician, brought an interpretive depth to jazz singing that redefined the art of phrasing.

The bebop era of the 1940s and 1950s transformed vocal jazz further. Sarah Vaughan's three-octave range and operatic control expanded the scat idiom, with her syllable choices ("shoo-doo-shoo-bee-ooo-bee") mirroring the harmonic complexity of the bop combos she performed with. In 1957, the trio of Dave Lambert, Jon Hendricks, and Annie Ross perfected vocalese -- the art of setting lyrics to previously recorded instrumental solos -- and were named "Number One Vocal Group in the World" by Melody Maker for five consecutive years. The 1960s brought bossa nova vocal jazz through Stan Getz and Astrud Gilberto, while the 1970s and 1980s saw The Manhattan Transfer and Bobby McFerrin push vocal jazz in pop and a cappella directions. McFerrin's four-octave range encompassed scat singing, polyphonic overtone singing, and vocal percussion, all demonstrated in unaccompanied solo performance.

Today, vocal jazz thrives across a spectrum from straight-ahead standards singing to experimental vocal improvisation. Cecile McLorin Salvant has revitalized the tradition with her dramatic storytelling and stylistic range, winning three Grammy Awards for Best Jazz Vocal Album. Chet Baker's cool, understated vocal legacy continues through a lineage of instrumentalist-singers, while ensembles like New York Voices carry the group vocal tradition forward. The genre's influence extends into neo-soul (Erykah Badu, Robert Glasper), pop (Norah Jones, Gregory Porter), and experimental music (Theo Bleckmann, Esperanza Spalding).

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Voice as lead instrument, typically accompanied by piano trio (piano, upright bass, drums), small combo (adding guitar, saxophone, or trumpet), or big band. Hammond organ trio format common in soul-jazz vocal settings. String arrangements for ballads. A cappella group formats (vocal bass, vocal percussion, harmony parts) in ensemble vocal jazz.
- **Vocals**: The defining characteristic is improvisatory freedom: scat singing (wordless improvisation using nonsense syllables), vocalese (lyrics set to instrumental solos), and melodic embellishment of standard melodies. Tonal qualities range from Ella Fitzgerald's clear, horn-like precision to Billie Holiday's intimate, speech-like phrasing to Sarah Vaughan's operatic richness. Vibrato use varies by era and style.
- **Production**: Acoustic, intimate recording aesthetic is the standard. Small-room ambience, close-miked vocals, natural instrument sounds. Classic recordings from Rudy Van Gelder's studio (Blue Note, Prestige) established the sonic template. Modern vocal jazz production may incorporate subtle compression and reverb but avoids heavy processing. Live performance in jazz clubs remains central to the art form.
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges from the exuberant swing of uptempo scat showcases to the aching intimacy of late-night ballads. Cool detachment (Chet Baker, Blossom Dearie) contrasts with passionate intensity (Nina Simone, Dianne Reeves). The mood palette includes playful, romantic, melancholic, sultry, defiant, and joyful.
- **Structure**: Standards typically follow 32-bar AABA or ABAB' song forms derived from the Great American Songbook (Gershwin, Porter, Ellington, Kern). Head-solos-head arrangement: melody statement, improvised choruses (instrumental and vocal), return to melody. Blues form (12-bar) is foundational. Vocalese follows the form of the original instrumental recording.
- **Tempo**: Ballads: 50-75 BPM. Medium swing: 100-140 BPM. Uptempo swing: 160-240+ BPM. Bossa nova: 120-150 BPM with a straight-eighth feel. Latin jazz vocals: 100-130 BPM.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABA song form lyrics follow the 32-bar standard structure: A sections share rhyme patterns, B section (bridge) contrasts. End rhyme AABB or ABAB within sections. Great American Songbook lyricists (Lorenz Hart, Ira Gershwin, Johnny Mercer) set the standard.
- **Rhyme quality**: Sophisticated wordplay: internal rhymes, mosaic rhymes (rhyming across multiple words), feminine rhymes. Cole Porter and Lorenz Hart exemplify the wit expected in jazz lyrics. Forced or predictable rhymes are beneath the genre's standards.
- **Verse structure**: A sections: typically 8 bars (4 lines of lyric). B section (bridge): 8 bars providing contrast. Full chorus: 32 bars. Introductory verses (rarely performed today) may precede the chorus. Scat sections replace lyrics with nonsense syllables.
- **Key rule**: Phrasing is everything. The lyric must sit naturally against the rhythmic feel, with room for the singer to push and pull against the beat. Write for conversational delivery, not metronomic precision. Leave space for melodic embellishment.
- **Avoid**: Rhythmically rigid lyrics that lock the singer into a single phrasing option. Overly literal or prosaic language -- jazz lyrics favor metaphor, wit, and emotional suggestion over direct statement. Excessive syllable density that prevents swing feel.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 100-130 BPM for medium swing. At ballad tempo (50-75 BPM): max 4 lines. Uptempo: 6-8 lines with shorter phrases. Topics: 1-2/verse. Leave natural pauses for instrumental fills. Suno handles jazz vocals best with clear melodic phrases and space.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Standards/Straight-Ahead** | Interpretations of the Great American Songbook with swing rhythm section; emphasis on phrasing, melodic embellishment, and emotional storytelling within the 32-bar form; the core of the vocal jazz tradition. | Ella Fitzgerald, Frank Sinatra, Sarah Vaughan, Tony Bennett |
| **Scat Singing** | Wordless vocal improvisation using nonsense syllables (doo-bop-she-bam) to create melodic lines mimicking instrumental solos; demands harmonic knowledge, rhythmic agility, and tonal control; the voice as horn. | Ella Fitzgerald, Sarah Vaughan, Bobby McFerrin, Al Jarreau |
| **Vocalese** | Lyrics written to fit pre-existing instrumental jazz solos note-for-note; requires memorizing complex bebop lines and adding intelligible words that match the rhythm and melody; the most technically demanding lyric form in popular music. | Lambert, Hendricks & Ross, The Manhattan Transfer, Jon Hendricks |
| **Cool Vocal Jazz** | Understated, intimate delivery with restrained vibrato and conversational phrasing; often paired with sparse, West Coast-style arrangements; emotional impact through what is withheld rather than expressed. | Chet Baker, Blossom Dearie, Julie London, Stacey Kent |
| **Soul Jazz Vocals** | Jazz singing infused with gospel, blues, and R&B inflections; earthier and more emotionally direct than bebop-derived styles; often features Hammond organ accompaniment and a groove-based rhythmic feel. | Nina Simone, Ray Charles, Donny Hathaway, Gregory Porter |
| **Bossa Nova Vocal** | Brazilian-influenced vocal jazz with a gently syncopated rhythmic feel, nylon-string guitar, and Portuguese or English lyrics; the whispered intimacy of Joao Gilberto's delivery defined the vocal aesthetic. | Astrud Gilberto, Joao Gilberto, Antonio Carlos Jobim, Diana Krall |
| **Vocal Group Jazz** | Multi-voice ensembles singing tight harmonies, trading solos, and performing arranged jazz repertoire; ranges from classic trios to modern five-part groups with vocal percussion; the choir tradition compressed into a small, virtuosic unit. | Lambert, Hendricks & Ross, Manhattan Transfer, New York Voices, Take 6 |
| **Experimental Vocal Jazz** | Extended techniques, prepared voice, electronics, and avant-garde improvisation applied to the jazz vocal tradition; pushes beyond scat into pure sound exploration; often intersects with contemporary art music. | Bobby McFerrin, Theo Bleckmann, Esperanza Spalding, Meredith Monk |

## Reference Tracks

- **Ella Fitzgerald - "How High the Moon" (Live in Berlin, 1960)** -- Fitzgerald's legendary live scat solo on this track is a masterclass in vocal jazz improvisation. She quotes multiple songs, navigates complex harmonic changes at breakneck tempo, and demonstrates why she earned the title "First Lady of Song."

- **Billie Holiday - "Strange Fruit"** -- Recorded in 1939, Holiday's performance of Abel Meeropol's anti-lynching poem demonstrated that jazz singing could be a vehicle for devastating social commentary. Her restrained, almost spoken delivery makes the horror more, not less, unbearable.

- **Sarah Vaughan - "Lullaby of Birdland"** -- Vaughan's 1954 recording showcases her astonishing range and bop-influenced scat singing. Her ability to shift from a breathy low register to a soaring operatic tone within a single phrase set her apart from every other jazz vocalist.

- **Frank Sinatra - "In the Wee Small Hours of the Morning"** -- The title track from Sinatra's 1955 concept album established the blueprint for the intimate jazz vocal ballad. Nelson Riddle's spare arrangement and Sinatra's behind-the-beat phrasing created a template for late-night emotional jazz singing.

- **Lambert, Hendricks & Ross - "Sing a Song of Basie"** -- The 1958 album put lyrics to Count Basie Orchestra recordings, with three voices replacing an entire big band. Jon Hendricks's lyric-writing and the trio's ensemble precision invented the vocalese album format.

- **Chet Baker - "My Funny Valentine"** -- Baker's fragile, almost whispered 1954 recording strips the Rodgers and Hart standard to its emotional core. His vulnerability and limited technical range became assets, proving that feeling could outweigh virtuosity in vocal jazz.

- **Bobby McFerrin - "Don't Worry, Be Happy"** -- The 1988 worldwide No. 1 hit was created entirely with layered vocal overdubs and body percussion, with no instruments. It proved that vocal jazz-influenced music could dominate pop charts while showcasing McFerrin's extraordinary vocal range.

- **The Manhattan Transfer - "Birdland"** -- Their 1979 vocal arrangement of Weather Report's jazz-fusion instrumental won the Grammy for Best Jazz Fusion Performance. The track demonstrated that vocal groups could tackle complex post-bop material with studio precision and pop appeal.

- **Nina Simone - "Feeling Good"** -- From the 1965 musical *The Roar of the Greasepaint -- The Smell of the Crowd*, Simone's recording builds from a hushed opening to a triumphant climax. Her classical piano training and gospel-blues delivery created a vocal jazz style that defied categorization.

- **Cecile McLorin Salvant - "You've Got to Give Me Some"** -- Salvant's performance of this 1929 Bessie Smith song showcases her ability to inhabit historical material with contemporary intelligence. Her three Grammy Awards for Best Jazz Vocal Album confirm her status as the tradition's current standard-bearer.

- **Astrud Gilberto & Stan Getz - "The Girl from Ipanema"** -- The 1964 recording introduced bossa nova to American audiences and became one of the most recognized jazz vocals in history. Gilberto's untrained, conversational delivery defined the bossa vocal aesthetic: cool, detached, effortlessly melodic.

- **Al Jarreau - "Take Five"** -- Jarreau's vocal version of the Dave Brubeck Quartet's odd-meter classic demonstrates his unique approach to scat singing, incorporating vocal percussion and sound effects. His ability to win Grammys in jazz, pop, and R&B categories reflects vocal jazz's genre-bridging potential.
