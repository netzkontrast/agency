# Barbershop

## Genre Overview

Barbershop is a style of a cappella close harmony singing that originated in African American communities in the American South during the 1870s and 1880s. Black quartets like The American Four and The Hamtown Students were among the first groups associated with the style, harmonizing popular songs, spirituals, and folk tunes in barbershops, on street corners, and at social gatherings. As historian James Weldon Johnson wrote of Jacksonville, Florida in that era, "every barbershop seemed to have its own quartet." The African influence is evident in the music's improvisational nature, the flexing of melody to produce harmonies, and the techniques of "swipes" and "snakes" — smooth glides between chords that remain hallmarks of the style. By the early 1900s, quartets like the American Quartet and the Peerless Quartet had turned songs like "Sweet Adeline" (1903) and "Shine On, Harvest Moon" (1908) into barbershop standards.

The style's popularity waned during the jazz age of the 1920s, but a powerful revival began in 1938 when Owen C. Cash and Rupert I. Hall founded the Society for the Preservation and Encouragement of Barber Shop Quartet Singing in America (SPEBSQSA, now the Barbershop Harmony Society) in Tulsa, Oklahoma. Sweet Adelines International followed in 1945 for women, and Harmony, Incorporated was established in Rhode Island in 1959. These organizations formalized the style's conventions, created competition frameworks, and preserved its repertoire. The Buffalo Bills quartet brought barbershop to Broadway in the 1957 musical *The Music Man* and its 1962 film adaptation, introducing millions to the sound. Competition became central to the culture, with International Quartet and Chorus Championships drawing thousands of competitors annually.

Today, barbershop thrives as both a competitive art form and a community singing tradition. The Barbershop Harmony Society's annual conventions remain major events, and the style has gone global — Sweden's Ringmasters became the first non-North American quartet to win the International Quartet Championship in 2012. Contemporary barbershop pushes beyond Tin Pan Alley standards into pop, rock, and musical theater repertoire while maintaining the style's harmonic DNA: consonant four-part chords that, when precisely tuned, produce audible overtones — the legendary "ringing chord" that is barbershop's acoustic signature. The Barberpole Cat Program preserves 12 standard songs that any barbershopper can sing on sight, ensuring the communal, pick-up-quartet tradition endures alongside the competitive elite.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: None — barbershop is strictly a cappella. All musical content comes from four voice parts: lead (melody, roughly baritone-tenor range), tenor (highest part, harmonizing above the lead), baritone (fills chord tones, often crossing above and below the lead), and bass (lowest part, providing harmonic foundation). No instruments, no vocal percussion, no electronic processing.
- **Vocals**: Homophonic texture — all four parts sing the same rhythm simultaneously, creating a "wall of harmony" effect. Precise intonation is critical; just-tuned intervals produce audible overtones (the "expanded sound" or "ringing chord"). Vocal delivery ranges from warm and lyrical in ballads to bright and punchy in uptune numbers. Vibrato is minimized to maintain chord lock. Dynamic expression through crescendos, decrescendos, and dramatic swells is central to performance.
- **Production**: Live performance is the primary format, traditionally with no amplification in competition settings. Studio recordings aim to capture the natural acoustic blend and overtone production. Minimal processing — barbershop's appeal is in the raw, unadorned vocal blend. Competition recordings use high-quality room mics to preserve spatial depth. The style resists heavy studio treatment; authenticity of blend is paramount.
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges from tender, sentimental ballads ("Lida Rose," "Heart of My Heart") to exuberant uptune comedy numbers. Nostalgia and romance are dominant moods. Emotional delivery is theatrical and direct — barbershop rewards expressive, audience-connected performance. Comedy and physical choreography ("stage presence") are valued in competition alongside vocal quality.
- **Structure**: Songs are typically 32-bar AABA or verse-chorus form, reflecting the Tin Pan Alley era standards that dominate traditional repertoire. Tags — short, harmonically rich codas, often 4-8 bars — are a signature element and sometimes performed as standalone pieces. Introductions (often rubato) and key changes are common. Contemporary arrangements incorporate more complex forms but maintain homophonic texture.
- **Tempo**: Ballads: 60-80 BPM with extensive rubato and tempo manipulation. Mid-tempo: 90-110 BPM. Uptunes: 120-160 BPM with driving, even rhythm. Tempo flexibility is a hallmark — songs often shift between rubato and strict time, with ritardandos and accelerandos used for emotional effect.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB (couplet) or ABAB — reflects the Tin Pan Alley standard songwriting tradition that provides most barbershop repertoire.
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean, perfect rhymes strongly preferred. The homophonic texture means all four parts pronounce every word simultaneously — near-rhymes and slant rhymes are more noticeable than in other vocal styles. Vowel matching across parts is critical for blend.
- **Verse structure**: 8-bar sections typical (reflecting 32-bar AABA form). Lines are short and singable — 6-10 syllables. Lyrics must leave room for sustained chords and dynamic swells.
- **Key rule**: Every vowel must be singable and sustainable in four-part harmony. Open vowels (ah, oh, ee) ring; closed vowels and diphthongs (ow, oi) require careful tuning. Write for the chord, not just the melody.
- **Avoid**: Rapid syllabic passages that prevent chord locking. Consonant-heavy words that interrupt sustained harmony. Lyrics that demand different rhythms from different parts (barbershop is homophonic). Modern slang that conflicts with the style's nostalgic warmth.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 80-110 BPM. Barbershop needs space for chords to ring and swells to breathe. Ballads may have only 2-3 lines per section. Topics: 1/verse. Uptunes can sustain 4-6 lines. Tags: 2-4 lines maximum.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Traditional Barbershop** | Classic Tin Pan Alley repertoire from the 1890s-1950s; sentimental ballads and novelty songs; strict four-part homophonic harmony; emphasis on chord ringing and tag singing; the competitive and social core of the barbershop tradition | Buffalo Bills, Schmitt Brothers, Confederates, Suntones |
| **Contemporary Barbershop** | Modern arrangements of pop, rock, Broadway, and contemporary songs adapted to barbershop style; expanded harmonic vocabulary including jazz voicings and chromatic movement; maintains four-part texture but pushes arrangement complexity; dominant in current competition | Ringmasters, Crossroads, Forefront, Signature |
| **Comedy Barbershop** | Novelty songs, physical comedy, and theatrical presentation; choreographed routines with synchronized movement; humorous lyrics and sight gags integrated with tight harmony; a beloved tradition in both competition and afterglow shows | Gas House Gang, Acoustix, Four Rascals |
| **Contest Barbershop** | Competition-optimized performance with precise attention to BHS judging categories: singing, music, and presentation; arrangements designed to maximize scoring through difficulty, contrast, and emotional connection; the elite tier of barbershop performance | Vocal Majority (chorus), Ambassadors of Harmony (chorus), OC Times, Ringmasters |
| **Sweet Adelines / Women's Barbershop** | Women's four-part barbershop harmony organized through Sweet Adelines International and Harmony, Incorporated; same harmonic principles as men's barbershop but with soprano and alto replacing tenor and lead; strong competition culture with distinct repertoire traditions | GQ (quartet), Rönninge Show Chorus, ACES (quartet) |
| **Youth Barbershop** | Young singers (high school and college age) performing barbershop through BHS youth programs and festivals; brings new energy and contemporary song choices; pipeline for future competitors; increasingly diverse and inclusive | Next Generation Barbershop, BHS Youth Chorus Festival participants |
| **Gospel Barbershop** | Spirituals and hymns arranged in four-part barbershop style; draws from the African American roots of barbershop harmony; emotional, church-influenced delivery with barbershop chord structures; celebrates the genre's origins in Black vocal harmony | Revival, The Happiness Emporium |
| **Chorus Barbershop** | Large ensembles (30-150+ members) singing in four-part barbershop harmony; massive choral sound with the precision of quartet tuning; visual presentation with synchronized choreography; International Chorus Championship is the pinnacle | Vocal Majority, Ambassadors of Harmony, Westminster Chorus, Masters of Harmony |

## Reference Tracks

- **Buffalo Bills - "Lida Rose" (from The Music Man)** — The quartet's performance in Meredith Willson's 1957 Broadway musical became the most famous barbershop moment in American entertainment. The gentle, swaying ballad with its seamlessly blended four-part harmony introduced millions to barbershop singing and remains a gateway song for the uninitiated.

- **The Chordettes - "Mr. Sandman"** — Released in 1954, this number-one hit brought barbershop-style close harmony to pop radio. The interlocking "bung bung" vocal intro and tight four-voice arrangement proved that barbershop principles could produce commercially dominant pop music.

- **Vocal Majority - "Stars and Stripes Forever"** — The Dallas chorus's arrangement of Sousa's march demonstrates the power of 100+ voices singing in barbershop harmony. Intricate vocal imitations of brass and percussion showcase what large-ensemble barbershop can achieve — an overwhelming wall of perfectly tuned harmony.

- **Ringmasters - "The A-Team"** — The Swedish quartet's gold-medal-winning set piece demonstrated that contemporary barbershop could incorporate modern pop arrangements while maintaining the genre's harmonic DNA. Their 2012 championship broke the North American monopoly on barbershop's top prize.

- **Acoustix - "Sweet Georgia Brown"** — Comedy meets virtuosity in this uptune arrangement featuring vocal jazz inflections, comedic timing, and razor-sharp harmony. Showcases the entertainment side of barbershop that thrives in afterglow shows and brings audiences to their feet.

- **Gas House Gang - "76 Trombones"** — Physical comedy and four-part harmony collide in this theatrical arrangement. The quartet's comedic choreography synchronized with tight vocal delivery demonstrates why presentation is judged alongside singing in barbershop competition.

- **The Suntones - "Watch What Happens"** — Smooth, lyrical barbershop at its finest. The 1961 champions' treatment of this standard defined the modern barbershop ballad approach — warm blend, controlled dynamics, and emotional delivery that treats each chord as a destination.

- **Ambassadors of Harmony - "Georgia On My Mind"** — The St. Louis chorus's arrangement showcases the emotional depth a large barbershop ensemble can achieve. Hoagy Carmichael's classic receives a treatment that ranges from intimate quartet texture to full-chorus crescendos.

- **Crossroads - "Can't Take My Eyes Off You"** — The 2009 champions' arrangement of the Frankie Valli classic demonstrates how barbershop arrangers transform pop hits into four-part harmony showcases, maintaining the song's energy while adding harmonic complexity and dynamic contrast.

- **Westminster Chorus - "The Impossible Dream"** — A competition chorus staple that demonstrates barbershop's capacity for dramatic, emotional storytelling. The arrangement builds from a soft, introspective opening to a full-voiced climax that exploits the genre's dynamic range.
