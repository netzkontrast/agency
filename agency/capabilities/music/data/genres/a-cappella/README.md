# A Cappella

## Genre Overview

A cappella music — vocal performance without instrumental accompaniment — is one of the oldest forms of human musical expression, predating the invention of every instrument. Its Western tradition traces back to Gregorian chant in medieval European churches, where unaccompanied voices carried sacred text through monophonic and later polyphonic settings. Renaissance composers like Josquin des Prez and Palestrina elevated the form into intricate multi-voice polyphony during the 15th and 16th centuries. Outside the church, secular a cappella traditions thrived in African American communities through spirituals, field hollers, and the close-harmony street-corner singing that would eventually birth doo-wop and barbershop. The term itself, meaning "in the manner of the chapel," reflects the genre's deep roots in liturgical practice.

The 20th century transformed a cappella from a primarily sacred or informal tradition into a commercial and competitive force. The Mills Brothers and the Ink Spots pioneered vocal group pop in the 1930s and 1940s, simulating instruments with their voices. Doo-wop groups dominated the 1950s, and by the 1960s, ensembles like The King's Singers (founded 1968) and The Swingle Singers (founded 1962) brought sophisticated vocal arrangements to concert halls. Bobby McFerrin's 1988 hit "Don't Worry, Be Happy" proved a cappella could top pop charts. The collegiate a cappella explosion of the 1990s and 2000s, fueled by groups at universities across the United States, created a massive amateur and semi-professional scene. The Sing-Off television series (2009-2014) and the Pitch Perfect film franchise (2012-2017) brought the genre to mainstream audiences on an unprecedented scale.

Today, a cappella encompasses everything from sacred choral works to beatbox-driven pop covers to experimental vocal art. Pentatonix, who won The Sing-Off in 2011, have sold millions of albums and demonstrated that a cappella acts can sustain arena-level careers. The International Championship of Collegiate A Cappella (ICCA) draws hundreds of competing groups annually. Technology has expanded the genre's possibilities: loop stations, vocal effects processing, and multi-track recording allow solo artists to build full arrangements from their own voice. Meanwhile, traditional ensembles like Chanticleer and VOCES8 continue to prove that unadorned human voices remain one of the most powerful instruments in music.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: None — all sound is produced by the human voice. Vocal parts typically include lead melody, harmony voices (soprano, alto, tenor, bass), vocal percussion (beatboxing), and vocal bass. Some contemporary groups use loop pedals and live vocal processing. The absence of instruments is the defining characteristic.
- **Vocals**: Ranges from pure classical tone (straight tone, controlled vibrato) to contemporary pop delivery (belting, runs, falsetto). Beatboxing provides rhythmic foundation through mouth-produced drum and percussion sounds. Vocal bass lines simulate electric bass through sub-harmonic singing techniques. Background vocals use syllables like "doo," "dah," "bah," "dm," and "jen" to create texture and rhythm.
- **Production**: Live performance is traditionally unprocessed. Studio recordings range from minimal (room mic, natural reverb) to heavily produced (multi-tracked, pitch-corrected, effects-laden). Contemporary a cappella production often adds compression, reverb, and EQ to individual vocal parts to create a polished, instrument-like sound. Some purists reject processing entirely.
- **Energy/Mood**: Extraordinarily versatile — sacred and meditative to high-energy pop; intimate jazz ballads to driving rhythmic arrangements; solemn choral works to comedic novelty performances. The emotional range is limited only by what the human voice can express.
- **Structure**: Varies by style. Contemporary pop a cappella follows standard verse-chorus-bridge form. Choral works may use through-composed or ABA structures. Collegiate arrangements typically feature a solo lead with backing harmonies, vocal percussion, and a climactic final chorus with key change. Tags (short repeated endings) are common in barbershop-influenced styles.
- **Tempo**: Full range from slow choral works (40-60 BPM) to up-tempo pop arrangements (120-140 BPM) to beatbox-driven tracks (80-160 BPM). Rubato and tempo flexibility are common in classical and jazz styles.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Varies by source material — a cappella groups primarily arrange existing songs, so rhyme scheme follows the original. For original compositions, ABAB and AABB are most common.
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean, singable rhymes preferred. Multi-part harmony demands vowel-friendly words — open vowels (ah, oh, ee) sustain and blend better than closed consonants. Avoid consonant clusters that disrupt vocal blend.
- **Verse structure**: Follows the conventions of the style being performed (pop verse-chorus, jazz AABA, choral through-composed). Original a cappella works often favor shorter phrases to allow vocal interplay between parts.
- **Key rule**: Vowel singability is paramount — every syllable must be pronounceable and sustainable across multiple vocal parts simultaneously. Background syllables must be carefully chosen for blend and rhythmic function.
- **Avoid**: Lyrics that require rapid consonant articulation across harmony parts (they blur). Words with nasal endings (ng, n) held for long durations in inner voices. Overly wordy passages in background parts — simplicity enables blend.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6 lines/verse** at 100-120 BPM. A cappella arrangements need breathing room for harmony parts to breathe and blend. Topics: 1-2/verse. Slower tempos allow denser harmonies; faster tempos need sparser lyrics.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Contemporary Pop A Cappella** | Modern vocal arrangements of pop, rock, and R&B songs featuring lead vocals over multi-part harmony with vocal percussion and vocal bass; production-heavy studio recordings; arrangements often reimagine originals with new harmonic and rhythmic treatments | Pentatonix, Pitch Perfect cast, Straight No Chaser, Home Free |
| **Collegiate A Cappella** | University-based groups performing pop covers and originals in competitive and performance settings; typically 10-16 members; strong tradition at Ivy League and liberal arts schools; features solo leads rotating among members with full-group backing arrangements | Beelzebubs (Tufts), Whiffenpoofs (Yale), Nor'easters (Northeastern), Socal VoCals (USC) |
| **Classical/Choral A Cappella** | Sacred and secular art music for unaccompanied voices; ranges from Renaissance polyphony to contemporary commissions; emphasis on blend, intonation, and interpretive nuance; performed by professional chamber choirs and cathedral ensembles | Chanticleer, The King's Singers, VOCES8, The Sixteen |
| **Vocal Jazz** | Jazz harmonies, scat singing, and swing feel adapted for voice groups; close voicings and chromatic harmony; improvisation within arranged frameworks; influenced by instrumental jazz combo and big band traditions | The Swingle Singers, Take 6, The Manhattan Transfer, The Real Group |
| **Gospel A Cappella** | Rooted in African American church tradition; call-and-response patterns, melismatic vocal runs, emotional intensity; four-to-six-part harmony with prominent bass and tenor leads; spiritual and inspirational lyrics | Take 6, The Fairfield Four, Sounds of Blackness, The Blind Boys of Alabama |
| **Beatbox/Vocal Band** | Groups that use extended vocal techniques to fully simulate a band with instruments; beatboxing provides drums, vocal bass provides low end, and additional voices create guitar, synth, and horn sounds; blurs the line between a cappella and full-band performance | Naturally 7, Pentatonix, Berywam, The Filharmonic |
| **Barbershop** | Four-part close harmony in homophonic texture; characterized by ringing overtones from precisely tuned chords; specific voice parts (lead, tenor, baritone, bass); competitive tradition with formal judging criteria | See [Barbershop](../barbershop/README.md) genre entry |
| **Doo-Wop** | 1950s vocal group harmony featuring nonsense syllables, prominent bass vocals, and simple harmonic progressions; street-corner origins; call-and-response between lead and backing voices | See [Doo-Wop](../doo-wop/README.md) genre entry |
| **World/Folk A Cappella** | Traditional vocal music from cultures worldwide including South African isicathamiya, Georgian polyphonic singing, Sardinian cantu a tenore, and Baltic choral traditions; each carries distinct tuning systems, ornamental styles, and social functions | Ladysmith Black Mambazo, The Bulgarian Voices Angelite, Rajaton, Huun-Huur-Tu |

## Reference Tracks

- **Pentatonix - "Daft Punk"** — A medley of Daft Punk hits arranged for five voices that demonstrated how modern a cappella could match electronic music's energy and complexity. Kevin Olusola's cello-beatboxing hybrid and Avi Kaplan's sub-bass vocals showed the genre's expanded sonic palette. The track helped Pentatonix win their Grammy for Best Arrangement, Instrumental or A Cappella in 2015.

- **Bobby McFerrin - "Don't Worry, Be Happy"** — The first a cappella song to reach number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1988. McFerrin performed every vocal part himself, layering lead, harmony, and percussion. The track proved that unaccompanied voices could compete commercially with fully produced pop.

- **The King's Singers - "Masterpiece"** — A showcase of the ensemble's signature six-voice blend moving seamlessly from Renaissance madrigal style to contemporary pop arrangement. Demonstrates the pure intonation and dynamic control that define classical a cappella at its highest level.

- **Take 6 - "Spread Love"** — Gospel-jazz a cappella with stacked harmonies, rhythmic vocal percussion, and spiritual intensity. Won the group their first Grammy in 1988 and established that a cappella could carry the harmonic sophistication of jazz without losing gospel's emotional directness.

- **The Swingle Singers - "Fugue in D Minor" (from Jazz Sebastian Bach)** — Ward Swingle's 1963 arrangement that launched the group's career, applying swing rhythm and jazz phrasing to Bach's keyboard fugues. The recording won the Grammy for Best Performance by a Chorus and proved that classical repertoire could swing.

- **Ladysmith Black Mambazo - "Homeless" (with Paul Simon)** — From the 1986 *Graceland* album, this track brought South African isicathamiya to global audiences. The call-and-response structure between Simon's English verses and the group's Zulu harmonies demonstrated a cappella's power as a cross-cultural bridge.

- **Straight No Chaser - "The 12 Days of Christmas"** — The 1998 Indiana University performance that went viral on YouTube in 2007, eventually landing the group a major-label record deal. The comedic arrangement, incorporating "Africa" by Toto mid-song, showed how collegiate a cappella could transcend campus audiences.

- **Chanticleer - "Ave Maria" (Josquin des Prez)** — The San Francisco ensemble's interpretation of Renaissance polyphony with immaculate blend, tuning, and phrasing. Demonstrates why Chanticleer earned the title "orchestra of voices" and why unaccompanied sacred music remains a cappella's spiritual core.

- **The Persuasions - "Good Times"** — Street-corner doo-wop and gospel roots stripped to their essence. Jerry Lawson's lead vocal and the group's raw harmonies, championed by Frank Zappa who signed them to his Straight Records label in 1970, proved that a cappella needed no production to move audiences.

- **Home Free - "Ring of Fire"** — A country a cappella arrangement of the Johnny Cash classic featuring Tim Foust's deep bass vocals anchoring the arrangement. The performance showcased how a cappella vocal bass can replace an entire rhythm section while maintaining the groove and swagger of country music.

- **Naturally 7 - "Wall of Sound"** — The group's signature "vocal play" technique, where each member simulates a specific instrument — drums, bass, guitar, brass — creating a full band sound from seven voices. Blurs the boundary between a cappella and full-band performance.

- **Rajaton - "Butterfly"** — Finnish vocal precision meeting pop accessibility. Tight Nordic blend, inventive vocal arrangements, and a demonstration of how Scandinavian a cappella groups bring their own aesthetic — clean, precise, and harmonically adventurous — to the contemporary scene.
