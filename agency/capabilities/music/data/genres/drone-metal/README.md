# Drone Metal

## Genre Overview

Drone metal emerged in the early 1990s when musicians began stripping doom metal down to its most elemental component: sustained, amplified heaviness. The genre's foundation was laid by Dylan Carlson's band Earth, formed in 1989 in Olympia, Washington. Carlson fused the crushing weight of Black Sabbath and the Melvins with the minimalist repetition of composers like La Monte Young and Tony Conrad. Earth's debut full-length, *Earth 2: Special Low Frequency Version* (1993), was a 75-minute, three-track exploration of sustained distorted guitar tones with no vocals, no conventional song structures, and no concessions to accessibility. Carlson called it "ambient metal," and it became the genre's foundational document.

Through the late 1990s, drone metal developed along several paths simultaneously. In 1998, Stephen O'Malley and Greg Anderson formed Sunn O))) in Seattle, naming themselves as a deliberate homage to Earth. Sunn O))) pushed the concept further into ritualistic performance art: robed figures generating walls of feedback and overtone in near-total darkness. Their collaborations with artists from other disciplines (Attila Csihar of Mayhem, Scott Walker, Ulver) expanded drone metal's artistic ambitions. In Japan, Boris (formed 1992 in Tokyo) and Corrupted (from Osaka) developed parallel approaches, with Boris moving fluidly between drone, noise, shoegaze, and pop across their vast discography. Meanwhile, acts like Khanate and Burning Witch (featuring O'Malley before Sunn O)))) explored the intersection of drone with extreme vocal terror.

Today, drone metal occupies a unique position at the intersection of metal, contemporary art music, and experimental sound. The genre's influence extends far beyond its own borders: post-metal's emphasis on sustained texture, blackgaze's wall-of-sound guitar approach, and the entire dark ambient scene all owe debts to drone metal's innovations. Modern practitioners like Bell Witch, Thou, and Primitive Man continue to find new emotional territory within the genre's deliberately limited palette. The annual Roadburn Festival in the Netherlands has become a gathering point for the drone and experimental metal community, demonstrating the genre's enduring vitality and its audience's dedication.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Heavily amplified and downtuned electric guitars (often through multiple amplifier stacks), bass guitar, minimal or no drums, synthesizers and electronics, feedback as a deliberate instrument. Some acts use cello, trombone, or other acoustic instruments processed through amplification. Guitar tunings are extremely low, sometimes tuned to A or below.
- **Vocals**: Often absent entirely. When present: deep, processed growls; chanted or ritualistic delivery; spoken word; guest vocalists from other extreme metal genres. Vocals are treated as another textural element rather than a melodic or narrative vehicle.
- **Production**: Maximalist volume and sustain are central. Emphasis on overtone, resonance, and the physical experience of amplified sound. Recording often captures room ambience and natural feedback. Some acts pursue lo-fi rawness; others (Sunn O)))) favor meticulous studio layering. Low-frequency content is prioritized.
- **Energy/Mood**: Meditative, oppressive, transcendent, ritualistic. The experience is physical as much as auditory -- drone metal is designed to be felt in the body through sustained low-frequency vibration. Slow enough to suspend the listener's sense of time. Can evoke both dread and bliss.
- **Structure**: Minimal or absent conventional structure. Pieces may consist of a single sustained tone or chord evolving over 10-30+ minutes. No verse, chorus, or bridge. Gradual evolution of texture and timbre replaces traditional development. Silence and near-silence function as compositional elements.
- **Tempo**: Effectively unmeasurable in many cases. When pulse exists: 20-60 BPM. Some acts incorporate extremely slow doom riffs (30-50 BPM) that border on drone through sheer duration. Rhythm is often implied by the natural pulse of feedback cycles rather than played.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Not applicable for most drone metal. When lyrics exist, they follow no fixed scheme. Repetition of single phrases or words is more common than verse structures.
- **Rhyme quality**: Irrelevant in most cases. Sound quality of individual words matters more than rhyme -- how a word resonates when sustained or screamed over a drone.
- **Verse structure**: No conventional verse structure. Single repeated phrases, ritualistic chanting, or extended vocal tones. Some acts use brief, isolated lyrical fragments across long instrumental passages.
- **Key rule**: Less is more. A single phrase repeated or sustained can carry more weight than paragraphs. Words function as vibration and texture, not narrative. If lyrics exist, they should feel elemental -- stripped to primal meaning.
- **Avoid**: Conventional song lyrics. Narrative storytelling. Rhyme schemes. Dense wordplay. Anything that implies traditional vocal pacing.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** (if any) at sub-60 BPM. Most drone metal works best as instrumental. If vocals are needed, use minimal text with long gaps. Extended instrumental tags essential. Topics: 1/song at most.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Pure Drone Metal | Sustained distorted guitar tones with no conventional song structure, minimal or no vocals, and extreme duration. The genre's foundation: amplified sound as meditative experience. Feedback and overtone as primary compositional elements. | Earth (early), Sunn O))), Eldar |
| Ritualistic Drone | Performance-art dimension: robed figures, incense, darkness, and sustained amplification creating a ceremonial atmosphere. Collaborations with vocalists and experimental musicians expand the sonic palette. | Sunn O))), Moss, Bong, OM |
| Drone Doom | Retaining more recognizable doom metal elements (slow riffs, occasional drums, identifiable song structure) while pushing toward drone's sustained textures and extreme duration. The bridge between doom and pure drone. | Bell Witch, Monarch!, Corrupted, Wormphlegm |
| Noise Drone | Incorporating harsh noise, feedback manipulation, and abrasive textures alongside sustained heaviness. More aggressive and confrontational than meditative drone. | Khanate, Burning Witch, The Body, Gnaw Their Tongues |
| Ambient Drone Metal | Softer, more atmospheric approach emphasizing beauty and space over crushing heaviness. Clean tones, reverb, and ambient textures create meditative soundscapes that retain metal's weight. | Earth (later), Nadja, Jesu, Aidan Baker |
| Blackened Drone | Fusing drone metal's sustained textures with black metal's tremolo picking and atmosphere. Hypnotic repetition of black metal riffs stretched to drone-like duration. Darker and more aggressive than ambient drone. | Paysage d'Hiver, Darkspace, Wold, Urfaust |

## Reference Tracks

- **Earth - "Seven Angels"** (1993) — From *Earth 2: Special Low Frequency Version*. Twenty minutes of sustained, distorted guitar with no drums, no vocals, and no conventional development. The sound that started drone metal: pure amplified weight slowed to geological time.
- **Sunn O))) - "It Took the Night to Believe"** (2005) — From *Black One*. Oren Ambarchi's contributions and the album's guest vocalists (including Wrest of Leviathan) create drone metal's darkest hour. Sustained tones become genuinely menacing.
- **Sunn O))) - "Big Church (Megszentsegtelenithetettleneskedheseitekert)"** (2009) — From *Monoliths & Dimensions*. A collaboration with the Budapest-based choir that demonstrates drone metal can achieve orchestral grandeur. The genre's most ambitious artistic statement.
- **Boris - "Absolutego"** (1996) — A single 65-minute track that evolves from quiet feedback to crushing doom. Boris at their most monolithic, before their stylistic diversification.
- **Boris - "Flood"** (2000) — Four movements spanning drone, doom, noise, and beauty. The final section erupts into one of the heaviest climaxes in drone metal history. Emotional range within extreme heaviness.
- **Khanate - "Skin Coat"** (2001) — Alan Dubin's vocals turn a crawling drone riff into genuine horror. Drone metal as psychological warfare -- the silences between notes are as terrifying as the sound.
- **Bell Witch - "Mirror Reaper"** (2017) — An 83-minute single composition that functions as a memorial for drummer Adrian Guerra. Funeral doom meets drone in one of heavy music's most emotionally devastating achievements.
- **Corrupted - "El Mundo Frio"** (2005) — Seventy-one minutes of a single evolving piece. Patient beyond measure, with dynamics that shift across a massive timescale. Crushing and transcendent.
- **Nadja - "Stays Demons"** (2003) — Drone metal through a shoegaze lens. Distortion becomes beautiful, and beauty becomes heavy. A gateway track for listeners approaching drone from ambient or post-rock.
- **OM - "At Giza"** (2007) — Bass and drums create meditative drone that channels Eastern spirituality. Al Cisneros's chanted vocals and mantra-like bass lines prove heaviness can be devotional.
- **The Body - "Even the Saints Knew Their Hour of Failure and Loss"** (2010) — Noise-drone extremity with choral vocals creating an unsettling sacred/profane collision. Drone metal as confrontational art.
