# Speedcore

## Genre Overview

Speedcore is the extreme velocity wing of hardcore techno, defined by tempos that begin where most electronic music ends — 300 BPM and upward, with some productions exceeding 1000 BPM. The genre emerged in the mid-1990s from the European gabber and hardcore techno scenes, particularly in Germany, the Netherlands, and Japan, as producers pushed tempo as the primary artistic parameter. If gabber at 160-200 BPM was already confrontational, speedcore asked: what happens when you remove all restraint and let the kick drum become a continuous tone?

The genre's sonic identity is built on distorted kick drums at frequencies so rapid they blur into pitched tones, noise elements that function as both texture and rhythm, and an aesthetic commitment to extremity that borders on absurdist performance art. At the lower end (300-400 BPM), speedcore retains recognizable kick-snare patterns and can accommodate melodic elements. At the upper extremes — splittercore (600+ BPM) and extratone (1000+ BPM) — individual beats become indistinguishable, producing a wall of tonal distortion that has more in common with noise music and harsh noise wall than with dance music.

Despite its apparent simplicity, speedcore has developed a surprisingly rich culture with dedicated festivals, labels (Psychik Genocide, Epileptik), and a loyal global fanbase. Japan's speedcore scene, led by artists like m1dy and Kobaryo, brought melodic sophistication and J-core influences that expanded the genre's palette beyond pure aggression. The European scene maintained closer ties to gabber and industrial traditions. Modern speedcore intersects with breakcore, digital hardcore, and the broader "extreme music" underground, while platforms like YouTube and Bandcamp have introduced the genre to curious listeners drawn by its reputation as the fastest music on earth.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Heavily distorted kick drums (the primary instrument), noise generators, distorted synthesizers, gabber-style hoovers, occasional melodic elements (stabby synths, trance leads in J-core variants), industrial percussion, feedback, white noise sweeps; at extreme tempos, kick drums become tonal elements rather than percussive ones

- **Vocals**: Rare; when present, typically screamed, distorted beyond recognition, or sampled from extreme music sources; spoken word samples used for shock value or dark humor; J-core speedcore may include pitched anime vocal samples; clean vocals almost never used

- **Production**: Extreme compression and distortion as core aesthetic, kick drum synthesis and layering as primary craft, noise layering, minimal mixing (loudness is the goal), lo-fi production valued alongside polished approaches; at higher tempos, traditional mixing concepts become irrelevant as the kick becomes a continuous tone

- **Energy/Mood**: Overwhelming, confrontational, cathartic, absurdist; ranges from genuinely aggressive to tongue-in-cheek extreme; physical impact at live events is punishing; some listeners describe a meditative quality in the wall of sound at extreme tempos; dark humor pervades the culture

- **Structure**: Minimal; tracks may build from lower to higher BPM, layer noise elements over a constant kick pattern, or simply maintain intensity throughout; breakdowns provide brief respite; J-core variants adopt more traditional verse-chorus structures with melodic hooks; many tracks are short (2-4 minutes)

- **Tempo**: 300-1000+ BPM (core speedcore: 300-600 BPM; splittercore: 600-1000 BPM; extratone: 1000+ BPM)

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: N/A — speedcore is overwhelmingly instrumental; when text appears, it is typically shouted phrases or samples
- **Rhyme quality**: Not applicable for most productions; any vocal content prioritizes impact over craft
- **Verse structure**: No standard structure; shouts, chants, or single repeated phrases if any vocals present
- **Key rule**: This is primarily an instrumental genre. If lyrics are needed, keep them to short, high-impact phrases that can cut through extreme distortion. Single words or short exclamations work best.
- **Avoid**: Extended lyrical passages; nuanced storytelling; anything requiring vocal clarity; singing
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **1-2 lines/section** if any vocals at all. Most speedcore is instrumental. At 300+ BPM, vocals are almost impossible to articulate — use sparingly as texture, not narrative. Shouted phrases only.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Speedcore** | 300-500 BPM distorted kicks with recognizable rhythmic patterns; the most "musical" variant; retains gabber DNA | The Speed Freak, Noisekick, Stormtrooper |
| **Splittercore** | 600-1000 BPM; kicks blur into continuous tones; bridges speedcore and noise music; extreme even by genre standards | Passenger of Shit, SRB, Djipe |
| **Extratone** | 1000+ BPM; individual beats become pure tonal frequency; conceptual extreme; more noise art than dance music | Extratone producers, experimental acts |
| **J-Speedcore** | Japanese speedcore with melodic elements, trance leads, and anime aesthetics; more musical and accessible than European variants | Kobaryo, m1dy, t+pazolite, USAO |
| **Terrorcore** | Dark, aggressive variant emphasizing horror and industrial aesthetics; slower than pure speedcore (250-400 BPM); disturbing samples | Noisekick, DJ Plague, Djipe |
| **Flashcore** | Glitchy, experimental speedcore with IDM influences; rhythmic complexity beyond simple kick repetition | Passenger of Shit, experimental producers |

## Reference Tracks

- **m1dy - "Speed Ball"** — J-speedcore at its finest: melodic trance leads and stabby synths over impossibly fast kicks; proves speedcore can have hooks and musicality without sacrificing intensity; gateway track for the genre

- **Kobaryo - "Galaxy Friends"** — Modern J-speedcore with euphoric melody and anime energy over extreme tempo; demonstrates how Japanese producers brought accessibility and joy to a genre known for aggression; rhythm game classic

- **The Speed Freak - "Songs for My Funeral"** — European speedcore manifesto: distorted kicks building from merely fast to incomprehensibly rapid; dark humor in the title reflects the genre's self-aware extremity; decades of craft distilled

- **Noisekick - "The Beauty of Chaos"** — Terrorcore-adjacent speedcore: dark, pummeling, relentless; industrial textures over punishing kick patterns; captures the genre's physical impact and confrontational ethos

- **Diabarha - "Uranoid"** — Pure velocity: splittercore-adjacent production where kicks become tonal rather than percussive; demonstrates the conceptual boundary between rhythm and frequency that defines extreme speedcore

- **t+pazolite - "Cheatreal"** — Melodic J-speedcore with happy hardcore influence: uplifting despite extreme tempo; shows the genre's range from dark to euphoric; popular in rhythm gaming communities
