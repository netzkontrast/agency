# Bounce

## Genre Overview

Bounce music emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s from New Orleans' housing projects and block parties, developing as a distinctly local variant of hip-hop that drew heavily on the city's deep traditions of second line brass bands, Mardi Gras Indian chants, and call-and-response performance. The genre's foundational moment is often traced to DJ Jimi's sampling of the Showboys' "Drag Rap" (also known as "Triggaman") in 1991 — that track's distinctive drum break and vocal sample became the rhythmic backbone of virtually every bounce track that followed. The sound crystallized in the clubs and housing projects of the Magnolia, Calliope, and Melpomene neighborhoods.

Bounce's sonic identity is built on the "Triggaman" beat or similar uptempo drum patterns, call-and-response chanting, and a relentless physicality designed to make people dance — specifically, the "bounce" dance involving rapid hip and body movements. The music is intensely participatory: MCs direct the crowd with specific dance commands, call out neighborhoods and wards, and engage in extended call-and-response sessions that can last for minutes. The vocal delivery is rapid, rhythmic, and often chanted rather than rapped in a traditional hip-hop sense. Production is deliberately simple and bass-heavy, prioritizing functionality over sophistication.

Bounce has remained a vital New Orleans cultural institution for over three decades while also achieving broader recognition. Artists like Juvenile, whose "Back That Azz Up" brought bounce-influenced production to mainstream hip-hop, and Big Freedia, who became a national figure as a bounce ambassador and LGBTQ+ icon, helped the genre reach wider audiences. The sissy bounce subgenre, led by openly LGBTQ+ performers like Big Freedia, Katey Red, and Sissy Nobby, is a particularly significant cultural development — it made bounce one of the first hip-hop subgenres with prominent queer artists at its center. Post-Hurricane Katrina, bounce became even more important as a marker of New Orleans cultural identity and community resilience.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: "Triggaman" drum break or similar uptempo programmed beats, heavy 808 bass, minimal melodic content (simple synth stabs or horn hits), bass-heavy kick drums, snare rolls, cowbell patterns, claps, whistle samples; production is deliberately stripped-down to serve the vocals and dance

- **Vocals**: Rapid-fire call-and-response chanting, crowd participation is integral, dance commands ("shake," "wobble," "bounce," "twerk"), neighborhood and ward shoutouts, repetitive chanted hooks, multiple MCs trading lines, high-energy and shouted; vocal delivery is percussive and rhythmic rather than melodic; ad-libs and crowd noise often left in recordings

- **Production**: Minimal and bass-heavy, built around looped drum breaks (especially "Triggaman"), simple arrangement with emphasis on rhythm and vocals, lo-fi and raw in classic productions, designed for massive sound system playback, aggressive low-end, tracks built for DJ sets with long intros and outros; modern productions are cleaner but maintain the stripped-down aesthetic

- **Energy/Mood**: High energy, confrontational, celebratory, physically demanding; party music with New Orleans attitude; prideful and community-oriented; sexually charged; competitive (MCs and dancers); unrelenting intensity from start to finish; the energy of a packed New Orleans club at 2 AM

- **Structure**: Call-and-response sections as primary structure, chanted hooks repeated extensively, verse-hook alternation but hooks dominate, breakdown sections with isolated drums or bass for dance spotlights, extended improvised sections where MCs work the crowd; tracks often 3-5 minutes but designed for DJs to extend

- **Tempo**: 100-115 BPM

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB — simple couplets; repetitive hooks with minimal variation; call-and-response overrides formal structure
- **Rhyme quality**: Simple, hard, percussive; words chosen for chantability and crowd response over poetic quality; repetition is the primary device
- **Verse structure**: 2-4 lines per verse section between extended hooks; hooks are the centerpiece, not verses
- **Key rule**: Write for the crowd. Every line should be chantable by a room full of people. Dance commands, neighborhood shoutouts, and call-and-response prompts. Repetition is power. The MC is a conductor — the audience is the instrument.
- **Avoid**: Introspective or quiet lyrics; complex vocabulary; long verses without crowd interaction; anything that cannot be shouted over massive bass; storytelling that interrupts the party energy
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 100-115 BPM. Hook-dominant with rapid chanting. Call-and-response essential. Topics: 1/track. Dancing, partying, neighborhood pride, physical movement.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Bounce** | The foundational sound built on "Triggaman" and similar breaks; raw, bass-heavy, call-and-response driven | DJ Jimi, Partners-N-Crime, Juvenile (early) |
| **Sissy Bounce** | LGBTQ+-led bounce with same musical template but lyrics addressing queer experience, sexuality, and empowerment; culturally groundbreaking | Big Freedia, Katey Red, Sissy Nobby |
| **Mainstream Bounce** | More polished, commercially oriented productions incorporating bounce rhythms into radio-friendly hip-hop | Juvenile, Lil Wayne (some works) |
| **Post-Katrina Bounce** | Bounce music addressing Hurricane Katrina's aftermath, community resilience, and diaspora; culturally significant | Various New Orleans artists |
| **Modern Bounce** | Updated production with cleaner mixes and contemporary hip-hop influence while maintaining bounce's participatory DNA | Big Freedia (recent), Ha Sizzle |

## Reference Tracks

- **DJ Jimi - "Where They At"** — Early bounce anthem that helped establish the genre's template: "Triggaman" drums, rapid-fire call-and-response chanting, and relentless energy designed for packed New Orleans clubs

- **Juvenile - "Back That Azz Up" (feat. Mannie Fresh & Lil Wayne)** — The track that brought bounce-influenced production to the entire world; Mannie Fresh's beat channels bounce energy into a mainstream hip-hop framework; one of the most iconic Southern hip-hop tracks ever

- **Big Freedia - "Gin in My System"** — Modern bounce at its most infectious; Big Freedia's commanding vocal presence and the track's relentless groove demonstrate why bounce endures as New Orleans' essential party music

- **Cheeky Blakk - "Twerk Something"** — A bounce landmark that helped popularize the term "twerk" years before it entered mainstream vocabulary; raw, commanding, and unapologetically physical

- **DJ Jubilee - "Get Ready, Ready"** — The blueprint for bounce's participatory structure: dance commands delivered with the authority of a drill sergeant and the joy of a celebration; New Orleans party music perfected

- **Magnolia Shorty - "Monkey on the D$ck"** — Exemplifies bounce's explicit, no-holds-barred energy; Shorty's rapid-fire delivery and commanding presence made her one of the genre's most celebrated voices before her tragic death in 2010
