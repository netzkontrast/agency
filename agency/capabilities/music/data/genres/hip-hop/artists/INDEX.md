## Reference Tracks

### Brock Berrigan
- "Diamond in the Rough" (Diamond in the Rough, 2015) — Title track of his breakout album; warm jazz sample flip over boom-bap drums, samples Armando Manzanero
- "The Celebration Song" — One of his most popular tracks; upbeat, feel-good, jazzy, groovy, and irresistibly head-nodding
- "Four Walls and an Amplifier" — Fan favorite showcasing layered production: sampled textures building into a full, warm composition
- "Monument Valley" (The Narrows, 2018) — Named for the iconic desert landscape; spacious production evoking wide open desert spaces
- "Angel's Landing" (Point Pleasant, 2017) — Named for the Zion National Park trail; atmospheric, bridging the New York era and Utah influence
- "Canyonlands" (Cadillac Mountain, 2018) — At 3:01, one of his longer tracks; demonstrates how the desert landscape expanded his compositions
- "Cadillac Mountain" feat. Philanthrope (Cadillac Mountain, 2018) — Collaboration with fellow chillhop producer; Berrigan's sound meshing with the broader lo-fi community
- "Sometime" feat. Allem Iversom (The Scenic Route, 2019) — Rare vocal feature adding a different dimension to typically instrumental work
- "King of the Rats" (The Narrows, 2018) — Highlighted by Beautiful Song of the Week; standalone composition rather than background music
- "Lee's Ferry" (Paradise Canyon, 2024) — Mature composition showing a decade-plus of refined production without losing essential warmth
- "License to Chill" (The Narrows, 2018) — Peak laid-back Berrigan: smooth, easy, effortlessly cool

### Immortal Technique
- "Dance with the Devil" (Revolutionary Vol. 1, 2001) — Definitive underground storytelling track; multi-verse narrative with devastating twist ending and narrator implication
- "Peruvian Cocaine" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Multi-perspective documentary of the cocaine trade; each verse shifts to a different position in the supply chain
- "You Never Know" feat. Jean Grae (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Tragic love story proving his narrative craft extends beyond political horror
- "Industrial Revolution" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — The only "Hip Hop Quotable" earned by an unsigned artist; pure lyrical showcase
- "The 4th Branch" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Dissection of media as a tool of state power; dense with specific references to media consolidation
- "Cause of Death" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Meticulously argued political track addressing 9/11, U.S. foreign policy, and the War on Terror
- "Point of No Return" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Aggressive political manifesto; rapid-fire delivery at peak intensity
- "The 3rd World" (The 3rd World, 2008) — Title track addressing neocolonialism and resource extraction; DJ Green Lantern production
- "Rich Man's World (1%)" (The Martyr, 2011) — Narrated from the ruling class perspective; inverts his usual viewpoint to expose wealth concentration logic
- "Civil War" feat. Killer Mike, Brother Ali & Chuck D (The Martyr, 2011) — Underground solidarity gathering; heaviest voices in conscious hip-hop on one track
- "Creation and Destruction" (Revolutionary Vol. 1, 2001) — Opening track of his debut; establishes intellectual ambition from the first bars
- "Obnoxious" (Revolutionary Vol. 2, 2003) — Battle-rap ferocity applied to political content; connection between battle origins and studio delivery

### Run the Jewels
- "Blockbuster Night Part 1" (Run the Jewels 2, 2014) — Pure adrenaline braggadocio; stripped-to-the-bone industrial beat with weaponized bassline
- "Close Your Eyes (And Count to Fuck)" feat. Zack de la Rocha (Run the Jewels 2, 2014) — Signature political anthem; de la Rocha's intensity meets industrial crunch
- "Lie, Cheat, Steal" (Run the Jewels 2, 2014) — Braggadocio pivots to systemic corruption critique; signature tonal whiplash from flex to politics
- "Legend Has It" (Run the Jewels 3, 2016) — Biggest mainstream crossover; featured in Marvel's Black Panther teaser trailer
- "Walking in the Snow" (RTJ4, 2020) — Most devastating political statement; Killer Mike's "I can't breathe" bars recorded months before George Floyd's murder
- "JU$T" feat. Pharrell & Zack de la Rocha (RTJ4, 2020) — Attack on capitalism and modern-day slavery; Pharrell's hooks make radical content accessible
- "Ooh LA LA" feat. Greg Nice & DJ Premier (RTJ4, 2020) — Old-school block-party energy meets industrial production; most danceable RTJ track
- "Nobody Speak" (DJ Shadow feat. Run the Jewels, 2016) — Iconic collaboration; viral music video of brawling politicians became political satire touchstone

### Kendrick Lamar
- "Sing About Me, I'm Dying of Thirst" (good kid, m.A.A.d city, 2012) — Multi-perspective storytelling; 12-minute epic with three first-person narrators
- "m.A.A.d city" (good kid, m.A.A.d city, 2012) — Beat-switch as narrative device; production shifts mirror escalating street violence
- "The Art of Peer Pressure" (good kid, m.A.A.d city, 2012) — First-person immersion; quiet confessional delivery recounting a night of crime
- "Wesley's Theory" (To Pimp a Butterfly, 2015) — Jazz-funk production showcase; Thundercat bass, Flying Lotus production, George Clinton feature
- "Alright" (To Pimp a Butterfly, 2015) — Anthem as institutional critique; personal affirmation, protest anthem, and theological statement
- "u" (To Pimp a Butterfly, 2015) — Vocal devastation; voice cracks, slurs, whispers through drunken self-loathing monologue
- "FEAR." (DAMN., 2017) — Triptych structure; three verses across three eras (age 7, 17, 27) exploring fear's evolution
- "DUCKWORTH." (DAMN., 2017) — Nonlinear timeline; true story of Top Dawg nearly killing Kendrick's father; twist ending reframes the album
- "Mother I Sober" (Mr. Morale & The Big Steppers, 2022) — Therapeutic confession; 7-minute excavation of generational trauma
- "Father Time" feat. Sampha (Mr. Morale & The Big Steppers, 2022) — Masculinity deconstruction; examining inherited emotional limitations
- "Not Like Us" (Single, 2024) — Weaponized storytelling in battle rap; G-funk production with surgical multi-layered entendres; 5x Grammy winner
- "Wacced Out Murals" (GNX, 2024) — West Coast homecoming; mariachi-hip-hop fusion establishing the album's return-to-roots thesis
