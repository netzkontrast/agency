# Kompa

## Genre Overview

Kompa (also spelled compas, konpa, or kompa direk) is Haiti's national popular music, created in 1955 by saxophonist and bandleader Nemours Jean-Baptiste in Port-au-Prince. Jean-Baptiste sought to modernize the traditional Haitian meringue (mereng) by slowing its tempo, adding electric guitars and a structured brass section, and incorporating elements from Latin music and jazz that were circulating through the Caribbean. His ensemble, Aux Callebasses, played their first show on July 26, 1955, at a Port-au-Prince venue -- a date that has since been commemorated as the birthday of konpa. The genre's name comes from the Spanish "compas" (rhythm, beat), and its signature innovation was the tanbou (drum) pattern: a steady, mid-tempo groove driven by the bass drum on beats one and three with a syncopated hi-hat pattern that gives kompa its distinctive, hypnotic pulse. Within a few years, kompa direk had displaced traditional meringue as Haiti's dominant popular music.

The genre's first great rivalry arrived quickly. Webert Sicot, who had played alongside Jean-Baptiste, broke away to form his own ensemble and created cadence rampa -- a slightly faster, jazz-inflected variant -- setting up a competitive dynamic that drove both artists to innovate. By the 1960s, kompa had spawned dozens of bands across Haiti and was spreading through the Caribbean diaspora. Tabou Combo, formed in Petionville in 1968, became kompa's most enduring international act, touring for over five decades and carrying the genre to audiences in the US, Europe, and across the Caribbean. The 1970s-80s brought the "mini-djaz" era, when smaller, younger bands -- Skah Shah, Les Difficiles de Petion-Ville, Bossa Combo -- incorporated funk, rock, and R&B influences into kompa's framework. Michel "Sweet Micky" Martelly brought a provocative, carnival-influenced energy to the genre in the 1990s before entering politics and becoming Haiti's president (2011-2016).

In the 21st century, kompa has continued to evolve while maintaining its rhythmic core. T-Vice, formed in Miami in 1992, and Carimi brought modern R&B, hip-hop, and electronic production into kompa, expanding its audience among the Haitian diaspora in North America. The genre has also influenced and been influenced by zouk (from the French Antilles), kizomba (from Angola/Portugal), and Afrobeats. In December 2025, UNESCO inscribed Haitian compas on its Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, recognizing it as a cornerstone of Haitian national identity. Kompa's emotional power lies in its groove: the steady, warm pulse that invites close-couple dancing, its romantic lyrical tradition, and its ability to make any room feel like a Haitian dance hall regardless of geography.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Electric guitar (often playing syncopated chords or melodic hooks), bass guitar (prominent, driving the tanbou groove), keyboards/synthesizers (pads, horn emulations, melodic lines), drum kit with the signature kompa bass-drum pattern, tanbou (hand drum in traditional settings), brass section (saxophone, trumpet, trombone in larger bands), rhythm guitar; modern kompa may reduce the live instrumentation in favor of programmed beats and synths
- **Vocals**: Smooth, melodic delivery in Haitian Creole (Kreyol) or French; romantic and emotionally warm; lead vocalist often supported by backing harmonies; vocal melodies are singable and hook-driven; some kompa features spoken-word dedications or shout-outs; the vocal style prioritizes melody and groove-locking over vocal acrobatics
- **Production**: Warm, bass-forward mix with the guitar and keyboards sharing melodic duties; the kompa groove (tanbou pattern) must be felt physically -- the bass drum and bass guitar together create a low-end foundation designed for close-couple dancing; modern productions incorporate R&B and pop polish; the guitar tone is typically clean or lightly chorused
- **Energy/Mood**: Romantic, sensual, warm, and groove-driven; designed for close-couple dancing (konpa lanmou -- love kompa); can also be festive and high-energy in carnival contexts; the mood is generally positive and emotionally accessible; late-night, dance-hall atmosphere
- **Structure**: Intro (often a guitar or keyboard hook), verse-chorus form, with an extended groove section that may feature instrumental solos, vocal improvisation, or call-and-response; the groove section is where dancers settle in -- it may last several minutes in live performance; breakdowns and builds that manipulate the intensity of the tanbou groove
- **Tempo**: 100-140 BPM; classic kompa direk 100-120 BPM (mid-tempo, dance-floor sweet spot), faster kompa and carnival kompa 120-140 BPM; the groove is steady and metronomic, designed for continuous dancing

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB or ABAB in verses; choruses are melodic hooks designed for crowd singing. Many kompa songs feature a combination of Haitian Creole and French lyrics.
- **Rhyme quality**: Haitian Creole and French rhyming conventions. Melodic fit and singability matter more than rhyme density. The hook must be immediately memorable and rhythmically locked to the kompa groove.
- **Verse structure**: 4-6 lines per verse, with verses leading to a strong melodic chorus. Extended groove sections may feature repeated phrases, call-and-response, or improvised vocal lines.
- **Key rule**: Kompa is love music. The lyrics express romantic devotion, desire, longing, and celebration of the beloved. The emotional tone is warm and sincere -- this is music for couples dancing close together. Even uptempo kompa maintains emotional warmth.
- **Avoid**: Aggressive or confrontational lyrics that fight the genre's romantic, communal spirit. Overly complex or literary language -- kompa lyrics should feel natural in Kreyol or French. Ignoring the dance context -- every line must work on a dance floor.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 110-125 BPM. Smooth, melodic delivery that rides the kompa groove. Leave space for instrumental hooks and extended dance sections. Topics: 1/verse, building a romantic narrative.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Kompa Direk** | The original form created by Nemours Jean-Baptiste: mid-tempo groove, brass section, electric guitar, and the signature tanbou pattern. The foundational kompa sound from which all variants descend. | Nemours Jean-Baptiste & Ensemble Aux Callebasses |
| **Cadence Rampa** | Webert Sicot's alternative to kompa direk: slightly faster tempo, more jazz-influenced harmony, and a distinct rhythmic feel. Created a productive rivalry with Jean-Baptiste's original style. | Webert Sicot |
| **Mini-Djaz** | 1970s-80s movement of smaller, younger bands incorporating funk, rock, and R&B into kompa. More electric guitar-forward than the original brass-heavy format. Named after the smaller ensemble size compared to the big-band era. | Tabou Combo, Skah Shah, Les Difficiles de Petion-Ville, Bossa Combo |
| **Kompa Nouvel Jenerasyon** | Modern kompa incorporating R&B, hip-hop, zouk, and electronic production. Polished, contemporary sound aimed at the Haitian diaspora in North America and Europe. | T-Vice, Carimi, Alan Cave, Nu Look |
| **Carnival Kompa** | High-energy, uptempo kompa designed for Haiti's carnival celebrations. More aggressive, more call-and-response, and built for massive outdoor crowds rather than intimate dance halls. | Sweet Micky (Michel Martelly), various carnival bands |
| **Kompa Love** | Slow, romantic kompa ballads with lush keyboard arrangements, gentle guitar, and intimate vocal delivery. The genre's most explicitly romantic face, designed for slow dancing. | Alan Cave, Gracia Delva |
| **Rabòday** | Fast, percussive kompa variant popular among younger Haitian audiences. Aggressive beats, rapid-fire vocals, and street-level energy that borrows from hip-hop and dancehall. | Various emerging artists |

## Reference Tracks

- **Nemours Jean-Baptiste & Ensemble Aux Callebasses - "Ti Carole"** -- One of the foundational kompa direk recordings from the genre's creator. The track establishes the signature tanbou groove, brass arrangement, and mid-tempo pulse that would define Haitian popular music for decades.
- **Tabou Combo - "New York City"** -- Kompa's international calling card. Tabou Combo's tight ensemble playing, infectious guitar hooks, and the driving kompa groove demonstrate why the band has sustained a career spanning over five decades and brought Haitian music to audiences worldwide.
- **Tabou Combo - "Mabouya"** -- A classic mini-djaz era track that showcases the genre's ability to incorporate funk and rock energy while maintaining the kompa rhythmic foundation. The guitar work and horn arrangement capture the 1970s-80s evolution of the genre.
- **Sweet Micky - "Ou La La"** -- Carnival kompa at its most exuberant. Michel Martelly's high-energy performance and the track's driving rhythm demonstrate kompa's capacity for explosive, crowd-commanding energy beyond its romantic ballad face.
- **T-Vice - "Kite'm Viv"** -- Modern kompa that blends the traditional groove with R&B smoothness and contemporary production. The track represents the diaspora generation's evolution of the genre, maintaining kompa's dance-floor DNA while reaching for international pop accessibility.
- **Carimi - "Baby I Miss You"** -- Polished contemporary kompa that demonstrates how the genre absorbed R&B and pop influences without losing its rhythmic identity. The production quality and vocal approach target both Haitian and international audiences.
- **Webert Sicot - "Haiti"** -- Cadence rampa from kompa's first great rival. Sicot's jazz-inflected approach and slightly faster tempo provide a counterpoint to Jean-Baptiste's original style, showing the genre's breadth even in its earliest years.
- **Alan Cave - "Se Pa Pou Dat"** -- Kompa love at its most refined. Cave's smooth vocal delivery over a gentle kompa groove creates an intimate atmosphere purpose-built for slow dancing. The track exemplifies the genre's romantic core.
- **Coupé Cloué - "C'est Lè"** -- The charismatic vocalist's raspy delivery and humorous lyrics show kompa's lighter, more playful side. Coupé Cloué proved that kompa could entertain and provoke laughter as effectively as it could inspire romance.
- **Nu Look - "I'm Ready"** -- 21st-century kompa with polished production, synth-driven arrangement, and English-language lyrics targeting the diaspora. The track shows how kompa continues to evolve while maintaining the groove that makes it immediately recognizable.
