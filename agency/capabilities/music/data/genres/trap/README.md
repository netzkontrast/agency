# Trap

## Genre Overview

Trap emerged in the early 2000s from the Southern United States hip-hop scene, primarily in Atlanta, Georgia. The term "trap" derives from the slang for locations where drug deals occur, and the genre's foundational artists drew from this environment to create music that reflected street narratives, hustling culture, and survival in marginalized communities. T.I.'s "Trap Muzik" (2003) and "Urban Legend" (2004) helped codify both the sound and terminology, while producers like DJ Toomp, Shawty Redd, and Drumma Boy established the sonic template that would define the genre.

The production style that emerged was stark and hypnotic: booming 808 kick drums tuned to melodic pitches, crisp snare cracks with heavy reverb, rapid-fire hi-hat patterns (often in triplet rhythms), and dark, atmospheric synthesizers. Unlike the sample-heavy boom-bap of East Coast hip-hop, trap favored synthetic soundscapes that created a tension-filled, cinematic feel. The sparse arrangements emphasized the impact of each element, with vast empty spaces filled by rolling percussion and bass frequencies that demanded powerful sound systems.

Gucci Mane's prolific output in the mid-2000s, particularly his collaborations with Zaytoven, pushed trap toward mainstream recognition, while Jeezy's "Let's Get It: Thug Motivation 101" (2005) demonstrated the genre's commercial potential. The late 2000s and early 2010s brought a new wave: Waka Flocka Flame's aggressive delivery, Lex Luger's bombastic production style, and Future's melodic innovations began reshaping trap's boundaries. By 2012, trap had become the dominant force in hip-hop, influencing everything from pop to electronic dance music.

The genre's influence extended far beyond hip-hop. EDM producers like Flosstradamus, Baauer, and RL Grime adapted trap's rhythmic signatures for festival crowds, creating "EDM trap" or "festival trap." Meanwhile, regional variations emerged: Chicago drill (Chief Keef, Lil Durk), Brooklyn drill (Pop Smoke, Fivio Foreign), UK drill (Headie One, Central Cee), and phonk (a lo-fi Memphis-influenced variant). Today, trap's sonic elements have become so ubiquitous that they underpin much of contemporary pop production, making it arguably the most influential hip-hop subgenre of the 21st century.

## Characteristics

### Production & Sound Design
- **808 Bass**: The genre's foundation. Roland TR-808 bass drums are pitched/tuned to create melodic basslines, often with long sustain and heavy sub-bass. Distortion and saturation add grit. Typical note lengths range from short, punchy hits to sustained drones that carry through entire bars
- **Drums**: Crisp, heavily compressed snares with long reverb tails; rapid hi-hat patterns often programmed in 1/16th or 1/32nd note triplets (creating the signature "hi-hat rolls"); sparse kick patterns that let the 808 bass dominate
- **Synthesizers**: Dark, atmospheric pads; detuned leads; minor key melodies; often filtered or phased. Brass/horn stabs, string sections, and ominous bell tones are common. Arpeggiated synths and plucks add movement
- **Samples**: Sparse vocal chops, horror movie atmospheres, cash register sounds, gun cocking effects, and street ambience. Unlike boom-bap, trap rarely samples full instrumental loops
- **FX Processing**: Heavy sidechain compression on synths/bass, liberal use of reverb and delay, pitch-shifted vocal effects, vinyl crackle (in phonk), distortion on 808s

### Tempo & Rhythm
- **BPM Ranges**:
  - Classic trap: 130-145 BPM (often written as 65-72.5 BPM in half-time feel)
  - Drill: 140-145 BPM (UK drill) / 135-145 BPM (Chicago/Brooklyn drill)
  - Melodic trap: 130-160 BPM
  - Phonk: 120-140 BPM (often slower, 60-70 BPM half-time)
  - EDM trap: 140-150 BPM (full-time feel for drops)
  - Rage/Hyperpop-influenced: 150-180 BPM
- **Rhythmic Feel**: Half-time groove is essential - kicks and snares hit at half the perceived tempo while hi-hats roll rapidly above. This creates the characteristic "slow bounce" feel
- **Hi-Hat Programming**: The signature element. Patterns include 1/32nd note rolls, triplet stutters, pitch-shifted hits, volume-automated swells, and rhythmic variations that create complexity within repetition

### Vocal Approaches
- **Delivery**: Ranges from rapid-fire technical rapping to melodic singing; ad-libs are essential (including "yeah," "skrrt," "brr," gun sounds)
- **Processing**: Auto-Tune (both corrective and as creative effect), vocal doubling, delay throws, reverb tails, pitch-shifted ad-libs
- **Flow Patterns**: Triplet flows became standard after Migos' "Versace" (2013); syncopated patterns that dance around the beat rather than landing squarely on it
- **Hook Styles**: Often melodic and sung rather than rapped; repeated phrases; chant-like simplicity designed for singalong

### Lyrical Themes
- **Street Narratives**: Drug dealing, violence, survival, loyalty, betrayal, police encounters
- **Flexing/Wealth**: Jewelry, cars, designer fashion, financial success
- **Partying**: Drugs, alcohol, clubs, hedonism
- **Relationships**: Often from perspectives of distrust, transactional dynamics, or heartbreak
- **Melodic Trap Addition**: Emotional vulnerability, mental health, addiction, depression (especially post-Juice WRLD/XXXTentacion wave)

### Song Structure
- **Standard Format**: Intro (4-8 bars, often with beat-only or filtered mix) > Hook/Chorus (8-16 bars) > Verse (16-24 bars) > Hook > Verse > Hook > Outro
- **Beat Drops**: Songs often begin with reduced elements that build to a "drop" when full 808 bass enters
- **Breakdowns**: Stripped sections featuring just vocals and minimal instrumentation before beat returns
- **Length**: Typically 2:30-3:30 for streaming optimization; features often extend to 4:00+

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB (loose couplet) or XAXA. Internal rhyme and assonance over complex multisyllabics.
- **Rhyme quality**: Near rhymes and melodic flow valued over technical perfection. Triplet flows common.
- **Verse structure**: 8–16 bars. Lower syllable counts (8–10 per bar) with more space and pauses.
- **Key rule**: Melodic delivery and attitude matter more than lyrical complexity. Snare on 3rd beat.
- **Avoid**: Overly dense wordplay that fights the beat's spacious feel.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **8 lines/verse** (4 couplets). Half-time feel (130-170 BPM = 65-85 effective). At 65-70 effective: max 6 lines. Sparse vocal placement — leave room for ad-libs and 808 slides. Topics: 1-2/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | BPM Range | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Southern/Atlanta Trap** | The foundational style originating in Atlanta featuring locked-in drum patterns, melodic hooks, soulful and cinematic synths, and street narratives about trap life. Production emphasizes tension and release with heavy 808 sub-bass. | 130-145 | T.I., Gucci Mane, Jeezy, Future, 2 Chainz |
| **Chicago Drill** | Darker, more nihilistic variant emerging from Chicago's South Side. Minimal melodies, brutal synth stabs, sliding 808s, and lyrics focused on gang violence and street survival. Chief Keef and Young Chop defined the sound circa 2012. | 135-145 | Chief Keef, Lil Durk, King Von, G Herbo, FBG Duck |
| **UK Drill** | British adaptation with darker production, sliding 808 bass patterns, and rapid flows influenced by grime and road rap. Distinct snare patterns and more melodic variation than Chicago drill. Emerged around 2015. | 140-145 | Pop Smoke, Headie One, Central Cee, Digga D, Unknown T |
| **Brooklyn Drill** | New York's adoption of UK drill production (particularly sliding 808s and drill patterns) combined with local rap styles. Pop Smoke's baritone delivery became the template. Emerged circa 2019. | 140-145 | Pop Smoke, Fivio Foreign, Sheff G, Sleepy Hallow |
| **Melodic Trap** | Softens trap's aggression with sung melodies, emotional lyrics, and softer production. Bridges trap and pop/R&B, featuring vulnerable themes about relationships, mental health, and drug use. Auto-Tune used expressively. | 130-160 | Future, Juice WRLD, Lil Baby, Gunna, Young Thug, Post Malone |
| **EDM Trap/Festival Trap** | Fusion of trap rhythms with electronic dance music production for festival and club contexts. Builds tension through layers, features massive bass drops, and emphasizes instrumental sections over vocals. Emerged circa 2012. | 140-150 | Flosstradamus, RL Grime, Baauer, Yellow Claw, TNGHT |
| **Phonk** | Lo-fi, sample-heavy trap style deeply rooted in Memphis rap traditions (Three 6 Mafia, DJ Paul). Slower tempos, chopped vocal samples, cowbell patterns, vintage horror aesthetics, and deliberately degraded sound quality. | 120-140 | DJ Smokey, Soudiere, Pharmacist, Kordhell, DJ Yung Vamp |
| **Drift Phonk** | High-energy phonk variant optimized for drift/racing video content. Faster tempos, aggressive cowbell patterns, distorted 808s, chopped vocal samples, and an aesthetic tied to car culture and internet memes. | 140-165 | DVRST, Kordhell, PRXJEK |
| **Rage Trap/SoundCloud Rap** | High-energy variant with distorted synths, screamed/aggressive vocal delivery, and chaotic production. Associated with SoundCloud's late-2010s wave and hyperpop-adjacent sounds. Often blurs lines with punk and metal energy. | 150-180 | Playboi Carti, Ken Carson, Yeat, Destroy Lonely |
| **Cloud Trap** | Atmospheric, ethereal trap with hazy synths, pitched-down vocals, and dreamy soundscapes. More introspective and melancholic than mainstream trap. Associated with Sad Boys/Drain Gang aesthetic. | 130-150 | Yung Lean, Bladee, Ecco2K |
| **Trap Metal** | Hybrid combining trap production with heavy metal/hardcore elements. Distorted 808s, screamed vocals, moshpit energy. Emerged from SoundCloud around 2016. | 130-170 | Scarlxrd, Ghostemane, City Morgue, $uicideboy$ |

## Reference Tracks

For style reference when prompting:

### Foundational Trap
| Track | Artist | Context |
|-------|--------|---------|
| "24s" | T.I. | DJ Toomp production that helped establish trap's sonic template circa 2003. Features the characteristic combination of rolling hi-hats, heavy 808 bass, and dark synth pads that would define the genre. T.I.'s flow demonstrates trap's balance between aggression and swagger. |
| "Icy" | Gucci Mane ft. Jeezy | 2005 collaboration that launched both artists' careers. Zaytoven's production features bright synth melodies over trunk-rattling 808s. The track's immediate impact in Atlanta's strip clubs proved trap's commercial viability. |
| "Soul Survivor" | Jeezy ft. Akon | 2005 crossover hit that brought trap to mainstream radio. Demonstrates how trap's dark production could accommodate melodic hooks. Akon's sung chorus over Jeezy's street narratives established a template for trap's pop accessibility. |
| "I Don't Like" | Chief Keef ft. Lil Reese | 2012 track that birthed Chicago drill and changed hip-hop permanently. Young Chop's minimal, menacing production over Chief Keef's aggressive, detached delivery. The raw, nihilistic sound influenced everything that followed. |

### Melodic Trap Evolution
| Track | Artist | Context |
|-------|--------|---------|
| "Mask Off" | Future | 2017 hit showcasing Future's melodic trap mastery. The hypnotic flute sample (from Tommy Butler's "Prison Song") over minimal trap production became inescapable. Demonstrates how trap absorbed world music elements while maintaining its core identity. |
| "XO Tour Llif3" | Lil Uzi Vert | 2017 emotional trap landmark. TM88's ethereal production under lyrics about heartbreak, drug use, and suicidal ideation captured emo-trap's zeitgeist. The hook "Push me to the edge, all my friends are dead" became generationally defining. |
| "Lucid Dreams" | Juice WRLD | 2018 track that cemented emotional/emo trap as commercial force. Samples Sting's "Shape of My Heart" over trap drums for a nostalgic, melancholic atmosphere. Juice WRLD's melodic freestyling and vulnerable lyrics about heartbreak set template for post-SoundCloud trap. |
| "Drip Too Hard" | Lil Baby & Gunna | 2018 track representing modern Atlanta melodic trap at its peak. Turbo's production is bright yet hard-hitting; both rappers demonstrate the melodic flow style that dominated late 2010s. Effortless swagger over pristine trap production. |

### Drill Variations
| Track | Artist | Context |
|-------|--------|---------|
| "Dior" | Pop Smoke | 2019 track that brought UK drill to American mainstream. The 808Melo beat features sliding 808 bass, syncopated hi-hats, and dark piano melody that defined Brooklyn drill's sound. Pop Smoke's deep baritone delivery over UK-style production created a new hybrid. |
| "Know Better" | Headie One | UK drill at its finest. Features the characteristic sliding 808 patterns, minor key melodies, and rapid-fire UK flow. Production exemplifies how UK drill differentiated itself from Chicago origins with more melodic complexity. |
| "Welcome to the Party" | Pop Smoke | Brooklyn drill's breakout moment. 808Melo's production features aggressive UK drill patterns adapted for New York sensibilities. The track's success opened doors for the entire Brooklyn drill movement. |

### EDM Trap & Phonk
| Track | Artist | Context |
|-------|--------|---------|
| "Harlem Shake" | Baauer | 2012 track that sparked both a viral dance craze and established EDM trap's festival potential. The contrast between sparse verse and explosive drop showcases how trap production translated to electronic dance contexts. |
| "Core" | RL Grime | 2014 track exemplifying EDM trap's evolution. Massive bass, intricate percussion programming, and build-and-release tension designed for festival crowds. Demonstrates trap's rhythmic signatures adapted for instrumental electronic music. |
