# Southern Hip Hop

## Genre Overview

Southern hip hop encompasses a vast and diverse collection of regional styles that emerged across the American South beginning in the late 1980s and early 1990s. While New York and Los Angeles dominated hip-hop's early commercial landscape, cities like Atlanta, Houston, Memphis, Miami, and New Orleans were developing distinct sounds that would eventually reshape the genre entirely. The Geto Boys in Houston, 2 Live Crew in Miami, and OutKast in Atlanta were among the first to break through nationally, proving that the South had its own stories to tell and its own ways of telling them.

The region's diversity is its defining characteristic. Houston developed chopped and screwed (DJ Screw's slowed-down, pitched-down remix technique) and later contributed to trap's evolution through artists like UGK and Scarface. Memphis birthed the dark, lo-fi production of Three 6 Mafia and the hypnotic repetition that would influence trap and phonk. Atlanta became hip-hop's commercial capital through OutKast's genre-bending innovation, Jermaine Dupri's pop crossovers, T.I.'s trap manifestos, and eventually the trap wave that produced Future, Migos, and Young Thug. Miami's bass music tradition, from 2 Live Crew through Trick Daddy and Rick Ross, emphasized low-end frequency and party energy. New Orleans bounce music, pioneered by DJ Jubilee and Juvenile, added call-and-response chants and triggaman drum patterns.

By the mid-2000s, Southern hip hop had become the dominant commercial force in rap, a position it maintains today. The region's emphasis on melody, repetition, bass weight, and danceability proved more commercially viable than lyrical complexity alone. From crunk's club energy to trap's global takeover, the South transformed hip-hop from a primarily lyric-driven art form into a producer-driven, sonically diverse ecosystem.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Roland TR-808 drum machine (especially bass and hi-hats), synthesizers, keyboards, occasional live instrumentation; production varies dramatically by subregion — Memphis lo-fi vs. Atlanta polish vs. Houston syrup
- **Vocals**: Diverse delivery styles: drawling Southern cadences, call-and-response chants, melodic hooks, ad-lib heavy; regional accents embraced rather than hidden; sing-rap hybrids common; multiple vocal textures within single tracks
- **Production**: Bass-heavy as unifying thread across all subregions; 808 kick as foundational element; ranges from Three 6 Mafia's dark minimalism to OutKast's lush experimentation; snap music's finger snaps; crunk's aggressive stabs; trap's hi-hat rolls
- **Energy/Mood**: Ranges from hypnotic and syrupy (chopped and screwed) to explosive and aggressive (crunk); party-oriented and body-moving as default; celebratory energy even in street narratives; communal and call-and-response oriented
- **Structure**: Hook-driven with memorable, chant-like choruses; verses often shorter (12 bars) to prioritize hooks; bridge sections common; call-and-response elements; ad-libs woven throughout; less rigid structure than East Coast
- **Tempo**: Wide range: 70-95 BPM for classic Southern bounce; chopped and screwed 60-70 BPM; crunk 130-150 BPM; trap 130-160 BPM (half-time feel at 65-80 BPM); snap music 95-105 BPM

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB (couplet) — Southern hip hop favors simple, punchy rhyme schemes that support hooks and chants. AAAA (monorhyme) common in repetitive styles.
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean end rhymes prioritized. Southern rap values delivery, cadence, and phonetic impact over technical complexity. Slant rhymes and near-rhymes common and accepted.
- **Verse structure**: 12-16 bars standard. 8-bar verses common in hook-heavy and crunk tracks. Shorter verses allow more hook repetition — the hook IS the song.
- **Key rule**: The hook must be chantable. If a crowd can't yell it back, it's not Southern enough. Melody and rhythm in the chorus matter more than verse complexity.
- **Avoid**: Overly dense, cerebral lyricism that fights the groove; ignoring regional slang and cadence; hooks that are too complex to chant.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6-8 lines/verse** at 85-95 BPM. Crunk and trap: max 6 lines. Chopped and screwed: max 5 lines (slow tempo). Topics: 1-2/verse to keep energy focused. Hooks: repeat 2x minimum.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Chopped and Screwed** | Houston technique: tracks slowed to 60-70% speed, pitch-shifted down, with chopped vocal stutters; hypnotic, syrupy feel; originated by DJ Screw | DJ Screw, Slim Thug, Paul Wall, Mike Jones |
| **Crunk** | High-energy Memphis/Atlanta club music; aggressive chants, heavy 808s, call-and-response; built for crowd participation | Lil Jon, Three 6 Mafia, Ying Yang Twins, Pastor Troy |
| **Trap** | Atlanta-originated style with 808 bass, rolling hi-hats, dark synths; drug trade narratives; became hip-hop's dominant sound post-2010 | T.I., Gucci Mane, Future, Migos |
| **Bounce** | New Orleans party music; triggaman drum pattern, call-and-response chants, high energy, twerk-oriented; deeply community-rooted | DJ Jubilee, Juvenile, Big Freedia, Mannie Fresh |
| **Memphis Horrorcore** | Dark, lo-fi Memphis production; horror imagery, occult references, aggressive delivery; influenced modern phonk revival | Three 6 Mafia, Lord Infamous, Tommy Wright III, Koopsta Knicca |
| **Snap Music** | Mid-2000s Atlanta minimalism; finger snaps as percussion, simple melodies, chant hooks; brief but influential era | D4L, Dem Franchize Boyz, Soulja Boy |
| **Dirty South** | Broad umbrella for bass-heavy, hook-driven Southern party rap; regional pride, street narratives, club energy | Ludacris, T.I., Jeezy, Lil Wayne |

## Reference Tracks

- **OutKast - "ATLiens"** — Organized Noize production with spacey synths and deep bass; Andre 3000 and Big Boi showcase Atlanta's lyrical ambition; proved Southern rap could be both innovative and cerebral

- **UGK - "Int'l Players Anthem (I Choose You)"** — DJ Paul production with Three 6 Mafia collaboration; Andre 3000's iconic opening verse; the ultimate Southern hip-hop crossover moment

- **Three 6 Mafia - "Tear Da Club Up"** — Dark Memphis production, aggressive chants, crowd-moving energy; proto-crunk at its most raw; the template for high-energy Southern club music

- **DJ Screw - "June 27th" (Screw tape)** — The definitive chopped and screwed experience: slowed vocals, pitched-down beats, hypnotic repetition; Houston codeine culture in sonic form

- **Juvenile - "Back That Azz Up"** — Mannie Fresh's bouncing production with New Orleans brass influence; the track that brought bounce to mainstream America; irresistible dance energy

- **T.I. - "What You Know"** — DJ Toomp's cinematic trap production with dramatic strings; T.I.'s authoritative delivery; the track that codified trap for mainstream audiences

- **Goodie Mob - "Cell Therapy"** — Organized Noize's haunting production; paranoid Southern consciousness; CeeLo Green's distinctive voice anchoring Dungeon Family's depth
