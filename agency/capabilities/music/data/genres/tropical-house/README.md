# Tropical House

## Genre Overview

Tropical house emerged in the early 2010s as a sun-soaked subgenre of deep house, blending warm synthesizers, steel drums, marimbas, and relaxed pop vocals into an accessible, feel-good sound. The term originated as a joke by Australian producer Thomas Jack around 2013, who used it to describe his laid-back, island-inflected take on house music. Norwegian producer Kygo (Kyrre Garvoll-Dahll) became the genre's breakout star with tracks like "Firestone" (2014) featuring Conrad Sewell and "Stole the Show" (2015) featuring Parson James, which charted across more than 20 countries and established tropical house as a global phenomenon. Earlier precedents include Bob Sinclar and Yves Larock, whose mid-2000s hits carried many of the same sonic characteristics, and Klangkarussell's 2013 single "Sun Don't Shine," which helped crystallize the sound alongside emerging producers like Robin Schulz and Klingande.

The genre's commercial peak arrived between 2014 and 2017, when tropical house dominated streaming playlists and radio rotation worldwide. Robin Schulz's remixes of "Waves" and "Prayer in C" brought the sound to European charts; Felix Jaehn's remix of OMI's "Cheerleader" reached number one in multiple countries; Lost Frequencies broke through with "Are You with Me" and "Reality"; and Sam Feldt's rework of "Show Me Love" became an international hit. The sound crossed into mainstream pop through collaborations with major artists -- Kygo's 2016 debut album *Cloud Nine* featured John Legend, Foxes, and Kodaline. By 2017, Ed Sheeran's "Shape of You" borrowed heavily from the tropical house palette, marking the genre's full absorption into mainstream pop production. European producers, particularly from Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium, Norway, and Sweden, drove the movement, leveraging SoundCloud and YouTube for distribution.

By the late 2010s, tropical house's distinct identity began dissolving into broader pop and electronic production. Its signature elements -- pan flute leads, marimba hooks, shuffled percussion -- became standard tools in pop songwriting rather than markers of a specific genre. The sound's influence persists in modern chill electronic playlists, summer pop, and the production approaches of artists like Gryffin, Matoma, and Seeb. Tropical house proved that electronic music could succeed commercially without aggressive drops or high BPMs, opening the door for softer electronic subgenres and the "chill house" movement that followed.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Steel drums, marimbas, pan flutes, warm pad synthesizers, acoustic guitar, plucked string sounds (pizzicato-style), soft kick drums, bright hi-hats, shakers, tambourines, bongos, snap claps, occasional saxophone and trumpet accents; synthesized and sampled tropical timbres rather than live recordings
- **Vocals**: Soft, breathy pop vocals with minimal processing; clean or lightly reverbed delivery; often features featured guest vocalists rather than a consistent lead; vocal chops and pitched samples used as melodic elements; romantic, wistful, or carefree tone
- **Production**: Four-on-the-floor kick pattern with muted, soft kicks; sidechained pads creating a pumping rhythm; pentatonic melodies favoring major keys; minimal builds rather than EDM-style drops; smooth rolling sub-bass; clean, polished mix with airy high end; reverb and delay on melodic elements for warmth and space
- **Energy/Mood**: Uplifting, carefree, warm, sun-soaked, breezy, romantic, nostalgic; designed for casual listening rather than club intensity; evokes beaches, sunsets, travel, and summer; relaxing without being sleepy
- **Structure**: Verse-chorus with gentle builds and soft transitions; avoids hard drops in favor of filter sweeps and gradual layering; typically 3-4 minutes; intro often features signature melodic hook (marimba, pan flute, or plucked synth); bridges common for dynamic contrast
- **Tempo**: 100-120 BPM, slower than typical house music (120-130 BPM); most tracks cluster around 100-110 BPM; four-on-the-floor pulse with shuffled or straight eighth-note hi-hats

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: ABAB or AABB -- simple, singable patterns that complement the breezy melodies
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean and accessible; near-rhymes and open vowel rhymes common; nothing forced or overly clever -- lyrics serve the mood, not the wordplay
- **Verse structure**: 4-8 lines per verse; short, direct phrases; pre-chorus common to build toward the hook
- **Key rule**: Lyrics must feel effortless -- tropical house vocals should sound like a conversation on a warm evening, not a performance; simplicity is the craft
- **Avoid**: Heavy or dark subject matter, complex wordplay, aggressive delivery, dense syllable packing, overly specific storytelling -- keep it universal and impressionistic
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6 lines/verse** at 100-110 BPM. Relaxed pacing leaves space for the instrumental hooks to breathe. Topics: 1-2/verse. Choruses are short (2-4 lines) and highly repetitive.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Tropical House** | The 2013-2016 core sound: marimba leads, soft kicks, breathy vocals, SoundCloud-to-mainstream pipeline; defined the genre's commercial peak and playlist identity | Kygo, Thomas Jack, Klingande, Bakermat |
| **Tropical Pop** | Mainstream crossover where tropical house elements (steel drums, marimba hooks, island rhythms) merge with full pop songwriting and major-label production; radio-optimized | Ed Sheeran, Justin Bieber (w/ Major Lazer), Sigala |
| **Chill Tropical** | Downtempo end of the spectrum; slower BPMs, ambient pads, more atmospheric than danceable; designed for relaxation playlists and background listening | Sam Feldt (downtempo tracks), Alle Farben, filous |
| **Euro Tropical** | European-driven variant emphasizing clean production, melodic hooks, and chart-friendly vocal features; German, Dutch, and Belgian producers dominant | Robin Schulz, Felix Jaehn, Lost Frequencies, Bakermat |
| **Tropical Bass** | Heavier low-end emphasis, more prominent kick drums, dancehall and reggaeton rhythmic influence; bridges tropical house and festival EDM | Major Lazer (influence), Jax Jones, Jonas Blue |
| **Sunset House** | Atmospheric, Balearic-influenced variant prioritizing warm textures and long mixes over pop vocal hooks; closer to deep house roots; favored by Ibiza-style venues | Gryffin, ODESZA (lighter tracks), Le Youth |
| **Nordic Tropical** | Scandinavian producers blending Nordic melancholy with tropical warmth; clean, spacious production with emotional depth; prominent piano and vocal features | Kygo, Matoma, Seeb, Broiler |

## Reference Tracks

- **Kygo - "Firestone" (feat. Conrad Sewell)** -- The track that launched tropical house into the mainstream in late 2014. Conrad Sewell's soaring vocal over Kygo's signature marimba-and-piano arrangement established the template: warm, emotional, and built around a single melodic hook that never needs a drop to captivate.

- **Thomas Jack - "Rivers"** -- Early genre-defining instrumental that showcases the saxophone-heavy, acoustic-leaning side of tropical house. Demonstrates the organic, beach-party feel that Thomas Jack envisioned when the genre name was first coined.

- **Kygo - "Stole the Show" (feat. Parson James)** -- Released in March 2015, this track reached number one in Norway, France, and Sweden, proving tropical house could compete with mainstream pop. Parson James's gospel-tinged vocal against Kygo's clean production demonstrated the genre's emotional range.

- **Robin Schulz - "Prayer in C" (Lilly Wood & The Prick remix)** -- The remix that broke Robin Schulz internationally in 2014. Takes a melancholic indie-folk original and transforms it with a shuffled beat, warm bass, and subtle tropical percussion, exemplifying the European remix-driven approach to the genre.

- **Klingande - "Jubel"** -- A 2013 instrumental built around a live saxophone melody over a deep house groove with tropical percussion. One of the earliest tracks to fuse live instrumentation with the emerging tropical house sound, predating the genre's commercial explosion.

- **Felix Jaehn - "Cheerleader" (OMI remix)** -- The 2015 remix that reached number one in over 50 countries. Takes a reggae-pop original and wraps it in the full tropical house production palette: steel drum accents, bright percussion, and a bouncing four-on-the-floor groove.

- **Lost Frequencies - "Are You with Me"** -- Belgian producer's 2014 breakout, sampling a country song by Easton Corbin and transforming it into a tropical house anthem. Demonstrates the genre's ability to repurpose unexpected source material into sun-soaked dancefloor tracks.

- **Sam Feldt - "Show Me Love" (EDX Remix influence)** -- Sam Feldt's 2015 rework of the Robin S. classic brought saxophone-driven tropical house to international charts. Clean production, warm bass, and a euphoric saxophone hook exemplify the Dutch approach to the genre.

- **Kygo - "It Ain't Me" (feat. Selena Gomez)** -- A 2017 collaboration that marked tropical house's full crossover into A-list pop territory. Gomez's vocal over stripped-back tropical production proved the genre could carry major pop narratives while maintaining its identity.

- **Bakermat - "One Day (Vandaag)"** -- An early 2013 track that sampled a Martin Luther King Jr. speech over saxophone-led deep house with tropical elements. Helped establish the Dutch saxophone-tropical pipeline and showed the genre could carry emotional weight beyond beach escapism.

- **Sigala - "Easy Love"** -- A 2015 UK number-one that samples Jackson 5's "ABC" over a piano-driven tropical house beat. Demonstrates the genre's pop accessibility and its ability to recontextualize classic samples for streaming-era audiences.

- **Gryffin - "Feel Good" (feat. Daya)** -- Guitar-driven tropical house from 2016 that showcases the genre's evolution toward live instrumentation and festival-ready arrangements, bridging the gap between bedroom listening and main-stage energy.
