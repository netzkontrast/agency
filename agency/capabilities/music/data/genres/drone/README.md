# Drone

## Genre Overview

Drone music is a minimalist genre built on sustained tones, extended notes, and slowly evolving harmonic fields, emphasizing duration, texture, and overtone interaction over melody, rhythm, or conventional song structure. The genre's conceptual origins trace to La Monte Young's *Trio for Strings* (1958) and his Theatre of Eternal Music ensemble in the 1960s, where Young, Tony Conrad, John Cale, and Marian Zazeela explored the psychoacoustic properties of sustained intervals played for hours at a time. Concurrently, Yves Klein's *Monotone-Silence Symphony* (1949) had prefigured the concept with its 20 minutes of sustained orchestral drone followed by 20 minutes of silence, though the work remained largely unknown until later decades.

The influence of Young's Theatre of Eternal Music radiated outward through multiple channels. John Cale brought drone aesthetics into rock music with The Velvet Underground, whose viola drones on *The Velvet Underground & Nico* (1967) and the extended feedback explorations of "Sister Ray" introduced sustained-tone concepts to popular music audiences. In the 1970s, Brian Eno absorbed drone principles into his ambient work, while Glenn Branca's guitar orchestras pushed massed string drones toward maximal intensity. The 1980s saw Sonic Youth's Thurston Moore and Lee Ranaldo integrate drone into alternative rock through open tunings and prepared guitars. Meanwhile, Earth's *Earth 2: Special Low Frequency Version* (1993) created the drone metal template, inspiring Sunn O))), who formed as an Earth tribute act and developed their own monumental approach to amplified drone on albums like *Monoliths & Dimensions* (2009).

Contemporary drone spans an enormous aesthetic range. Stars of the Lid craft orchestral, luminous drone ambient on *The Tired Sounds of Stars of the Lid* (2001) and *And Their Refinement of the Decline* (2007). William Basinski's *The Disintegration Loops* (2002) used decaying tape loops to create one of the most emotionally resonant drone works of the 21st century. Tim Hecker pushes drone into digital noise and glitch territories, while Éliane Radigue spent decades creating single-oscillator drone works on her ARP 2500 synthesizer before transitioning to acoustic instruments. The genre continues to thrive in both academic and underground contexts, with artists like Sarah Davachi, Kali Malone, and Ellen Arkbro carrying drone into new compositional territory while maintaining its fundamental commitment to deep listening and sustained attention.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Electric guitar (often heavily amplified with extreme sustain), synthesizers (modular, ARP 2500, Moog), bowed strings (violin, viola, cello), pipe organ, tanpura, singing bowls, feedback systems, tape loops, brass instruments in sustained configurations, harmonium
- **Vocals**: Rarely present; when used, typically sustained tones, overtone singing, or heavily processed vocal drones; spoken word occasionally layered beneath the drone texture
- **Production**: Emphasis on sustain, resonance, and overtone interaction; minimal editing; long recording takes; analog warmth preferred but digital granular synthesis increasingly common; room acoustics and natural reverb often integral to the sound
- **Energy/Mood**: Meditative, hypnotic, transcendent, sometimes overwhelming or confrontational (drone metal); ranges from deeply peaceful to physically intense; demands sustained attention and rewards deep listening
- **Structure**: Non-linear; single sustained tones or slowly shifting harmonic fields; pieces can last from 10 minutes to several hours; gradual evolution rather than sections or movements; climax through accumulation rather than contrast
- **Tempo**: Effectively tempo-less; no rhythmic pulse in pure drone; drone metal variants may have extremely slow tempos of 20-40 BPM; rhythm, when present, is geological rather than musical

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: N/A — almost entirely instrumental. Drone music rarely features lyrics.
- **Rhyme quality**: Irrelevant. Vocals are textural elements, not narrative vehicles.
- **Verse structure**: No traditional structure. If text is present, it exists as sustained syllables, chanting, or sparse spoken fragments beneath the drone.
- **Key rule**: The drone itself is the content. Everything serves sustained tone and overtone interaction.
- **Avoid**: Conventional lyrics, verse-chorus structure, rhythmic vocal delivery — anything that would shift focus from the drone texture.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **0-2 lines** across the entire piece. Often fully instrumental. If vocals present, single sustained syllables or whispered fragments. Topics: 0-1 per piece.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Drone Metal** | Extremely amplified, distorted guitar drones at crushing volume; rooted in doom metal and sludge; physical, visceral experience through sustained heaviness and feedback | Earth, Sunn O))), Boris (drone works), Nadja |
| **Drone Ambient** | Gentle, luminous sustained tones; orchestral or synthesizer-based; meditative and contemplative; often beatific or melancholic in character | Stars of the Lid, Éliane Radigue, Sarah Davachi |
| **Minimalist Drone** | Academic tradition rooted in La Monte Young; pure intervals, just intonation, extended duration; focused on psychoacoustic phenomena and overtone interaction | La Monte Young, Tony Conrad, Phill Niblock |
| **Tape Loop Drone** | Drone created through degrading, layered, or manipulated tape loops; embraces entropy, decay, and the physical properties of magnetic tape as compositional material | William Basinski, Leyland Kirby, The Caretaker |
| **Guitar Drone** | Sustained tones generated specifically through electric guitar techniques: e-bow, volume swells, infinite sustain pedals, open tunings, and controlled feedback | Glenn Branca, Rhys Chatham, Rafael Toral |
| **Acoustic Drone** | Drone produced through acoustic instruments: pipe organ, bowed strings, brass, harmonium; emphasizes natural resonance and room acoustics | Kali Malone, Ellen Arkbro, Charlemagne Palestine |
| **Noise Drone** | Harsh, abrasive sustained textures; merges drone with noise music; confrontational and physically demanding listening experience | Merzbow (drone works), Prurient, Kevin Drumm |
| **Hindustani Drone** | Classical Indian tradition of sustained drone accompaniment via tanpura or shruti box; the oldest continuous drone tradition in world music | Pandit Pran Nath, La Monte Young (influenced by), raga tradition |

## Reference Tracks

- **La Monte Young - "The Second Dream of the High-Tension Line Stepdown Transformer"** — Foundational minimalist drone: sustained brass tones in just intonation creating beating frequencies and overtone interactions; demonstrates how a single sustained interval can fill an entire sonic universe

- **Tony Conrad - "Four Violins"** — Raw, unprocessed violin drones sustained for over 30 minutes; the acoustic intensity and microtonal beating patterns established the aesthetic of acoustic drone as a physical experience

- **Earth - "Seven Angels"** — From *Earth 2* (1993), the track that invented drone metal: a single riff stretched to geological timescales through extreme amplification, distortion, and sustain; the heaviest music imaginable at its slowest

- **Sunn O))) - "Alice"** — From *Monoliths & Dimensions* (2009): drone metal expanded into orchestral territory with brass, choir, and production by Steve Albini; proved drone metal could be compositionally sophisticated

- **Stars of the Lid - "Requiem for Dying Mothers, Part 2"** — Luminous drone ambient: layered strings and guitar textures creating a slowly breathing harmonic field; emotionally rich without a single rhythmic element

- **William Basinski - "d|p 1.1"** — The opening of *The Disintegration Loops* (2002): a melodic tape loop slowly degrading over an hour, magnetic particles literally falling off the tape; entropy as elegy, decay as beauty

- **Éliane Radigue - "Adnos II"** — Single-oscillator ARP 2500 drone unfolding over 70 minutes; demonstrates how infinite patience and deep listening can reveal entire worlds within a sustained tone

- **Phill Niblock - "A Trombone Piece"** — Massed trombone recordings layered to create microtonal beating patterns; acoustic drone at its most physically immersive, designed for extremely high volume playback

- **Glenn Branca - "Lesson No. 1"** — Massed electric guitars creating a drone from rhythm: repeated strummed patterns merge into a wall of overtones; the link between punk energy and drone aesthetics

- **Tim Hecker - "In the Fog III"** — From *Ravedeath, 1972* (2011): pipe organ recordings digitally processed into dissolving drone textures; where acoustic drone meets digital destruction

- **Sarah Davachi - "Perfumes III"** — Contemporary drone that bridges electronic and acoustic: organ and synthesizer tones interact in a slowly shifting harmonic field; demonstrates the genre's continued vitality

- **Kali Malone - "Sacrificial Code II"** — Pipe organ in just intonation: slow-moving intervals that exploit the physical resonance of the instrument and its architectural space; sacred music reimagined through drone aesthetics
