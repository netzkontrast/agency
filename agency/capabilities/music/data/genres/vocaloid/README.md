# Vocaloid

## Genre Overview

Vocaloid is both a voice synthesis technology and the music culture built around it. Yamaha Corporation released the first VOCALOID engine in 2003, but the revolution began with VOCALOID2 in 2007, when Crypton Future Media released Hatsune Miku on August 31, 2007 -- a software voicebank packaged with a turquoise-haired anime character design by KEI. Earlier Crypton voicebanks MEIKO (2004) and KAITO (2006) had modest sales, but Miku exploded on the Nico Nico Douga video-sharing platform, sparking a wave of amateur music production that redefined Japanese internet culture. The character-as-instrument concept -- where the software's avatar becomes a performing persona -- was Crypton's breakthrough innovation, inspiring the Kagamine Rin/Len twins (2007), Megurine Luka (2009), and hundreds of third-party voicebanks.

The Vocaloid production community grew rapidly from 2007 through the early 2010s. Pioneering producers (called "P" in the community, short for "producer") like ryo (supercell), who uploaded "Melt" and "World is Mine" in 2007-2008, and kz (livetune), whose "Tell Your World" was featured in a Google Chrome commercial, achieved mainstream recognition. wowaka brought aggressive electronic rock to the platform before his untimely death in 2019, while DECO*27 became one of the most prolific pop-rock producers. Several producers transitioned to mainstream careers: ryo produced for anime and J-pop, Kenshi Yonezu (formerly Hachi) became one of Japan's biggest solo artists, and Mitchie M achieved fame for tuning Miku's voice to sound nearly human. The culture produced its own concert format -- holographic live performances where Miku performs before thousands of fans, beginning with Animelo Summer Live in 2009.

Today, Vocaloid sits within a broader vocal synthesis ecosystem that includes Synthesizer V (Dreamtonics, with its AI-powered SynthV Studio 2 released in 2025), UTAU (free, open-source), OpenUTAU, and Crypton's own Piapro Studio. The community remains active on platforms like Nico Nico Douga, YouTube, and Piapro, and the Hatsune Miku franchise has expanded into video games (Project DIVA series), live concerts, brand collaborations, and global touring. Vocaloid music spans every genre from pop and rock to electronic, metal, jazz, and classical, unified not by a single sound but by the use of synthesized vocals as the primary voice.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Synthesized vocal lines from Vocaloid, SynthV, UTAU, or similar engines serve as the lead "singer." Backing instrumentation varies enormously by producer: electronic production (synthesizers, drum machines, sequenced bass) dominates, but acoustic instruments (piano, guitar, strings, brass) are common. Many producers are multi-instrumentalists who compose, arrange, and mix entirely in a DAW.
- **Vocals**: Synthesized singing voice with a characteristic artificial quality that ranges from obviously robotic to near-human depending on the engine, voicebank, and tuning skill. Each voicebank has a distinct tonal character (Miku: bright, youthful; GUMI: warmer, more natural; Kagamine Rin: energetic, sharp; Luka: mature, bilingual Japanese/English). Advanced tuning techniques include pitch manipulation, breath insertion, dynamics editing, and growl/falsetto simulation.
- **Production**: Typically high-density DAW production with layered synthesizers, programmed drums, and detailed mixing. Production quality ranges from bedroom demos to professional studio polish. The community values self-sufficiency: many producers handle composition, arrangement, mixing, mastering, and video creation alone. Compression, sidechain, and effects processing follow modern pop and electronic standards.
- **Energy/Mood**: Spans the full spectrum. Upbeat, hyperactive pop (kz, Mitchie M) to melancholic balladry (supercell's early work) to aggressive rock (wowaka) to ambient electronica to metal. The synthesized vocal quality can create an emotional distance that producers exploit for themes of loneliness, alienation, and digital identity, or overcome with skillful tuning to convey warmth and sincerity.
- **Structure**: Follows J-pop conventions: verse (A-melo) - verse (B-melo) - chorus (sabi) - verse - chorus - bridge (C-melo) - final chorus. Intros and outros common. Songs typically 3:30-5:00 in length. Some producers (Hachi/Kenshi Yonezu, PinocchioP) experiment with unconventional structures.
- **Tempo**: Heavily genre-dependent. Pop: 120-140 BPM. Rock: 140-180 BPM. Ballads: 70-100 BPM. Electronic/dance: 128-150 BPM. High-speed Vocaloid ("speed pop"): 180-240+ BPM, exploiting the software's ability to articulate faster than human singers.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Japanese lyrics typically use vowel-based rhyme and assonance rather than strict Western end-rhyme. Common patterns: AABB or free rhyme in verses, with chorus lines emphasizing repetition and hook phrases. English-language Vocaloid follows Western pop conventions (ABAB, AABB).
- **Rhyme quality**: Vowel rhyme (in-rhyme) is the standard in Japanese: matching final vowel sounds (e.g., -ai/-kai, -iru/-miru). Internal assonance and syllable-count matching per line are more important than Western-style consonant-based end rhyme. Wordplay and double meanings (kakekotoba) valued.
- **Verse structure**: A-melo: typically 4-8 lines. B-melo: 2-4 lines, building tension. Sabi (chorus): 4-8 lines, the emotional and melodic peak. C-melo (bridge): 2-4 lines before final chorus. Total: 40-60 lines for a full song.
- **Key rule**: The chorus (sabi) must be immediately memorable and singable. Vocaloid songs live or die by their sabi -- this is the hook that gets covered, remixed, and shared. A weak sabi kills a song regardless of verse quality.
- **Avoid**: Overly complex vocabulary that obscures the sabi hook. Lyrics that ignore the synthesized voice's articulation limitations (excessive consonant clusters in any language). Long, unbroken phrases without rhythmic variation -- the synthetic voice needs clear phrasing cues.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **6-8 lines/verse** at 130-150 BPM. Vocaloid songs tend toward higher lyric density than Western pop. Topics: 2-3/verse. For Suno, write in the target language (Japanese or English) and specify the vocal character desired. Suno cannot use actual Vocaloid voicebanks but can approximate the aesthetic.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Vocaloid Pop (VOCA-POP)** | Bright, hook-driven pop songs with polished production, catchy sabi melodies, and upbeat energy; the most commercially accessible Vocaloid style; often features Miku or GUMI; emphasis on vocal tuning quality and mainstream appeal. | kz (livetune), Mitchie M, DECO*27, HoneyWorks |
| **Vocaloid Rock (VOCA-ROCK)** | Guitar-driven rock with synthesized vocals; ranges from melodic pop-rock to aggressive post-hardcore; distorted guitars, driving drums, and energetic vocal lines; one of the most popular subgenres on Nico Nico Douga. | wowaka, DECO*27, Neru, 40mP |
| **High-Speed/Denpa** | Extremely fast tempos (180-240+ BPM) exploiting the software's superhuman articulation speed; dense, hyperactive production; often playful or chaotic in mood; a distinctly Vocaloid phenomenon impossible with human singers. | Hachi (Kenshi Yonezu), cosMo@Bousou-P, sasakure.UK |
| **Ballad/Emotional** | Slower, piano-driven songs focusing on melancholic or bittersweet themes; careful vocal tuning to maximize emotional expression; string and acoustic guitar arrangements common; tests the producer's ability to make a synthetic voice convey genuine feeling. | supercell (ryo), Doriko, 40mP, Orangestar |
| **Electronic/EDM** | Synthesizer-heavy production drawing from trance, house, drum and bass, and dubstep; the synthetic vocal quality integrates naturally with electronic textures; often features heavy vocal processing (vocoder, filter sweeps, glitch editing). | kz (livetune), Nem, Eight, sasakure.UK |
| **Metal/Screamo** | Heavy guitar riffs, blast beats, and aggressive production paired with Vocaloid vocals tuned for growling or screaming effects; a niche but dedicated community; pushes the software's expressive range to extremes. | Utsu-P, Kikuo, Devilish-P |
| **Experimental/Art** | Avant-garde compositions using Vocaloid as a sound design tool rather than a conventional singer; abstract structures, noise elements, and unconventional vocal tuning; the artistic fringe of the community. | PinocchioP, Hachi (Kenshi Yonezu), Kikuo |

## Reference Tracks

- **supercell (ryo) feat. Hatsune Miku - "World is Mine"** -- Uploaded in 2008, this pop track established the template for character-driven Vocaloid music, with Miku cast as a demanding "princess." It was among the first Vocaloid songs to reach one million views on Nico Nico Douga and remains one of the most covered and remixed songs in the community.

- **supercell (ryo) feat. Hatsune Miku - "Melt"** -- Posted to Nico Nico Douga in December 2007, "Melt" is a gentle love song that demonstrated Vocaloid could convey genuine emotion. Its success (over 14 million views on NND) proved the platform's viability for full-length pop songwriting.

- **kz (livetune) feat. Hatsune Miku - "Tell Your World"** -- Featured in a 2012 Google Chrome commercial broadcast internationally, this electro-pop track brought Vocaloid to a global audience outside the existing fanbase. Its uplifting synth production and accessible melody represent VOCA-POP at its most polished.

- **wowaka feat. Hatsune Miku - "Rolling Girl"** -- A 2010 rock track about struggling against social pressure, with driving guitars and Miku's voice pushed to its expressive limits. wowaka's intense, high-tempo style influenced a generation of Vocaloid rock producers.

- **DECO*27 feat. Hatsune Miku - "Rabbit Hole"** -- One of DECO*27's modern hits, demonstrating his evolution from early pop-rock into a more electronic, hook-driven sound. His consistent output over 15+ years makes him one of the community's most reliable and recognizable producers.

- **Hachi (Kenshi Yonezu) feat. Hatsune Miku - "Matryoshka"** -- A 2010 collaboration between Hachi and GUMI/Miku that combined experimental electronic production with surreal, cryptic lyrics. It went massively viral and presaged Yonezu's transition to a solo career as one of Japan's biggest pop artists.

- **cosMo@Bousou-P feat. Hatsune Miku - "The Disappearance of Hatsune Miku"** -- An extreme high-speed track (240+ BPM) that became a defining technical showcase for both the software and the rhythm game community. It exemplifies Vocaloid's ability to produce music physically impossible for human performers.

- **Mitchie M feat. Hatsune Miku - "Viva Happy"** -- Mitchie M's meticulous vocal tuning makes Miku sound nearly human, with natural breath, dynamics, and articulation. The track became a benchmark for production quality in the community and a popular rhythm game staple.

- **PinocchioP feat. Hatsune Miku - "God-ish"** -- A 2020 viral hit that uses bouncy pop production to deliver sharp social commentary about internet culture and identity. PinocchioP's ability to embed serious themes in catchy music represents the artistic maturity of the Vocaloid community.

- **Orangestar feat. IA - "Asu no Yozora Shoukaihan"** -- A 2014 uptempo pop track with soaring melodies and youthful energy that became one of the most-covered Vocaloid songs of its era. Its emotional directness and memorable chorus exemplify the community's post-2012 generation.

- **Kikuo feat. Hatsune Miku - "Love Me, Love Me, Love Me"** -- Beneath a deliberately cute, music-box aesthetic lies deeply unsettling content about obsessive love. Kikuo's ability to create dissonance between surface sound and lyric meaning represents the experimental edge of Vocaloid music.

- **ryo (supercell) feat. Hatsune Miku - "Black Rock Shooter"** -- Released in 2008, this rock track spawned an entire multimedia franchise including anime, manga, and games. It demonstrated that Vocaloid songs could launch creative properties beyond music, establishing the producer-as-IP-creator model.
