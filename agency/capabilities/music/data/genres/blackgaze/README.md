# Blackgaze

## Genre Overview

Blackgaze emerged in the mid-2000s as a fusion of black metal's intensity with shoegaze's ethereal beauty, creating a genre that channels tremolo-picked guitars and blast beats toward transcendence rather than aggression. The genre's origin traces to French musician Neige, who released Alcest's *Le Secret* EP in 2005 -- a two-track recording that combined Burzum-style black metal with the dreamy textures of Cocteau Twins and My Bloody Valentine. That EP, pressed in just 1,000 copies, planted the seed. Alcest's full-length debut *Souvenirs d'un Autre Monde* (2007) abandoned harsh vocals almost entirely, fully realizing the blackgaze vision: tremolo picking reframed as shimmering melody, blast beats as propulsive shimmer, and reverb-drenched production creating a wall of luminous sound.

While Alcest defined blackgaze's template, the genre gained wider recognition through several parallel developments. Neige's side project Amesoeurs blended post-punk and black metal on their 2009 self-titled album. Germany's Lantlos, also featuring Neige, explored similar territory on *. neon* (2010). But the genre's mainstream breakthrough came with Deafheaven's *Sunbather* (2013), which landed on year-end lists from Pitchfork to NPR, introducing blackgaze to audiences far beyond metal. George Clarke's shrieked vocals over Kerry McCoy's soaring tremolo riffs and post-rock dynamics proved that extreme metal could be genuinely beautiful without sacrificing intensity. The album polarized metal purists but undeniably expanded the genre's reach.

The modern blackgaze scene is global and diverse. Japanese act Mono has increasingly incorporated blackgaze elements alongside their post-rock foundation. Violet Cold from Azerbaijan blends electronic textures with blackgaze's core sound. MoL from Denmark and Unreqvited from Canada push the genre in progressive and atmospheric directions respectively. Deafheaven themselves moved toward pure shoegaze on *Infinite Granite* (2021) before returning to heavier territory. The genre's influence now extends into post-metal, atmospheric black metal, and even indie rock, with its core tension between beauty and extremity continuing to generate compelling music.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Electric guitars (heavily reverbed and layered, tremolo picking as primary technique), bass guitar, drums (blast beats and double bass alongside more restrained patterns), keyboards and synthesizers for ambient textures, occasional acoustic guitar. Guitar pedals (reverb, delay, chorus, tremolo) are essential to the shoegaze wall-of-sound aesthetic.
- **Vocals**: Harsh shrieked vocals (black metal tradition) contrasted with clean singing, ethereal vocals, or whispered passages. Some tracks shift between all three. Vocals are often mixed into the wall of sound rather than sitting on top, blurring intelligibility into texture.
- **Production**: Reverb-heavy, lush, and layered. Guitars are stacked to create dense, shimmering walls of sound. Treble-forward but warmer than traditional black metal. Bass and drums provide foundation beneath the guitar wash. Cleaner and more polished than raw black metal, but not sterile -- the haze is intentional.
- **Energy/Mood**: Bittersweet, yearning, melancholic, euphoric. Emotional intensity through beauty rather than aggression. Cathartic crescendos. Nostalgia and longing are recurring emotional states. The juxtaposition of harsh vocals against beautiful instrumentation creates the genre's defining tension.
- **Structure**: Dynamic shifts between quiet atmospheric passages and full-band eruptions. Songs typically 5-10 minutes. Post-rock-influenced builds. More structured than atmospheric black metal but less conventional than standard rock forms. Instrumental passages and ambient interludes common.
- **Tempo**: 100-170 BPM. Blast beat sections: 140-170 BPM. Shoegaze-influenced passages: 100-130 BPM. Mid-tempo grooves: 110-140 BPM. Tempo changes within songs are standard, often shifting between driving blast sections and swaying half-time passages.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Loose or absent. Emotional resonance matters more than formal rhyme. Free verse and impressionistic phrasing dominate.
- **Rhyme quality**: When present, slant rhyme, assonance, and vowel matching preferred. The vocals are often partially obscured by harsh delivery or reverb, so sonic quality of words matters more than end-rhyme precision.
- **Verse structure**: Variable. Shorter phrases during blast sections (shrieked delivery limits syllable density), longer flowing lines during clean passages. 4-8 lines per section typical.
- **Key rule**: Evoke feeling, not narrative. Abstract imagery -- light, water, memory, seasons, childhood, dreams. Personal and introspective. The lyrics should feel like fragments of emotion rather than complete stories.
- **Avoid**: Aggressive or violent imagery (this is not black metal's domain here). Gore, Satanism, or misanthropy. Overly literal or prosaic language. Forced rhyme that disrupts the dreamlike flow.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 120-160 BPM. Harsh vocal sections: fewer words, let the wall of sound breathe. Clean sections: more melodic phrasing. Instrumental breaks are essential. Topics: 1-2/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Classic Blackgaze | The foundational sound: black metal tremolo picking and blast beats fused with shoegaze reverb, ethereal melodies, and dreamy atmospheres. Clean and harsh vocals alternate. Alcest's original vision. | Alcest, Les Discrets, Amesoeurs, Lantlos |
| Post-Black / Blackgaze | Heavier variant that retains more black metal intensity while incorporating post-rock dynamics and shoegaze textures. Harsh vocals more prominent. Greater emphasis on crescendo-based song structures. | Deafheaven, Wolves in the Throne Room, Oathbreaker, MoL |
| Atmospheric Blackgaze | Emphasizes ambient and atmospheric elements over intensity. Longer, more meditative compositions with extended clean passages. Synthesizers and ambient textures play a larger role. | Unreqvited, Sadness, Sylvaine, An Autumn for Crippled Children |
| Electronic Blackgaze | Incorporates electronic production, synth-wave textures, and programmed beats alongside traditional blackgaze instrumentation. Modern and genre-blending. | Violet Cold, Astronoid, Heretoir, Bosse-de-Nage |
| Depressive Blackgaze | Drawing from DSBM's emotional extremity but channeled through shoegaze beauty rather than raw aggression. Deeply melancholic, with themes of grief, isolation, and yearning. | Thy Light, Apati, ColdWorld, Totalselfhatred |
| Progressive Blackgaze | Complex song structures, unusual time signatures, and progressive rock influence within the blackgaze framework. More technically ambitious and compositionally adventurous. | MoL, Harakiri for the Sky, White Ward, Liturgy |

## Reference Tracks

- **Alcest - "Ecailles de Lune, Part 1"** (2010) — Blackgaze perfection. Neige's tremolo picking serves ethereal beauty rather than aggression. Clean vocals, shoegaze textures, and childhood nostalgia create genuine transcendence from black metal's building blocks. The genre's purest expression.
- **Alcest - "Autre Temps"** (2012) — From *Les Voyages de l'Ame*. Almost entirely clean, with gentle guitar melodies and Neige's fragile singing. Demonstrates how far blackgaze can travel from black metal while retaining its emotional core.
- **Deafheaven - "Dream House"** (2013) — *Sunbather*'s explosive opener. Kerry McCoy's tremolo riffs and George Clarke's screams over post-rock dynamics brought blackgaze to audiences who had never heard of Alcest. The pink album cover alone signaled a different kind of black metal.
- **Deafheaven - "Sunbather"** (2013) — The title track's interlude of spoken word and piano gives way to triumphant guitar crescendos. A masterclass in using dynamic contrast to generate overwhelming emotional impact.
- **Amesoeurs - "Les Ruines Humaines"** (2009) — Post-punk coldness meets black metal tremolo. Audrey Sylvain's clean vocals against blast beats create an eerie, urban atmosphere distinct from Alcest's pastoral dreamscapes.
- **Les Discrets - "Song for Mountains"** (2010) — Gentle post-rock melodies build into blackgaze intensity. Autumnal atmosphere and visual art sensibility make this a complete aesthetic experience.
- **Lantlos - ".neon"** (2010) — Title track from the album that expanded blackgaze's emotional vocabulary. Urban melancholy and neon-lit imagery replace nature themes, pointing toward the genre's future diversity.
- **MoL - "Jord"** (2018) — Danish precision meets blackgaze emotion. Technical guitar work and progressive structures demonstrate the genre's capacity for compositional sophistication.
- **Sylvaine - "Abeyance"** (2018) — Solo artistry at its most intimate. Clean and harsh vocals from the same throat, layered over atmospheric guitar work that bridges blackgaze and ambient.
- **Harakiri for the Sky - "Funeral Dreams"** (2014) — Austrian blackgaze at its most emotionally direct. Soaring post-hardcore melodies and black metal intensity merge into cathartic release.
- **Wolves in the Throne Room - "I Will Lay Down My Bones Among the Rocks and Roots"** (2007) — Eighteen minutes of cascading tremolo and nature worship. The Cascadian scene's intersection with blackgaze, where atmospheric black metal and shoegaze textures dissolve into each other.
- **Violet Cold - "Noir Kid"** (2020) — Electronic beats, hip-hop samples, and blackgaze tremolo collide. Proof that the genre's future lies in fearless hybridization.
