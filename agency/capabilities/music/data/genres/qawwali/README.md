# Qawwali

## Genre Overview

Qawwali is a form of Sufi devotional music originating in the Indian subcontinent during the 13th century. The word derives from the Arabic "qaul" (utterance), and the tradition is attributed to Amir Khusrau (1253-1325), a poet, musician, and spiritual disciple of the Sufi saint Hazrat Nizamuddin Auliya in Delhi. Khusrau fused Persian, Arabic, Turkic, and North Indian musical traditions into a devotional vocal form designed to induce states of spiritual ecstasy (wajd or hal) in both performers and listeners. Qawwali developed within the Chishti Order of Sufism, whose saints regarded music (sama) as a legitimate path to divine communion — a position that placed them at odds with more orthodox Islamic scholars who viewed music with suspicion. For centuries, qawwali remained tied to Sufi shrines (dargahs) across South Asia, performed at the tombs of saints during urs (death anniversary) celebrations and weekly gatherings, with repertoire passed down through hereditary lineages of professional qawwal families.

The tradition's global breakthrough came through Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan (1948-1997), a Pakistani vocalist from a qawwal family lineage spanning over 600 years. Nusrat took over his family's qawwali party in 1971 after the death of his uncle Mubarak Ali Khan and spent the 1970s and early 1980s releasing hundreds of cassettes across Pakistan and India, building a reputation for extraordinary vocal range, stamina, and improvisational daring. His international career began at the 1985 WOMAD festival, organized by Peter Gabriel, and accelerated through a series of albums on Gabriel's Real World Records — most notably *Mustt Mustt* (1990), produced with Michael Brook, which reached No. 14 on the Billboard World Music chart and which British musician Nitin Sawhney described as having "changed the face of British music forever." Nusrat recorded 125 albums over his career (a Guinness World Record for a qawwali artist), collaborated with Eddie Vedder on the *Dead Man Walking* soundtrack, and was named among NPR's 50 Great Voices and Rolling Stone's 200 Greatest Singers of All Time. His ability to stretch a single qawwali performance across hours of escalating intensity while maintaining absolute vocal control remains unmatched.

Modern qawwali exists along a spectrum from strict traditional practice to full-blown fusion. At the traditional end, hereditary qawwal families — the Sabri Brothers, Rizwan-Muazzam (Nusrat's nephews), the Warsi Brothers — continue performing at shrines and concert halls with the core ensemble of harmonium, tabla, dholak, and hand clapping. At the fusion end, Pakistan's Coke Studio television series has become a laboratory for qawwali hybridization, pairing qawwals with rock, pop, and electronic producers — Rahat Fateh Ali Khan's Coke Studio collaborations and Rizwan-Muazzam's appearances have introduced the form to younger audiences across South Asia. Abida Parveen, often called the "Queen of Sufi Music," has expanded the tradition's gender boundaries, performing a related but distinct Sufi vocal repertoire rooted in the kafi poetry tradition of Sindh and Punjab. In the West, groups like Riyaaz Qawwali (based in the United States) perform traditional repertoire for diasporic and non-South Asian audiences. The devotional core remains: qawwali is music whose purpose is to dissolve the boundary between lover and Beloved, human and divine.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Harmonium (hand-pumped reed organ providing melodic drone and accompaniment, typically played by the lead singer or a dedicated player), tabla (tuned drum pair for rhythmic articulation), dholak (double-headed barrel drum for heavier rhythmic drive), hand clapping by the chorus (sustained throughout the performance as rhythmic reinforcement); some ensembles add sarangi (bowed fiddle) or sitar for melodic color; modern fusion settings may incorporate guitar, bass, synthesizer, or drum kit, but traditional qawwali deliberately limits instrumentation to foreground the voice
- **Vocals**: The defining element — powerful, ornamented singing with extensive use of taans (rapid melodic runs), meend (gliding between notes), and sargam (solmization passages where the singer improvises using note names); the lead vocalist (main qawwal) drives the performance while one or two side singers echo, harmonize, or provide call-and-response; the chorus (humnawa) reinforces refrains and claps; vocal stamina is paramount, as traditional performances can extend for hours; the voice builds from controlled, meditative phrases in the opening to full-throated ecstatic delivery at the climax
- **Production**: Traditional qawwali recordings prioritize the live ensemble sound with minimal studio processing — close-miked vocals, natural room ambience, and the physical sound of hand clapping audible in the mix; fusion recordings (Mustt Mustt, Night Song, Coke Studio sessions) layer electronic textures, reverb, and Western instrumentation around the vocal core; the harmonium's reedy sustain functions as a continuous melodic drone beneath the voice
- **Energy/Mood**: Structured escalation from contemplative calm to overwhelming intensity; performances begin slowly and quietly, building through repetition, rhythmic acceleration, and vocal improvisation toward a peak of spiritual ecstasy; the emotional register centers on divine love, longing, and surrender — the pain of separation from the Beloved and the joy of union; audience participation (swaying, calling out, entering trance states) is expected and encouraged, not incidental
- **Structure**: A traditional qawwali follows a loose but recognizable form — an introductory alap (unmetered vocal improvisation), followed by the main composition built on a poetic text with a recurring refrain (the nagma or asthai); the lead singer improvises extensively between returns to the refrain, incorporating sargam passages, taans, and spontaneous textual variations; there is no fixed song length — performances expand or contract based on audience response and spiritual momentum; in a full mehfil (gathering), the sequence follows a prescribed thematic order: hamd (praise of God), naat (praise of the Prophet Muhammad), manqabat (praise of saints), and then ghazal or kafi poetry exploring divine love
- **Tempo**: Begins slowly, typically around 60-80 BPM during the opening alap and initial verses; accelerates through the body of the performance to 120-160 BPM during climactic sections; the rhythmic acceleration (known as the qaul section building to a jhala-like frenzy) is central to inducing ecstatic states; hand clapping shifts from a slow pulse to rapid, driving patterns that push both performers and listeners toward transcendence

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Based on Urdu/Persian ghazal and kafi poetic forms; ghazal-derived texts use the matla (opening couplet with both lines rhyming) followed by independent couplets (sher) where only the second line rhymes with the matla's rhyme scheme (AA BA CA DA); kafi poetry uses a refrain (tek) with verses (bait) that return to the refrain after each stanza
- **Rhyme quality**: Rich, multisyllabic end rhymes drawn from Urdu, Persian, and Punjabi poetic traditions; the radif (repeating word or phrase at the end of each couplet's second line) and qafia (the rhyming word before the radif) are structural requirements in ghazal-based texts; slant rhyme is rare in classical texts but appears in modern compositions
- **Verse structure**: Highly repetitive by design — the refrain (asthai/nagma) returns after each verse section; individual couplets are repeated multiple times with increasing intensity before the singer moves to the next; repetition is functional, not filler — each return deepens the meditative and ecstatic effect; the lead singer may repeat a single line dozens of times, varying ornamentation, dynamics, and emotional weight with each iteration
- **Key rule**: Lyrics serve as a vehicle for spiritual transport, not narrative storytelling; the text is a launching pad for vocal improvisation, and the most powerful moments in a qawwali often occur when the singer departs from the written lyric entirely into sargam or spontaneous devotional exclamation; the words must be sturdy enough to bear extreme repetition without losing meaning
- **Avoid**: Literal or prosaic language that resists the metaphorical layering central to Sufi poetry (the Beloved is simultaneously human lover and God, wine is spiritual intoxication, the tavern is the shrine); forced Western pop structure that eliminates the gradual escalation and open-ended form; trivializing the devotional register by treating qawwali as merely "energetic world music"
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 90-110 BPM for a mid-tempo qawwali with strong repetition of the refrain. Slow devotional: **2-4 lines** at 70-85 BPM with extended melodic phrasing. Uptempo ecstatic: **4-6 lines** at 120-140 BPM with driving hand-clap rhythm. Themes: divine love, spiritual longing, praise of saints and prophets, the paradox of separation and union.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Traditional Shrine Qawwali** | The foundational form performed at Sufi dargahs during urs celebrations and weekly gatherings; strictly acoustic with harmonium, tabla, dholak, and hand clapping; repertoire drawn from centuries-old Persian, Urdu, and Punjabi devotional texts; performances can last hours, with the qawwal responding to the spiritual state of the audience | Sabri Brothers, Bahauddin Qawwal, Meraj Ahmed Nizami |
| **Hamd** | Qawwali in praise of Allah; traditionally opens any formal qawwali gathering (mehfil); tends toward the contemplative and majestic, with slower tempos and restrained ornamentation in the opening before building intensity | Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, Aziz Mian |
| **Naat** | Praise of the Prophet Muhammad; follows hamd in the traditional sequence; emotional register ranges from tender devotion to ecstatic celebration; "Tajdar-e-Haram" by the Sabri Brothers is the most recognized example | Sabri Brothers, Amjad Sabri, Rahat Fateh Ali Khan |
| **Manqabat** | Praise of Sufi saints, particularly Ali ibn Abi Talib and the founders of Sufi orders; "Man Kunto Maula" (attributed to Amir Khusrau) is the archetypal manqabat and remains a staple of qawwali repertoire across all lineages | Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, Sabri Brothers, Abida Parveen |
| **Kafi** | Devotional poetry in Punjabi and Sindhi drawing on the romantic-mystical verse of poets like Shah Hussain, Bulleh Shah, and Shah Abdul Latif Bhittai; folk-inflected melodies distinct from the Persian-Urdu classical qawwali tradition; strong association with Sindhi and Punjabi Sufi culture | Abida Parveen, Pathanay Khan |
| **Ghazal Qawwali** | Urdu ghazal poetry (secular love poetry with spiritual double meanings) performed in qawwali style with full ensemble and escalating intensity; bridges the devotional and romantic registers; Aziz Mian was known for extending single ghazals into hour-long performances | Aziz Mian, Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, Rahat Fateh Ali Khan |
| **Fusion Qawwali** | Qawwali vocals and compositional structures combined with Western instrumentation and production; pioneered by Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan's collaborations with Michael Brook and Peter Gabriel on Real World Records; maintains the vocal intensity and repetitive structure while adding guitar, bass, synthesizer, and studio processing | Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, Rizwan-Muazzam |
| **Coke Studio Qawwali** | Television-produced qawwali collaborations blending traditional qawwali with pop, rock, and electronic elements; Pakistan's Coke Studio has been the primary platform since 2008; arrangements are shorter (5-10 minutes vs. traditional 30-60+ minutes) and structured for broadcast | Rahat Fateh Ali Khan, Rizwan-Muazzam, Amjad Sabri |
| **Bollywood/Filmi Qawwali** | Qawwali adapted for Hindi cinema, typically shortened to 5-7 minutes with choreographed dance sequences; the devotional text is often repurposed for romantic expression; "Khwaja Mere Khwaja" from *Jodhaa Akbar* (2008, A.R. Rahman) is a notable example that maintained genuine devotional weight | A.R. Rahman, Rahat Fateh Ali Khan |
| **Bangla Qawwali** | Bengali-language qawwali tradition centered in Bangladesh and West Bengal; draws on the Sufi traditions of the Bengal region with Bengali poetic texts; less internationally known but maintains its own distinct lineage and repertoire | Various regional performers |

## Reference Tracks

- **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan - "Allah Hoo"** — The quintessential opening piece of a Nusrat concert; a philosophical meditation on the name of God that begins as a simple invocation and builds through layered repetition and vocal improvisation into a state of ecstatic intensity; demonstrates the core qawwali principle of using repetition as a vehicle for transcendence

- **Sabri Brothers - "Tajdar-e-Haram" (1982)** — The most widely recognized naat in qawwali history; a plea to the Prophet Muhammad for grace and mercy, written by Payam Saihalwi and recorded for the Pakistani film *Sahaaray*; the Sabri Brothers' choral delivery and building intensity established the template for devotional qawwali in popular consciousness

- **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan - "Mustt Mustt" (1990, produced by Michael Brook)** — The track that opened Western ears to qawwali; Nusrat's vocal power set against Michael Brook's guitar textures and electronic production on Peter Gabriel's Real World Records; the title means "intoxicated" in Punjabi — Sufi metaphor for spiritual ecstasy

- **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan - "Yeh Jo Halka Halka Suroor Hai"** — A romantic-mystical qawwali where the "intoxication" described is simultaneously earthly desire and divine love; demonstrates the characteristic Sufi double meaning where every love lyric operates on both human and spiritual registers

- **Aziz Mian - "Hashr Ke Roz Yeh Poochhunga"** — The longest commercially released qawwali at over 115 minutes; Aziz Mian's marathon improvisation on a single philosophical theme demonstrates the form's capacity for extended ecstatic performance; the intensity never drops across nearly two hours

- **Sabri Brothers - "Bhar Do Jholi Meri"** — A supplication (dua) asking for divine generosity; one of the Sabri Brothers' most beloved compositions, later performed by Amjad Sabri and adapted by A.R. Rahman for the Bollywood film *Bajrangi Bhaijaan* (2015); illustrates how qawwali repertoire passes through generations

- **Abida Parveen - "Tere Ishq Nachaya"** — A kafi by Bulleh Shah performed with Abida's characteristic contralto power; demonstrates the Punjabi Sufi poetry tradition's folk-inflected approach to ecstatic devotion, distinct from the Persian-Urdu classical qawwali mainstream

- **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan - "Tumhe Dillagi Bhool Jani Padegi"** — A ghazal by Purnam Allahabadi performed in qawwali style; the text operates as romantic poetry on the surface while carrying Sufi undertones of spiritual surrender; later re-recorded by Rahat Fateh Ali Khan in a contemporary arrangement

- **Amjad Sabri & Rahat Fateh Ali Khan - "Aaj Rang Hai" (Coke Studio Season 9)** — A collaborative performance pairing two qawwali lineages on Pakistan's Coke Studio; demonstrates how the television format compresses traditional qawwali's extended form into a structured arrangement while preserving the escalating energy and call-and-response vocal interplay

- **A.R. Rahman - "Khwaja Mere Khwaja" from *Jodhaa Akbar* (2008)** — A rare Bollywood qawwali that maintains genuine devotional weight rather than repurposing the form for romance; Rahman's orchestration layers strings and electronic textures beneath traditional qawwali vocals; the text is a manqabat praising Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti, the founder of the Chishti Order in India

- **Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan - "Man Kunto Maula"** — Attributed to Amir Khusrau himself, this manqabat praising Ali ibn Abi Talib is among the oldest continuously performed pieces in the qawwali repertoire; Nusrat's version builds from a meditative opening to a furious climax, showcasing the full dynamic range of the form

- **Fareed Ayaz & Abu Muhammad - "Kangna Moray"** — A traditional qawwali performed with strict adherence to classical form by artists whose lineage traces directly to Amir Khusrau's tradition; demonstrates the unbroken connection between 13th-century Sufi musical practice and contemporary performance
