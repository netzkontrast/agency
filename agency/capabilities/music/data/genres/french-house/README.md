# French House

## Genre Overview

French house emerged from the Parisian club scene in the mid-1990s, rooted in a generation of French DJs and producers who grew up on American disco, Italo-disco, and early Chicago house. Thomas Bangalter's solo releases on his Roulé label in 1995-1996 were among the earliest examples of the sound, alongside Motorbass (Philippe Zdar and Étienne de Crécy), whose 1996 album *Pansoul* pioneered the technique of building entire tracks from filtered disco loops. Daft Punk's 1997 debut *Homework* brought the style international recognition, while DJ Falcon, Cassius, and Alan Braxe developed the sound further through a tight-knit Parisian scene centered around clubs like the Rex and labels like Roulé, Crydamoure, and Solid.

The genre's commercial breakthrough came in 1997-1998 with a rapid succession of crossover hits. Daft Punk's "Around the World" reached charts worldwide in 1997, Cassius scored a UK Top 10 with "Cassius 1999," and Stardust's "Music Sounds Better with You" (a collaboration between Thomas Bangalter, Alan Braxe, and vocalist Benjamin Diamond) became the defining single of summer 1998. Modjo's "Lady (Hear Me Tonight)" extended the wave into 2000, sampling Chic's "Soup for One" through the characteristic French house filter. Air, Dimitri from Paris, and Bob Sinclar expanded the movement's sonic range from downtempo lounge to full dancefloor energy. The UK press labeled the broader movement "French touch," though purists distinguish the filter-heavy dancefloor sound (French house) from the more eclectic French electronic scene.

French house's influence remains embedded in modern electronic music. Daft Punk's 2001 album *Discovery* and 2013's *Random Access Memories* carried French house aesthetics into pop and nu-disco territory, while Justice's 2007 debut *†* pushed the sound toward distortion-heavy electro. Contemporary producers like Breakbot, The Magician, and Purple Disco Machine keep the filtered disco sample tradition alive. The genre's DNA runs through nu-disco, blog house, and the broader 2020s disco revival, with its signature compression pumping and filter sweeps now standard production tools across electronic music.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Sampled disco and funk loops (often from late 1970s-early 1980s European and American records), Roland TR-909 and TR-808 drum machines, synthesizers (Juno-106, Minimoog), electric bass (sampled or played), vocoder, talk box, occasional live guitar and strings from source samples
- **Vocals**: Often sampled and looped from disco/funk records; vocal hooks tend to be short, repetitive phrases rather than full verses; vocoder and talk box processing common; when original vocals appear, they are typically smooth, soulful, and understated; pitched vocal samples used as melodic elements
- **Production**: Heavy use of resonant low-pass filter sweeps on sampled loops (the genre's defining technique), aggressive sidechain compression creating a pumping/breathing effect, phaser and flanger effects, tape saturation and analog warmth, four-on-the-floor kick patterns, sample chopping and looping, master bus compression (notably the Alesis 3630 compressor pushed hard)
- **Energy/Mood**: Euphoric, warm, celebratory, sensual; ranges from laid-back late-night grooves to high-energy dancefloor peaks; an underlying sense of nostalgia for disco's golden era filtered through modern electronic production; playful and hedonistic
- **Structure**: Loop-based with gradual builds through filter opening and element layering; breakdowns strip to minimal elements before rebuilding; typically 4-7 minutes for DJ-friendly mixes; shorter radio edits common for singles; structural evolution through filtering rather than traditional verse-chorus form
- **Tempo**: 115-130 BPM, with the majority sitting at 120-128 BPM; straight four-on-the-floor feel; occasional swing or shuffle inherited from source disco samples

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Not strongly applicable — French house tracks are often instrumental or built on short sampled vocal loops. When original lyrics exist, loose AABB or free-form structures dominate.
- **Rhyme quality**: Relaxed and conversational. Repetition and catchphrase-style hooks are more important than rhyme sophistication.
- **Verse structure**: Minimal — 2-4 line vocal hooks repeated over evolving production. Full verse-chorus structures are rare; vocal content serves the groove rather than narrative.
- **Key rule**: Less is more. A single memorable vocal phrase looped and filtered can carry an entire track. Do not overwrite — the production is the star.
- **Avoid**: Dense lyrical content, complex narratives, aggressive or dark vocal delivery. French house vocals should feel effortless and warm, never forced or verbose.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 120-128 BPM. French house is loop-driven — Suno tracks benefit from short, repeated vocal phrases rather than full verses. Topics: 1/verse max. Instrumental sections and filter builds should dominate.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Filter House** | The core French house sound: disco and funk samples processed through resonant low-pass filters with automated sweeps, creating the genre's signature "wah-wah" effect; four-on-the-floor kick, heavy sidechain compression, warm analog aesthetic | Daft Punk (early), Cassius, Alan Braxe, Thomas Bangalter |
| **French Touch Pop** | More melodic and song-oriented branch incorporating original vocals, pop song structures, and crossover appeal while retaining filtered production and disco sampling techniques | Modjo, Stardust, Bob Sinclar, Martin Solveig |
| **Electro French House** | Harder, more distorted evolution incorporating aggressive compression, distorted bass, and electro influences; bridges French house and electro-house; heavier dancefloor energy | Justice, SebastiAn, Kavinsky, Busy P |
| **Nu-Disco French Touch** | Modern continuation emphasizing clean production, live instrumentation alongside samples, funk guitar, and a more polished aesthetic while preserving the filtered sample tradition | Breakbot, Lifelike, The Magician, Purple Disco Machine |
| **Lounge / Downtempo French Touch** | Slower, more atmospheric side of the movement incorporating trip-hop textures, jazz samples, and ambient electronics; designed for listening rather than dancefloors | Air, Dimitri from Paris, Alex Gopher |
| **Crydamoure Sound** | Named after Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo's label; emphasizes raw, chopped disco samples with minimal processing beyond filtering and compression; lo-fi warmth, stripped-back arrangements | Le Knight Club, Crydamoure compilations, Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo |
| **Blog House / Ed Banger** | Late-2000s Parisian evolution with maximalist, distorted production, blog-era internet distribution, and rock energy applied to electronic music; descended from French house but heavier and noisier | Justice, Uffie, Mr. Oizo, Busy P |

## Reference Tracks

- **Daft Punk - "Around the World"** — French house's global calling card from the 1997 album *Homework*: a deliberately repetitive vocal hook over a pulsing bass line and four-on-the-floor kick, demonstrating how a single phrase and relentless groove can build hypnotic dancefloor energy through production rather than songwriting complexity

- **Stardust - "Music Sounds Better with You"** — The BBC called it "four minutes of French funk built upon a brutally efficient four-bar loop." Thomas Bangalter, Alan Braxe, and Benjamin Diamond distilled the genre to its essence in 1998: a chopped Chaka Khan guitar sample, sidechain-pumped bass, and a breathy vocal hook repeated until it becomes a mantra

- **Modjo - "Lady (Hear Me Tonight)"** — The 2000 hit that proved French house could produce pure pop: Chic's "Soup for One" guitar filtered and looped under Yann Destagnol's falsetto, creating an instantly recognizable anthem that brought the genre to mainstream radio worldwide

- **Cassius - "Cassius 1999"** — Philippe Zdar and Boombass crafted a groovy, swaggering filter house track that reached UK Top 10 in early 1999; its funky bass line and chopped vocal samples exemplify the genre's ability to transform disco source material into something entirely contemporary

- **Thomas Bangalter - "Outrun"** — A 1996 Roulé label release that established the raw filter house template: a relentless four-on-the-floor kick, aggressively filtered loop, and heavy sidechain compression creating the pumping, breathing effect that became the genre's sonic signature

- **Daft Punk - "One More Time"** — The 2000 lead single from *Discovery* pushed French house into pop territory: Romanthony's vocal over a cascading synth melody and four-on-the-floor groove, demonstrating the genre's capacity for unabashed euphoria and emotional directness

- **Étienne de Crécy - "Prix Choc"** — From the landmark 1996 compilation *Super Discount*: a minimal, pounding filter house track that strips the formula to its essentials, showcasing how a single filtered loop and kick pattern can generate relentless dancefloor momentum

- **Justice - "D.A.N.C.E."** — The 2007 single that bridged French house and blog-era electro: children's choir vocals, distorted synths, and filtered disco DNA processed through a heavier, more aggressive aesthetic, marking the genre's evolution into a new generation

- **Air - "Sexy Boy"** — The 1998 *Moon Safari* single showcasing the downtempo, cinematic side of French touch: vocoder-processed vocals, warm analog synths, and a laid-back groove that expanded the movement's sonic palette beyond dancefloor-only territory

- **Breakbot - "Baby I'm Yours"** — A 2012 track demonstrating French house's continuity into the modern era: live funk guitar, smooth vocals from Irfane, and classic filtered production techniques updated with contemporary polish, proving the genre's disco-sampling tradition remains vital

- **Bob Sinclar - "Love Generation"** — The 2005 hit that brought French house's feel-good energy to global pop radio: a children's choir hook, uplifting production, and four-on-the-floor pulse that reduced the genre to its most accessible, celebratory form

- **Motorbass - "Pansoul"** — The 1996 title track from one of French house's earliest full albums: Philippe Zdar and Étienne de Crécy built entire compositions from filtered disco loops before the technique had a name, establishing the production blueprint that Daft Punk and Cassius would refine
