# Gypsy Jazz

## Genre Overview

Gypsy jazz (also known as jazz manouche or sinti jazz) was born in Paris in the early 1930s, created almost single-handedly by Belgian Romani guitarist Django Reinhardt. In 1934, Reinhardt and French violinist Stephane Grappelli formed the Quintette du Hot Club de France, evolving from informal backstage jams at the Hotel Claridge on the Champs-Elysees. The group's all-string lineup, with no drums or wind instruments, was revolutionary: lead guitar and violin carried melodies, two rhythm guitars provided the driving "la pompe" accompaniment, and double bass anchored the bottom end. Reinhardt's playing was all the more extraordinary because a caravan fire in 1928 had severely damaged his left hand, leaving him with full use of only two fingers for fretting.

The Quintette du Hot Club de France became one of the most original groups in recorded jazz history, blending American swing with Romani musical traditions, French musette, and classical virtuosity. Reinhardt absorbed the influence of Louis Armstrong and Duke Ellington, then filtered it through his own cultural heritage and prodigious technique to create something wholly new. The group recorded prolifically through the 1930s and into the 1940s, with Reinhardt and Grappelli reuniting after World War II for further recordings. Reinhardt continued to evolve, incorporating bebop influences in his later work before his death in 1953 at age 43. His legacy is one of the few cases where a single musician essentially invented an entire genre.

From the 1970s onward, Romani performers like Bireli Lagrene and Stochelo Rosenberg carried the tradition forward with astonishing technical ability. Lagrene, who was called a "Django reborn" when he emerged as a teenager in the early 1980s, went on to explore fusion and other styles while periodically returning to manouche roots. Today, gypsy jazz thrives globally, with Hot Club-style ensembles performing in cities from New York to Tokyo. Annual festivals like Django Reinhardt Festival in Samois-sur-Seine (held from 1968 to 2014) and Django in June in the United States sustain the community. German guitarist Joscho Stephan and Dutch violinist Tim Kliphuis represent a new generation keeping the music vital while expanding its boundaries.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Lead acoustic guitar (Selmer-Maccaferri or similar large-bodied oval or D-hole guitar), rhythm guitar(s) providing "la pompe" (percussive two-beat chord strumming), violin, double bass; no drums in traditional format; accordion and clarinet in some lineups; occasional mandolin; all acoustic, no amplification in purist settings

- **Vocals**: Primarily instrumental; when vocals appear, they follow the French chanson or swing tradition; light, rhythmic singing; scat-influenced; the guitar and violin are the "voices" of the genre; some modern groups incorporate vocals more prominently

- **Production**: Acoustic, natural room sound; emphasis on capturing the live energy of the ensemble; minimal processing; warmth and resonance of wooden instruments central to the aesthetic; vintage recordings have a lo-fi charm that modern recordings sometimes emulate; stereo image places instruments as they would sit in a room

- **Energy/Mood**: Exuberant, virtuosic, swinging, and joyful at uptempo; tender and romantic on ballads; playful and competitive during trading solos; a cafe-terrace atmosphere; sophisticated but never stuffy; celebration of life and musical skill; the energy of a Parisian street performance

- **Structure**: Head-solos-head format; 32-bar AABA standards and original compositions; each soloist takes one or more choruses; rhythm guitar maintains steady "la pompe" throughout; unison melody statements by guitar and violin; codas and tags common; 3-6 minutes typical

- **Tempo**: 120-280 BPM; medium swing at 140-180 BPM; blazing uptempo at 220-280+ BPM; ballads at 60-100 BPM; "la pompe" rhythm drives the feel regardless of tempo; straight swing feel, not shuffle

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABA following standard jazz song form; when lyrics exist, they follow French chanson or American songbook conventions
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean, singable rhymes; the genre is overwhelmingly instrumental, so lyrics are secondary to melody and rhythm
- **Verse structure**: 8-bar sections following 32-bar form; lyrics serve the melody rather than driving the composition
- **Key rule**: Gypsy jazz is fundamentally an instrumental genre. When lyrics are present, they should be light, swinging, and not impede the rhythmic drive. The melody is king.
- **Avoid**: Heavy lyrical content that weighs down the swing; slow, ponderous vocal delivery; anything that disrupts the "la pompe" groove; overly modern or colloquial language
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 140-220 BPM. Light, swinging vocal phrasing over acoustic guitar. Mostly instrumental. Topics: 1/track. Romance, Paris, travel, celebration.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Traditional Manouche** | Faithful to Django's original Hot Club format: acoustic guitars, violin, bass; "la pompe" rhythm; swing-era repertoire and original compositions in the Reinhardt style | Django Reinhardt, Stochelo Rosenberg, Fapy Lafertin |
| **Modern Gypsy Jazz** | Expands the traditional palette with influences from bebop, Latin, and contemporary jazz; more complex harmony and extended techniques while maintaining the acoustic string-ensemble format | Bireli Lagrene, Joscho Stephan, Adrien Moignard |
| **Gypsy Jazz Vocal** | Incorporates singers performing standards and original material over the traditional manouche instrumentation; chanson and swing vocal influences | Cyrille Aimee, Dorado Schmitt |
| **Gypsy Swing** | Emphasizes the danceable, swing-era side of the genre; uptempo, crowd-pleasing repertoire; performance-oriented with showmanship | The Rosenberg Trio, Hot Club of San Francisco |
| **Gypsy Jazz Fusion** | Blends manouche guitar technique with elements of rock, flamenco, or electronic music; acoustic foundation with occasional amplification or effects | Bireli Lagrene (fusion period), Tchavolo Schmitt |
| **Valse Manouche** | Waltz-time gypsy jazz (3/4); romantic, flowing melodies; "la pompe" adapted to triple meter; Django's waltz compositions form the core repertoire | Django Reinhardt (waltzes), Stochelo Rosenberg |

## Reference Tracks

- **Django Reinhardt - "Minor Swing"** (1937) — The most famous gypsy jazz composition: a minor-key melody played in unison by guitar and violin over the driving "la pompe" rhythm, followed by improvised choruses that showcase Reinhardt's superhuman technique and melodic invention on acoustic guitar.

- **Quintette du Hot Club de France - "Nuages"** (1940) — Django's most beautiful ballad: a slow, dreamy melody that floats over gentle chord changes, demonstrating that gypsy jazz could be tender and romantic as well as blazingly fast. "Nuages" means "clouds" in French, and the music drifts like one.

- **Django Reinhardt - "Djangology"** (1935) — An uptempo showcase for Reinhardt's rhythmic creativity and harmonic sophistication, built on rhythm changes at high speed. The interplay between Django and Grappelli is telepathic, each pushing the other to greater heights.

- **Stephane Grappelli - "Sweet Georgia Brown"** — Grappelli's violin swings through this standard with warmth, wit, and effortless technique, demonstrating the elegance that made him one of jazz's greatest improvisers across any instrument.

- **Bireli Lagrene - "Stella by Starlight"** (from *Gipsy Project*, 2001) — The modern manouche master reinterprets a jazz standard with Django's rhythmic DNA intact but a contemporary harmonic awareness that expands the genre's vocabulary without abandoning its swing foundation.

- **Stochelo Rosenberg - "Bossa Dorado"** (from *Seresta*, 1994) — Rosenberg's technical command is staggering: crystal-clear arpeggios at impossible speeds over bossa nova-inflected harmony, showing how modern gypsy jazz can absorb influences from Brazilian music while remaining rooted in the manouche tradition.

- **The Rosenberg Trio - "Caravan"** (1994) — Duke Ellington's exotic composition reimagined as a gypsy jazz tour de force, with the trio's three guitars creating a wall of acoustic energy that captures the live excitement of the genre at its most thrilling.

- **Django Reinhardt - "Belleville"** (1945) — A post-war recording showing Django absorbing bebop influences: faster harmonic rhythm, chromatic passing tones, and a forward-looking approach that proved gypsy jazz could evolve beyond its swing-era origins.

- **Joscho Stephan - "Hungaria"** (from *Guitar Heroes*, 2015) — Stephan and Bireli Lagrene duet on this Django classic, demonstrating how the manouche tradition passes between generations through direct musical conversation, with both guitarists pushing each other to dazzling heights.

- **Django Reinhardt - "I'll See You in My Dreams"** (1939) — A perfect example of the Hot Club format in action: unison head, violin solo, guitar solo, then a final chorus where all the parts come together in swinging, joyful interplay. The essence of gypsy jazz in three minutes.
