# Future Funk

## Genre Overview

Future funk emerged around 2012-2013 as a high-energy, dance-oriented evolution of vaporwave. Where vaporwave slowed and pitched down samples from 1980s and 1990s pop, smooth jazz, and elevator music to create an ironic commentary on consumer culture, future funk took a fundamentally different approach — speeding up and chopping samples from Japanese city pop, disco, and funk records to create genuinely joyful, danceable music. The genre crystallized through the work of producers like Macross 82-99, Saint Pepsi (now Skylar Spence), and Yung Bae, who posted their tracks on Bandcamp and SoundCloud alongside anime-inspired artwork that became inseparable from the sound.

The genre's source material is crucial to its identity. Japanese city pop from the late 1970s and 1980s — artists like Tatsuro Yamashita, Mariya Takeuchi, and Taeko Ohnuki — provides the melodic and harmonic foundation, prized for its lush production, sophisticated chord progressions, and warm analog recording quality. Future funk producers chop these sources into rhythmic loops, layer them over punchy four-on-the-floor house beats, add sidechain compression for a characteristic pumping effect, and sometimes pitch vocals up for an energized, chipmunk-adjacent quality. The result is music that functions simultaneously as a celebration of its source material and as a standalone dance genre.

Future funk's cultural identity is tightly bound to internet aesthetics. The visual language — anime characters, VHS tracking artifacts, neon gradients, Japanese text — creates a nostalgic fantasy version of 1980s Japan filtered through Western internet culture. The genre peaked in visibility around 2015-2017, with artists like Yung Bae crossing into broader recognition. As the initial wave settled, the genre faced questions about the sustainability of a sample-dependent form, with some producers moving toward more original compositions while maintaining the aesthetic. Future funk remains an active niche with dedicated communities on YouTube, Reddit, and Bandcamp, and its influence is visible in the broader nu-disco and internet music landscape.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Sampled city pop, disco, and funk records as primary harmonic and melodic content; four-on-the-floor kick drums (house-influenced); added percussion (claps, rim shots, shakers); bass reinforcement over sampled basslines; occasional original synth parts layered over samples; sidechain compression creating the genre's signature pumping feel

- **Vocals**: Sampled from source material (usually Japanese city pop); often pitched up slightly for energy; chopped rhythmically; rarely original vocals; the sampled vocal fragments carry melody and emotion; some producers use vocal chops as rhythmic elements divorced from lyrical meaning

- **Production**: Sample-based with house-influenced drum programming; heavy sidechain compression on samples ducking to the kick; high-pass filtering for clarity; layered drum processing; lo-fi textures (vinyl crackle, tape hiss) for warmth; mix centered on the sample with drums punching through; relatively simple arrangements emphasizing groove over complexity

- **Energy/Mood**: Joyful, energetic, nostalgic, warm; party music with a wistful undercurrent; celebratory but with an awareness of the past; summery and bright; the feeling of dancing alone in your room at 2 AM to music from a decade before you were born

- **Structure**: Sample-loop structures; 4-8 bar loops with variations through filtering, vocal chops, and drum pattern changes; breakdowns that isolate the sample before the beat returns; tracks often short (2-4 minutes); DJ-friendly intros and outros in longer edits; some tracks follow the structure of their source material

- **Tempo**: 112-128 BPM; classic disco/house tempo range; most tracks sit comfortably at 115-124 BPM

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: N/A for sample-based tracks; for original compositions: ABAB or AABB — pop-oriented and catchy
- **Rhyme quality**: Bright, clean, singable; pop sensibility; if writing original lyrics for a future funk track, prioritize catchiness and warmth
- **Verse structure**: 4 lines per verse; pop-standard; hook-driven with memorable chorus phrases
- **Key rule**: Future funk is primarily sample-based, so original lyrics are uncommon. When writing lyrics for the style, channel the warmth and romance of city pop — love, summer, nightlife, dancing. Keep it light and joyful. Japanese loan words or phrases are acceptable given the genre's aesthetic.
- **Avoid**: Darkness, aggression, melancholy, irony, cynicism; anything that contradicts the genre's fundamentally joyful energy; complex or heavy subject matter
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 112-128 BPM. Bright, catchy phrasing. Pop-friendly hooks. Topics: 1/track. Joy and warmth over depth. Upbeat, clear delivery with possible pitched vocal effects.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Future Funk** | Core sound: city pop samples over house beats with sidechain compression; anime-adjacent visuals; lo-fi warmth | Macross 82-99, Saint Pepsi |
| **Future Boogie** | Funkier, more groove-oriented; draws more from Western disco and boogie sources; less reliant on Japanese samples | Yung Bae, Moe Shop |
| **Lo-Fi Future Funk** | Deliberately degraded audio quality; heavier use of VHS and tape artifacts; more atmospheric and less dance-oriented | Various Bandcamp producers |
| **Original Future Funk** | Original compositions in the future funk style rather than sample-based; maintains the aesthetic while avoiding copyright issues | Night Tempo (some works), Moe Shop |

## Reference Tracks

- **Saint Pepsi - "Cherry Pepsi"** — The track that introduced thousands to future funk: a pitched-up city pop sample over a pumping house beat, impossibly catchy and bursting with joy; distills the genre's appeal into three minutes of pure serotonin

- **Macross 82-99 - "Fun Tonight"** — Dense sample collage that captures the maximalist end of future funk; layers of chopped city pop over driving beats; the anime-inspired visual identity of the release became iconic for the genre's aesthetic

- **Yung Bae - "Japanese Disco Edit 2"** — Groove-focused future funk that prioritizes dancefloor functionality; clean sample selection and tight drum programming; demonstrates the genre's potential as legitimate dance music beyond internet novelty

- **Night Tempo - "Showa Groove" series** — Bridging original city pop with future funk production; respectful treatment of source material that introduced many listeners to the Japanese artists being sampled; culturally significant curation

- **Moe Shop - "NOTICE ME"** — Evolution of future funk toward original composition; retains the genre's joyful energy and pop-anime aesthetic while incorporating original vocals and production; points toward the genre's sustainable future

- **Desired - "Timeless"** — Atmospheric future funk that prioritizes mood over dancefloor energy; dreamy sample manipulation creating a wistful, nostalgic quality; demonstrates the genre's emotional range beyond pure party music
