# Brostep

## Genre Overview

Brostep is a high-energy subgenre of dubstep characterized by aggressive mid-range bass synthesis, heavily processed "wobble" sounds, and a focus on maximal impact over the restrained, sub-bass-driven minimalism of original UK dubstep. The style evolved from the UK tearout dubstep sound pioneered by producers like Caspa, Rusko, and Coki in the late 2000s, who began pushing dubstep's bass frequencies upward from sub-bass into the more audible mid-range spectrum. Rusko's *O.M.G!* (2010) is regarded as one of the first fully articulated statements of this heavier, more extroverted approach, trading the dark, sparse atmosphere of Croydon dubstep for bright, aggressive energy designed for large sound systems and festival crowds.

American producer Skrillex transformed brostep from a UK underground variant into a global phenomenon. His 2010 EP *Scary Monsters and Nice Sprites* was the genre's commercial breakthrough, introducing millions to aggressive bass drops, frenetic tempo shifts, and maximalist sound design. Skrillex won three Grammy Awards at the 2012 ceremony, bringing dubstep-derived music to mainstream recognition for the first time. The term "brostep" itself emerged as a pejorative from UK dubstep purists who viewed the American-led commercialization as a betrayal of dubstep's roots in London sound system culture and dub reggae. Simultaneously, Canadian producers Excision and Datsik developed their own strain of heavy bass music that fed into the brostep ecosystem, while UK acts Doctor P and Flux Pavilion on Circus Records bridged the transatlantic divide.

By 2013-2014, brostep's mainstream momentum slowed as EDM audiences moved toward big-room house and future bass, but the genre's DNA persisted in bass music production. Artists like Zomboy, Virtual Riot, and Eptic kept the aggressive bass aesthetic alive while incorporating new sound design techniques. The melodic dubstep movement, led by producers like Seven Lions and Illenium, softened brostep's edges with trance-influenced melodies. In the 2020s, a revival of interest in heavy bass music has seen renewed appreciation for brostep's foundational producers, with Skrillex's return albums *Quest for Fire* and *Don't Get Too Close* (both 2023) demonstrating the genre's continued evolution.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Software synthesizers (Massive, Serum, FM8), heavily processed bass patches, aggressive leads, synthesized vocal chops, drum machines with layered percussion, sub-bass layers, white noise risers, impact samples
- **Vocals**: Pitch-shifted vocal samples, chopped and glitched vocal fragments, aggressive MC-style shouts, pop vocal hooks in commercial variants, robotic processing through vocoders and talkbox emulation
- **Production**: Extreme sound design focus; mid-range "wobble" bass created through LFO modulation of filters; heavy sidechain compression; layered bass drops; precise automation; loudness-war-era mastering; complex FM synthesis for metallic, growling bass textures
- **Energy/Mood**: Aggressive, explosive, confrontational, euphoric, manic, party-oriented; designed for maximum physical impact on dance floors and festival stages; energy builds to cathartic release at the drop
- **Structure**: Intro → build-up → drop → breakdown → second build → second drop; the "drop" is the focal point, featuring the heaviest bass and most intense sound design; breakdowns provide contrast before re-escalation; typically 3-5 minutes
- **Tempo**: 140-150 BPM with half-time feel creating perceived tempo around 70-75 BPM; the half-time groove is essential to the head-nodding, bass-weight feel; some tracks shift between half-time and full-time sections

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Minimal; drops are instrumental. Vocal hooks use simple AABB or call-and-response.
- **Rhyme quality**: Simple, catchy, repetitive. The vocal serves as a melodic hook or hype element, not a lyrical showcase.
- **Verse structure**: Short vocal phrases, 2-4 lines per section. Build-up vocals escalate tension before instrumental drops.
- **Key rule**: The drop is king. Vocals exist to set up the bass drop, not the other way around.
- **Avoid**: Complex or introspective lyrics that distract from physical energy. Long vocal passages that delay the drop.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/section** at 140 BPM half-time. Vocal hooks brief and punchy. Most musical content is in the instrumental drop. Topics: 1/track max, often just a repeated phrase or concept.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Tearout Dubstep** | The UK predecessor to brostep; heavier, mid-range-focused dubstep that maintained some connection to original dubstep's rhythmic sophistication while pushing into more aggressive territory | Caspa, Rusko, Coki, N-Type |
| **Riddim** | Stripped-down, repetitive variant focused on minimal arrangements and extremely heavy, sustained bass tones; less melodic than mainstream brostep, more focused on pure bass weight and sound design | Infekt, Virtual Riot (riddim works), Subtronics |
| **Melodic Dubstep** | Softer brostep variant incorporating trance melodies, emotional chord progressions, and clean vocals; designed for festival mainstages with broader emotional appeal | Seven Lions, Illenium, Said The Sky |
| **Deathstep** | Ultra-aggressive variant pushing brostep into extreme metal-adjacent territory; blast-beat-style drum programming, guttural bass growls, maximum intensity | Code: Pandorum, Moth, 1788-L |
| **Color Bass** | Post-brostep evolution blending heavy bass drops with bright, melodic elements and genre-fluid production; playful rather than purely aggressive | Chime, Au5, Leotrix |
| **Festival Dubstep** | Commercial brostep optimized for massive sound systems and festival crowds; pop vocal hooks, predictable build-drop structures, maximum crowd participation | Excision, Borgore, Datsik |

## Reference Tracks

- **Rusko - "Cockney Thug"** — Proto-brostep from the UK tearout scene: mid-range wobble bass pushed to the foreground over heavy sub-bass and breakbeat-influenced rhythms; demonstrated that dubstep could be extroverted and aggressive while maintaining its UK roots

- **Caspa & Rusko - "Rock Bottom"** — The transatlantic bridge: UK dubstep weight meeting accessible energy; the wobble bass patterns here became the template that American producers would amplify into brostep

- **Skrillex - "Scary Monsters and Nice Sprites"** — The track that defined brostep for a generation: glitched vocal samples, frenetic tempo shifts, and a mid-range bass drop that rewrote the rules of electronic music production; Grammy-winning, genre-creating

- **Skrillex - "Bangarang" (feat. Sirah)** — Commercial brostep perfected: vocal hook meets explosive bass drop, designed for both radio and festival sound systems; showed brostep could be a pop format

- **Flux Pavilion - "Bass Cannon"** — The name says it all: a pure bass-weight statement that became a brostep anthem; the build-drop structure executed with maximum impact

- **Flux Pavilion - "I Can't Stop"** — Crossover brostep with a vocal hook that penetrated mainstream consciousness; sampled widely and featured in commercials, proving brostep's commercial viability

- **Doctor P - "Sweet Shop"** — UK brostep with carnival energy: playful vocal samples over heavy wobble bass; bridged the gap between UK dubstep heritage and American festival culture

- **Knife Party - "Bonfire"** — Electro-brostep hybrid: Rob Swire's production precision applied to aggressive bass music; showed how brostep could incorporate elements from other electronic genres

- **Excision - "X Rated" (feat. Messinian)** — Festival brostep at maximum intensity: MC vocals hyping the crowd over crushing bass drops; designed for massive sound systems and mosh pits

- **Zomboy - "Nuclear (Hands Up)"** — Post-peak brostep that kept the genre's energy alive: clean sound design, effective builds, and drops that satisfied the bass music faithful during the genre's commercial downturn

- **Virtual Riot - "Energy Drink"** — Sound design showcase: technically complex bass patches and production tricks that pushed brostep's sonic vocabulary forward; influenced a generation of bedroom producers

- **Seven Lions - "Days to Come" (feat. Fiora)** — Melodic dubstep landmark: trance melodies and emotional vocals integrated with heavy bass drops; proved brostep could carry genuine emotional weight
