# Electronic

## Genre Overview

Electronic music encompasses a vast range of styles united by the use of electronic instruments, synthesizers, drum machines, and digital production techniques. The genre's roots trace back to the early 20th century with experimental composers like Karlheinz Stockhausen and Pierre Schaeffer's musique concrete, but it crystallized into a distinct popular music form through Kraftwerk's pioneering work in the 1970s. Their albums "Autobahn" (1974) and "Trans-Europe Express" (1977) established the template for machine-driven pop music that would influence everything from synth-pop to techno.

The 1980s saw electronic music bifurcate into accessible synth-pop (Depeche Mode, New Order) and underground club culture (Chicago house, Detroit techno). The 1990s brought the UK rave explosion, IDM's cerebral experiments, and ambient's meditative depths. The 2000s-2010s witnessed EDM's mainstream breakthrough, the synthwave revival's nostalgic reinterpretation of 80s sounds, and the continued evolution of experimental electronic forms.

Today, electronic music production tools are democratized through affordable software, leading to unprecedented diversity. The genre spans from arena-filling EDM drops to intimate headphone-listening ambient, from retro-futurist synthwave to avant-garde sound design. What unites this diversity is the primacy of sound itself as the musical material - texture, timbre, and atmosphere often take precedence over traditional melody and harmony.

## Characteristics

### Sound Design & Production
- **Instrumentation**: Synthesizers (analog, digital, modular), drum machines (808, 909, LinnDrum), samplers, software instruments, field recordings
- **Production Approach**: Ranges from warm analog saturation to pristine digital clarity; layering is essential with 20-100+ tracks common
- **Sound Design Focus**: Timbre manipulation, filter sweeps, LFO modulation, granular synthesis, sound morphing
- **Mixing Style**: Wide stereo imaging, sidechain compression (pumping effect), heavy use of reverb and delay for space

### Rhythmic Characteristics
- **BPM Ranges**: Highly variable by subgenre
  - Ambient/Downtempo: 60-90 BPM (or beatless)
  - Synth-pop: 100-130 BPM
  - Synthwave: 80-118 BPM
  - House/Deep House: 118-130 BPM
  - Techno: 125-150 BPM
  - IDM: Variable, often shifting or irregular
- **Drum Programming**: Four-on-the-floor kicks, syncopated hi-hats, breakbeats, or deliberately mechanical/quantized patterns
- **Groove**: Can range from robotic precision (Kraftwerk) to humanized swing (Boards of Canada) to chaotic glitch (Autechre)

### Vocal Treatment
- **Optional Element**: Many subgenres are primarily instrumental
- **When Present**: Often processed through vocoder, pitch correction, reverb, or heavy effects
- **Styles**: Deadpan delivery (Kraftwerk), emotional pop vocals (synth-pop), whispered intimacy (trip-hop), sampled fragments (IDM)

### Emotional Range
- **Euphoric**: Build-and-release tension in EDM, ecstatic synth melodies
- **Melancholic**: Minor keys, detuned synths, nostalgic 80s textures
- **Meditative**: Droning ambient, repetitive minimal patterns
- **Dark**: Industrial textures, distorted beats, dystopian atmospheres
- **Cerebral**: Complex rhythms, unexpected transitions, intellectual engagement

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Rhyme is secondary to repetition. When present, loose and incidental.
- **Rhyme quality**: Optional. Forced rhymes sound amateur in electronic music.
- **Verse structure**: Minimal vocals. Short phrases (2–8 words) repeated as loops or hooks. Space between phrases.
- **Key rule**: Less is more. Repetition is the defining characteristic — same phrase repeating creates hypnotic effect. Too many words ruin the track.
- **Avoid**: Dense lyrics. Narrative complexity. Traditional verse-chorus structure (unless synthwave). Over-rhyming.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4 lines/verse** at 120-140 BPM. Production is the star — vocals are texture. Fewer syllables, more space for drops and builds. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | BPM Range | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Ambient** | Atmospheric, textural, meditative soundscapes that prioritize tone and atmosphere over rhythm. Often beatless or with minimal percussion. Designed for deep listening or environmental enhancement. | 60-90 or beatless | Brian Eno, Aphex Twin (Selected Ambient Works), Stars of the Lid |
| **Synthwave/Retrowave** | 1980s-inspired electronic music featuring analog synth sounds, gated reverb drums, and nostalgic cinematic atmospheres. Often evokes neon-lit cityscapes, VHS aesthetics, and retro-futurism. | 80-118 | Kavinsky, Gunship, The Midnight, Perturbator, Carpenter Brut |
| **Synth-Pop** | Melodic, accessible electronic pop combining synthesizers with traditional song structures. Emerged in late 1970s/early 1980s, balancing electronic experimentation with pop sensibility. | 100-130 | Depeche Mode, Pet Shop Boys, CHVRCHES, New Order, OMD |
| **IDM (Intelligent Dance Music)** | Experimental electronic music featuring complex rhythms, unconventional structures, and cerebral sound design. Despite the name, often more suited for headphone listening than dancing. | Variable, 70-160 | Aphex Twin, Autechre, Boards of Canada, Squarepusher, Clark |
| **Downtempo/Chillout** | Relaxed, atmospheric electronic music with mid-tempo grooves. Emphasizes smooth textures, mellow beats, and sophisticated production. Associated with lounge culture and late-night listening. | 70-100 | Air, Massive Attack, Zero 7, Bonobo, Thievery Corporation |
| **New Age/Space Music** | Meditative electronic music blending synthesizers with acoustic instruments. Emphasizes tranquility, spirituality, and cosmic themes. Often used for relaxation or meditation. | 60-90 or beatless | Vangelis, Jean-Michel Jarre, Kitaro, Tangerine Dream |
| **Darksynth** | Aggressive offshoot of synthwave with heavier distortion, industrial influences, and horror/sci-fi themes. Combines 80s aesthetics with modern metal and industrial intensity. | 100-140 | Perturbator, Carpenter Brut, Dan Terminus, GosT |
| **Minimal/Microhouse** | Stripped-down electronic music focusing on subtle variations in repetitive patterns. Emphasizes space, restraint, and hypnotic grooves over dramatic changes. | 118-130 | Ricardo Villalobos, Richie Hawtin, Kompakt Records artists |
| **Electro** | Robotic, funk-influenced electronic music with syncopated beats and vocoder vocals. Originated in early 1980s as hip-hop's electronic cousin, revived in 2000s. | 110-135 | Kraftwerk, Afrika Bambaataa, Egyptian Lover, Justice |
| **Industrial/EBM** | Harsh, aggressive electronic music with distorted textures, mechanical rhythms, and often dark or confrontational themes. EBM adds dancefloor-oriented beats. | 110-140 | Nine Inch Nails, Front 242, Skinny Puppy, KMFDM |

## Reference Tracks

For style reference when prompting:

| Track | Artist | Style | Context |
|-------|--------|-------|---------|
| "Nightcall" | Kavinsky | Synthwave | The quintessential synthwave track. Features filtered vocals, driving arpeggios, and cinematic atmosphere. The Drive (2011) soundtrack placement defined the modern synthwave aesthetic. Note the sparse arrangement with massive reverb space. |
| "Enjoy the Silence" | Depeche Mode | Synth-pop | Perfect blend of melancholy and accessibility. Sparse arrangement with iconic bassline, atmospheric pads, and Dave Gahan's emotional vocal delivery. Template for dark but radio-friendly synth-pop. |
| "An Ending (Ascent)" | Brian Eno | Ambient | From the Apollo album, this track exemplifies ambient music's power through simplicity. Slowly evolving string-like synthesizers create profound emotional depth without rhythm or traditional structure. |
| "Roygbiv" | Boards of Canada | IDM/Downtempo | Exemplifies BoC's nostalgic warmth - detuned synths, lo-fi hip-hop beats, warbly textures suggesting degraded VHS or childhood memories. Melodic IDM that prioritizes emotion over complexity. |
| "Orinoco Flow" | Enya | New Age | Demonstrates new age's layered vocal approach and ethereal production. Multi-tracked voices create choir-like textures; synthesizers and acoustic elements blend seamlessly. |
| "Trans-Europe Express" | Kraftwerk | Electro/Pioneer | The blueprint for electronic pop. Motorik rhythm, vocoder vocals, repetitive patterns, and themes of technology and travel. Every synth-pop and techno artist traces lineage here. |
| "Teardrop" | Massive Attack | Trip-hop/Downtempo | Defining trip-hop moment with harpsichord sample, heartbeat-like kick, and Elizabeth Fraser's ethereal vocals. Dark, intimate, cinematic - the template for atmospheric electronic pop. |
| "Kelly Watch the Stars" | Air | Downtempo/French Touch | Moon Safari-era Air at their dreamiest. Vocoder vocals, vintage synth tones, and sophisticated pop songcraft. Represents French electronic music's romantic, retro-tinged approach. |
| "Windowlicker" | Aphex Twin | IDM/Experimental | Richard D. James at his most accessible-yet-weird. Funk-influenced beat programming, complex sound design, and twisted vocal manipulation. Shows IDM can groove while remaining experimental. |
| "Blue Monday" | New Order | Synth-pop/Electronic Rock | Best-selling 12" single ever. Oberheim DMX drum machine, sequenced bassline, and Bernard Sumner's detached vocals. Bridged post-punk atmosphere with electronic dance floor functionality. |
| "Turbo Killer" | Carpenter Brut | Darksynth | Heavy, aggressive synthwave with metal influences. Distorted leads, punishing drums, and horror-film atmosphere. Represents synthwave's heavier, more intense evolution. |
| "Da Funk" | Daft Punk | French House | Gritty, filtered synth riff over driving house beat. Lo-fi yet funky, showing how simple elements can create an iconic hook. Foundation of French house's filter-heavy approach. |
| "Tech Support" | Gunship | Vocal Synthwave | Modern synthwave with rock-influenced production and strong vocal hooks. Shows how the genre evolved to incorporate traditional songwriting alongside retro aesthetics. |
| "The Mother We Share" | CHVRCHES | Modern Synth-pop | Contemporary synth-pop with Lauren Mayberry's soaring vocals over pulsing synthesizers. Demonstrates how 2010s synth-pop balanced indie sensibility with electronic production. |
| "Rubycon Part One" | Tangerine Dream | Berlin School | 17-minute journey through sequencer patterns and evolving textures. Represents the "Berlin School" approach - long-form, hypnotic, and endlessly unfolding electronic composition. |
