# Electroswing

## Genre Overview

Electroswing fuses the brass-heavy, rhythmically swung sounds of 1920s-1940s jazz and swing with modern electronic production techniques drawn from house, breakbeat, and hip-hop. The genre's earliest stirrings appeared in the 1990s when hip-hop producers began sampling vintage swing recordings, but these were largely one-off experiments. The French label G-Swing released material as early as 1992 that blended swing samples with electronic beats, though the sound leaned closer to funky house than anything distinctly its own. It was not until the mid-2000s that electroswing coalesced into a recognizable genre, driven primarily by European producers who saw untapped potential in pairing Depression-era brass and vocals with four-on-the-floor kick drums and sidechain compression.

Austrian producer Parov Stelar and Viennese colleague Waldeck were among the first to give the sound a defined identity. Waldeck's 2007 album *Ballroom Stories* became an early prototype for the movement, evoking speakeasy glamour through trip-hop-inflected electronic production layered with jazz-age samples. Parov Stelar's prolific output from 2004 onward -- beginning with *Rough Cuts* (2006) and breaking through internationally with *Coco* (2009) -- established the sample-based production template that defined the genre's first decade. Meanwhile, French band Caravan Palace brought a live-band energy to electroswing with their 2008 self-titled debut, mixing hot-club-style jazz instrumentation with house rhythms and performing as a full ensemble rather than relying solely on vintage samples. Their 2015 single "Lone Digger," propelled by a striking animated music video, surpassed 400 million YouTube views and remains the genre's biggest viral moment.

Today, electroswing occupies a unique position in electronic music: too retro for mainstream EDM, too electronic for jazz purists, and beloved by a dedicated global audience that spans festival circuits, swing dance communities, and YouTube algorithm discoveries. The scene remains heavily European, with strongholds in France, Austria, Germany, Belgium, and the UK. Production approaches range from sample-heavy bedroom productions built around dusty 78 RPM recordings to full live bands with brass sections performing alongside laptop producers. The genre continues to evolve through cross-pollination with drum and bass, glitch hop, and future bass, while its core identity -- vintage swing aesthetics meeting modern dance floor energy -- remains intact.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Brass samples or live brass (trumpet, trombone, saxophone), vintage vocal samples from 1920s-1940s recordings, electronic drum machines (TR-808, TR-909), synthesized basslines, piano (sampled ragtime/stride or synthesized), upright bass samples, clarinet, DJ scratches and turntablism in hip-hop-influenced variants, finger snaps, handclaps, and percussion loops. Live acts like Caravan Palace and The Electric Swing Circus combine traditional instruments (guitar, double bass, horns) with MPCs, synth pads, and electronic processing.

- **Rhythm and Feel**: The defining tension of electroswing is the collision between swung rhythms (triplet-based shuffle inherited from jazz) and straight electronic beats (quantized four-on-the-floor house kicks). Most tracks anchor the low end with a steady electronic kick on every beat while layering swung brass, vocal, and percussion elements on top. Tempos sit primarily in the 120-140 BPM range, overlapping with house music's sweet spot. Sidechain compression is used heavily, ducking melodic elements against the kick to create the characteristic "pumping" effect borrowed from house production.

- **Production**: Sample-based production is the genre's backbone -- producers dig through vintage 78s, big band recordings, and public domain jazz to find brass hits, vocal phrases, and melodic fragments that can be chopped, looped, and layered over modern beats. Filtering (high-pass sweeps, low-pass buildups) creates tension and release. Vinyl crackle and tape hiss are often added deliberately to bridge the aesthetic gap between old and new. Sidechain compression, stereo widening, and modern mastering loudness standards coexist with deliberate lo-fi texture on the sampled elements.

- **Vocals**: Range from sampled vintage recordings (often uncredited 1920s-1940s vocalists) to contemporary singers performing in a retro style. Vocal chops -- short, pitched, and rhythmically sliced fragments -- are a signature technique. When original vocals are used, they often adopt a playful, theatrical delivery that nods to cabaret and vaudeville without pure imitation. Scat singing, call-and-response, and spoken word samples appear frequently. Rap and spoken-word verses feature in hip-hop crossover variants (swing hop).

- **Structure**: Follows electronic dance music conventions more than jazz ones: intro, build, drop, breakdown, second build, second drop, outro. The "drop" typically unleashes the full brass sample or horn section over the kick drum. Breakdowns strip to atmospheric elements -- vinyl crackle, a lone piano figure, a filtered vocal snippet -- before rebuilding energy. Tracks run 3-5 minutes for streaming, 5-7 minutes for DJ-oriented versions. Traditional jazz structures (head-solos-head, 32-bar AABA) are largely absent, replaced by loop-based repetition with gradual layering.

- **Mood and Aesthetic**: Playful, celebratory, and irreverent. The genre leans into the glamour and hedonism of the jazz age -- speakeasies, Charleston dancing, Art Deco visuals, pinstripe suits -- while filtering it through modern club culture energy. There is an inherent humor in the collision of eras, and the best electroswing tracks embrace that tension rather than treating the vintage material as sacred. The mood ranges from carefree party energy to darker, moodier pieces with trip-hop influence, but the dominant register is uptempo fun.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Minimal -- most tracks rely on sampled vocal phrases or short hooks rather than full lyric sheets. When original lyrics are written, ABAB or AABB patterns work well with the genre's bouncy feel.
- **Rhyme quality**: Playful, punchy, and rhythmic. Wordplay and double entendres in the jazz tradition. Internal rhyme and syncopated delivery valued over complex multi-syllabic schemes.
- **Verse structure**: Short vocal sections (4-8 lines) alternating with instrumental brass-driven sections. Hooks are typically 1-2 lines repeated. Extended lyrical passages are rare -- the brass and production carry the track.
- **Key rule**: Vocals must serve the groove, not fight it. Phrasing should lock into the swung rhythm or play against the straight electronic beat for rhythmic tension. Theatrical, cabaret-influenced delivery works better than naturalistic singing.
- **Avoid**: Dense lyric blocks that compete with the instrumental arrangement. Overly serious or earnest tone that clashes with the genre's playful energy. Lyrics that don't leave room for brass breaks and instrumental drops.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 120-140 BPM. Groove-driven with short, catchy phrases. Room for instrumental breaks between vocal sections. Topics: 1/verse max. Vocal chop sections can use single repeated words or phrases.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Swing House** | The most prominent electroswing variant; four-on-the-floor house beats merged with swing brass samples, sidechain compression, and filtered builds; club-oriented with DJ-friendly structures and steady tempos around 125-130 BPM | Parov Stelar, Jamie Berry, Bart&Baker |
| **Live Electroswing** | Full bands performing with traditional instruments (brass, guitar, double bass) alongside electronic elements (MPCs, synths, drum pads); more dynamic and improvisational than sample-based production; high-energy live shows | Caravan Palace, The Electric Swing Circus, Swingrowers |
| **Swing Hop** | Hip-hop beats and production techniques (boom-bap drums, scratching, sampling) merged with jazz and swing samples; often instrumental with head-nodding grooves rather than dance floor energy; closer to trip-hop pacing | ProleteR, Chinese Man, Minimatic |
| **Glitch Swing** | Aggressive sound design, stuttered edits, and glitch-hop production techniques applied to swing samples; heavier bass emphasis with wobble and neuro-bass elements; harder-edged and more experimental | Kormac, Wolfgang Lohr |
| **Vintage Lounge Electroswing** | Downtempo, atmospheric approach blending swing and jazz samples with trip-hop, downtempo, and lounge production; moodier and more cinematic than club-oriented variants; suitable for cocktail bars as much as dance floors | Waldeck, Tape Five, Swing Republic |
| **Swing 'n' Bass** | Drum and bass tempos (160-180 BPM) and breakbeats merged with swing brass and vocal samples; high energy with rolling breaks and choppy sample manipulation; emerged from UK bass music culture | Dutty Moonshine, Slamboree |
| **Nu Jazz Electroswing** | Jazz-influenced production with live instrumentation, complex harmony, and electronic textures; less sample-dependent, more focused on original compositions that blend jazz vocabulary with electronic arrangement | Caro Emerald, Gabin |
| **Festival Electroswing** | Big-room production designed for main stage festival sets; maximized drops, buildups, and crowd-pleasing dynamics; heavier bass and louder mastering than underground variants; crossover appeal with mainstream EDM | Parov Stelar (live), Caravan Palace (live), C2C |

## Reference Tracks

- **Parov Stelar - "Booty Swing" (2009)** — The electroswing production blueprint; a perfectly chosen vintage vocal sample ("it don't mean a thing if it ain't got that swing") layered over a driving four-on-the-floor house beat with sidechain compression and filtered brass hits; demonstrated that swing samples could anchor a modern dance track without irony or pastiche; became the genre's most recognized track through commercial licensing and viral spread

- **Parov Stelar - "Catgroove" (2009)** — Infectious groove built on a choppy vintage vocal sample with tight brass stabs and a relentless house beat; the track's YouTube popularity (driven by a viral street dance video) introduced millions to electroswing who had never heard the term; exemplifies the genre's ability to make decades-old recordings feel contemporary through rhythmic recontextualization

- **Caravan Palace - "Lone Digger" (2015)** — Electroswing's biggest viral moment; live band energy with aggressive electronic production, punchy brass hooks, and rapid-fire vocals over a driving beat; the animated music video (400M+ YouTube views) proved the genre could achieve massive reach; represents the evolved, harder-edged sound of second-generation electroswing that moved beyond pure sample collage

- **Caravan Palace - "Jolie Coquine" (2008)** — Early Caravan Palace at their most jazz-forward; hot-club guitar and clarinet over a bouncing electronic beat with playful French vocals; shows the live-band approach to electroswing before the genre's sound hardened; infectious energy that captures the joy of the acoustic-electronic collision

- **Waldeck - "Make My Day" (2007)** — From the influential *Ballroom Stories* album; trip-hop pacing meets jazz-age atmosphere with sultry vocals and smoky production; represents the moodier, more cinematic end of the electroswing spectrum; lounge sophistication over dancefloor intensity; a prototype track for the entire movement

- **Jamie Berry - "Delight" feat. Octavia Rose (2015)** — UK bass music meets 1920s swing; wobble bass and heavy sidechain compression driving under vintage-styled vocals and brass samples; pushed electroswing toward heavier, more bass-forward territory; Octavia Rose's vocal became one of the genre's most recognizable contemporary performances

- **ProleteR - "April Showers" (2011)** — Swing hop at its most accessible; a 1935 vocal sample ("March winds and April showers") layered over boom-bap hip-hop drums with jazz instrumentation; platinum-certified with 30+ million streams; bridged the gap between hip-hop sampling culture and the electroswing scene; proof that the genre could thrive outside the club context

- **Tape Five - "Bad Boy Good Man" (2010)** — Lounge-inflected electroswing with cinematic flair; smooth production blending bossa nova warmth with swing brass and subtle electronic textures; represents the genre's sophisticated, continental European aesthetic; cocktail-bar electroswing at its most polished

- **The Electric Swing Circus - "Everybody" (2013)** — Live electroswing showcasing the full-band approach; dual female vocals over gypsy-jazz guitar, electric double bass, and MPC-triggered electronic elements; captures the energy of the genre's live performance culture where acoustic and electronic worlds collide in real time

- **Caro Emerald - "A Night Like This" (2009)** — Electroswing's pop crossover moment; full orchestral arrangement with jazz vocals over a dance-friendly beat; charted across Europe and proved the genre's commercial viability beyond niche audiences; accessible without sacrificing the swing-electronic fusion at its core

- **Chinese Man - "I've Got That Tune" (2011)** — Trip-hop weight meets swing sampling; heavy bass, turntablism, and a darker production aesthetic that sits at the genre's boundary with hip-hop; represents the grittier, more underground end of the electroswing spectrum where jazz samples serve bass-heavy beatmaking

- **Boogie Belgique - "Forever and Ever" (2014)** — Atmospheric, downtempo electroswing with vinyl-drenched texture and cinematic pacing; Belgian production style emphasizing mood and space over dance-floor functionality; vintage jazz samples treated as ambient texture rather than rhythmic hook; the genre's contemplative side
