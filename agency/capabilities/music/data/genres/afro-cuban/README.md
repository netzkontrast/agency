# Afro-Cuban

## Genre Overview

Afro-Cuban music refers to the family of genres born from the meeting of West African religious and rhythmic traditions with Spanish colonial melody and song forms on the island of Cuba. The Yoruba people, brought to Cuba as enslaved laborers and known there as Lucumi, preserved their spiritual practices through cabildos -- self-organized social clubs that kept alive the drumming, chanting, and call-and-response singing of the Santeria religion. Alongside the Yoruba, Congolese (Palo), Dahomey (Arara), and Calabar (Abakua) communities each contributed distinct percussion vocabularies and dance traditions. From this convergence emerged the clave -- a five-note rhythmic cell, played in either 2-3 or 3-2 orientation, that became the organizing principle for nearly all Cuban popular music. The clave is not merely a pattern; it is a temporal framework that governs the placement of every melodic, harmonic, and rhythmic element in the ensemble.

Son cubano, born in the mountains of eastern Cuba in the late 19th century, fused Spanish guitar traditions with African rhythmic sensibility and became the foundation genre from which mambo, cha-cha-cha, and eventually salsa would grow. In the 1940s, Arsenio Rodriguez -- the blind tres virtuoso known as "El Ciego Maravilloso" -- expanded the traditional son septeto into the conjunto format by adding congas, a piano, and a multi-trumpet horn section, creating the template for modern salsa. Simultaneously, Perez Prado and Damaso Perez Prado took the syncopated "new rhythm" section of the danzon and built it into mambo, a big-band dance music that conquered New York ballrooms. In parallel, rumba -- the percussion-driven, street-born music of Havana and Matanzas solares -- kept the most direct African elements alive through its three forms: the slow, narrative yambu; the flirtatious, medium-tempo guaguanco; and the fast, virtuosic, solo-male columbia.

By the second half of the 20th century, Afro-Cuban music had branched into dozens of directions. Machito and Mario Bauza fused Cuban rhythms with bebop to create Afro-Cuban jazz (cubop) in 1940s New York. Chano Pozo brought the sacred bata drums into Dizzy Gillespie's band, co-writing "Manteca" and permanently altering the course of American jazz. In Cuba itself, Los Van Van invented songo in the 1970s by weaving funk, rock, and electronic instruments into son structures, while Irakere merged Afro-Cuban folklore with jazz fusion. By the 1990s, NG La Banda and other Havana groups had forged timba -- an aggressive, harmonically dense, street-smart music that folded rumba, funk, hip-hop, and jazz into the son framework. The 1997 Buena Vista Social Club album, recorded with veteran musicians in Havana's EGREM studios, sold over eight million copies and introduced a global audience to the classic son and bolero repertoire, proving that Afro-Cuban music's emotional depth transcended language, geography, and generation.

## Characteristics

- **Clave as organizing principle**: All Afro-Cuban music is governed by the clave -- a two-bar rhythmic cell in either 2-3 (son clave) or 3-2 (rumba clave) orientation. Every instrument, vocal phrase, and arrangement decision aligns to or plays against this pattern. Playing "in clave" is not optional; crossing the clave (placing accents on the wrong side) is the fundamental error in this music.
- **Layered polyrhythm**: Multiple independent rhythmic parts interlock to create a composite groove. Congas play tumbaos (repeating patterns with open tones and slaps), timbales provide cascara (shell pattern) and bell, bongos add martillo patterns, and claves anchor the whole structure. No single instrument carries the full rhythm -- the groove exists only in the ensemble interaction.
- **Call-and-response (coro-soneo)**: A lead singer (sonero) improvises melodic and lyrical variations while a chorus (coro) repeats a fixed refrain. The tension between the spontaneous soneo and the anchoring coro drives the montuno section of son and salsa, building energy through accumulation rather than chord changes.
- **Tumbao and montuno patterns**: The piano plays repeating ostinato figures called montunos (or guajeos) that outline the harmony while locking to the clave. The bass plays its own interlocking tumbao, often anticipating beat one by landing on the "and" of beat four. These interlocking patterns create the rhythmic propulsion that defines the groove.
- **Afro-religious percussion roots**: The bata drums (iya, itotele, okonkolo) of Yoruba/Lucumi Santeria ceremonies, the yuka drums of Congolese Palo traditions, and the Abakua biankomeko drums all feed directly into secular Afro-Cuban music. Rumba's three-drum setup (tumba, segundo/tres-dos, quinto) mirrors ceremonial drum hierarchies.
- **Brass and tres as melodic voices**: The Cuban tres (a six-string, three-course instrument) carries melodic guajeos in son and son montuno. In larger ensembles, trumpet sections deliver punchy, syncopated figures (mambos) that punctuate the arrangement. Trombones took on a larger role in 1970s salsa and became central to timba's aggressive sound.

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: ABAB or AABB in verse sections, with the coro (chorus) typically a short, memorable phrase repeated as a refrain. The montuno section favors improvised soneo over the repeating coro rather than structured verse.
- **Rhyme quality**: Spanish-language rhyming conventions. Consonant rhyme (rima consonante) and assonance (rima asonante) are both standard. Rhythmic fit to the clave matters more than rhyme precision.
- **Verse structure**: Verses set up the narrative or theme, then the montuno section (coro-soneo) takes over for the second half of the song, building energy through vocal improvisation. Many Afro-Cuban songs spend more time in the montuno than in structured verses.
- **Key rule**: Lyrics must lock to the clave. Stressed syllables fall on clave strokes; phrasing breathes with the rhythmic cycle, not against it. In rumba, the lead singer (diana) opens with abstract, melismatic vocalization before the narrative lyrics begin.
- **Avoid**: Phrasing that fights the clave or ignores the rhythmic pocket. Overly wordy verses that leave no room for the coro-soneo interplay. Treating Afro-Cuban lyrics like standard pop verse-chorus -- the montuno is the destination, not a bridge.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 90-180 BPM depending on style. Leave space for coro responses and instrumental breaks. In son and salsa, the montuno section should breathe -- short soneo phrases alternating with coro. Topics: 1 per verse, with the coro providing the emotional or thematic anchor.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | BPM Range | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Son Cubano** | The foundational Afro-Cuban genre, born in eastern Cuba's Oriente province in the late 19th century. Blends Spanish guitar and vocal traditions with African rhythmic patterns organized around the son clave. The classic septeto format (tres, guitar, bass, bongos, maracas, claves, trumpet) defined the golden age. Arsenio Rodriguez expanded it into the conjunto format in the 1940s, adding congas, piano, and multiple trumpets. | 120-160 | Arsenio Rodriguez, Septeto Nacional, Trio Matamoros, Compay Segundo |
| **Mambo** | Big-band dance music developed from the danzon's syncopated final section. Perez Prado popularized it in Mexico City and New York in the late 1940s-50s with driving brass arrangements, shouted interjections, and explosive energy. Tito Puente and Machito developed a more jazz-inflected New York mambo style. | 140-200 | Perez Prado, Tito Puente, Machito & His Afro-Cubans, Beny More |
| **Rumba** | Percussion-driven street music born in the solares of Havana and Matanzas in the 1880s among poor Afro-Cuban communities. Three main forms: yambu (slow, narrative, no vacunao), guaguanco (medium, flirtatious couple dance with vacunao), and columbia (fast, virtuosic solo male dance in 6/8). UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2016. | 80-180 | Los Munequitos de Matanzas, Yoruba Andabo, Clave y Guaguanco |
| **Guaguanco** | The most popular form of rumba, a medium-tempo couple dance built on seduction and challenge. The male dancer attempts the vacunao (a pelvic thrust gesture symbolizing conquest) while the female deflects. Polyrhythmic drumming on tumbadora, segundo, and quinto drives the energy, with the quinto improvising against the fixed patterns. | 90-130 | Los Munequitos de Matanzas, Clave y Guaguanco, Yoruba Andabo |
| **Son Montuno** | The sophisticated evolution of son cubano developed by Arsenio Rodriguez in the 1940s. Extended the montuno section with complex horn arrangements, piano guajeos, and multi-layered percussion. The direct precursor to salsa -- Rodriguez's conjunto format and arranging approach became the blueprint that Fania-era musicians built on. | 130-170 | Arsenio Rodriguez, Chappotin y Sus Estrellas, Roberto Faz |
| **Timba** | Contemporary Cuban dance music that emerged in the 1990s, fusing son, rumba, funk, hip-hop, and jazz into a dense, aggressive, rhythmically complex style. Features gear-shifting tempo changes, complex conga patterns drawing from rumba, and street-level lyrical attitude. Born from the innovations of Los Van Van (songo) and Irakere (Afro-Cuban jazz fusion). | 130-180 | Los Van Van, NG La Banda, Charanga Habanera, Manolin "El Medico de la Salsa" |
| **Songo** | Rhythmic style created by Los Van Van percussionist Jose Luis Quintana ("Changuito") in the 1970s at the direction of bandleader Juan Formell. Blends son's clave framework with funk, rock, and pop elements, incorporating drum kit, synthesizers, and electric bass alongside traditional percussion. The rhythmic foundation from which timba later grew. | 110-150 | Los Van Van, Adalberto Alvarez y Su Son |
| **Afro-Cuban Jazz (Cubop)** | Fusion of Afro-Cuban rhythmic structures with bebop harmony and improvisation, born in 1940s New York. Machito and Mario Bauza's Afro-Cubans were the first ensemble to systematically combine Cuban rhythm sections with jazz horn writing. Chano Pozo's collaboration with Dizzy Gillespie brought sacred bata drum patterns into jazz. | 120-200 | Machito & His Afro-Cubans, Dizzy Gillespie, Chano Pozo, Irakere, Chucho Valdes |
| **Danzon** | Cuba's official national dance from the late 19th century, derived from European contradanse filtered through Haitian and Cuban traditions. Formal, structured, and elegant, with a signature charanga instrumentation (flute, violins, piano, timbales, guiro, bass). The danzon's syncopated final section (the "nuevo ritmo") became the seed from which mambo grew. | 100-130 | Orquesta Aragon, Antonio Arcano y Sus Maravillas, Enrique Jorrin |
| **Cha-Cha-Cha** | Developed by violinist and composer Enrique Jorrin in the early 1950s as a slowed-down, simplified mambo that was easier to dance. Named for the shuffling sound dancers' feet made on the floor. Bright, melodic, and accessible, with the charanga format (flute and violin lead) giving it a lighter feel than brass-heavy mambo. | 110-130 | Orquesta Aragon, Enrique Jorrin, Orquesta America |
| **Bolero Cubano** | Romantic ballad form originating in Santiago de Cuba in the late 19th century. Slow tempo, guitar-based (later with full orchestration), and focused on themes of love, loss, and longing. Cuban bolero influenced the entire Latin American romantic music tradition. The trovador (troubadour) singing style emphasizes emotional intimacy. | 60-90 | Beny More, Trio Matamoros, Omara Portuondo, Ibrahim Ferrer |
| **Latin (cross-reference)** | The broader Latin genre entry covers salsa, bachata, reggaeton, cumbia, and other styles that evolved from or alongside Afro-Cuban foundations. See [Latin](../latin/README.md) for the full ecosystem. | -- | -- |

## Reference Tracks

1. **Don Azpiazu & His Havana Casino Orchestra - "El Manisero" (The Peanut Vendor)** - The song that started it all internationally. Moises Simons' son-pregon, imitating a peanut vendor's street cry, became the first million-selling Cuban recording in the US when Don Azpiazu recorded it for RCA Victor in 1930. The performance introduced American audiences to congas, bongos, claves, and maracas, and launched the "rumba craze" that lasted through the 1940s.

2. **Arsenio Rodriguez - "El Reloj de Pastora"** - A masterclass in son montuno from the man who invented the form. Rodriguez's tres carries the melodic guajeo while his expanded conjunto -- congas, piano, multi-trumpet section -- builds the layered rhythmic architecture that would become salsa's blueprint. Every element locks to the clave with the precision of a Swiss watch.

3. **Perez Prado - "Mambo No. 5"** - The mambo at its most explosive. Prado's brass section hits like a freight train over a driving rhythm section, punctuated by his trademark vocal grunts. The arrangement demonstrates mambo's transformation of Cuban rhythm into a big-band spectacle designed for packed dance floors.

4. **Beny More - "Bonito y Sabroso"** - "El Barbaro del Ritmo" at his most commanding, leading his Banda Gigante through a son montuno that showcases his extraordinary vocal range and rhythmic flexibility. More shifts effortlessly between precise phrasing and improvised soneo, demonstrating why he remains Cuba's most revered popular singer.

5. **Tito Puente - "Oye Como Va"** - Cha-cha-cha perfection from the timbales master. The organ vamp and Puente's timbales drive a groove so infectious that Carlos Santana would later rock-ify it into a global hit. Demonstrates the New York Latin jazz approach to Afro-Cuban rhythm -- sophisticated, tight, and designed for the Palladium dance floor.

6. **Machito & His Afro-Cubans - "Tanga"** - Often cited as the first true Afro-Cuban jazz recording. Mario Bauza's arrangement layers bebop horn voicings over a full Cuban rhythm section, establishing the template for cubop. The track captures the exact moment when Cuban and American musical traditions fused into something neither could have produced alone.

7. **Los Munequitos de Matanzas - "Llora Como Llore"** - Rumba guaguanco from Matanzas, the cradle of rumba. Three drums, claves, and voices -- nothing else. The quinto improvises against the locked patterns of the segundo and tumba while the lead singer's soneo rides over the coro's refrain. This is Afro-Cuban music at its most elemental and powerful.

8. **Buena Vista Social Club - "Chan Chan"** - Compay Segundo's deceptively simple son, built on a descending bass line and a syncopated tumbao that locks into the clave with effortless grace. The 1996 recording became the opening track of the Buena Vista Social Club album and introduced millions of listeners to the warmth and emotional depth of classic Cuban son.

9. **Ibrahim Ferrer - "Dos Gardenias"** - Bolero cubano at its most intimate. Ferrer's voice, rediscovered at age 70 after years of semi-retirement, carries decades of lived experience in every phrase. The spare arrangement -- piano, bass, light percussion -- gives his voice all the room it needs to deliver a love song with devastating emotional precision.

10. **Celia Cruz & Johnny Pacheco - "Quimbara"** - The Queen of Salsa at full power. Cruz's commanding voice improvises soneo phrases over the coro with the confidence of someone who had been doing it for decades. The rhythm section locks into a son montuno groove while Pacheco's flute weaves through the arrangement. Pure salsa energy rooted in Afro-Cuban tradition.

11. **Los Van Van - "Sandunguera"** - Songo in action. Juan Formell's bass lines, Changuito's revolutionary drum patterns, and the band's trombone-driven arrangements demonstrate how Los Van Van modernized son without abandoning its clave foundation. The track bridges 1970s Cuban innovation and the timba explosion that followed.

12. **Irakere - "Bacalao Con Pan"** - Afro-Cuban jazz fusion at its wildest. Chucho Valdes' piano runs, bata drum patterns drawn from Santeria ceremonies, and rock-influenced energy collide in a track that obliterates genre boundaries. Irakere proved that Afro-Cuban sacred music and jazz improvisation could coexist and combust.

13. **Dizzy Gillespie & Chano Pozo - "Manteca"** - The founding document of Afro-Cuban jazz. Pozo's conga patterns and Gillespie's bebop trumpet meet over a riff co-written by both men and Gil Fuller. Recorded in 1947, it permanently altered the trajectory of American jazz by demonstrating that African-derived Cuban rhythms and bebop harmony were not just compatible but explosive together.
