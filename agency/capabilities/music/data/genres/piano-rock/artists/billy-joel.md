# Billy Joel — Deep Dive

## Overview

Billy Joel is an American singer-songwriter and pianist from the Bronx, New York, raised in Hicksville, Long Island. Active from the late 1960s to the present day as a touring artist, Joel released thirteen studio albums between 1971 and 2001, sold over 160 million records worldwide, and became the fourth-best-selling solo artist in United States history (85 million RIAA-certified units). He is a six-time Grammy Award winner, a Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1999), a Songwriters Hall of Fame inductee (1992), a Kennedy Center honoree (2013), and a recipient of the Library of Congress Gershwin Prize for Popular Song (2014). He is the first non-classical musician displayed in Steinway Hall, where his portrait hangs alongside Franz Liszt and Sergei Rachmaninoff.

Joel's music is built on classical piano training that began at age four, filtered through the rock and roll, R&B, jazz, and Tin Pan Alley traditions of mid-century New York. His songwriting combines extraordinary melodic gifts with narrative specificity and blue-collar storytelling — character sketches of bartenders, factory workers, Vietnam veterans, and Long Island suburbanites rendered with cinematic detail. His genre range is remarkable: piano rock, pop balladry, new wave, doo-wop, jazz, classical composition, and arena rock, often within a single album. Phil Ramone produced his greatest commercial and critical work across six albums (1977-1986), capturing his touring band's live energy with warmth and clarity at A&R Recording Studios in Manhattan.

Joel stopped making pop albums after River of Dreams (1993) but continued performing live for decades, culminating in a record-breaking monthly residency at Madison Square Garden (2014-2024) that spanned 104 consecutive shows and 150 lifetime performances at the venue. In February 2024, he released "Turn the Lights Back On," his first original pop single in over two decades. His touring career ended in early 2025 following a diagnosis of normal pressure hydrocephalus.

## Early Life and Pre-Solo Career

- **Born:** May 9, 1949, in the Bronx, New York City
- **Raised:** Hicksville, Long Island, New York (middle-class suburb)
- **Parents:** Howard Joel (born Helmut Joel, German-Jewish immigrant) and Rosalind Nyman Joel (English-Jewish immigrant). Howard was a classically trained pianist and engineer; the parents divorced when Billy was young.
- **Piano training:** Began lessons at age four at his mother's insistence. Studied classical piano — Bach, Beethoven, Mozart — with discipline and technical rigor. This classical foundation gave Joel an instinctive command of structure, harmony, and melody that would underpin his entire career.
- **The Beatles moment:** Like countless musicians of his generation, seeing the Beatles on The Ed Sullivan Show catalyzed his desire to pursue popular music. At 14, he began playing in bands.
- **The Echoes / Lost Souls (mid-1960s):** Joel's first band, playing British Invasion covers. Later renamed the Lost Souls.
- **The Hassles (1967-1969):** Joel and drummer Jonathan Small joined this Long Island pop band at age 18. Two albums for United Artists — The Hassles (1967) and Hour of the Wolf (1969) — both commercially unsuccessful.
- **Attila (1969-1970):** Heavy rock duo with Jonathan Small. One self-titled album on Epic Records (1970). Disbanded when Joel began an affair with Small's wife, Elizabeth, whom he later married.
- **Depression and hospitalization (1970):** After losing his record deal, Joel suffered severe depression and was admitted to Meadowbrook Hospital following a suicide attempt (ingesting furniture polish). His farewell note became the basis for the song "Tomorrow Is Today."

## Key Collaborators

### Phil Ramone — Producer (1977-1986)

The most important creative partnership of Joel's career. Ramone produced six consecutive albums: The Stranger (1977), 52nd Street (1978), Glass Houses (1980), The Nylon Curtain (1982), An Innocent Man (1983), and The Bridge (1986). Joel and Ramone first met when Joel played Carnegie Hall in 1976; they had dinner at Fontana di Trevi, an Italian restaurant across the street, which inspired "Scenes from an Italian Restaurant."

Joel had previously worked with producer Jim Guercio (who wanted him to use Elton John's band) and sought out George Martin (who preferred session players over Joel's touring band). Ramone was the first producer who loved the energy of Joel's live band and wanted to capture it in the studio. Their sessions at A&R Recording Studios (corner of 52nd Street and 7th Avenue in Manhattan) produced some of the era's best-sounding records, using a Neve 8068 console, Studer tape machines, Pultec EQP-1As, and four Fairchild compressors. Ramone typically used a Beyerdynamic M160 on Joel's vocals.

Their partnership earned Grammy Awards for Record of the Year ("Just the Way You Are," 1979) and Album of the Year (52nd Street, 1980). Joel later inducted Ramone into the Songwriters' Hall of Fame in 2010.

### The Lords of 52nd Street — Core Band (~1975-1989)

Joel's classic backing band, named by Phil Ramone, featured musicians recruited from a Long Island band called Topper:

- **Liberty DeVitto** — Drums (1975-2005). Aggressive, hard-hitting New York drummer. Doug Stegmeyer recommended him because Joel wanted "a New York-type drummer, aggressive and hard hitting." Played on every album from Turnstiles through River of Dreams. Discharged from Joel's touring band in 2006 for undisclosed reasons. Eventually reconciled with Joel in 2020; Joel wrote the foreword for DeVitto's memoir.
- **Doug Stegmeyer** — Bass (1975-1989). Dubbed "The Sergeant of the Billy Joel Band." Recruited from the band Topper. Left (reportedly forced out) before Storm Front in 1989. Died by suicide in August 1995 at his Long Island home.
- **Russell Javors** — Guitar (1975-1989). Childhood friend of DeVitto. Left alongside Stegmeyer before Storm Front.
- **Richie Cannata** — Saxophone, keyboards (1975-1981). Added jazz and R&B textures to Joel's sound. His saxophone is featured prominently on The Stranger and 52nd Street.

In 2014, DeVitto, Cannata, and Javors (with Stegmeyer inducted posthumously) entered the Long Island Music Hall of Fame. They subsequently formed the Lords of 52nd Street as a touring act.

### Other Notable Collaborators

- **Mick Jones** (Foreigner) — Produced Storm Front (1989)
- **Danny Kortchmar** — Produced River of Dreams (1993)
- **Phil Woods** — Jazz saxophonist who played the iconic solo on "Just the Way You Are"
- **Mark Rivera** — Saxophone, multi-instrumentalist (1982-present). Joel's longest-serving band member.
- **Richard Hyung-ki Joo** — British-Korean pianist who performed Joel's classical compositions on Fantasies & Delusions (2001)

## Albums

### Cold Spring Harbor (1971)

- **Label:** Family Productions
- **Producer:** Artie Ripp
- **Sound:** Singer-songwriter piano rock. Named for Cold Spring Harbor, a Long Island hamlet.
- **The mastering disaster:** A production error sped up the master tapes by approximately 10%, making Joel's voice sound unnaturally high-pitched — "like a chipmunk" in his own words. The botched release effectively killed the album commercially. Reissued in corrected form by Columbia in 1983.
- **Contractual nightmare:** Joel's deal with Artie Ripp's Family Productions proved exploitative and reportedly cost millions to escape. This experience shaped Joel's lifelong wariness of the music business.
- **Key track:** "She's Got a Way" — later re-recorded live for Songs in the Attic (1981) and became a hit.

### Piano Man (1973)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Michael Stewart
- **Sound:** Piano-driven folk-rock, singer-songwriter. Warm, story-driven, waltz-time signatures.
- **Context:** Joel had signed with Columbia after a live radio broadcast on WMMR-FM Philadelphia in 1972, where "Captain Jack" became the station's most-requested track and an underground East Coast hit.
- **Key tracks:** "Piano Man" (the signature song — waltz-time piano ballad narrating characters in a piano bar, became the genre's anthem), "Captain Jack" (unflinching suburban drug narrative)
- **Significance:** Established Joel's identity as a narrative songwriter and piano-playing rock musician. "Piano Man" became one of the most recognizable songs in popular music.

### Streetlife Serenade (1974)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Michael Stewart
- **Sound:** More polished pop-rock. Los Angeles sessions (Joel had relocated to LA).
- **Key tracks:** "The Entertainer" (sardonic commentary on the music industry), "Streetlife Serenader"
- **Notes:** Joel was dissatisfied with the LA production sound and the album's direction. Considered a transitional work.

### Turnstiles (1976)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Billy Joel (self-produced)
- **Sound:** New York-centric piano rock. Joel returned to New York and assembled his touring band for the first time in the studio.
- **Context:** Joel fired producer Jim Guercio (who wanted to use Elton John's band members) and self-produced. This was the first album with Liberty DeVitto, Doug Stegmeyer, Russell Javors, and Richie Cannata.
- **Key tracks:** "New York State of Mind" (jazz-inflected love letter to New York City — Joel described it as a "Promethean moment" of inspiration), "Prelude/Angry Young Man" (rapid-fire piano figure inspired by playing the drum part from the Surfaris' "Wipe Out" on piano), "Say Goodbye to Hollywood" (Phil Spector-esque wall of sound), "Summer, Highland Falls"
- **Significance:** First album with the classic band. New York as thematic center established. Artistic breakthrough even if not yet a commercial one.

### The Stranger (1977)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone
- **Recorded:** A&R Recording Studios, New York City, July-August 1977
- **Sound:** Piano-driven pop-rock with jazz and R&B sophistication. Warm, organic production capturing the band's live energy. Phil Woods' saxophone adds jazz elegance.
- **Key tracks:** "Scenes from an Italian Restaurant" (seven-minute multi-movement piano rock epic — jazz piano opening shifts into driving rock narrative), "Just the Way You Are" (Grammy Record of the Year and Song of the Year — the famous sax solo was spliced from six different takes by Ramone using analog tape and razor blades), "Movin' Out (Anthony's Song)" (blue-collar frustration set to driving piano), "Only the Good Die Young" (controversial uptempo rock about Catholic girls), "Vienna" (waltz-time meditation on patience)
- **Commercial performance:** Columbia's best-selling release at the time. Over 10 million copies sold. 12x Platinum (Diamond) in the US. Four Top-25 singles.
- **Significance:** Joel's artistic and commercial breakthrough. Established him as a household name. The definitive Joel album for many fans.

### 52nd Street (1978)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone
- **Recorded:** A&R Recording Studios, New York City
- **Sound:** Jazzier than The Stranger, with prominent saxophone, flugelhorn, and jazz-influenced arrangements. Named for the Manhattan street historically associated with jazz clubs.
- **Key tracks:** "My Life" (defiant pop-rock anthem), "Big Shot" (biting character sketch of a socialite), "Honesty" (power ballad), "Zanzibar" (jazz-rock fusion with trumpet solo by Freddie Hubbard)
- **Commercial performance:** Joel's first #1 on the Billboard 200. Grammy Award for Album of the Year (1980) and Best Male Pop Vocal Performance. 7x Platinum in the US.
- **Historic milestone:** In 1982, CBS/Sony released 52nd Street as one of the first commercial compact discs ever manufactured, alongside the launch of Sony's CDP-101 CD player in Japan.

### Glass Houses (1980)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone
- **Sound:** Hard-edged rock — Joel's most aggressive album. New wave and punk influences. A deliberate pivot away from the softer balladry that had earned him mainstream success.
- **Key tracks:** "You May Be Right" (raucous rock opener), "It's Still Rock and Roll to Me" (Joel's first #1 single on the Hot 100 — spent 11 weeks in the Top 10), "Don't Ask Me Why" (Latin-tinged pop), "Sometimes a Fantasy" (new wave-influenced)
- **Commercial performance:** #1 on Billboard 200. 7x Platinum. Grammy for Best Male Rock Vocal Performance.
- **Significance:** Proved Joel could rock hard without losing his melodic identity. Cover art — Joel throwing a rock through a glass house — was a visual statement of intent.

### The Nylon Curtain (1982)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone
- **Sound:** The most ambitious and experimental Joel album. Dense, layered production inspired by late-period Beatles (particularly Lennon's work). Social commentary wrapped in ornate pop arrangements. Synthesizers and studio techniques pushed beyond anything Joel had attempted.
- **Key tracks:** "Allentown" (Rust Belt economic despair — steel town losing its industry), "Goodnight Saigon" (harrowing Vietnam veteran narrative — written after extensive interviews with vets), "Pressure" (anxious synth-driven new wave), "Laura," "Where's the Orchestra?"
- **Commercial performance:** #7 on Billboard 200. 2x Platinum. Grammy nominated for Album of the Year.
- **Context:** Recorded during an extremely difficult period — Joel was in a near-fatal motorcycle accident and going through his divorce from Elizabeth.
- **Significance:** Critical reassessment point. Many critics who had dismissed Joel came around on the strength of this album's ambition and social themes.

### An Innocent Man (1983)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone
- **Sound:** A joyful homage to the music Joel grew up with in the 1950s and early 1960s — doo-wop, Motown, girl groups, early R&B, and Phil Spector's wall of sound. A deliberate about-face from The Nylon Curtain's darkness.
- **Key tracks:** "Uptown Girl" (Four Seasons-style pop — originally about Elle Macpherson, later associated with Christie Brinkley, whom Joel married in 1985), "Tell Her About It" (Motown-influenced — Joel's second #1 single), "An Innocent Man" (Ben E. King-style soul ballad), "The Longest Time" (a cappella doo-wop), "Keeping the Faith" (nostalgic R&B)
- **Commercial performance:** #4 on Billboard 200. 7x Platinum. Grammy nominated for Album of the Year. Three Top-10 singles.
- **Significance:** Demonstrated Joel's genre mastery — convincingly inhabiting 1950s and 1960s styles without pastiche. The contrast with The Nylon Curtain showcased his range.

### The Bridge (1986)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Phil Ramone (final Ramone-produced Joel album)
- **Sound:** Eclectic — rock, pop, jazz, blues. Guests include Ray Charles ("Baby Grand") and Steve Winwood.
- **Key tracks:** "A Matter of Trust" (straight-ahead rock), "Baby Grand" (duet with Ray Charles — Joel's idol), "This Is the Time," "Modern Woman"
- **Commercial performance:** #7 on Billboard 200. 2x Platinum.
- **Notes:** Last Joel/Ramone collaboration. Generally considered a minor Joel album, bridging (as the title suggests) his classic period and later work.

### Storm Front (1989)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Mick Jones (Foreigner)
- **Sound:** Arena rock with polished, sometimes overproduced arrangements. New band lineup — Stegmeyer, Javors, and Cannata were gone.
- **Key tracks:** "We Didn't Start the Fire" (rapid-fire recitation of 20th-century historical events — Joel's third and final #1 single), "I Go to Extremes," "The Downeaster 'Alexa'" (folk-influenced fishing village narrative), "Leningrad" (Cold War reflection)
- **Commercial performance:** #1 on Billboard 200. 4x Platinum. "We Didn't Start the Fire" reached #1 on the Hot 100.
- **Notes:** Mixed critical reception. Some found the Mick Jones production too slick, the songwriting more uneven than previous peaks.

### River of Dreams (1993)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Producer:** Danny Kortchmar
- **Sound:** Mature, mid-tempo, Beatlesque. Thematically ambitious — suburban malaise, spiritual searching, socioeconomic commentary. Returns to some of the introspective territory of The Nylon Curtain.
- **Key tracks:** "The River of Dreams" (gospel-influenced title track with handclaps and a cappella sections — Joel's last major hit), "Lullabye (Goodnight, My Angel)" (tender waltz written for daughter Alexa), "No Man's Land" (suburban sprawl commentary), "All About Soul"
- **Commercial performance:** Entered Billboard 200 at #1. 4x Platinum.
- **Context:** First album without Liberty DeVitto, Doug Stegmeyer, or David Brown. A different band, a different producer, a different era.
- **Significance:** Joel's final pop album. After its release, he announced he would no longer write pop music, though he continued touring extensively.

### Fantasies & Delusions (2001)

- **Label:** Columbia Records
- **Performer:** Richard Hyung-ki Joo (British-Korean pianist)
- **Sound:** Classical piano compositions. Joel composed the pieces; Joo performed them. Waltzes, nocturnes, inventions, and fantasies in a Romantic style.
- **Significance:** Joel's only formal classical album, fulfilling a lifelong connection to classical music. Reached #1 on the Billboard Classical chart. A curiosity in the discography but a genuine expression of Joel's musical identity.

## Musical Analysis — What Makes Billy Joel Sound Like Billy Joel

### Piano

Joel's piano technique is rooted in classical training (Bach, Beethoven, Mozart from age four) but deployed in service of rock, pop, and jazz. His playing is rhythmically aggressive — he frequently treats the piano as a percussion instrument. He has described the piano figure in "Prelude/Angry Young Man" as playing the drum part from the Surfaris' "Wipe Out" on piano. His dynamic range is enormous, from barely-there ballad touches to full-arm percussive attacks. Key characteristics:

- **Rhythmic drive:** Joel's piano propels songs forward rhythmically, not just harmonically. Staccato chord stabs, driving eighth-note patterns, syncopated grooves.
- **Blues and rock inflections:** Pentatonic scales, bluesy licks, boogie-woogie patterns filtered through classical technique.
- **Classical sophistication:** Neapolitan sixths, chromatic passing chords, dominant pedal points, Beethoven quotations ("This Night" borrows from the slow movement of the Pathetique Sonata). Tension-and-release philosophy drawn directly from classical training.
- **Dynamic extremes:** Intimate, barely-touched verses exploding into full-force choruses. Joel described his approach: "I grew up playing classical piano, so I have tension and release philosophy from that training. I hate boogie for the sake of boogie; it's got to be tension and release."

### Vocals

Joel's voice is a powerful, flexible tenor with a distinctive New York edge. He can deliver:
- Intimate, conversational ballad singing ("Vienna," "Lullabye")
- Full-throated rock belting ("You May Be Right," "It's Still Rock and Roll to Me")
- Soulful R&B and doo-wop ("An Innocent Man," "The Longest Time")
- Jazz-inflected phrasing ("New York State of Mind," "Zanzibar")

His vocal style is emotionally direct without being overwrought — a blue-collar sincerity that never tips into melodrama.

### Songwriting

Joel's songwriting operates on several levels simultaneously:

- **Narrative storytelling:** Character sketches with cinematic specificity. "Piano Man" catalogs piano bar regulars. "Scenes from an Italian Restaurant" follows Brenda and Eddie through marriage and divorce. "Allentown" documents Rust Belt economic collapse through specific detail.
- **Cinematic imagination:** Joel uses musical gestures the way film composers use scoring — the film noir soundtrack quality of "Prelude," the Coplandesque Americana of "The Ballad of Billy the Kid." He draws on the gestural vocabulary of film music to evoke images and sequences.
- **Melodic gift:** Joel's melodies feel inevitable, as if they already existed and he simply discovered them. This is his greatest compositional strength — melodies that lodge in the ear immediately and permanently.
- **Genre as palette:** Unlike artists who develop within a single style, Joel treats genres as expressive tools. The Nylon Curtain is late Beatles. An Innocent Man is doo-wop and Motown. Glass Houses is new wave rock. Each album's style serves its emotional content.
- **Avoidance of love-song cliches:** Joel has surprisingly few pure love songs. His lyrics tackle blue-collar frustration, suburban malaise, historical sweep, Vietnam trauma, spiritual searching, and music-industry cynicism more than romance.

### Production

The Ramone era (1977-1986) defined Joel's recorded sound: warm, organic, live-band energy with enough studio polish to compete on radio. Key elements:
- Piano centered in the mix, warm and present without over-compression
- Live-band feel — the touring band's interaction captured rather than constructed
- Jazz elements (saxophone, flugelhorn, trumpet) integrated naturally
- Dynamic range preserved — Joel's pianissimo-to-fortissimo capability intact

Post-Ramone albums had more varied production approaches (Mick Jones' arena rock sheen on Storm Front, Danny Kortchmar's mid-tempo warmth on River of Dreams).

### Genre Versatility

Joel's range across his discography is exceptional:
- **Piano rock:** The Stranger, 52nd Street, Turnstiles
- **Hard rock / new wave:** Glass Houses
- **Beatles-influenced art pop:** The Nylon Curtain
- **Doo-wop / Motown / R&B:** An Innocent Man
- **Arena rock:** Storm Front
- **Gospel-influenced pop:** River of Dreams
- **Classical:** Fantasies & Delusions

This range is not dilettantism — Joel inhabits each style with conviction and craft, adapting his piano technique, vocal approach, and songwriting to suit the genre.

### Cited Influences

Ray Charles (Joel's favorite singer and his Rock Hall inductee), The Beatles (particularly Lennon), Beethoven ("a god"), The Rolling Stones, Dave Brubeck, Bob Dylan, Randy Newman, Paul Simon, Phil Spector, Motown, Brill Building songwriters, Tin Pan Alley tradition. He described his own compositional approach as having "a classical sensibility."

## Lyrical Themes

### Blue-Collar New York

Joel's most distinctive lyrical territory. "Piano Man," "Movin' Out," "Allentown," "The Downeaster 'Alexa'" — working people struggling with economic realities, rendered with empathy and specificity. Joel grew up in a middle-class Long Island suburb and channeled Long Island and New York City life into his songwriting with documentary precision.

### Storytelling and Character Sketches

Joel is fundamentally a narrative songwriter. "Piano Man" catalogs a room full of characters. "Scenes from an Italian Restaurant" follows a couple through decades. "The Ballad of Billy the Kid" mythologizes the outlaw. "Captain Jack" unflinchingly portrays suburban drug culture. His songs are populated by specific people in specific places doing specific things.

### Disillusionment and Cynicism

"The Entertainer" skewers the music industry. "Angry Young Man" charts youthful idealism curdling. "Pressure" captures modern anxiety. "Allentown" and "No Man's Land" document American decline. Joel's cynicism is grounded in observation rather than pose.

### Nostalgia and Memory

An Innocent Man is pure nostalgia for 1950s music. "Keeping the Faith" celebrates teenage memories. "This Is the Time" and "Lullabye" meditate on time's passage. Joel's nostalgia is affectionate without being saccharine.

### Tenderness

"Just the Way You Are," "She's Got a Way," "Lullabye (Goodnight, My Angel)," "Vienna." Joel's tender songs avoid sentimentality through specificity and restraint. "Lullabye" — written for his daughter Alexa — is a genuine waltz that treats mortality with gentle honesty.

## Live Performance and Touring

### Madison Square Garden Residency (2014-2024)

In December 2013, Joel was named Madison Square Garden's first-ever music franchise, joining the New York Knicks and Rangers. Beginning in January 2014, he played one show per month "as long as the demand continues." The residency lasted over a decade:

- **104 consecutive residency shows** (record for most consecutive performances by any artist at MSG)
- **150 lifetime performances** at the venue (record for most lifetime performances by any artist)
- Over 1.9 million tickets sold to fans from all 50 states and 120+ countries
- Notable guests included Bruce Springsteen, Sting, Paul Simon, Tony Bennett, Olivia Rodrigo, John Mayer, Miley Cyrus, and Axl Rose
- The 100th residency show (March 28, 2024) was televised on CBS with guests Jerry Seinfeld and Sting
- Final show: July 25, 2024, with Axl Rose as guest
- Joel first set the MSG performance record in 2006 with 12 consecutive shows, earning a banner raised to the rafters

### End of Touring (2025)

Joel's touring career ended in February 2025 following concert postponements and a diagnosis of normal pressure hydrocephalus.

### "Turn the Lights Back On" (2024)

In February 2024, Joel released his first original pop single in over two decades via Columbia Records. He performed it at the Grammy Awards ceremony.

## Reference Tracks (Billy Joel Essential Listening)

### The Signature
- **"Piano Man"** (Piano Man, 1973) — The genre's anthem. Waltz-time piano, narrative lyrics cataloging piano bar regulars. Defined the image of a piano-playing rock musician for a generation. Harmonica intro, storytelling verses, singalong chorus.

### The Stranger Era
- **"Scenes from an Italian Restaurant"** (The Stranger, 1977) — Multi-movement piano rock epic. Jazz piano opening, tempo shifts, driving rock narrative across seven minutes. Joel's most ambitious single-track storytelling achievement.
- **"Movin' Out (Anthony's Song)"** (The Stranger, 1977) — Blue-collar frustration set to a driving piano riff and Richie Cannata's sax. Class consciousness as pop-rock.
- **"Vienna"** (The Stranger, 1977) — Waltz-time meditation on slowing down. Tender, patient, builds from solo piano to full arrangement. One of his most emotionally complex songs.

### Genre Range
- **"It's Still Rock and Roll to Me"** (Glass Houses, 1980) — Hard-edged new wave pivot. Aggressive piano over rock drums. First #1 single.
- **"Allentown"** (The Nylon Curtain, 1982) — Rust Belt economic despair filtered through Beatles-esque production. Social commentary at its most vivid.
- **"Uptown Girl"** (An Innocent Man, 1983) — Four Seasons-style doo-wop pop. Demonstrates Joel's ability to inhabit a genre completely.
- **"The River of Dreams"** (River of Dreams, 1993) — Gospel-influenced pop with handclaps and a cappella sections. Joel's final major hit.
