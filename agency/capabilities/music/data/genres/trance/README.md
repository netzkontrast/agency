# Trance

## Genre Overview

Trance is a hypnotic form of electronic dance music characterized by repetitive melodic phrases, driving four-on-the-floor beats, and dramatic build-ups and breakdowns designed to induce an altered state of consciousness. Emerging from the German techno scene in Frankfurt and Berlin in the early 1990s, trance synthesized elements of Detroit techno's mechanical precision, Chicago house's groove, and European synth-pop's melodicism into something transcendent.

The genre's name reflects its core purpose: to create a trance-like state through repetition, gradual evolution, and emotional manipulation. Unlike techno's industrial austerity or house's funk-derived warmth, trance prioritizes emotional journey - tension building through filtered sweeps and arpeggiated sequences, release through euphoric drops and soaring melodies. A typical trance track functions as a narrative arc, often spanning 7-10 minutes to allow proper development of themes.

Trance reached mainstream peak popularity between 1998-2004, when superstar DJs like Paul Oakenfold, Tiesto, and Armin van Buuren became arena headliners and festival draws. The genre fragmented into numerous subgenres as it evolved - progressive trance emphasized hypnotic minimalism, uplifting trance maximized emotional impact, psytrance explored darker psychedelic territory, and vocal trance courted pop crossover appeal.

After a commercial decline in the mid-2000s as electro-house dominated, trance experienced a revival in the 2010s with artists like Aly & Fila and Factor B championing a return to classic sounds, while newer producers integrated modern production techniques. Today, trance maintains a dedicated global following with major festivals (Transmission, Dreamstate, Luminosity) and institutions like Armin van Buuren's A State of Trance radio show (running since 2001) keeping the community vibrant.

## Characteristics

### Tempo & Rhythm
- **BPM Range**: 125-150 BPM (core), with psytrance/hard trance pushing to 145-160 BPM and progressive/dream trance sitting lower at 120-132 BPM
- **Foundation**: Four-on-the-floor kick drum pattern, typically punchy and present but not overwhelming
- **Hi-hats**: Open hi-hats on off-beats, closed hi-hats on eighth or sixteenth notes
- **Percussion**: Layered claps, snares on 2 and 4, ride cymbals, occasional ethnic percussion in Goa/psytrance styles
- **Groove**: Driving but hypnotic, emphasis on forward momentum rather than syncopation

### Instrumentation & Sound Design
- **Lead Synthesizers**: Sawtooth and supersaw stacks, pluck sounds, filtered square waves, characteristic "trance lead" patches with long attack and release
- **Pads**: Lush, evolving pad textures providing harmonic bed, often with slow filter modulation
- **Arpeggios**: Sixteenth-note arpeggiated sequences, often the signature hook of a track, gated and sidechained
- **Basslines**: Rolling, pulsing bass (often 303-influenced in early trance), sidechain-compressed against kick
- **FX**: Sweeping white noise risers, reversed cymbals, impact sounds, vocal chops, filtered atmospheres
- **Hardware Legacy**: Roland TB-303 (acid lines), Roland JP-8000 (supersaw), Access Virus, Korg MS-2000

### Vocals
- **Style**: Ethereal, processed, often female, treated as an instrument rather than focal point
- **Processing**: Heavy reverb, delay throws, vocoding, pitch-shifting, granular effects
- **Content**: Abstract, spiritual, or romantic themes; repetitive phrases that become mantras
- **Placement**: Typically in breakdowns or as melodic counterpoint, not continuous throughout

### Production Techniques
- **Filtering**: Extensive use of low-pass and band-pass filter automation to build tension
- **Sidechain Compression**: Pumping effect tied to kick drum, creating characteristic "breathing" sound
- **Layering**: Multiple synth layers creating thick, evolving textures
- **Automation**: Heavy parameter automation - cutoff, resonance, volume, pan, effects sends
- **Reverb/Delay**: Long reverb tails (3-6 seconds), ping-pong delays, tempo-synced delays
- **Build-ups**: White noise sweeps, rising pitch, increasing complexity, snare rolls

### Structure
- **Extended Form**: 6-10 minute tracks designed for DJ mixing, with 1-2 minute intros/outros
- **Intro**: Establishes groove, introduces elements gradually (kick, hats, bass, arps)
- **Build-up**: 2-4 minutes of increasing tension through filtering, layering, risers
- **Breakdown**: Stripped elements, focus on melodic content, emotional peak preparation
- **Drop/Climax**: Full arrangement returns with maximum energy, melodic payoff
- **Second Breakdown**: Optional, often more emotional than first
- **Outro**: Gradual element removal, preparing for next track mix

### Energy & Mood
- **Emotional Arc**: Journey from anticipation through tension to euphoric release
- **Atmosphere**: Otherworldly, transcendent, spiritually-tinged
- **Dynamics**: Significant contrast between sparse breakdowns and full drops
- **Intention**: Designed to induce trance-like states through repetition and emotional manipulation

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Loose — no traditional verse/chorus structure.
- **Rhyme quality**: When present, ethereal and uplifting. Near rhymes fine.
- **Verse structure**: Single hook phrase repeated at intervals. Often mezzo-soprano to soprano female vocals drenched in delay/reverb.
- **Key rule**: Themes of love, hope, transcendence. Dreamy vocal tones. No verse/chorus — one central hook repeated.
- **Avoid**: Dense lyrics. Grounded/mundane themes. Verse-chorus structure.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 128-150 BPM. Euphoric build-up needs vocal space. Simple, uplifting phrases timed to build/drop. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | BPM | Description | Characteristics | Reference Artists |
|-------|-----|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Classic/Euro Trance** | 135-145 | Original Frankfurt/Berlin sound from early-mid 1990s | Melodic but raw, Roland TB-303 acid lines, simpler arrangements than modern trance, rave energy | Sven Vath, Jam & Spoon, Dance 2 Trance, Cosmic Baby |
| **Progressive Trance** | 128-138 | Emphasis on gradual evolution over immediate gratification | Longer builds (8-12 minutes), hypnotic repetition, subtle changes, deeper grooves, less overt melody | Sasha, John Digweed, Way Out West, Bedrock |
| **Uplifting Trance** | 136-142 | Maximalist emotional approach, anthem-oriented | Soaring supersaw melodies, major key progressions, obvious emotional peaks, festival-ready drops | Armin van Buuren, Ferry Corsten, Aly & Fila, Factor B |
| **Vocal Trance** | 132-140 | Prominent sung vocals with pop sensibility | Full verse-chorus song structures, emotional lyrics, crossover appeal, radio-friendly edits | Above & Beyond, Dash Berlin, Gareth Emery, BT |
| **Goa Trance** | 130-150 | Spiritual, psychedelic, originated in Goa party scene | Eastern melodic influences, ethnic percussion, acid squelches, extended hypnotic arrangements, spiritual/cosmic themes | Astral Projection, Man With No Name, Juno Reactor, Hallucinogen |
| **Psytrance** | 140-150 | Darker evolution of Goa, psychedelic intensity | Complex layered percussion, twisted acid lines, aggressive basslines, minimal melody, mind-bending sound design | Infected Mushroom, Vini Vici, Astrix, Ace Ventura |
| **Full-On Psytrance** | 145-148 | Accessible psytrance with clear melodies | Driving basslines with melodic elements, Israeli scene dominance, festival-oriented energy | Yahel, Skazi, GMS, Talamasca |
| **Tech Trance** | 138-145 | Techno-influenced, harder edge | Minimal melodic content, emphasis on groove and drive, darker atmosphere, industrial touches | Tilt, Cygnus X, Marco V, Thomas Bronzwaer |
| **Hard Trance** | 145-160 | Maximum energy approach | Distorted kicks, aggressive synths, breakbeat influences, harder than mainroom but more melodic than hardstyle | Scooter, Yoji Biomehanika, Tidy Trax artists, Technikal |
| **Dream Trance** | 120-135 | Softer, atmospheric, downtempo-influenced | Lush pads, gentle melodies, introspective mood, ambient textures, Balearic influences | Chicane, Nalin & Kane, Ayla, York |
| **Balearic Trance** | 125-135 | Ibiza-influenced, sun-drenched atmosphere | Warm production, guitar elements, mediterranean feel, sunset/sunrise set appropriate | Chicane, Roger Shah, Sunlounger |
| **Orchestral/Epic Trance** | 136-142 | Cinematic production with orchestral elements | String sections, brass, choir samples, film-score influences, maximum drama | Tiesto (Just Be era), RAM, Simon Patterson |

## Reference Tracks

### Progressive Trance
- **Sasha - "Xpander" (1999)** - Defines progressive trance architecture; 10-minute journey through evolving textures and subtle melodic development; Sasha's signature track that bridged trance and progressive house
- **Way Out West - "Ajare" (1997)** - UK progressive trance at its finest; hypnotic groove with gradual builds; exemplifies the Deconstruction label sound
- **Bedrock - "Heaven Scent" (1999)** - John Digweed's alias; dark, driving progressive with haunting melody; peak-time weapon that defined an era

### Uplifting Trance
- **Armin van Buuren - "Communication" (1999)** - The track that established AvB's sound; classic uplifting progression with that supersaw lead; still played 25+ years later
- **Ferry Corsten - "Beautiful" (2002)** - Peak-era Dutch trance production; massive breakdown-to-drop structure; defines the uplifting anthem formula
- **Aly & Fila - "Beyond The Lights" (2017)** - Modern uplifting trance done right; proves the classic sound still resonates; emotional without being saccharine

### Vocal Trance
- **Above & Beyond feat. Zoe Johnston - "Good For Me" (2009)** - Anjunabeats emotional vocal trance blueprint; perfect integration of song and production; became acoustic concert staple
- **Tiesto feat. BT - "Love Comes Again" (2004)** - Peak commercial trance moment; BT's production genius meets Tiesto's festival instincts; defined 2000s vocal trance
- **Gareth Emery feat. Christina Novelli - "Concrete Angel" (2012)** - Modern vocal trance anthem; emotional storytelling; proved vocal trance viability in EDM era

### Goa/Psytrance
- **Astral Projection - "Mahadeva" (1997)** - Goa trance essential; Eastern spirituality encoded in electronic form; hypnotic 9-minute journey through Indian-influenced sound design
- **Infected Mushroom - "Becoming Insane" (2007)** - Crossover psytrance moment; aggressive yet accessible; metal guitar integration that divided purists but expanded audience
- **Hallucinogen - "LSD" (1994)** - Simon Posford's genre-defining track; pure psychedelic intensity; arguably the first "modern" psytrance track

### Classic/Euro Trance
- **Jam & Spoon - "Stella" (1992)** - Frankfurt trance foundation; simple but effective; shows genre's origins before complexity crept in
- **Sven Vath - "Accident in Paradise" (1993)** - German trance pioneer at his melodic peak; harder edge than later trance; important historical reference

### Dream/Balearic Trance
- **Chicane - "Saltwater" (1999)** - Dream trance perfection; Maire Brennan's vocals (Clannad) over Balearic production; sunset soundtrack defining Ibiza sound
- **Nalin & Kane - "Beachball" (1997)** - Summer anthem; filtered disco sample with trance sensibility; crossover hit that brought trance to mainstream

### Tech Trance
- **Cygnus X - "Superstring" (1993/2000)** - Essential tech trance; the "Orange Theme" mix became ubiquitous; pure groove over melody

### Anthems That Defined Eras
- **Tiesto - "Adagio for Strings" (2005)** - Samuel Barber's classical piece given trance treatment; controversial but undeniably impactful; peak mainstream trance moment
- **Paul van Dyk - "For an Angel" (1994/1998/2009)** - Multiple versions spanning decades; Berlin trance in its purest form; remained relevant through constant reimagining
- **Gouryella - "Gouryella" (1999)** - Tiesto + Ferry Corsten collaboration; ethereal trance at its most sublime; influenced countless producers
