# Trap Metal

## Genre Overview

Trap metal (also called ragecore, death rap, or scream rap) is a fusion genre that combines trap music's rhythmic structures and 808-driven production with heavy metal's distorted guitars, screamed vocals, and aggressive energy. The genre's roots trace to the mid-2010s SoundCloud scene, where artists like Bones, Sybyr (formerly BlackSmurf), and XXXTentacion began layering aggressive, screamed performances over trap beats with metallic textures. XXXTentacion's "LOOK AT ME!" (2015, re-released 2017) was an early viral catalyst, fusing mosh-pit energy with trap drums. The sound crystallized further when UK rapper Scarlxrd released "HEART ATTACK" (2017), widely considered the first pure trap metal track — a full commitment to screamed vocals over distorted 808s and metal-influenced production that drew a clear line in the genre's sand.

The genre's golden era ran roughly from 2017 to 2019, when several key releases established trap metal as a distinct movement rather than a novelty. Ghostemane's *N/O/I/S/E* (2018) brought industrial and black metal influences into the formula, creating a darker, more atmospheric variant. City Morgue — the duo of ZillaKami and SosMula — released *Vol. 1: Hell or High Water* (2018), arguably the genre's most important album, fusing guitar-forward production with brawling vocals and cultivating mosh-heavy live shows that bridged rap and metal concert culture. Scarlxrd's *DXXM* (2018) demonstrated the style's capacity for sustained album-length intensity. These artists collectively proved that trap metal was more than a SoundCloud curiosity — it had a dedicated audience hungry for music that lived at the intersection of hip-hop aggression and metal catharsis.

While trap metal's initial peak was brief, its influence has proven durable. The genre laid groundwork for the broader "rage" aesthetic in hip-hop, influencing artists like Playboi Carti (whose *Whole Lotta Red* incorporated aggressive vocal approaches) and the rage beat movement. Artists like KORDHELL, KSLV Noh, and INTERWORLD have pushed trap metal's production into gaming content and viral video territory, while Ghostemane and ZillaKami continue to evolve the sound. The genre maintains strong connections to the gaming and extreme sports content creator communities, where its high-energy, short-burst format serves as natural soundtrack material. Trap metal's lasting contribution is proving that metal and hip-hop can fuse at the structural level — not just as samples or features, but as a genuinely unified genre with its own production techniques, vocal approaches, and audience.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Heavily distorted 808 bass (often clipping and saturated), distorted electric guitars (both real and synthesized), blast-beat-inspired hi-hat patterns, industrial synthesizers, power chords, screamed vocal samples, bass drops; raw, minimal mixing is deliberate aesthetic choice
- **Vocals**: Screamed, shouted, and growled delivery dominates; alternation between screamed verses and melodic or spoken passages for dynamic contrast; guttural vocals influenced by metalcore and deathcore; some artists maintain rap-style delivery with metal intensity; vocal distortion and processing common
- **Production**: Distorted 808s pushed to clipping as primary bass instrument; guitar riffs (often palm-muted chugs or power chords) layered over trap drum patterns; industrial textures and noise elements; minimal and raw mixing style; heavy compression and limiting for maximum loudness; beat drops and breakdowns borrowed from EDM and metalcore
- **Energy/Mood**: Aggressive, cathartic, confrontational, chaotic, mosh-pit energy, adrenaline-fueled; oscillates between building tension and explosive release; anger and frustration as primary emotional registers; nihilistic but physically empowering; designed to provoke physical response
- **Structure**: Short songs (1.5-3 minutes); verse-chorus or verse-drop structure; builds toward beat drops and breakdowns; bridges often strip to minimal elements before explosive return; some tracks are essentially one sustained assault with minimal structural variation; intros may feature ambient or clean sections for contrast
- **Tempo**: Typically 130-170 BPM; some aggressive tracks push to 180+ BPM; half-time breakdowns at 65-85 BPM effective tempo; blast-beat sections can exceed 200 BPM; tempo changes within tracks common for dynamic impact

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB (couplet) — simple and percussive; rhyme serves rhythmic aggression more than lyrical complexity; shouted delivery makes complex schemes difficult to parse
- **Rhyme quality**: Simple end rhymes and near rhymes; hard consonant sounds (/k/, /t/, /d/, /g/) favored for percussive impact; rhyme precision secondary to energy and delivery intensity
- **Verse structure**: 8-12 bars; shorter verses common given the genre's brief song lengths; some tracks feature continuous screamed passages without clear verse/hook division; call-and-response or chant-style hooks
- **Key rule**: Energy over everything — lyrics must match the production's intensity; delivery should feel like it could start a mosh pit; every line should hit like a fist
- **Avoid**: Soft or introspective passages that undercut the aggression (unless deliberately used for contrast before a drop); complex wordplay that requires calm listening; overlong verses that fatigue the screamed delivery
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 140-170 BPM. Screamed vocals need space — Suno cannot sustain wall-to-wall screaming. Use 4 lines for maximum intensity sections. Breakdowns: 2-4 lines with different delivery. Topics: 1/verse, pure aggression.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Pure Trap Metal** | The core sound: screamed vocals over distorted 808s and trap drums; minimal guitar, emphasis on distorted bass as primary heavy element; the most hip-hop-forward variant | Scarlxrd, XXXTentacion (aggressive tracks), Jasiah |
| **Guitar Trap Metal** | Guitar riffs (real or synthesized) as primary melodic and rhythmic element over trap drums; closest to nu-metal and metalcore in instrumentation; mosh-pit-oriented | City Morgue (ZillaKami & SosMula), Cameronazi |
| **Industrial Trap** | Industrial music textures — noise, power electronics, mechanical rhythms — merged with trap structure; darker and more atmospheric; black metal and industrial influences | Ghostemane, Ho99o9, PRXJEK |
| **Rage Beats** | High-energy production designed for viral content and gaming; less emphasis on metal instrumentation, more on distorted bass and aggressive drops; often instrumental or near-instrumental | KORDHELL, KSLV Noh, INTERWORLD, NXGHTMVRE |
| **Witch House Trap** | Slower, more atmospheric variant incorporating witch house aesthetics; eerie synths, reversed samples, occult imagery; dark and hypnotic rather than explosive | Ghostemane (slower material), Crystal Castles influence |
| **Nu-Metal Rap** | Explicitly bridges trap metal with nu-metal's rap-rock fusion; turntable scratching, DJ elements, bounce-style rhythms alongside metal guitars; more diverse sonically | Ho99o9, City Morgue, Fever 333 (adjacent) |
| **Hardcore Trap** | Influenced by hardcore punk and beatdown hardcore; breakdown-heavy, gang-vocal chants, two-step rhythms merged with trap 808s; pit-focused live shows | ZillaKami (solo), SosMula, Ramirez |

## Reference Tracks

- **Scarlxrd - "HEART ATTACK"** — The first pure trap metal track by most accounts: Scarlxrd's full-commitment screamed delivery over distorted 808s, no compromise between rap and metal, just fusion; the track that drew a line and said this is a genre now

- **City Morgue - "33rd Blakk Glass"** — *Vol. 1: Hell or High Water*'s signature track: ZillaKami's guitar-driven production, dual screamed vocals, mosh-pit energy captured in recording; proved trap metal could sustain album-length intensity with genuine musicality

- **City Morgue - "SHINNERS 13"** — Guitar riffs meeting trap drums at maximum aggression: ZillaKami's metalcore-influenced playing over 808s, SosMula's chaotic energy; the track that made City Morgue the genre's benchmark live act

- **Ghostemane - "Mercury: Retrograde"** — Industrial trap metal: black metal blast beats, distorted bass, Ghostemane's alternation between screams and whispers; demonstrated the genre's capacity for atmospheric darkness beyond straightforward aggression

- **XXXTentacion - "LOOK AT ME!"** — The viral catalyst: before trap metal had a name, this track's raw aggression and mosh-pit energy introduced millions to the concept of screamed vocals over bass-heavy production; not pure trap metal but essential to its origin story

- **Scarlxrd - "BXILING PXINT"** — *DXXM*'s most intense moment: sustained screamed assault over crushing distorted production; demonstrated that Scarlxrd could maintain trap metal's intensity across a full project without dilution

- **Ghostemane - "AI"** — *N/O/I/S/E*'s centerpiece: industrial textures, shifting dynamics from whispered to screamed, black metal guitar tremolo over trap structure; the most musically ambitious track in early trap metal

- **ZillaKami - "CHAINS"** — Solo ZillaKami at his heaviest: metalcore guitar work driving the production, throat-shredding vocals, minimal but devastating; demonstrates the guitar-forward variant's raw power

- **KORDHELL - "MURDER IN MY MIND"** — Trap metal meets viral content: distorted production, aggressive energy optimized for gaming montages and short-form video; shows how the genre's intensity translates to content creator culture

- **Ho99o9 - "Bone Collector"** — Punk-rap-metal at its most unhinged: noise production, confrontational delivery, genre boundaries disintegrating; proves trap metal connects to a broader lineage of aggressive genre fusion

- **Jasiah - "Crisis"** — Accessible trap metal: alternates between melodic passages and screamed intensity, creating dynamic range within the genre's framework; showed trap metal could reach audiences who might find the purest form too abrasive

- **INTERWORLD - "METAMORPHOSIS"** — Cinematic rage: building atmospheric production that erupts into transcendent aggression; demonstrates trap metal's evolution toward emotional catharsis beyond pure anger
