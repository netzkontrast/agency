# Ragtime

## Genre Overview

Ragtime is a syncopated, piano-driven American musical genre that flourished from the 1890s through the 1920s, earning its name from the "ragged time" rhythmic technique of placing melodic accents between the steady beats of the left hand. Rooted in African American musical traditions including banjo music, cakewalks, minstrel show tunes, and march forms, ragtime emerged from the saloons, sporting houses, and social clubs of the Midwest — particularly Sedalia and St. Louis, Missouri — before sweeping the nation through sheet music sales that made it America's first commercially dominant popular music genre. Scott Joplin, the undisputed "King of Ragtime," elevated the form from barroom entertainment to composed art music, publishing "Maple Leaf Rag" in 1899 to become the first million-selling piece of sheet music by an African American composer.

Ragtime's cultural impact extended far beyond the piano bench. It was the soundtrack of the Progressive Era, accompanying the rise of Tin Pan Alley, the popularity of player pianos (which could reproduce ragtime's demanding technical passages mechanically), and the emergence of a new American popular culture that challenged Victorian propriety. Conservative critics condemned ragtime's syncopation as morally corrupting and racially dangerous, but audiences embraced it enthusiastically. Ragtime songs with lyrics became massive hits alongside the instrumental piano rags, and ragtime bands brought the style to dance halls and outdoor concerts. The genre directly influenced early jazz — Jelly Roll Morton straddled both worlds, transforming ragtime's composed structures into the more improvisational language of New Orleans jazz. By the mid-1920s, ragtime had largely been absorbed into jazz and popular song, but major revivals occurred in the 1970s (sparked by the use of Joplin's music in the film *The Sting*) and continue through contemporary performers who keep the repertoire alive.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Piano is the primary instrument, though ragtime bands featured combinations of banjo, guitar, mandolin, brass instruments, woodwinds, and percussion; ragtime orchestrations arranged piano pieces for dance bands and theater orchestras; the banjo was ragtime's earliest instrument before the piano dominated; player pianos (pianolas) were a major distribution medium
- **Vocals**: Instrumental rags are the genre's core, though ragtime songs feature conversational, often humorous vocal delivery with syncopated phrasing; coon songs (a problematic but historically significant subgenre) featured dialect lyrics; later ragtime songs adopted Tin Pan Alley pop conventions
- **Production**: Acoustic and natural — originally performed live in saloons, parlors, and concert halls; modern recordings emphasize the acoustic piano's full dynamic range with natural room ambience; period-appropriate recordings capture the bright, percussive attack of early 20th-century pianos
- **Energy/Mood**: Joyful, buoyant, and infectious; the interplay between syncopated right hand and steady left hand creates inherent rhythmic tension and release; ranges from lighthearted and playful to elegant and sophisticated; slower rags can be contemplative and lyrical
- **Structure**: Multi-strain form inherited from marches: typically AABBACCDD or AABBCC with each strain 16 bars; key changes between strains (often moving to the subdominant); an introduction of 4-8 bars; no improvisation in classic ragtime — fully composed and notated; trio section often shifts to a contrasting key
- **Tempo**: 100-140 BPM; Joplin famously cautioned "do NOT play this piece fast" — moderate tempos allow the syncopation to breathe; slow rags around 80-100 BPM; fast novelty rags push to 140-160 BPM but sacrifice the genre's characteristic elegance

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: AABB or ABAB — straightforward couplets matching the march-form strain structure
- **Rhyme quality**: Clean, conversational rhymes; wordplay and humor valued over sophistication
- **Verse structure**: 4-8 lines per verse, matching 16-bar strain lengths; refrains common in ragtime songs
- **Key rule**: Syncopation in the melody must be reflected in lyric stress — place emphasized syllables on the off-beats where ragtime accents fall
- **Avoid**: Overly complex vocabulary; lyrics that fight the syncopated rhythm; dark or heavy subject matter that contradicts ragtime's buoyant energy
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **4-6 lines/verse** at 100-140 BPM. Syncopated phrasing with room for rhythmic interplay. Light, conversational tone. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Classic Ragtime** | Formally composed piano rags in multi-strain form with sophisticated harmony; the art music of the genre, fully notated with no improvisation expected; Joplin's ideal of ragtime as serious composition | Scott Joplin, James Scott, Joseph Lamb |
| **Novelty Ragtime** | Flashy, technically demanding piano rags emphasizing speed and virtuosity over elegance; emerged in the 1920s as pianists pushed tempos and added crowd-pleasing tricks; sometimes called "novelty piano" | Zez Confrey, Felix Arndt, Roy Bargy |
| **Ragtime Song** | Vocal ragtime with syncopated melodies over ragtime accompaniment; dominated Tin Pan Alley in the 1900s-1910s; Irving Berlin's "Alexander's Ragtime Band" (1911) was the biggest hit despite being barely syncopated | Irving Berlin, Ben Harney, Ernest Hogan |
| **Ragtime Band / Orchestral Ragtime** | Piano rags arranged for ensembles — brass bands, dance orchestras, theater pit bands; brought ragtime to audiences beyond the piano parlor; John Philip Sousa's band performed ragtime arrangements | John Philip Sousa (arrangements), Arthur Pryor, James Reese Europe |
| **Stride Piano** | Ragtime's direct evolution: left hand "strides" between bass notes and mid-range chords while right hand improvises more freely; bridge between ragtime and jazz piano; Harlem tradition from the 1920s | James P. Johnson, Fats Waller, Willie "The Lion" Smith |
| **Folk/Country Ragtime** | Ragtime adapted for guitar, banjo, and fiddle in rural settings; finger-picked guitar rags became a distinct tradition in the American South; less formally composed, more improvisational | Blind Blake, Blind Boy Fuller, Rev. Gary Davis |

## Artists

|--------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| Scott Joplin | Classic Ragtime | *Scott Joplin: Complete Piano Rags* (Joshua Rifkin), *The Entertainer* (various) | "King of Ragtime"; composed the definitive rags including "Maple Leaf Rag," "The Entertainer," "Elite Syncopations"; wrote the opera *Treemonisha*; insisted ragtime was serious art music; posthumous Pulitzer Prize (1976) |
| James Scott | Classic Ragtime | *James Scott: Classic Ragtime* (various performers) | "Crown Prince of Ragtime"; elegant, sophisticated compositions including "Frog Legs Rag," "Grace and Beauty," "Climax Rag"; more harmonically adventurous than Joplin; based in Carthage and Kansas City, Missouri |

| Eubie Blake | Ragtime/Stride | *The Eighty-Six Years of Eubie Blake* | Ragtime-to-stride transitional figure; "Charleston Rag" (composed c. 1899), co-wrote Broadway musical *Shuffle Along* (1921); performed until age 99; living link between ragtime era and modern times |
| Jelly Roll Morton | Ragtime/Early Jazz | *Jelly Roll Morton: The Complete Library of Congress Recordings* | Self-proclaimed "inventor of jazz" who bridged ragtime and jazz; "Jelly Roll Blues," "King Porter Stomp"; added improvisation and blues feeling to ragtime structures; documented ragtime history in Library of Congress recordings |
| Scott Hayden | Classic Ragtime | *Scott Hayden Rags* (various) | Joplin's student and collaborator; co-composed "Sunflower Slow Drag" with Joplin; graceful, melodic compositions; part of Sedalia's ragtime circle |

| William Bolcom | Ragtime Revival | *Bolcom Plays His Own Rags* | Contemporary composer who wrote new rags honoring the classic tradition; Pulitzer Prize winner; kept ragtime composition alive as a living art form |

## Reference Tracks

- **Scott Joplin - "Maple Leaf Rag" (1899)** — The defining ragtime composition and first million-selling sheet music by an African American; four contrasting strains with a key change to the subdominant at the trio; moderate tempo allows the syncopation to sing rather than rush; the template for all classic ragtime that followed

- **Scott Joplin - "The Entertainer" (1902)** — Became iconic through the 1973 film *The Sting*; lighter and more playful than "Maple Leaf Rag" with graceful melodic lines and elegant syncopation; demonstrates ragtime's charm and accessibility; the piece that sparked the 1970s ragtime revival

- **Scott Joplin - "Solace: A Mexican Serenade" (1909)** — Ragtime's lyrical side: a slow, habanera-influenced piece that proves the genre could be tender and sophisticated; tango rhythms merged with ragtime syncopation; demonstrates Joplin's range beyond the uptempo rag

- **James Scott - "Grace and Beauty" (1909)** — Technically demanding and harmonically rich, showcasing ragtime's sophistication at its peak; flowing melodic lines with intricate syncopation; demonstrates that classic ragtime could rival European art music in compositional ambition

- **Jelly Roll Morton - "King Porter Stomp" (1906/1923)** — The bridge between ragtime and jazz: Morton's composition retains ragtime's multi-strain form but adds blues inflections and improvisational swagger; later became a swing-era standard when Fletcher Henderson arranged it for Benny Goodman

- **Eubie Blake - "Charleston Rag" (c. 1899)** — One of the earliest rags composed, predating even "Maple Leaf Rag" in conception; demonstrates the raw, pre-publication ragtime style that emerged from East Coast parlors and clubs; rhythmically adventurous and technically demanding
