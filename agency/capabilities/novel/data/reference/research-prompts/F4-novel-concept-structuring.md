# F4 — How to Structure a Novel CONCEPT: 2026 Methodologies Survey

> **Audience:** Gemini Deep Research (or equivalent).
> **Deliverable expected:** ≤ 5500 tokens, cited markdown.
> **Self-contained:** all context Gemini needs is in this prompt.

## Question

What are the **canonical methodologies for structuring a novel CONCEPT**
— the phase BEFORE outlining a plot — and how do practitioners in 2026
actually shape the seed of a novel into a workable foundation?

A "novel concept" is the answer to: "What is this novel ABOUT, who is
it about, what does it argue, and what makes it worth writing?" It
precedes any beat sheet, scene list, or chapter outline. It's the
substance the outline gives form to.

Surface the **structured approaches** practitioners use, comparing them
across:

- **What artefacts** the methodology produces (logline, premise,
  character ladder, theme statement, world-bible, comp-titles, etc.).
- **What sequence** of artefact production it recommends (logline
  first? Character first? Theme first?).
- **What gates** the methodology uses to validate the concept is
  workable (the "is this story REALLY worth writing?" question).
- **What outputs** it hands off to outlining (a clean foundation, a
  proposal-ready document, a query-letter-ready premise).

## What Gemini needs to cover

### A. The concept-shaping artefacts

For each, give the canonical definition + how practitioners format it:

- **The logline / one-sentence summary** — the high-concept, often
  agent-pitch-ready single sentence ("a young wizard discovers he's
  the chosen one and must defeat the dark lord at boarding school").
  Origins (Hollywood), patterns (the "X meets Y" formula, the "When
  X, Y must Z, or else W" formula).
- **The premise** — the central argument or "what if?" of the novel.
  Distinct from the logline (premise = what the novel SAYS; logline
  = what HAPPENS).
- **The central question (dramatic question)** — what the reader
  wants answered ("Will Frodo destroy the ring?").
- **The theme statement / controlling idea** — Robert McKee's
  controlling idea; Lisa Cron's third-rail; the moral argument.
- **The character ladder** — protagonist, antagonist, supporting cast
  with their wants/needs/fears/lies (Truby ladder; Save-the-Cat
  template; Cron character ladder).
- **The character arc** — internal change blueprint (Maass; K.M.
  Weiland's "Creating Character Arcs").
- **The world bible / setting document** — for SF/F especially; the
  Brandon Sanderson laws of magic; the world-anvil approach.
- **The comp titles** — comparable published books that position
  the novel for agents/publishers (one for genre, one for tone).
- **The query letter / book proposal** — the agent-facing distillation
  (200–300 words).
- **The genre conventions checklist** — the obligatory scenes for the
  story's genre (Coyne's Story Grid genre obligations).
- **The story bible** (story-bible distinct from world-bible) —
  internal-author reference document tracking characters, settings,
  rules, timeline.

### B. The methodology-specific concept pipelines

How each major methodology orders concept-shaping:

- **Save the Cat! Writes a Novel** (Jessica Brody) — logline first,
  then the beat sheet's setup beats (opening image, theme stated,
  setup). Concept = logline + theme + protagonist's flaw.
- **Story Grid** (Shawn Coyne) — genre first, then global story,
  then internal-vs-external story, then obligatory scenes. Concept
  = genre + global controlling idea + obligatory scene list.
- **The Snowflake Method** (Randy Ingermanson) — one-sentence summary
  first, then expand to paragraph, then character summaries, then
  expand the paragraph to a page synopsis. Concept = the page synopsis.
- **K.M. Weiland's outlining method** — the premise sentence first,
  then character arcs (positive, negative, flat), then the brain-
  storming-with-questions phase. Concept = arc-anchored premise.
- **Donald Maass's emotional craft method** — start with the
  protagonist's emotional truth, the moment-of-essential-conflict.
  Concept = the emotional argument.
- **John Truby's 22-step + moral argument** — premise plus moral
  argument first, then character web, then story-world. Concept =
  the moral argument document.
- **Lisa Cron's "Story Genius" 11-question method** — what does the
  protagonist think life is about? what's the inciting incident?
  what's the misbelief? Concept = answer to the 11 questions.
- **Dramatica's pre-story** — throughline assignments, plot dynamics
  (Resolve/Growth/Approach/Mental Sex), genre / class selections.
  Concept = the storyform.
- **The traditional MFA "germ" method** — start with character or
  image, develop through journaling. Concept = a clear narrative seed.

### C. The validation gates

When does a methodology say "your concept is workable, you can outline
now" vs. "your concept needs more work"?

- **The "premise test"** patterns (Brody's beat-sheet test; Cron's
  story-question check; Maass's emotional-resonance check).
- **The 10–15 question gates** various authors use (Cron's 11; Weiland's
  pre-outline questions; the Truby moral-argument validation).
- **Workshop-feedback gates** — MFA workshop traditions; writer's-
  group concept critique; beta-readers BEFORE outlining.
- **Market validation gates** — comp-title checks, agent-cold-email
  tests, query-letter dry runs.

### D. The discovery vs. structure poles

Concept-shaping methodologies vary in how PRESCRIPTIVE they are:

- **Highly prescriptive** (Snowflake, Save-the-Cat) — fill in the
  template, the structure leads.
- **Inquiry-driven** (Cron, Weiland) — ask the questions, the answers
  reveal the structure.
- **Discovery-friendly** (Maass, McKee's "controlling idea") — name
  the spark, the rest emerges.
- **Theory-led** (Dramatica) — make the structural selections, the
  story-form constrains the rest.

### E. The 2024–2026 evolution

- **AI-assisted concept-shaping** — prompts for logline generation,
  premise critique, character-ladder elaboration (cite specific tools
  / prompts from 2024+ — see F3 for full landscape).
- **Genre conventions shifts 2022–2026** — what's changed in the
  obligatory-scenes lists for major genres (romance, fantasy, thriller,
  literary).
- **The "elevator pitch" inflation** — agents asking for shorter and
  shorter pitches; the rise of TikTok-style 30-second pitches.
- **Comp-title difficulty** — 2024+ market difficulty makes
  comp-title-finding harder; what's the methodology response?

## Method (verification rules)

- **Each methodology's concept pipeline** must be cited to a specific
  chapter / page in the canonical book (or page on the author's site).
- **Each artefact format** must show the canonical structure (e.g.
  "The logline format Save-the-Cat teaches is: <verbatim quote or
  paraphrase with citation>").
- **Each validation gate** must be sourced — not invented.
- **2024+ evolution claims** require 2024+ evidence — a 2018 blog
  post about "what agents want" is not current.
- **Distinguish methodology from author opinion** — many craft books
  contain both prescriptive methodology and author-specific taste.
  Flag taste as taste.

## Anti-patterns (Gemini should avoid)

- Treating every methodology's "10-step plan" as equally valid.
  Surface where they actually conflict.
- Listing artefacts that NO methodology actually requires (e.g.
  "story beats" — that's outlining, not concept).
- Confusing concept with outlining. The concept phase ends BEFORE
  beat sheets; F5 covers outlining.
- Citing query-letter / book-proposal patterns from pre-2022 — the
  market has shifted; cite recent.
- Treating "I had an idea in the shower" as a methodology. Sourceable
  methodologies only.
- Recommending the Hero's Journey as a concept-shaping methodology.
  It's a structure framework, not a concept-shaping one (though it
  can be misused as such).
- Skipping the validation gates section. The "is this workable?"
  question is critical and most surveys skip it.
- Confusing high-concept (pitch) with literary concept (theme). Both
  matter, both are different artefacts.
